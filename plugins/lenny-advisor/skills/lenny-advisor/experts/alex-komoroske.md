# Alex Komoroske

**Founder; formerly Head of Corporate Strategy at Stripe and longtime product/strategy leader at Google** | **Independent startup founder; previously Stripe and Google** | Expertise: product strategy, organizational design, AI product thinking, systems thinking

## Bio
Alex Komoroske is a deeply original product and strategy thinker who spent 13 years at Google across Search, DoubleClick, Chrome’s Open Web Platform, and Google Maps AR, then served as Head of Corporate Strategy at Stripe. He now builds a startup reimagining the web for the AI era.

His perspective matters because he consistently applies first-principles and systems thinking to messy, high-uncertainty problems: how products compound, how organizations distort reality, and how AI changes the basis of competition. He is especially useful when conventional product playbooks feel too rigid or too legacy-bound.

## Signature Frameworks

### Gardener vs. Builder mindset
- **When to use**: When building products, teams, or strategies in uncertain environments where compounding dynamics, ecosystems, or emergent behavior matter more than linear execution.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify opportunities that can grow on their own rather than only through direct effort.
  2. Look for seeds with compounding potential, such as ecosystems, network effects, or self-reinforcing loops.
  3. Plant many cheap, low-risk experiments instead of overcommitting to one grand plan.
  4. Watch for signals of organic growth or user pull.
  5. Incrementally invest more only in the ideas that show signs of accelerating on their own.
  6. Continuously seek disconfirming evidence and stop watering ideas that stop growing.
- **Example**: Build a small open-source tool in a few hours. If nobody uses it, the cost was low; if developers start using it and it attracts more users and developers, keep investing as the ecosystem compounds.

### 70/30 team portfolio for emergence
- **When to use**: When leading a product team inside a larger organization and you need both credibility with leadership and room for breakthrough work.
- **Steps**:
  1. Spend roughly 70% of team effort on clearly valuable, legible work that everyone agrees matters.
  2. Use that visible execution to create organizational trust and cover.
  3. Reserve about 30% for exploratory bets, weird ideas, and seed-planting.
  4. Let motivated team members pursue promising ideas they already have energy for.
  5. Protect early-stage ideas from premature scrutiny until they have enough signal to survive.
- **Example**: Let a junior PM pursue a seemingly silly idea if it has plausible upside and they are energized by it; even if it fails, they grow, and if it works, the upside can be disproportionate.

### Adjacent Possible + North Star
- **When to use**: When setting product strategy or roadmap in ambiguous environments where long-term ambition is needed but exact plans are unknowable.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define a low-resolution North Star 3-5 years out.
  2. Make sure the North Star is plausible to diverse stakeholders and exciting if achieved.
  3. Survey the adjacent possible: the small set of actions within arm’s reach that are feasible now.
  4. Choose the next step with the steepest gradient toward the North Star.
  5. Take the step, observe how the world reconfigures, and reassess the next adjacent move.
  6. Update the North Star gradually as reality changes, without jerking strategy around.
- **Example**: Instead of trying to leap directly to an end state, take safe, reasonable steps that each make sense on their own while still pulling toward a coherent long-term direction.

### Strategy salons / nerd clubs
- **When to use**: When you need cross-functional idea generation, strategic sensemaking, or a safe place for half-formed ideas to evolve without formal review overhead.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with a very small seed group of people who already enjoy discussing the topic.
  2. Give the group an intentionally nerdy, opt-in identity so only intrinsically motivated people join.
  3. Set explicit norms: collaborative debate, “yes, and,” and critique framed as curiosity rather than attack.
  4. Add new members slowly, prioritizing diversity of perspective and minimizing the chance of introducing a norm-breaking jerk.
  5. Actively feed the community with prompts, side conversations, and invitations to share ideas publicly in the group.
  6. Create recurring live moments with guaranteed quorum and follow up with notes that generate FOMO.
  7. Let the best ideas emerge bottom-up rather than steering the group toward predetermined conclusions.
- **Example**: At Google, he created “Navel Gazers” to connect people across 12 fragmented efforts in the same problem space, producing strategic insights that pairwise executive reviews would have obscured.

### Organizational kayfabe lens
- **When to use**: When trying to understand why large organizations drift away from reality, suppress bad news, or continue obviously flawed strategies.
- **Steps**:
  1. Recognize that organizations often run on shared fictions everyone acts as if are true.
  2. Notice how small status distortions compound as information rolls upward.
  3. Understand that people often hide bad news for self-protection and to avoid destabilizing the system.
  4. Create mechanisms for disconfirming evidence to surface safely and incrementally.
  5. Avoid forcing truth to arrive only as a catastrophic “ground truth button” moment.
  6. Design leadership norms that reward early bad-news reporting and reduce the cost of honesty.
- **Example**: A project marked green instead of yellow because a manager expects to fix it before the next review; repeated across layers, leadership ends up far from ground truth.

### Slime mold organizational design
- **When to use**: When designing or leading larger organizations that can no longer be steered like small startups.
- **Steps**:
  1. Acknowledge that coordination cost grows rapidly with the number of people involved.
  2. Decide whether to optimize for coherence or autonomy.
  3. If optimizing for coherence, accept slower pivots, more process, more program management, and more slack.
  4. If optimizing for autonomy, split into smaller semi-independent units and tolerate visible inconsistency.
  5. Drive the vehicle you actually have rather than pretending a big organization is still a sports car.
- **Example**: Apple prioritizes coherent integrated experiences, while AWS tolerates many different ways of doing things in exchange for autonomy and anti-fragility.

### Taste as differentiation in the AI era
- **When to use**: When building products or creating work in a world where AI lowers the cost of producing average-quality output.
- **Steps**:
  1. Assume generic execution is increasingly commoditized.
  2. Compare your output to what an LLM would produce from the same prompt.
  3. Identify what is distinctive, personal, and resonant in your perspective.
  4. Lean into the ideas, frames, and expressions that others find compelling and non-generic.
  5. Use curiosity and play to discover weird, native-to-the-medium possibilities rather than copying old playbooks.
- **Example**: WebSim is an AI-native, weird, compelling product that reveals new patterns, unlike simply slapping AI onto an existing SaaS playbook.

## Core Advice
- **When you face a disruptive new technology like LLMs**: Do not apply mature playbooks from the previous era by default; instead, play, experiment, and look for weird native behaviors the new technology enables — because disruptive technologies change hidden assumptions like cost structures and the basis of competition.
- **When you face AI product opportunities**: Design around the squishiness of LLMs instead of treating them like perfect oracles — because they often do roughly what you meant, not exactly what you said.
- **When you face pressure to become more valuable in the AI era**: Develop taste, curiosity, and a distinctive point of view rather than only becoming a more efficient generic operator — because standing out depends less on output volume and more on differentiation.
- **When you face nonstop meetings and reactive work**: Create protected reflection time each week to process notes, identify patterns, and turn repeated explanations into reusable artifacts — because deep thinking requires space.
- **When you face a large organization that seems disconnected from reality**: Build channels for bad news and disconfirming evidence to surface early and safely — because information gets distorted upward through self-protective behavior.
- **When you face uncertainty about which big bet will work**: Plant many cheap seeds and invest incrementally only in the ones that show self-reinforcing traction — because you do not need to predict the winner in advance if the cost of planting is low.
- **When you face skepticism about exploratory work on your team**: Use a 70/30 split: keep most effort on legible value creation and reserve a smaller portion for emergent bets — because visible execution creates trust and protects fragile ideas.
- **When you face roadmap planning in ambiguous markets**: Set a low-resolution North Star and then choose the next adjacent possible step that most strongly pulls you toward it — because this balances ambition with realism.
- **When you face the need for better strategic thinking across functions**: Create small opt-in strategy salons or nerd clubs with explicit “yes, and” norms and slowly add diverse perspectives — because low-stakes, high-trust environments are better for half-formed ideas.
- **When you face a task you keep procrastinating on**: Constrain the time available and use momentum from small wins rather than giving the task a large vague block of time — because tasks have activation energy.
- **When you face self-control or habit-building challenges**: Use clear always-rules instead of fuzzy sometimes-rules whenever possible — because bright-line rules reduce decision fatigue.
- **When you face career choices or project choices**: Do things that give you energy and that you are proud of — because energy increases output and pride prevents self-betrayal.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Great product work comes from strong top-down planning, precise execution, and controlling outcomes. → **Their view**: Design for emergence and garden systems rather than tightly building everything to plan — because compounding loops and ecosystems create outsized value.
- **Conventional**: In product and strategy, you should know the answer before acting and justify decisions with detailed forecasts. → **Their view**: Take cheap, reversible steps and respond to real signals — because forecasts create false precision in uncertain environments.
- **Conventional**: As organizations scale, leaders should keep steering them like startups. → **Their view**: Large organizations must be led like the vehicles they actually are: slower big rigs or swarms of smaller autonomous units — because coordination costs rise sharply with scale.
- **Conventional**: AI’s biggest value will show up first in obvious company-wide systems. → **Their view**: A lot of AI’s early value is individual, hidden, and hard for organizations to see — because people may not advertise that they’re using it.
- **Conventional**: The best way to use LLMs is to have them generate final answers autonomously. → **Their view**: LLMs are better as magical duct tape or a thinking partner — because they are powerful but squishy.
- **Conventional**: Professionalism means being serious and polished. → **Their view**: Playfulness, weirdness, and curiosity are often prerequisites for discovering genuinely new product patterns — because the interesting opportunities initially look alien.
- **Conventional**: Incremental steps are timid; bold leaps create transformation. → **Their view**: Transformative outcomes often come from a sequence of small adjacent moves guided by a coherent North Star — because big leaps in unknown environments are often imaginary.
- **Conventional**: Cliches are shallow and unhelpful. → **Their view**: Many cliches are profound truths people dismiss only because they were not ready to hear them — because timing matters.
