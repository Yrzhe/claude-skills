# Megan Cook

**Head of Product, Jira** | **Atlassian** | Expertise: product-management, leadership, remote-work, product-strategy

## Bio
Megan Cook leads product for Jira at Atlassian and has spent nearly 11 years growing with the company. Her perspective is especially valuable because she combines product leadership with hands-on experience as an analyst, developer, and agile coach, giving her a rare blend of strategic, technical, and team-culture instincts.

She is known for building high-performing product teams, launching new products inside a large company, and designing operating systems for remote collaboration. If you need advice on scaling product work without losing speed, candor, or customer focus, Megan is the kind of leader to listen to.

## Signature Frameworks

### Psychological Safety Through Structured Play
- **When to use**: When a team becomes overly formal, hesitant to speak up, or starts producing incremental ideas instead of bold ones.
- **Steps**:
  1. Notice signs of fear: polished feedback, less candid discussion, and reduced openness in meetings.
  2. Create deliberate spaces for low-stakes feedback and experimentation.
  3. Use smaller peer groups so people can share early drafts without intimidation.
  4. Normalize failure by having leaders share their own mistakes and learnings.
  5. Model vulnerability and openness as a leader.
  6. Repeat the practice regularly so people build the muscle of giving and receiving feedback.
- **Example**: Megan split her PM org into small peer feedback groups that meet every two weeks to review rough drafts, strategies, and experiments.

### Remote Work Operating System
- **When to use**: When teams are distributed and need to preserve productivity, connection, and decision quality without relying on office proximity.
- **Steps**:
  1. Intentionally bring people together a few times a year to strengthen relationships.
  2. Protect deep work by synchronizing calendar blocks across leaders.
  3. Treat meetings as problem-solving sessions, not status updates.
  4. Document decisions, assumptions, and project kickoffs rigorously.
  5. Use async video/audio updates for cross-time-zone communication.
  6. Keep one-on-ones short and frequent, with calendar flex for urgent issues.
- **Example**: Atlassian’s product team gets together about every six months, while the broader team meets in person about four times a year for work, workshops, and fun.

### Buy-In as a Journey, Not a Meeting
- **When to use**: When you need executive or cross-functional support for a product decision, especially one that affects multiple teams.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with the problem and the value, not the final solution.
  2. Bring affected stakeholders in early, before the proposal is fully baked.
  3. Share hypotheses, facts, and principles explicitly.
  4. Ask for the specific outcome you want from the meeting: decision, feedback, or help testing assumptions.
  5. Use a concise narrative: current situation, what changed, and implications.
  6. Bring strong supporting data and know it deeply.
  7. Iterate with stakeholders as the idea develops so they become advocates.
- **Example**: For a Jira homepage/template initiative, Megan involved every potentially impacted product team early and kept returning with designs, data, and user test results.

### Scrappy Validation for Internal or New Product Bets
- **When to use**: When launching a new product or major initiative inside a large company and you need to prove demand before scaling investment.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with a very small team and a narrow scope.
  2. Use stage gates to validate the idea step by step.
  3. Build prototypes and test with interested customers early.
  4. Show the pain and the opportunity visually and emotionally.
  5. Make it easy for other teams to help by reducing their burden.
  6. Keep updates frequent to maintain momentum.
  7. Expand only after the idea shows clear traction or product-market fit.
- **Example**: Jira Product Discovery moved through wonder, explore, make, impact, and scale, with investment increasing only as each stage proved out.

### Customer-Driven Product Expansion
- **When to use**: When deciding whether to create a new product line or adjacent offering from an existing product.
- **Steps**:
  1. Look for repeated customer behavior that signals unmet needs.
  2. Identify where existing users are hacking together workarounds.
  3. Validate that the adjacent use case is meaningfully different from the core product.
  4. Build a product tailored to that segment instead of forcing them into the original workflow.
  5. Use the new product as a learning ground for ideas that may later flow back into the core product.
- **Example**: Atlassian saw non-developer teams using Jira awkwardly and launched Jira Work Management to better serve those use cases.

### Think 10X and Three-to-Five Years Ahead
- **When to use**: When evaluating a feature, automation, or product idea that seems useful but may have broader strategic potential.
- **Steps**:
  1. Ask what the idea could become if it were 10x bigger.
  2. Check whether it could apply to more users, more products, or more workflows.
  3. Consider whether the current design is artificially limiting the opportunity.
  4. Use the long-term view to shape the product architecture and scope now.
- **Example**: Megan’s team built automation for Jira status updates, but she later realized it could have been a company-wide service across all products.

## Core Advice
- **When your team starts giving overly polished feedback and avoiding disagreement**: Create recurring, small peer feedback groups where people review rough drafts and are expected to critique constructively — because regular exposure lowers fear, builds trust, and makes candid feedback feel normal instead of threatening.
- **When remote work is making people feel disconnected**: Bring the team together intentionally a few times a year for work, workshops, and fun rather than trying to recreate office presence daily — because intentional in-person connection boosts both productivity and relationships for months.
- **When your calendar is packed with fragmented meetings and you can’t get into flow**: Block long, synchronized deep-work windows across your leadership team and protect them aggressively — because PMs need 3-4 hours to enter deep work and fragmentation destroys creative output.
- **When a meeting is really just a status update**: Move it async and reserve live meetings for problem-solving or decision-making — because meeting time is scarce and should be used for high-value collaboration.
- **When you need buy-in for a proposal that affects multiple teams**: Involve impacted stakeholders early, before the idea is fully formed, and keep iterating with them — because early involvement surfaces risks, builds advocates, and prevents late-stage surprises.
- **When you’re presenting an idea and want to seem credible without pretending to know everything**: State your facts, hypotheses, and unknowns clearly, and explain how you’ll test them — because openly exposing uncertainty increases trust because it shows rigor rather than false certainty.
- **When a product improvement is hard to fund because it isn’t flashy**: Start with a small, low-cost slice that can prove value quickly — because small bets are easier to approve and can create momentum for larger investment later.
- **When stakeholders don’t feel the urgency of a usability or quality problem**: Show the pain visually with prototypes, customer videos, and concrete examples — because visual and visceral evidence creates emotional buy-in much faster than abstract arguments.
- **When a new product idea is inside a large company with heavy process**: Give the team startup-like freedom and a smaller operating model until product-market fit is proven — because large-company process can slow down early validation and obscure whether the idea is actually viable.
- **When you’re tempted to add lots of people to a promising new initiative**: Resist scaling the team too early and keep the group small until the direction is validated — because too many people too soon slows learning and makes it harder to pivot.
- **When you’re evaluating a feature or automation idea**: Ask how it could be generalized to more users, more products, or a bigger platform opportunity — because the missed upside is often not the feature itself but the broader capability it could unlock.
- **When difficult cross-functional tensions keep getting postponed**: Schedule a recurring “fight club” meeting specifically for conflict and hard conversations — because a dedicated slot creates the right mindset and prevents small issues from becoming major problems.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Play and work are opposites. → **Their view**: The opposite of play is fear, not work. — because play creates openness, presence, and flow; fear makes teams cautious, incremental, and less innovative.
- **Conventional**: To get buy-in, you should show up with a polished final proposal and win the room. → **Their view**: Buy-in is a journey, not a single meeting. — because the best outcomes come from early stakeholder involvement, iterative feedback, and shared ownership.
- **Conventional**: Remote teams need more meetings to replace hallway conversations. → **Their view**: More meetings usually hurt productivity; remote teams need protected deep work and intentional async communication. — because calendar fragmentation destroys flow, while structured connection and documentation preserve both speed and alignment.
- **Conventional**: Big companies should apply the same process and standards to every new initiative. → **Their view**: New products need a startup-like operating model and different standards than the core business. — because early-stage bets need speed and learning, not the full weight of enterprise process.
- **Conventional**: A feature that ships and gets used is a success. → **Their view**: A feature can still be a failure if it doesn’t reveal or unlock the bigger opportunity. — because Megan’s automation feature worked, but she missed the chance to generalize it into a much larger platform capability.
- **Conventional**: Failure is something to avoid or hide. → **Their view**: Failure stories are often a hiring signal and should be discussed openly. — because how someone reflects on failure reveals vulnerability, learning ability, and growth mindset.
