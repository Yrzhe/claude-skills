# Itamar Gilad

**Product management coach and author of _Evidence Guided_** | **Independent; formerly Gmail, YouTube, Microsoft** | Expertise: product strategy, product management, experimentation, leadership

## Bio
Itamar Gilad is a longtime product leader who has worked on major products including Gmail and YouTube, and previously at Microsoft. His perspective matters because he has seen both the upside of disciplined product discovery and the cost of opinion-driven bets, including large-scale failures and successful iterative product work.

He is best known for advocating an evidence-guided approach to product development: define outcomes, test assumptions cheaply, and increase investment only as confidence rises. He is especially valuable when teams are stuck in roadmap theater, HiPPO decision-making, or feature-first planning.

## Signature Frameworks

### Evidence-Guided Product Development
- **When to use**: When teams are making product decisions based mostly on opinions, seniority, politics, or rigid plans rather than learning.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define clear user and business outcomes before committing to solutions.
  2. Treat ideas as hypotheses rather than plans.
  3. Gather evidence progressively through research, tests, and experiments.
  4. Increase investment only as confidence rises.
  5. Kill, pivot, or continue ideas based on evidence rather than conviction.
- **Example**: Gilad contrasts Google+ integration in Gmail, which was driven top-down and consumed massive resources, with Gmail's tabbed inbox, which was iteratively researched, tested, and validated before scaling.

### GIST Model
- **When to use**: When an organization wants a practical operating model for becoming more evidence-guided across planning, prioritization, discovery, and execution.
- **Steps**:
  1. **Goals**: Define the outcomes and metrics that matter.
  2. **Ideas**: Generate and prioritize hypothetical ways to achieve those goals.
  3. **Steps**: Break ideas into validation and implementation milestones that build evidence.
  4. **Tasks**: Execute the concrete work items in tools like Jira or Kanban.
- **Example**: For onboarding improvement, Gilad describes a goal like reducing average onboarding time, an idea like an onboarding wizard, and steps such as usability tests with mockups, prototype tests, and then an A/B test.

### Value Exchange Loop
- **When to use**: When setting company or team goals and trying to align user value with business value.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the core value delivered to users.
  2. Choose a metric that measures that value delivered (North Star metric).
  3. Choose a metric that measures value captured by the business (top KPI such as revenue or profit).
  4. Create a feedback loop between value delivered and value captured.
  5. Use both metrics to align teams and prioritize work.
- **Example**: Gilad cites WhatsApp measuring messages sent as value delivered, and Airbnb measuring nights booked as a strong example of a real North Star metric.

### Metrics Tree
- **When to use**: When teams need to understand what drives their North Star metric or top KPI and where to focus resources.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with the top user-value metric and top business metric.
  2. Break each into component drivers and sub-metrics.
  3. Map overlaps between the trees to find leverage points.
  4. Assign teams or ownership around important branches.
  5. Use the tree to estimate likely impact and improve alignment.
- **Example**: Gilad explains that a metrics tree helps teams see that improving a lower-level metric like activation rate may only have a small effect on the top metric unless it sits on a critical branch.

### ICE Prioritization
- **When to use**: When evaluating many competing product ideas and trying to prioritize more objectively.
- **Steps**:
  1. Estimate **Impact**: how much the idea could move the goal.
  2. Estimate **Confidence**: how strong the evidence is behind your impact and ease estimates.
  3. Estimate **Ease**: how easy the idea is to execute.
  4. Compare ideas using these dimensions rather than opinion or politics.
  5. Re-rank ideas as new evidence emerges.
- **Example**: Gilad recommends using ICE to let teams, not just managers, choose which ideas to test first, especially on a GIST board.

### Confidence Meter
- **When to use**: When teams overstate certainty about ideas and need a shared language for how strong their evidence really is.
- **Steps**:
  1. Place evidence on a scale from very low confidence to high confidence.
  2. Recognize that opinions, decks, and thematic alignment provide very weak confidence.
  3. Use reviews, estimates, and anecdotal data as modest confidence builders.
  4. Use market data, user research, and competitive analysis for stronger support.
  5. Use tests, experiments, and release results for the highest confidence.
- **Example**: Gilad argues that saying an idea is strong because it fits the company strategy or current trend like AI should count as very low confidence, while a successful test or experiment should count much higher.

### FACTR Validation Ladder
- **When to use**: When deciding how to validate an idea cheaply before fully building and launching it.
- **Steps**:
  1. **Assessment**: check alignment, model the business case, map assumptions, and review risks.
  2. **Fact-finding**: gather data through analytics, surveys, interviews, field research, and competitive analysis.
  3. **Tests**: run low-cost product tests like fake door, smoke, Wizard of Oz, concierge, and usability tests.
  4. **Experiments**: run controlled experiments such as A/B or multivariate tests.
  5. **Release results**: learn from staged rollouts, percent launches, holdbacks, and real launches.
- **Example**: For Gmail tabbed inbox, one early test was a Wizard of Oz setup where users saw a fake HTML inbox while humans manually sorted the top 50 emails behind the scenes.

### GIST Board
- **When to use**: When teams are overly delivery-focused, disconnected from goals, or trapped between roadmap planning and task execution.
- **Steps**:
  1. List a small number of team key results or goals.
  2. Add the current ideas being pursued to achieve those goals.
  3. Add the next validation steps for each idea.
  4. Review the board regularly as a team.
  5. Remove weak ideas, add new ones, and update steps based on evidence.
- **Example**: Gilad suggests teams meet around the board every week or two to discuss whether they are pursuing the right ideas, how goals are trending, and what validation steps are blocked.

### Outcome Roadmapping
- **When to use**: When traditional feature roadmaps are forcing teams to ship low-confidence ideas by fixed dates.
- **Steps**:
  1. Replace feature commitments with outcome commitments.
  2. State the result to achieve by a time period rather than the feature to ship.
  3. Keep solution space open while confidence is still low.
  4. Move to delivery commitments only once an idea has high enough confidence.
  5. Use evidence from steps and experiments to decide what gets built.
- **Example**: Instead of saying “launch feature X by October,” Gilad recommends saying things like “reduce churn by Q4” or “grow usage in India by a target amount.”

## Core Advice
- **When you face a founder or executive with a strong product opinion**: Do ask, respectfully, “Where’s your evidence?” and bring data or test results into the discussion — because evidence gives less-senior people a credible way to challenge authority and often changes minds more effectively than counter-opinions.
- **When you face unclear priorities and team misalignment**: Do define a North Star metric for user value and a top KPI for business value, then map metrics trees underneath them — because this creates shared direction, clarifies tradeoffs, and helps teams focus on the few levers that actually move outcomes.
- **When you face too many competing ideas and political prioritization**: Do evaluate ideas with ICE and explicitly score confidence based on evidence quality, not conviction — because a structured prioritization method reduces HiPPO decision-making and makes tradeoffs more transparent and repeatable.
- **When you face pressure to build a large MVP before learning**: Do start with cheaper validation methods like fake door tests, Wizard of Oz tests, smoke tests, or usability tests — because these methods let you learn quickly and cheaply before committing engineering resources to the wrong solution.
- **When you face a delivery-heavy culture where engineers are disengaged from outcomes**: Do involve the team in choosing ideas and defining validation steps, not just executing tasks — because giving teams context and ownership over goals, ideas, and learning milestones increases engagement and improves decision quality.
- **When you face a roadmap full of fixed feature commitments**: Do shift from release roadmaps to outcome roadmaps wherever confidence is still low — because feature deadlines lock teams into low-confidence bets, while outcome roadmaps preserve flexibility to learn and adapt.
- **When you face uncertainty about whether to keep investing in an idea**: Do tie the level of investment to the current level of confidence and stop climbing the evidence ladder once risk is low enough — because this avoids both overbuilding weak ideas and over-testing trivial, low-risk changes.
- **When you face the urge to transform your whole organization at once**: Do start with the layer where your biggest pain is: goals, ideas, steps, or tasks — because incremental adoption is more sustainable and avoids process fatigue from trying to overhaul everything simultaneously.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Great product leaders or founders can reliably pick the right big bets through vision and conviction. → **Their view**: Even strong leaders should have their ideas challenged and strengthened by evidence — because opinion-led bets like Google+ can waste enormous resources, while evidence-guided discovery improves the odds of finding what users actually want.
- **Conventional**: Speed means shipping code to production as fast as possible. → **Their view**: The real metric is time to outcomes, not time to production — because shipping the wrong thing quickly is slower overall than learning early and building the right thing.
- **Conventional**: An MVP is a small but real product you build and launch. → **Their view**: You often should not build a real product first; you should fake it first — because low-cost tests like Wizard of Oz and fake door experiments can validate assumptions before engineering invests in full implementation.
- **Conventional**: If a competitor has a feature, that validates the idea. → **Their view**: Competitor features are weak evidence and should not be treated as proof — because competitors are also uncertain and may be wrong; copying them often leads to poor decisions.
- **Conventional**: Roadmaps should specify what features will ship by when. → **Their view**: Roadmaps should focus on outcomes until confidence is high enough to commit to a solution — because feature-based roadmaps suffocate learning and force teams to deliver low-confidence ideas.
- **Conventional**: Agile teams should focus on executing tickets while PMs handle strategy and discovery. → **Their view**: Developers should participate in discovery and validation, not just delivery — because separating planning from execution creates mistrust, weak context, and disengaged teams; involving builders in discovery improves ownership and results.
