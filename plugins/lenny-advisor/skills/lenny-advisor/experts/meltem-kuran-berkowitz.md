# Meltem Kuran Berkowitz

**Head of Growth** | **Deel** | Expertise: growth, SEO, paid acquisition, B2B marketing

## Bio
Meltem Kuran Berkowitz leads growth at Deel and helped scale the company from under $1M ARR to nearly $300M ARR while keeping the business EBITDA positive. Her perspective matters because she is intensely focused on efficient, high-intent growth: channels that convert, content that answers real demand, and teams that are measured on revenue rather than vanity metrics.

She is especially valuable for founders and growth leaders who need to build repeatable acquisition systems in B2B without wasting money on broad awareness or low-intent traffic.

## Signature Frameworks

### Build the skeleton before the makeup
- **When to use**: When starting growth for an early-stage company or resetting a growth strategy.
- **Steps**:
  1. Make sure the website exists and loads quickly.
  2. Confirm search engines can find and index the site.
  3. Ensure potential users can actually find the site through search or content.
  4. Create content to answer the questions people are already asking.
  5. Only then invest in paid ads or broader acquisition.
- **Example**: You cannot run paid ads successfully if the website loads in four-plus seconds.

### Question-first cheap channel strategy
- **When to use**: When looking for low-cost acquisition channels in B2B.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify where your target users ask questions.
  2. Monitor those places with alerts or keyword tracking.
  3. Answer the question directly and helpfully.
  4. Only after solving the problem, mention your product as an option.
  5. Repeat in communities, search, and social channels where answers persist and compound.
- **Example**: Deel set up alerts for Reddit questions about international hiring and responded with helpful answers.

### SEO traffic-light prioritization
- **When to use**: When deciding which SEO keywords and topics deserve content investment.
- **Steps**:
  1. Generate a large keyword list related to the business.
  2. Rank keywords by search volume.
  3. Classify each keyword by intent: green for high purchase intent, yellow for mixed intent, red for low/no purchase intent.
  4. Work through green keywords from highest volume to lowest, then yellows.
  5. Often skip reds entirely.
- **Example**: Deel reviewed up to 700 keywords for a content series and prioritized those with strong intent to buy payroll or EOR solutions.

### Search-intent content creation
- **When to use**: When writing SEO content that should actually rank and convert.
- **Steps**:
  1. Understand why someone typed the keyword.
  2. Check what Google is currently surfacing for that query.
  3. Map the next questions the searcher is likely to ask.
  4. Write a concise answer that ends the search, rather than sending the user back to Google.
  5. Use SEO tools to ensure readability, keyword placement, and coverage.
- **Example**: Deel’s EOR content explains what an employer of record is, what it is not, and its limitations because those are the follow-up questions users ask.

### Functional-plus-regional growth org design
- **When to use**: When scaling a growth/marketing team across multiple geographies.
- **Steps**:
  1. Keep specialist functions centralized so experts learn together.
  2. Build functional teams for disciplines like paid, content, product marketing, and brand.
  3. Add regional marketers who understand local markets and opportunities.
  4. Have regional teams partner with central specialists to execute local strategy.
  5. Allocate resources based on TAM, opportunity, and competitive landscape.
- **Example**: A marketer in Turkey works with Deel’s central paid team to launch Turkey-specific campaigns rather than owning a fully separate paid team.

## Core Advice
- **When you are trying to grow a B2B product cheaply**: Go where people are already asking for help and answer their questions with real value — because B2B buyers usually want their problem solved, not to be sold to.
- **When you want to use Reddit or similar communities for acquisition**: Set keyword alerts and respond manually with useful advice, not promotional copy — because helpfulness earns trust and automation/self-promotion gets ignored.
- **When you are considering SEO early on**: Only invest heavily if people are actively searching for a solution to the problem you solve — because content cannot be discovered if the market isn’t searching.
- **When you write SEO content**: Optimize for ending the search, not stuffing keywords — because Google rewards content that fully satisfies intent.
- **When you are choosing SEO topics**: Prioritize high-intent keywords first and ignore low-intent queries until later — because traffic without conversion is a distraction.
- **When your content team is tempted to cut corners**: Treat SEO as a time-consuming craft and do the keyword research and fact checking thoroughly — because shortcuts produce weak resources that do not rank or convert.
- **When you are deciding whether to run awareness campaigns in B2B**: Delay awareness until you have tapped the bottom of the funnel and know what messaging resonates — because early awareness is slow, expensive, and often wasted.
- **When you have already covered the major paid channels**: Expand into long-tail paid placements like niche sites, newsletters, podcasts, and review sites — because small channels can add up to meaningful lead flow.
- **When you are evaluating paid channels**: Measure downstream revenue and payback, not just lead volume — because lead volume can hide poor customer quality.
- **When you are hiring for growth early**: Hire people who have owned bottom-funnel or revenue KPIs before, not just top-of-funnel metrics — because growth should be accountable to business outcomes.
- **When you are building an early growth team**: Hire one leader per function, prove the theory, then expand the team — because it reduces overhiring risk.
- **When you are joining or hiring for an early-stage company**: Look for people who are willing to do the smallest, messiest tasks themselves — because startup growth requires hands-on execution.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Growth should start with paid ads and awareness campaigns to get the word out. → **Their view**: Start with fundamentals and high-intent channels first; awareness is often a waste early in B2B — because if the site is slow, the message is unclear, and demand doesn’t exist yet, paid awareness burns money.
- **Conventional**: SEO is mostly about inserting the right keywords and publishing lots of content. → **Their view**: SEO is about answering the searcher’s question so completely that the search ends — because usefulness and intent matter more than repetition.
- **Conventional**: Any company with an audience overlap should partner. → **Their view**: Only partner with organizations that are trusted sources for the specific question your users ask — because shared audience alone doesn’t create trust.
- **Conventional**: Marketing success is measured by leads and traffic. → **Their view**: Growth teams should be measured by revenue and payback — because lead volume can look good while producing poor customers.
- **Conventional**: A growth team should be organized by region so each market owns its own specialists. → **Their view**: Keep specialists centralized and pair them with regional operators — because centralized experts learn faster and maintain quality.
- **Conventional**: Brand awareness should be built from day one. → **Their view**: In B2B, awareness is usually a later-stage tactic after bottom-funnel demand is established — because early buyers are already searching for solutions.
