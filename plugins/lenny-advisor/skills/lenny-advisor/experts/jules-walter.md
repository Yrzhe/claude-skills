# Jules Walter

**Product Lead** | **YouTube** | Expertise: product-management, growth, leadership, career-development

## Bio
Jules Walter is a product leader at YouTube who previously served as Slack’s first growth PM, where he helped lead monetization and mobile during a period of rapid expansion. He also co-founded Black Product Managers Network and CodePath, which gives him a strong perspective on both building products and building people.

His perspective matters because he combines hands-on growth execution with unusually practical advice on skill-building, mentorship, and career development. He is especially useful for PMs who want to get better fast through deliberate practice rather than abstract theory.

## Signature Frameworks

### IQ vs. EQ skill development
- **When to use**: When deciding what PM skills to prioritize at different stages of your career.
- **Steps**:
  1. Separate PM skills into IQ/hard skills and EQ/soft skills.
  2. Early in your career, focus more heavily on IQ skills.
  3. As scope increases, shift more attention to EQ skills.
  4. Use self-awareness and feedback to identify which EQ sub-skills matter most for you.
- **Example**: At Slack, Jules focused early on execution, product sense, strategy, and interviewing, then later had to invest much more in communication, leadership, and management.

### Outcome-backward skill building
- **When to use**: When you need to quickly learn a new PM capability in a real work context.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define a concrete outcome that proves the skill improved.
  2. Work backward to identify the knowledge and behaviors required.
  3. Read just enough to ask better questions.
  4. Find experts or mentors and ask targeted questions.
  5. Apply what you learn immediately.
  6. Review results and iterate.
- **Example**: When Jules joined Slack without growth experience, he set a goal to ship experiments that would improve activation, then learned from growth experts like Bangaly Kaba and Adriel Frederick.

### Understand, identify, execute
- **When to use**: When tackling growth problems, especially activation and onboarding.
- **Steps**:
  1. Understand why users are not activating or retaining.
  2. Identify the biggest opportunities and hypotheses.
  3. Execute experiments quickly.
- **Example**: At Slack, Jules used this approach to diagnose user drop-off, partner with research and data, and ship onboarding changes on mobile that improved activation by double-digit percentages.

### One-skill-at-a-time deliberate practice
- **When to use**: When trying to improve a PM skill deeply instead of collecting scattered tips.
- **Steps**:
  1. Choose one skill for a fixed period.
  2. Commit to weekly practice.
  3. Study strong examples and artifacts.
  4. Apply the skill in your own work every week.
  5. Get feedback and refine continuously.
- **Example**: Jules learned strategy by reviewing many strategy artifacts over months while practicing strategy on his own product every week.

### Reverse-engineer excellence
- **When to use**: When you want to learn from strong PMs, documents, presentations, or meetings inside a high-performing company.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify people or artifacts considered excellent.
  2. Study them closely.
  3. Ask what questions the artifact answers and what structure it uses.
  4. Observe iterations and feedback, not just final outputs.
  5. Save templates and patterns.
- **Example**: Jules collected strong strategy docs, exec updates, and emails, and asked to observe how top PMs drafted strategy before seeing the final version.

### Small-ask mentorship building
- **When to use**: When trying to build relationships with mentors, especially senior or busy people.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify someone strong in a specific area.
  2. Make the smallest possible initial ask.
  3. Apply their advice in real work.
  4. Follow up with results.
  5. Deepen the relationship gradually.
  6. Look for ways to help them too.
- **Example**: After hearing a product leader speak at Slack, Jules emailed a very specific follow-up question instead of asking for a generic mentorship call, which eventually became an ongoing relationship.

### Specific-feedback elicitation
- **When to use**: When you need actionable feedback, especially on EQ skills.
- **Steps**:
  1. Ask about a very specific behavior or skill.
  2. Offer your own self-critique if helpful.
  3. React with enthusiastic gratitude.
  4. Look for patterns across repeated feedback.
  5. Use trusted relationships to surface subjective feedback.
- **Example**: Jules might ask, “I’m working on executive presence—did you feel that in this presentation?” and then thank the person warmly so they keep being honest.

### Strengths and shadow-side analysis
- **When to use**: When deciding whether to double down on strengths versus over-focusing on weaknesses.
- **Steps**:
  1. Notice what others repeatedly say you’re good at.
  2. Treat that as a likely strength.
  3. Identify the trait behind it.
  4. Find the shadow side of that trait.
  5. Learn to dial it up or down by context.
- **Example**: Jules realized that asking great questions is a strength, but without context it can make him sound junior; his ability to simplify complex problems can also make him stay quiet too long.

## Core Advice
- **When you face a PM interview process**: Do dozens of mock interviews, ideally with people who understand the format — because interviewing is a skill, and live repetition beats mental rehearsal.
- **When you face the challenge of breaking into product management**: Enter through a startup or an internal transition — because there is no single standard path, and these routes lower the barrier.
- **When you face a new skill gap in your PM role**: Define a concrete outcome that proves mastery, then learn backward from it — because urgency makes learning practical.
- **When you face a growth or activation problem**: Spend more time understanding why users are not activating before jumping to solutions — because better diagnosis leads to better experiments.
- **When you face the need to improve strategy, execution, or communication**: Study excellent artifacts and people around you — because concrete examples teach faster than theory.
- **When you face difficulty improving EQ skills**: Focus on one skill for three to six months — because EQ changes through repeated behavioral practice.
- **When you face the need for honest feedback**: Ask in a specific way and respond with visible gratitude — because people give better feedback when the ask is concrete and safe.
- **When you face a lack of self-awareness**: Work with mentors, coaches, or trusted peers — because outside observers can reveal blind spots you can’t see.
- **When you face the challenge of finding a mentor**: Start with the smallest possible ask — because busy people respond more readily to low-friction requests.
- **When you face the challenge of maintaining a mentor relationship**: Bring specific problems, follow up, and help them too — because reciprocity makes the relationship durable.
- **When you face uncertainty about your strengths**: Ask what others say you’re good at that you dismiss as obvious — because natural strengths are often undervalued.
- **When you face stress in interviews or high-stakes meetings**: Practice enough that even your worst performance is good enough — because stress degrades performance and over-preparation reduces downside.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Soft skills are secondary to hard PM skills. → **Their view**: EQ skills become the limiting factor as you get more senior — because leadership, influence, and self-awareness drive success at scale.
- **Conventional**: To get better at PM, consume lots of content and tips. → **Their view**: Reading alone does very little — because real improvement comes from deliberate practice and repetition.
- **Conventional**: To find a mentor, ask directly for mentorship or a coffee chat. → **Their view**: Start with the smallest possible ask — because low-friction requests build trust and momentum.
- **Conventional**: Weaknesses should be the main focus of development. → **Their view**: Double down on strengths and manage the shadow side — because strengths create disproportionate leverage.
- **Conventional**: Feedback should be objective and specific to obvious mistakes. → **Their view**: Subjective feedback about how you come across is often the most valuable — because perception strongly affects career progression.
