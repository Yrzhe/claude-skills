## Signature Frameworks

### Functional org over GM structure
- **When to use**: When a multi-product company wants deeper technical excellence, shared standards, and coordinated adoption of major platform shifts like AI.
- **Steps**:
  1. Recognize when independent business-unit silos are creating identity drift and fragmented technical practices.
  2. Reframe the company around its technology DNA rather than only business-line ownership.
  3. Move from GM-led product silos to functional leadership, with engineering and design reporting into unified organizations.
  4. Standardize levels, tools, policies, and technical language across teams.
  5. Use the unified structure to drive shared platform investments, AI adoption, and talent mobility.
- **Example**: At Block, engineering and design were reorganized from separate business-unit teams across Square, Cash App, Afterpay, and TIDAL into single functional organizations, which Dhanji says was key to becoming more AI-native.

### Start small, then build momentum
- **When to use**: When exploring new products, technologies, or strategic bets with high uncertainty.
- **Steps**:
  1. Begin with a small experiment, prototype, or hack-week project.
  2. Test whether the idea solves a real problem or validates a thesis.
  3. Share early versions with internal and external users to gather feedback.
  4. Expand only after the concept shows traction or strategic value.
  5. Avoid overstaffing or overbuilding before reality validates the idea.
- **Example**: Goose began as one engineer’s side project based on the thesis that agents would unlock LLM value. Cash App and Block’s early Bitcoin product also started as small hack-week style experiments.

### Manual hours saved as AI ROI metric
- **When to use**: When evaluating whether AI tools are creating real productivity gains across different functions.
- **Steps**:
  1. Collect self-reported time savings from teams using AI tools regularly.
  2. Validate those reports with operational metrics such as PR volume, feature throughput, and related output measures.
  3. Use data science to combine multiple signals into a more reliable estimate.
  4. Compare results across functions and codebase types rather than assuming uniform gains.
  5. Continuously revisit the metric because model capabilities and use cases improve rapidly.
- **Example**: Block tracks manual hours saved across engineering, support, legal, and risk teams, and estimates it is trending toward 20-25% of manual hours saved company-wide, with AI-forward engineering teams reporting 8-10 hours saved per week.

### Use AI where it is strongest today
- **When to use**: When deciding where to deploy AI first instead of expecting it to solve every hard problem immediately.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify repetitive, tool-heavy, or coordination-heavy workflows.
  2. Prioritize use cases where AI can orchestrate across systems rather than replace deep expert judgment.
  3. Enable non-technical teams to build lightweight tools for themselves.
  4. Automate background tasks such as testing, vulnerability analysis, and ticket triage.
  5. Keep humans focused on architecture, taste, prioritization, and cross-portfolio judgment.
- **Example**: Block found strong gains from non-technical teams building internal tools with Goose and from mobile UI test automation with Gosling, while senior architectural decisions still required humans.

### Question the base assumption before automating
- **When to use**: When teams want to buy a tool, build a tool, or automate a process.
- **Steps**:
  1. Pause before choosing between vendor software and internal tooling.
  2. Ask whether the underlying process is necessary at all.
  3. Consider whether changing the workflow would eliminate the need for tooling.
  4. Only then decide whether to buy, build, or automate.
  5. Keep decisions anchored to company purpose and customer value.
- **Example**: Dhanji described teams debating whether to buy a vendor tool or build an internal app with Goose, while his intervention was often to ask whether the process itself should exist.

## Core Advice
- **When engineering practices are fragmented across business units**: do a structural reset toward a functional organization with unified engineering and design leadership — because org structure strongly shapes standards, reuse, and AI adoption.
- **When AI adoption is low but pressure is high**: deploy AI in workflows where it is already effective today — because the biggest gains come from practical use cases, not moonshots.
- **When productivity claims feel vague**: measure manual hours saved alongside output metrics — because self-reports alone are noisy, but combined signals are credible.
- **When executives want company-wide AI adoption**: use the tools daily yourself — because leadership behavior accelerates cultural change.
- **When asked to buy or build more internal software**: question whether the process should exist first — because automation can distract from core purpose.
- **When hiring in an AI-shifting environment**: hire for learning mindset and adaptability — because tool capabilities change too fast for static expertise to be enough.
- **When launching a new product or bet**: start with a small experiment or hack-week prototype — because evidence beats ambition.
- **When trying to understand AI’s value for your own work**: use it on a real task, not just in theory — because hands-on use reveals strengths and limits quickly.
- **When debating refactors or code elegance**: check customer value first — because product success is not tightly coupled to code quality.
- **When tempted to replace every SaaS tool with custom software**: apply a core-purpose filter — because maintenance and distraction can outweigh the savings.
