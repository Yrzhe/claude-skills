# Christopher Lochhead

**Author, podcaster, advisor, and co-creator of Category Pirates** | **Category Pirates** | Expertise: strategy, positioning, go-to-market, leadership

## Bio
Christopher Lochhead is a three-time public company CMO, bestselling author of *Play Bigger* and *Niche Down*, and a longtime advisor to venture-backed startups. He is best known as the “Godfather of Category Design,” a perspective that argues legendary companies don’t just compete in markets—they create and own new ones.

His viewpoint matters because he consistently challenges conventional startup and marketing thinking. If you want to build a company that becomes the default answer in a new space, rather than a better option in an old one, his framework is especially useful.

## Signature Frameworks

### Category Design
- **When to use**: When you want to build a large, enduring company and avoid competing as a feature-better alternative in an existing market.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with the problem, not the product.
  2. Decide whether you want to capture existing demand or create new demand.
  3. Define a new or reframed problem that matters deeply to customers.
  4. Design the market category around that problem and your distinct solution.
  5. Get a meaningful percentage of the market to agree with your definition of the problem and solution.
  6. Scale the category so your company becomes the category king or queen.
- **Example**: Gojo reframed handwashing from “use bar soap” to “wash hands without a disgusting bar soap,” leading to liquid soap, and later reframed again to “wash hands without water,” leading to hand sanitizer and Purell.

### Frame, Name, and Claim
- **When to use**: When introducing a new category or trying to shift how customers understand an existing problem.
- **Steps**:
  1. Frame the problem in a new way so people see the status quo differently.
  2. Name the category or concept with language that creates a new mental model.
  3. Claim the space by consistently educating the market and associating your company with that new language.
- **Example**: Elisha Otis struggled to sell the “safety elevator” until he renamed it the “vertical railway,” which helped people understand the new value and imagine skyscrapers.

### Languaging
- **When to use**: When your innovation is hard to understand using existing vocabulary or when you need to justify radically different value and pricing.
- **Steps**:
  1. Listen carefully to the words customers and the market already use.
  2. Avoid describing a new thing entirely with old language.
  3. Introduce strategic new language that bridges from what people know to what they need to understand.
  4. Repeat that language until it becomes the category’s default vocabulary.
- **Example**: Starbucks changed the perceived value of coffee by teaching customers new language like “grande” and “latte,” enabling a major price/value shift from commodity coffee.

### Backcasting
- **When to use**: When planning a startup or category strategy and you want to avoid being constrained by the present or by incumbent assumptions.
- **Steps**:
  1. Reject the premise of how the world works today.
  2. Imagine a future 5 years out where your company has succeeded beyond expectations.
  3. Make that future concrete: customers, company size, product, impact, market.
  4. From that future state, look backward and identify what had to happen to make it real.
  5. Use those insights to guide present-day strategy.
- **Example**: Lochhead contrasts normal startup planning—forecasting from today forward—with backcasting from a future category-leading position to determine what must be built and changed.

### Damming the Demand
- **When to use**: When you want to redirect demand from an existing category without directly competing feature-for-feature.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the current category customers use to solve a job.
  2. Expose the hidden downside or limitation of that status quo.
  3. Present a new category as a better way to solve the underlying need.
  4. Compete category-to-category rather than product-to-product.
- **Example**: Spinning redirected demand from outdoor cycling by saying, in effect, “get the workout without risking getting hit by a car.” Peloton then redirected demand from spinning by saying, “why drive to the gym when you can do it at home?”

### Lightning Strikes vs. Peanut Butter Marketing
- **When to use**: When planning go-to-market and marketing spend, especially for startups that need to matter intensely to a specific audience instead of being vaguely present all year.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the exact audience you need to influence.
  2. Find where they already gather, ideally in native digital communities.
  3. Create a provocative category point of view that reframes a problem.
  4. Concentrate effort into a short, high-intensity campaign or launch moment.
  5. Use that moment to drive word of mouth and category awareness.
- **Example**: Lochhead argues most marketers spread budget evenly across quarters (“peanut butter”), while category designers should create concentrated launch moments more like Hollywood movie releases.

### Magic Triangle: Product, Company, Category
- **When to use**: When building a company and deciding where to focus; especially useful when teams over-index on product alone.
- **Steps**:
  1. Treat product, company, and category as equally important.
  2. Build a strong product that solves a meaningful problem.
  3. Build a company capable of delivering and scaling that value.
  4. Design the category so the product has a market context where it can win.
- **Example**: Lochhead emphasizes that products fail not because product is unimportant, but because teams neglect category design and assume the best product wins on its own.

## Core Advice
- **When you face a crowded market and are tempted to win by being a better version of an existing product**: define a new category or reframe the problem instead of positioning as “better” — because customers already have a mental model, and “better” often gets compared away.
- **When you face early-stage product and messaging decisions**: spend more time understanding and articulating the problem than polishing the solution story — because the problem creates the category.
- **When you face the challenge of explaining a novel product that people don’t immediately understand**: do strategic languaging — because old language traps innovation inside old categories.
- **When you face strategic planning for a startup or new initiative**: backcast from a future successful state rather than forecasting from today’s constraints — because forecasting encourages incremental thinking.
- **When you face a go-to-market plan that spreads budget thinly across many channels and months**: run one or two concentrated lightning strikes aimed at a specific audience — because intensity creates salience and word of mouth.
- **When you face pressure to tell your founder story or showcase product features as the main marketing strategy**: educate around the customer’s problem and the new way of thinking — because people care more about their own problems than your journey.
- **When you face growth challenges in a niche you already dominate**: expand the category vision before someone else defines the larger market — because a company can only grow as large as its category.
- **When you face the question of who to target first in a new market**: focus on super consumers — because a small percentage of buyers often drive most profits and shape the zeitgeist.
- **When you face hiring interviews and want to uncover what really matters**: ask open-ended prompts like “Is there anything else?” — because important information often appears after the formal interview ends.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: The best product wins. → **Their view**: The category makes the product; the company that designs and owns the category wins — because customers evaluate products through the lens of the category they understand.
- **Conventional**: Product-market fit is the key milestone for startups. → **Their view**: Product-market fit is backwards and dangerous — because it encourages fitting a product into an existing market instead of designing a market for the product.
- **Conventional**: Positioning is essential strategic work for startups. → **Their view**: Modern positioning is often “for losers” — because positioning against competitors usually means accepting their frame.
- **Conventional**: Brand and distribution are enough to beat incumbents. → **Their view**: Even massive brand and distribution fail without category reframing — because copycat solutions don’t create a new reason to switch.
- **Conventional**: Marketing should maximize reach and frequency with always-on campaigns. → **Their view**: It’s better to matter intensely for a short period than to be irrelevant all year — because concentrated attention cuts through noise.
- **Conventional**: Disruption means replacing old categories. → **Their view**: Great category design often expands or redirects demand — because many innovations grow the total market rather than simply killing the old one.
