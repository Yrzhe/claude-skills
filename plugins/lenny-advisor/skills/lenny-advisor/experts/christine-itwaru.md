# Christine Itwaru

**Product operations leader** | **Pendo** | Expertise: product operations, product management, leadership, go-to-market alignment

## Bio
Christine Itwaru is a longtime product operations leader at Pendo who helped shape one of the earlier formal product ops functions. She brings a rare perspective as a former PM who understands both the day-to-day pain of product teams and the systems needed to help them scale.

Her perspective matters because she treats product ops as more than process management: it is a lever for customer insight synthesis, internal transparency, launch readiness, and strategic alignment across product and go-to-market teams.

## Signature Frameworks

### Product Ops as System + Role
- **When to use**: When a product organization is growing and needs to clarify what product operations actually is and whether it requires dedicated people.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define product operations first as a system that helps product teams thrive.
  2. Then define it as a person or team that partners with PMs and advises product leadership.
  3. Use the system to remove noise, improve transparency, and support better strategic decisions.
  4. Decide which parts should be handled by people versus process or automation.
- **Example**: Christine describes product ops as both “a thing you do” and also a person or group that partners with PMs and becomes a strategic advisor to the CPO.

### Voice of Customer Synthesis Loop
- **When to use**: When customer feedback is fragmented across sales, success, support, NPS, and product teams, and PMs need clearer signal.
- **Steps**:
  1. Collect qualitative and quantitative inputs from revenue teams, product analytics, feedback tools, and NPS.
  2. Bring cross-functional teams together to review the inputs collectively rather than in silos.
  3. Package the relevant insights for PMs, including segment-level patterns, prospect vs. customer differences, and high-priority risks.
  4. Validate or challenge the synthesized insights with user research.
  5. Use the output to guide planning, prioritization, and customer education.
- **Example**: At Pendo, product ops brought sales, success, and professional services into the same room so they could see they were hearing different but related customer needs, then fed PMs a synthesized view including risk data, high-priority deals, and segment-specific feedback.

### Transparency and Readiness Framework
- **When to use**: When launches are failing because internal teams know something is coming but do not know how to prepare customers or use the new capability.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify where transparency is breaking down across product, sales, success, and customers.
  2. Separate “knowing something is coming” from “knowing what to do with it.”
  3. Create a repeatable communication mechanism that explains value, usage, and readiness actions.
  4. Train internal teams on how the product change affects their role and customer conversations.
  5. Measure whether inbound questions improve in quality and whether teams are better prepared.
- **Example**: After a bad launch at Pendo, Christine helped create systems like a product digest that focused less on release dates and more on how internal teams should get ready, why the change mattered, and how to talk about it.

### Stand Up, Automate, Move Up
- **When to use**: When product ops risks becoming a permanent manual patch instead of a strategic function.
- **Steps**:
  1. Stand up the process or system needed to solve the current operational pain.
  2. Stabilize it and make it repeatable.
  3. Hand it off, automate it, or simplify it where possible.
  4. Redeploy product ops capacity toward higher-value strategic work like retention, growth, and customer experience.
- **Example**: Christine describes her own goal at Pendo as standing up the needed systems, then either handing them off or automating them so the team could focus on more strategic work such as retention, growth, and in-app experience.

### Survey-Based Product Ops Prioritization
- **When to use**: When deciding where a new product ops function should focus first and how to demonstrate value.
- **Steps**:
  1. Survey PMs on where their time is going and how much time is spent firefighting versus with customers.
  2. Survey revenue teams on how often they need to ask product questions and where confusion exists.
  3. Quantify the biggest friction points across both groups.
  4. Prioritize interventions where transparency and readiness gaps are largest.
  5. Track changes in inbound quality and team alignment over time.
- **Example**: Pendo surveyed PMs and revenue teams to understand time spent firefighting, time with customers, and frequency of questions, then used that data to prioritize transparency improvements.

## Core Advice
- **When PMs are overloaded with internal questions**: Audit what PMs are accountable for and move operational noise away from them — because PM time is best spent with customers and engineers, not repetitive stakeholder support.
- **When introducing product ops and facing resistance**: Get executive buy-in first from the CEO, CPO, or head of product — because role clarity and sponsorship prevent PMs from seeing product ops as “stepping on toes.”
- **When customer feedback is fragmented**: Run a structured voice-of-customer synthesis process — because PMs need signal, not noise, and cross-functional synthesis reveals what actually matters.
- **When launches keep going poorly**: Build readiness materials, not just release announcements — because teams need to know what the change means and how to act on it.
- **When building product ops from scratch**: Start with the system, not the org chart — because process and communication can solve many problems before you need a large team.
- **When product ops boundaries are unclear**: Support PM success without removing direct customer exposure — because firsthand customer understanding is core to product management.
- **When planning is inconsistent in a less mature org**: Use planning standardization as an entry point — because it’s often the most visible pain point and a practical wedge into broader enablement.
- **When debating whether product ops is only for B2B**: Look at complexity, not business model — because large B2C companies can need product ops just as much.
- **When manual ops work keeps piling up**: Build it, automate it, then move on — because product ops should evolve into strategic value, not permanent busywork.
- **When engineers lack customer empathy**: Bring engineers into customer meetings — because firsthand exposure improves planning and increases confidence.
- **When evaluating a product ops job**: Scrutinize the success criteria in the job description — because the role varies widely and ambiguity can hide mismatched expectations.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Product ops is a brand-new function created by recent trends. → **Their view**: The problems have always existed; only the formal role is new — because growth made alignment, insight synthesis, and readiness gaps visible enough to specialize.
- **Conventional**: Product ops is mainly a B2B function. → **Their view**: It is not inherently B2B — because large B2C organizations also face cross-functional complexity and readiness issues.
- **Conventional**: Ops teams are mostly signs of inefficiency and should be minimized. → **Their view**: Ops functions are often a natural sign of growth and maturity — because scaling organizations need systems, though those systems should eventually be automated or handed off.
- **Conventional**: A bad launch means the PM failed at communication. → **Their view**: A bad launch often reveals a missing system — because repeatable readiness and transparency processes prevent the same failure from recurring.
- **Conventional**: Product ops is just process management or program management with a new label. → **Their view**: The differentiator is deep product, customer, and business fluency — because product ops should be strategic, not merely administrative.
