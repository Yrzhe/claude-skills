# Bangaly Kaba

**Director of Product Management** | **YouTube** | Expertise: growth, product management, leadership, strategy

## Bio
Bangaly Kaba is a veteran product and growth leader who has scaled products at Facebook, Instagram, Instacart, and YouTube. His perspective matters because he combines hands-on growth execution with a strong systems view of leadership: he cares less about flashy tactics and more about understanding the real problem, building the right team environment, and creating repeatable ways to win.

He is especially valuable when you need advice on growth diagnosis, PM leadership, team development, or deciding whether your career blocker is your environment or your skills.

## Signature Frameworks

### Impact = Environment × Skills
- **When to use**: When deciding where to work, whether to stay in a role, or how to increase your career impact and likelihood of promotion.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define impact as the primary output to optimize for, rather than compensation or title.
  2. Assess environment factors: manager, resources, scope, team, compensation, and culture.
  3. Score each environment factor honestly on a scale from near 0 to 2.
  4. Assess skill factors such as communication, influence, strategic thinking, and execution.
  5. Identify which constraints are structural versus personal-development related.
  6. Decide whether to improve the environment, build missing skills, or change roles/teams.
- **Example**: At Facebook, Kaba had strong scope and a strong team, but too much scope, insufficient support, and manager changes created burnout and reduced his ability to have impact.

### Understand, Identify, Execute
- **When to use**: When teams are tempted to jump into building solutions before deeply understanding the problem, user needs, or root causes.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with first-principles understanding of what is actually happening.
  2. Create explicit roadmap space for “understand work.”
  3. Use data, instrumentation, user research, funnel analysis, and technical investigation.
  4. Identify the right solution opportunities.
  5. Execute on the highest-conviction opportunities while continuing parallel understand work.
  6. Use learnings from shipped work and understand work to plan the next cycle.
- **Example**: At Instagram, the signup flow initially only logged starts and completions. The team first instrumented the full funnel to see drop-offs, while also fixing obviously broken parts of the experience.

### Identify, Justify, Execute (Anti-pattern)
- **When to use**: As a warning sign when teams are rationalizing a preselected idea instead of discovering the right one.
- **Steps**:
  1. Someone proposes a feature or idea.
  2. The team pulls data selectively to justify that idea.
  3. The team spends significant time building it.
  4. The launch underperforms because the underlying problem was never truly understood.
- **Example**: A team celebrates a launch, then sees flat metrics the next day because they built something that seemed plausible but did not solve the real user need.

### Managing Complex Change
- **When to use**: When trying to change team culture, improve execution, or diagnose why an organization is stuck.
- **Steps**:
  1. Assess whether the team has a clear vision.
  2. Assess whether the team has the necessary skills.
  3. Assess whether incentives align with the desired behavior.
  4. Assess whether resources are sufficient.
  5. Assess whether there is a concrete action plan.
  6. Diagnose the failure mode and intervene on the right lever.
- **Example**: At YouTube, Kaba found teams lacked a shared mental model for product craft, so he created a deck and shared language to build the missing skills component.

### Adjacent User Theory
- **When to use**: When a product is growing quickly, entering new markets, or trying to unlock the next wave of users beyond current power users.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define who the current user is and why they use the product.
  2. Identify the next adjacent user who could use the product but currently does not.
  3. Observe how that adjacent user behaves differently.
  4. Use the product in the adjacent user’s state, ideally from a fresh account or fresh context.
  5. Watch for declining cohort curves or weaker activation/retention from newer users.
  6. Adapt onboarding, flows, and value delivery to fit the adjacent user’s needs.
- **Example**: At Instagram, users in new markets had different phones, lower tech familiarity, and different expectations. Conversion and retention degraded even without product changes, signaling the next adjacent users needed a different experience.

### PM as Team Sport / Coaching Tree
- **When to use**: When leading PMs or product organizations and trying to scale through people rather than individual heroics.
- **Steps**:
  1. Treat product leadership as coaching, not as being the “CEO of the product.”
  2. Recognize that not everyone needs to be a star; strong role players matter.
  3. Help each person understand their role and maximize their contribution.
  4. Invest in coaching and skill-building across the team.
  5. Measure success partly by the leaders and operators you develop.
- **Example**: Kaba points to former team members who went on to lead growth at Stripe, become CPO at Chief, or run Stories at Instagram as evidence that coaching others is part of the job.

### Bloom’s Taxonomy for Team Development
- **When to use**: When diagnosing why PMs or managers are struggling and deciding how to coach them.
- **Steps**:
  1. Determine whether the person lacks basic knowledge.
  2. Check whether they comprehend the concept but cannot apply it.
  3. Check whether they can apply it but cannot analyze across scenarios.
  4. Check whether they can synthesize the work into business context.
  5. For managers, assess whether they can evaluate and prioritize across multiple teams and domains.
  6. Coach at the specific level where the breakdown occurs.
- **Example**: A PM may understand machine learning conceptually but not have enough applied experience to know there are multiple valid approaches.

## Core Advice
- **When you face career stagnation or are unsure whether to stay**: optimize for impact, not compensation — because impact drives scope, trust, promotions, and eventually compensation.
- **When you face a difficult manager or blocked org**: understand what your manager is optimizing for and frame your needs in terms of impact — because managers control key environmental levers.
- **When you face pressure to ship faster but keep launching low-impact work**: reserve roadmap space for understand work — because root-cause clarity increases future velocity.
- **When you face poor funnel visibility**: instrument first — because you cannot improve what you cannot see.
- **When you face roadmap uncertainty**: mix low-effort/high-impact execution with 3–4 high-value understand projects — because learning and shipping should compound together.
- **When you face a skill gap as a PM**: combine theory with observation of strong operators — because PM craft is learned through both concepts and pattern recognition.
- **When you face mentor scarcity**: ask for specific introductions, not generic mentorship — because specificity leads to better matches.
- **When you face slowing growth in new cohorts**: analyze adjacent users — because growth often stalls when the product no longer fits the next user.
- **When you face a product that works for power users but not newcomers**: dogfood from a fresh account — because internal experts often miss the new-user experience.
- **When you face culture or execution problems**: diagnose vision, skills, incentives, resources, and action plan — because symptoms often come from different missing ingredients.
- **When you face a new leadership role**: listen deeply before changing things — because people adopt change more readily when they feel understood.
- **When you face a company decision**: talk to both current and former employees — because triangulation reveals both strengths and failure modes.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Optimize your career primarily for compensation, title, or prestige. → **Their view**: Optimize for impact; compensation and scope are downstream outputs of impact — because people who create clarity and results earn trust and broader responsibility.
- **Conventional**: If you want growth, ship more experiments faster. → **Their view**: Slow down to do understand work so you can speed up later — because blind execution creates activity without signal.
- **Conventional**: Product managers are the CEO of the product. → **Their view**: PM is a team sport where the PM is a coach and orchestrator — because success depends on enabling the whole team.
- **Conventional**: A single great mentor is enough. → **Their view**: Build a stable of mentors with different strengths — because different problems require different expertise.
- **Conventional**: All follows or connections are equally valuable. → **Their view**: Not all connections are equal; optimizing for celebrity follows can hurt retention — because meaningful friend graphs matter more than vanity metrics.
- **Conventional**: Top-of-funnel acquisition is the main growth lever. → **Their view**: The biggest opportunities often sit between onboarding and habit formation — because activation and retention determine whether acquisition compounds.
