# Bangaly Kaba

**Director of Product Management** | **YouTube** | Expertise: growth, product-management, leadership, strategy

## Bio
Bangaly Kaba is a veteran product and growth leader who has scaled products at Facebook, Instagram, and Instacart, and now leads product management at YouTube. His perspective matters because he combines hands-on growth execution with a strong systems view of leadership: he cares less about flashy tactics and more about how teams build durable impact.

He is especially useful when you need to diagnose why growth is stalling, why a team is underperforming, or how to develop PMs and leaders in a repeatable way.

## Signature Frameworks

### Impact = Environment × Skills
- **When to use**: When deciding where to work, whether to stay in a role, or how to increase career impact and promotion odds.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define impact as the main thing to optimize for.
  2. Assess environment factors: manager, scope, resources, team, culture, compensation.
  3. Score each factor honestly from near 0 to 2.
  4. Assess skill factors: communication, influence, strategy, execution.
  5. Separate structural constraints from personal skill gaps.
  6. Decide whether to improve the environment, build skills, or change roles.
- **Example**: At Facebook, Bangaly had strong scope and a strong team, but too much scope, insufficient support, and manager changes reduced his ability to have impact and led to burnout.

### Understand, Identify, Execute
- **When to use**: When teams want to build too quickly before understanding the real problem.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with first-principles understanding of what is happening.
  2. Reserve roadmap space for “understand work.”
  3. Use data, research, funnel analysis, and technical investigation.
  4. Identify the best solution opportunities.
  5. Execute on the highest-conviction bets.
  6. Feed learnings back into the next cycle.
- **Example**: At Instagram, the signup flow initially only tracked starts and completions. The team first instrumented the full funnel to see where users dropped off, while also fixing obvious broken parts of the experience.

### Managing Complex Change
- **When to use**: When trying to change culture, improve execution, or diagnose why an organization is stuck.
- **Steps**:
  1. Check whether the team has a clear vision.
  2. Check whether the team has the right skills.
  3. Check whether incentives align with desired behavior.
  4. Check whether resources are sufficient.
  5. Check whether there is a concrete action plan.
  6. Intervene on the missing lever, not the symptom.
- **Example**: At YouTube, Bangaly saw teams lacked a shared mental model for product craft, so he created a deck and shared language to build the missing skills component.

### Adjacent User Theory
- **When to use**: When a product is growing, entering new markets, or trying to reach the next wave of users.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the current user and why they use the product.
  2. Identify the next adjacent user.
  3. Observe how that user behaves differently.
  4. Use the product from their perspective, ideally with a fresh account.
  5. Watch cohort curves and activation/retention for newer users.
  6. Adapt onboarding and product flows to fit their needs.
- **Example**: At Instagram, users in new markets had different phones and lower tech familiarity, and conversion/retention degraded even without product changes. That signaled the product needed to fit the adjacent user better.

### PM as Team Sport / Coaching Tree
- **When to use**: When leading PMs or product orgs and trying to scale through people, not heroics.
- **Steps**:
  1. Treat product leadership as coaching, not “CEO of the product.”
  2. Value role players, not just stars.
  3. Help each person understand their role.
  4. Invest in coaching and skill-building.
  5. Measure success by the leaders you develop.
- **Example**: Bangaly points to former team members who went on to lead growth at Stripe, become CPO at Chief, or run Stories at Instagram as evidence that leadership is about developing others.

### Bloom’s Taxonomy for Team Development
- **When to use**: When diagnosing why PMs or managers are struggling.
- **Steps**:
  1. Check for basic knowledge gaps.
  2. Check whether they can apply the concept.
  3. Check whether they can analyze across scenarios.
  4. Check whether they can synthesize into business context.
  5. For managers, assess whether they can prioritize across teams and domains.
  6. Coach at the exact level where the breakdown occurs.
- **Example**: A PM may understand machine learning conceptually but not have enough applied experience to know there are multiple valid approaches.

## Core Advice
- **When you face career stagnation**: optimize for impact, not compensation — because impact drives scope, trust, promotions, and eventually compensation.
- **When you feel blocked by your manager or org**: understand what your manager is optimizing for and frame your asks in terms of impact — because managers control key environmental levers.
- **When your team ships a lot but learns little**: reserve explicit time for understand work — because root-cause understanding raises future win rate.
- **When visibility into the funnel is weak**: instrument first — because you can’t fix what you can’t see.
- **When prioritizing a roadmap**: mix low-effort/high-impact execution with 3–4 high-value understanding projects — because you need both momentum and learning.
- **When building PM skill quickly**: combine theory with observation of strong operators — because craft is learned both conceptually and behaviorally.
- **When looking for mentors**: ask for a specific introduction, not a generic mentor — because specificity produces better matches.
- **When growth slows in new cohorts**: analyze adjacent users — because products often fail to serve the next wave of users.
- **When a product works for power users but not new users**: dogfood with a fresh account — because long-lived accounts hide onboarding and activation problems.
- **When culture or execution is off**: diagnose vision, skills, incentives, resources, and action plan — because symptoms often come from different missing ingredients.
- **When stepping into a leadership role**: listen deeply before changing things — because people accept change more readily when they feel understood.
- **When evaluating a company**: talk to both current and former employees — because triangulation reveals both strengths and failure modes.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Optimize your career for compensation, title, or prestige. → **Their view**: Optimize for impact — because compensation and scope are downstream of impact.
- **Conventional**: Ship more experiments faster to grow. → **Their view**: Slow down to understand the problem first — because blind execution creates activity, not signal.
- **Conventional**: PM is the CEO of the product. → **Their view**: PM is a coach in a team sport — because success depends on enabling the whole team.
- **Conventional**: One great mentor is enough. → **Their view**: Build a stable of mentors — because different problems require different expertise.
- **Conventional**: All growth signals are equally valuable. → **Their view**: Some connections are harmful if they don’t create meaningful user relationships — because retention depends on real value, not vanity metrics.
- **Conventional**: Top-of-funnel acquisition is the main growth lever. → **Their view**: Activation and habit formation are often the biggest opportunities — because acquisition without retention is a leaky bucket.
