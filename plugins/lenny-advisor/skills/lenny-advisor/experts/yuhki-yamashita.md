# Yuhki Yamashita

**Chief Product Officer** | **Figma** | Expertise: product-management, design, go-to-market, strategy

## Bio
Yuhki Yamashita is the Chief Product Officer at Figma, where he has helped shape product strategy, product-led/community-led growth, and the company’s unusually tight relationship with customers. He brings a rare cross-functional lens from leadership roles at Microsoft, YouTube, and Uber, making his perspective especially valuable for teams trying to connect product vision, design execution, and market adoption.

## Signature Frameworks

### PM storytelling as synthesis + memorability
- **When to use**: When you need to align stakeholders, communicate a product direction, or make a complex problem actionable.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start from raw inputs: notes, opinions, research, and data.
  2. Synthesize disparate observations into a single coherent thesis or framework.
  3. Reset your own context and explain the problem as if the audience knows nothing.
  4. Make the story memorable enough that others can repeat it internally.
  5. Tie the story to a clear action so it drives decisions, not just understanding.
- **Example**: Distill a messy product review into one clear takeaway that leaders can repeat in meetings, turning insight into a “meme” that shapes decisions.

### Why-first product leadership
- **When to use**: When teams are making many local decisions and need a shared direction without over-specifying the solution.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the underlying problem and motivation behind the work.
  2. Make the “why” explicit and shared across PM, design, and engineering.
  3. Let designers and engineers choose the “what” and “how” locally.
  4. Use the shared why to scale decision-making across the team.
  5. Go one level deeper with “why” to uncover root causes and bigger opportunities.
- **Example**: At YouTube, teams needed a clear why so designers and engineers could make good decisions independently, rather than waiting for detailed PM specs.

### Customer proximity loop
- **When to use**: When building products that need strong market fit, fast feedback, or community advocacy.
- **Steps**:
  1. Put the product in front of real users early.
  2. Listen carefully to feedback and implement improvements quickly.
  3. Return to users with the updated product to deepen trust.
  4. Use multiple feedback channels, not just the loudest ones.
  5. Treat community members as co-shapers and eventual evangelists.
- **Example**: Figma repeatedly showed the browser-based design editor to designers, incorporated feedback, and built a community that later advocated for the product.

### Balanced feedback portfolio
- **When to use**: When customer feedback is noisy, polarized, or dominated by vocal minorities.
- **Steps**:
  1. Collect feedback from multiple sources: tweets, support tickets, sales conversations, research, and data.
  2. Treat social feedback as canaries in the coal mine rather than the full truth.
  3. Balance sentiment with actual usage and behavioral data.
  4. Look for blind spots by comparing dissatisfied-user channels with prospect/perception channels.
  5. Use the mix of inputs to decide what deserves deeper investigation.
- **Example**: A private “Concerning Tweets” channel can flag issues, but tweets are only one input among many.

### Commitment-based planning
- **When to use**: When OKRs feel performative, hard to measure, or disconnected from what teams actually do.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with a clear headline: what is the team trying to do philosophically?
  2. Describe the commitment in legible language that everyone understands.
  3. Make sure it inspires action and reflects real day-to-day work.
  4. Add metrics only after the intent is clear.
  5. Iterate on the measurement model as the team becomes more sophisticated.
- **Example**: At Figma, they experimented with replacing rigid OKRs with “commitments” and report-card-style self-assessments before reintroducing more rigor.

### Internal dogfooding as quality control
- **When to use**: When you want to raise product quality and uncover issues through real usage.
- **Steps**:
  1. Make the company use the product in everyday workflows.
  2. Create incentives or templates that naturally move internal work into the product.
  3. Increase the number of hours employees spend in the product.
  4. Let internal users surface friction through real use, not abstract feedback.
  5. Use those experiences to build personal accountability for fixing issues.
- **Example**: Figma moved from memos to decks so PMs would build presentations in Figma, and used FigJam for performance calibrations and product reviews.

### Community-led growth
- **When to use**: When selling a product depends on internal champions and peer advocacy rather than traditional top-down sales.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify passionate users inside target companies.
  2. Equip them with stories, data, and product visibility.
  3. Help them make the case internally to leadership and adjacent teams.
  4. Build community spaces and programs that strengthen user-to-user connection.
  5. Let the sales team amplify the champion rather than replace them.
- **Example**: Figma’s sales team helps internal design champions build the case for wider adoption, while programs like Friends of Figma deepen advocacy.

## Core Advice
- **When you face a messy product discussion with many opinions**: do the synthesis work and distill it into one clear takeaway — because PM value comes from turning scattered viewpoints into a coherent direction.
- **When your team is debating features without agreement on the underlying problem**: own the “why” before debating the “what” or “how” — because shared purpose lets designers and engineers make strong local decisions.
- **When you need to improve storytelling as a PM**: reset your context and explain the problem as if the audience knows nothing — because it helps you escape the curse of knowledge.
- **When a product insight needs to spread inside the company**: turn it into a memorable phrase, meme, or repeatable story — because repeatable ideas are more likely to drive action.
- **When you’re deciding what customer feedback to trust**: use a balanced portfolio of inputs instead of over-weighting the loudest voices — because each channel reveals different blind spots.
- **When a customer asks for a feature**: ask why they want it, then ask why the underlying problem exists — because the root cause may reveal a bigger opportunity.
- **When your team’s planning process feels fake or overly metric-driven**: start with a legible headline and a real commitment before forcing metrics — because metrics without intent are often post-rationalizations.
- **When internal teams are not feeling the product’s pain firsthand**: get them using the product in their own workflows as much as possible — because direct usage creates accountability.
- **When an engineer or designer wants to fix a real issue they experienced**: let them move quickly instead of blocking them with top-down process — because bottom-up energy often improves quality faster.
- **When you’re hiring PMs or product leaders**: probe for their ability to frame controversy, explain both sides, and make a boring problem compelling — because that reveals storytelling and perspective-taking.
- **When your product depends on adoption inside large organizations**: help your internal champion become a superhero with data, stories, and product visibility — because champions drive the strongest growth motion.
- **When you’re building a product that changes how people work**: equip users with a philosophy, not just a tool — because people champion a new way of working, not just software.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: PMs should own the idea and define the solution in detail. → **Their view**: PMs should primarily own the “why”; the idea, what, and how can come from customers, designers, and engineers — because clear purpose scales decision-making better than centralized solution design.
- **Conventional**: OKRs are the best way to manage product teams. → **Their view**: Rigid OKRs often become performative or disconnected from real work; sometimes a simple headline or commitment is better — because teams can optimize for the wrong or unmovable metrics.
- **Conventional**: The loudest customer feedback should drive priorities. → **Their view**: Vocal feedback is only one signal and should be treated as a canary, not the source of truth — because a balanced mix prevents blind spots.
- **Conventional**: Product-led growth is mostly about self-serve acquisition and conversion. → **Their view**: At Figma, growth is better understood as community-led growth, where internal champions drive adoption — because passionate users inside companies are the real growth engine.
- **Conventional**: Great product teams should avoid bottom-up exceptions and stick to roadmap priorities. → **Their view**: If someone on the team experiences a real issue and wants to fix it, that bottom-up energy should usually be encouraged — because personal accountability often leads to faster, better quality improvements.
