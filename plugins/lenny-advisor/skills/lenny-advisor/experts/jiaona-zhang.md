# Jiaona Zhang

**Senior Vice President of Product** | **Webflow** | Expertise: product-management, leadership, design, strategy

## Bio
Jiaona Zhang is the SVP of Product at Webflow and a Stanford lecturer in product management. She has led product at companies like Airbnb, Dropbox, WeWork, and Pocket Gems, giving her a rare cross-company view of what actually works in product strategy, execution, and leadership.

Her perspective matters because she combines strong product taste with operational discipline. She is especially useful when you need to decide what quality bar to ship, how to frame a roadmap, or how to grow as a PM without relying on formal authority.

## Signature Frameworks

### Minimum Lovable Product
- **When to use**: When deciding what quality bar to ship for a new product or feature, especially in competitive markets or when users have strong alternatives.
- **Steps**:
  1. Understand the user’s current alternatives and tolerance for roughness.
  2. Define what would make the experience feel genuinely good, not merely functional.
  3. Prioritize doing a smaller number of things extremely well.
  4. Add selective “pixie dust” in a few high-impact areas that create delight.
  5. If your team cannot reach lovable quality alone, consider whether partners or the ecosystem can supply the missing value.
- **Example**: At Webflow, Zhang evaluated features like memberships and logic by asking whether the team could get beyond “viable” to “lovable,” and whether the ecosystem could help provide the missing polish.

### Problem-First Product Thinking
- **When to use**: When teams are tempted to jump straight to solutions, especially early PMs or companies reacting to competitors.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with the user problem, not a preselected solution.
  2. Clarify the real underlying need.
  3. Separate the problem from the implementation idea.
  4. Generate multiple solution options.
  5. Choose the option that best fits company strengths and economics.
- **Example**: For Airbnb Plus, she argued the team should have focused on the real problem—helping guests know what they were getting—rather than defaulting to inspections and managed inventory.

### Big Vision, Phased Learning
- **When to use**: When pursuing ambitious bets that are uncertain, expensive, or operationally complex.
- **Steps**:
  1. Set a bold long-term vision.
  2. Define the current phase as a learning or prototyping phase.
  3. Use unscalable methods temporarily to test assumptions.
  4. Specify what you are trying to learn in a fixed time window.
  5. Set short-interval milestones with clear go/no-go criteria.
  6. Stop or pivot before sunk-cost thinking takes over.
- **Example**: Reflecting on Airbnb Plus, she recommended quarter-by-quarter milestones and short learning windows instead of letting a multi-year effort continue unchecked.

### Story-Driven Roadmapping
- **When to use**: When creating roadmaps and prioritization plans that teams and stakeholders need to understand and rally around.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the core story of what the team is trying to achieve.
  2. Organize the roadmap into themes rather than a flat list of projects.
  3. Explain why these themes matter for users and the business.
  4. Let detailed projects and execution plans sit underneath the themes.
  5. Document the roadmap in a written doc, not just a spreadsheet or deck.
  6. Link to live execution systems like Jira instead of maintaining stale snapshots.
- **Example**: She contrasted a roadmap story with a spreadsheet of RICE scores, arguing that teams need to understand why a body of work matters, not just see ranked rows.

### Qualitative-First OKRs
- **When to use**: When setting OKRs that should drive ambition and real outcomes rather than sandbagging.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define qualitatively what “crushing it” would look like.
  2. Clarify the spirit of the outcome for users and the business.
  3. Translate that into measurable goals only after the qualitative success state is clear.
  4. Prefer ambitious targets that may miss over safe targets that guarantee green.
  5. Break large ambitions into milestone paths across quarters.
  6. Evaluate learning and progress, not just whether the metric was green.
- **Example**: She said she would rather see red or yellow OKRs with real learning than all-green OKRs where users and the company feel no meaningful difference.

### First 90 Days as a Product Leader
- **When to use**: When joining a new company in a senior product leadership role and needing to build context quickly.
- **Steps**:
  1. Map the people you need to learn from across functions, levels, and tenure.
  2. Front-load conversations with your direct team, leadership peers, and frontline experts.
  3. Synthesize context on both product issues and organizational dynamics.
  4. Identify what should keep moving versus what needs more research or rethinking.
  5. Create a concrete plan with owners before major transitions or absences.
  6. Build trust before spending too much political capital on change.
- **Example**: Before maternity leave at Webflow, she scheduled 40–50 conversations, identified strategic gaps, assigned research owners, and aligned leadership and the board on key issues like engineering hiring.

### Build a PM Reputation Around a Superpower
- **When to use**: When early- to mid-career PMs want to accelerate growth and earn more responsibility.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify a capability you can become exceptional at.
  2. Lean into projects that showcase that strength repeatedly.
  3. Deliver consistently enough that people associate you with that capability.
  4. Use that reputation to earn larger and more complex opportunities.
  5. Evolve your superpower as your role shifts from IC to manager and leader.
- **Example**: Zhang first became known for analytics in gaming, then for execution and complex launches at Dropbox, which led to more responsibility.

## Core Advice
- **When leadership loves a product idea but you think the solution is wrong**: Understand the real user problem, validate the economics, and return with better options — because pushback is more persuasive when it supports the original intent.
- **When you’re under pressure to ship an MVP quickly**: Define the minimum lovable experience and focus on a few things done extremely well — because users compare you to alternatives, not to your internal timeline.
- **When your roadmap feels ambiguous**: Create a narrative with themes and why they matter — because teams align better around story than around scored rows in a spreadsheet.
- **When OKR planning risks sandbagging**: Start with a qualitative definition of success and set ambitious goals that may miss — because safe OKRs often hide lack of ambition.
- **When a strategic bet is uncertain and expensive**: Break it into short learning phases with explicit milestones and go/no-go decisions — because ambition without checkpoints leads to sunk-cost traps.
- **When you’re new in a senior leadership role**: Schedule conversations across functions and levels, not just with your direct reports — because context comes from both leadership narratives and frontline reality.
- **When hiring pressure is high but proof is limited**: Tie hiring to milestone gates and demonstrated results — because over-hiring creates drag and painful layoffs.
- **When you’re early in your PM career**: Become excellent at one identifiable capability and build a reputation around it — because people promote PMs who are known for something concrete.
- **When considering adjacent opportunities**: Anchor decisions in why users already love your product — because chasing competitors can dilute your core advantage.
- **When you’re outside your expertise as a leader**: Ask for help from peers, founders, engineers, mentors, and your team — because strong leadership is collaborative, not omniscient.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: PMs are mini-CEOs who decide what gets built. → **Their view**: PMs have very little formal authority and succeed through influence, problem understanding, and editing options — because the role is about alignment, not command.
- **Conventional**: An MVP should be as minimal as possible. → **Their view**: In many markets, the right target is a minimum lovable product — because “barely viable” often loses to better alternatives.
- **Conventional**: Roadmaps should be rigorous spreadsheets with scoring frameworks. → **Their view**: Roadmaps should primarily be stories with themes and rationale — because humans align around purpose, not false precision.
- **Conventional**: Good OKRs are green OKRs. → **Their view**: All-green OKRs can signal insufficient ambition — because red or yellow can reflect real stretch and learning.
- **Conventional**: Big visions require aggressive hiring. → **Their view**: Hiring should follow milestone proof, not ambition alone — because over-hiring often causes waste and layoffs.
- **Conventional**: Winning means responding directly to competitors. → **Their view**: Start with why users already love your product and build from that core — because compounding your strengths beats trend-chasing.
