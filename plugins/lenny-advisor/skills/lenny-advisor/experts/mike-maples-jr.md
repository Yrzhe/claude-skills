# Mike Maples Jr.

**Founding Partner** | **Floodgate** | Expertise: seed investing, startup idea generation, product strategy, venture capital

## Bio
Mike Maples Jr. is a legendary early-stage investor and founding partner at Floodgate, one of the earliest seed-stage venture firms. He has backed companies like Twitter, Lyft, Twitch, Okta, Rappi, and Applied Intuition, and is known for a sharp theory of how breakthrough startups are actually born.

His perspective matters because he does not just evaluate startups by market size or execution quality. He looks for the deeper ingredients of category creation: inflections, insights, and founder-future fit.

## Signature Frameworks

### The Three Elements of Breakthrough Startup Ideas
- **When to use**: When evaluating or generating a startup idea and trying to determine whether it can break through versus merely compete.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify one or more external inflections that create a new possibility.
  2. Develop a non-obvious insight that harnesses those inflections to change behavior.
  3. Assess founder-future fit: whether the founder/team is authentically suited to that future.
  4. Stress test whether the idea is from the future rather than an extension of the present.
  5. Iterate on implementation if the insight is right but the first product expression is wrong.
- **Example**: Lyft: the iPhone 4S GPS chip was the inflection, “Airbnb for cars” was the insight, and the founders were well-positioned to build for that future.

### Inflection Stress Test
- **When to use**: When assessing whether a market shift is real enough to support a startup.
- **Steps**:
  1. Name the specific new thing that was introduced.
  2. Define the specific empowerment it creates and for whom.
  3. Identify the conditions required for that empowerment to be realized.
  4. Check whether those conditions are likely to persist or could be reversed by regulation, customer behavior, or platform decisions.
- **Example**: Telemedicine during COVID: shelter-in-place made cross-state telemedicine legal and reimbursable, empowering patients and doctors; the key question was whether those rules would revert after the emergency.

### Earned Secret Discovery
- **When to use**: When trying to uncover a startup idea that others have not yet seen.
- **Steps**:
  1. Get hands dirty with a new technology, behavior, or ecosystem.
  2. Spend enough time at the cutting edge to notice what is missing.
  3. Actively look for surprises rather than only validating assumptions.
  4. Treat unexpected findings as signals of an undiscovered opportunity.
  5. Translate the discovered secret into a product or company thesis.
- **Example**: Chegg discovered students would rent textbooks for more than expected by testing multiple price points, revealing a secret about demand and willingness to pay.

### Movement-Based Go-to-Market
- **When to use**: When launching a startup that needs early believers, not just customers.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define a higher-purpose future that people can believe in.
  2. Frame the current world as inadequate without directly attacking the incumbent.
  3. Turn the incumbent's strength into a weakness by showing why the alternative is better for a specific group.
  4. Recruit early believers who are emotionally committed, not merely pragmatic.
  5. Let the movement accumulate until the heresy becomes conventional wisdom.
- **Example**: Airbnb’s “live like a local” framing created a movement around a different travel experience rather than simply saying hotels were bad.

### Hero's Journey Startup Storytelling
- **When to use**: When pitching customers, investors, or employees and needing them to feel like protagonists in the future you are creating.
- **Steps**:
  1. Describe the world that is and the pain or limitation in it.
  2. Describe the world that could be.
  3. Introduce the founder/company as the guide, not the hero.
  4. Give the audience a reason to believe they can win by joining the journey.
  5. Tailor the story to the specific audience's version of heroism.
- **Example**: Lyft’s pink mustache and app story made ride-sharing feel less scary and helped riders see themselves as part of the new future.

### Pattern-Breaking Company Building Inside a Large Organization
- **When to use**: When trying to build a breakthrough product inside an incumbent company.
- **Steps**:
  1. Treat the new initiative as a distinct business, not a small extension of the core.
  2. Keep the effort relatively invisible early so it is not pulled back into the mothership's norms.
  3. Give it autonomous leadership and a separate operating model.
  4. Use small bets that can fail without threatening the core business.
  5. Decide whether the acquisition or initiative is meant to extend the current business or break the pattern entirely.
- **Example**: Lockheed’s Skunkworks and IBM’s PC effort were cited as examples of separate, maverick teams that could create something genuinely new.

## Core Advice
- **When you are evaluating a startup idea and want to know if it can become a breakout company**: Check whether it has at least one real inflection, a non-obvious insight, and founder-future fit — because breakthrough companies usually have all three.
- **When you think you have a good market opportunity because it is big and underserved**: Do not start from the market definition incumbents already created; look for a slightly more ambiguous opportunity that lets you change the rules — because obvious markets trap founders in the incumbent’s frame.
- **When you are trying to identify an inflection**: Ask what specific new thing just appeared, who it empowers, and under what conditions that empowerment will actually be realized — because this separates real turning points from hype.
- **When you are exploring a new technology or behavior**: Actively seek surprises instead of only validating your hypothesis — because surprises often reveal the undiscovered opportunity.
- **When you are early in product-market fit and a customer is lukewarm**: Diagnose whether the insight is wrong, the implementation is wrong, or you are talking to the wrong customer — because weak response does not always mean the opportunity is bad.
- **When you are building a startup and want to find early believers**: Focus on people who already live in the future you are building for — because they understand the value immediately and can co-create the product.
- **When you are pitching and getting too much generic feedback**: Do not let every objection reshape the deck; keep the core insight clear — because trying to satisfy everyone creates a Franken deck.
- **When you are choosing between a broadly liked product and one intensely loved by a smaller group**: Prefer the product that forces a choice rather than a comparison — because startups win by being meaningfully different.
- **When you are launching a new product and people may resist it**: Use storytelling and symbols that reduce fear and make the new behavior feel natural — because adoption is often emotional before it is rational.
- **When you are building a movement around a startup**: Frame the product as part of a higher-purpose future, not as a direct attack on the incumbent — because identity and aspiration are stronger than feature comparison.
- **When you are working inside a large company on a breakthrough initiative**: Make the project small enough to fail, autonomous enough to stay distinct, and separate enough to avoid being pulled back into the core business — because pattern-breaking efforts need different operating rules.
- **When you are tempted to seek universal approval for your idea**: Accept that most people may not like it; look for the small group that is desperate for it — because great startups often begin with intense love from a few.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Startups win by executing better than incumbents. → **Their view**: Startups usually win by proposing a radically different future that disorients the incumbent — because incumbents have structural advantages and startups need asymmetric warfare.
- **Conventional**: The best startup ideas are obvious if you analyze a big market and unmet needs. → **Their view**: The best ideas are often non-consensus and slightly ambiguous — because they harness a future that is not yet obvious.
- **Conventional**: Founders should seek broad validation and make everyone like the idea. → **Their view**: Great ideas often repel most people and attract a small group of irrationally enthusiastic believers — because if everyone likes it, it is probably too close to consensus.
- **Conventional**: A founder should be the hero of the startup story. → **Their view**: The early believers are the heroes; the founder is the guide — because people join the future, not the founder’s self-image.
- **Conventional**: Being coachable means listening to all advice and incorporating it. → **Their view**: The right kind of founder is often disagreeable — because breakthrough founders need conviction and independence.
- **Conventional**: A startup pitch should anticipate and answer every objection. → **Their view**: Over-answering objections can weaken the pitch — because clarity about the core insight matters more than exhaustive rebuttal.
