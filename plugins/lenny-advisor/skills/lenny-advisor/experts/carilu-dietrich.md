# Carilu Dietrich

**Advisor to hypergrowth CEOs and CMOs; former CMO and former head of marketing at Atlassian** | **Independent advisor; previously Atlassian, Miro, Segment, 1Password** | Expertise: growth, go-to-market, leadership, product-led growth

## Bio
Carilu Dietrich is a veteran B2B SaaS marketing and growth leader who helped lead marketing at Atlassian through its IPO and now advises hypergrowth companies like Miro, Segment, 1Password, Bill.com, and Productboard. Her perspective matters because she has seen what actually scales in modern SaaS: strong product pull, durable word of mouth, and company-wide alignment around the next growth stage.

She is especially valuable when a company is trying to move from good to great—whether that means entering the C-suite, shifting from PLG to sales-assisted growth, or expanding upmarket without breaking the business.

## Signature Frameworks

### Five ways to prepare for the C-suite
- **When to use**: When you want to accelerate from functional leadership into executive roles.
- **Steps**:
  1. Tie your responsibilities directly to revenue and understand how your work affects business outcomes.
  2. Learn to think and speak in the language of the CEO and board, especially finance and business performance.
  3. Understand how the whole company system works, not just your function.
  4. Build strong cross-functional relationships by doing good work and helping others.
  5. Choose high-momentum companies where growth creates larger roles and faster career progression.
- **Example**: She contrasted her slower growth at Oracle, where her team grew from 5 to 7 over five years, with Atlassian, where her team grew from 15 to 100 in four years.

### Company selection scorecard
- **When to use**: When evaluating whether to join, advise, or invest time in a company.
- **Steps**:
  1. Check Rule of 40 performance.
  2. Assess the quality of investors and the quality of recent funding rounds.
  3. Look at customer love signals such as Net Promoter Score.
  4. Review net dollar retention to understand expansion strength.
  5. Examine recent growth rate.
  6. Check burn rate and financial durability.
  7. Assess whether the company is a category leader, including analyst validation like Gartner or Forrester.
  8. Review Glassdoor and employee sentiment.
- **Example**: She cited Snowflake’s exceptional net dollar retention as a benchmark for a very healthy business.

### Hypergrowth formula
- **When to use**: When trying to understand what enables companies to grow at exceptional rates.
- **Steps**:
  1. Build an amazing product that users genuinely love.
  2. Create organic, inbound, and viral word-of-mouth loops so users bring in other users.
  3. Ride the lightning by hiring leaders who have already seen the next stage of scale.
- **Example**: She pointed to Miro’s collaborative whiteboard usage spreading naturally across teams and 1Password being brought from company to company by users.

### Word-of-mouth growth engine
- **When to use**: When you want to strengthen organic growth without relying primarily on paid acquisition.
- **Steps**:
  1. Empower internal experts to become thought leaders in the market.
  2. Create strong content in existing communities and channels where your audience already learns.
  3. Build your own customer community so happy users can influence prospects.
- **Example**: Atlassian grew community through local user groups where they paid for beer and pizza and let users connect around products and development topics.

### PLG vs sales-led decision framework
- **When to use**: When deciding whether a product should be self-serve, sales-assisted, or sales-led.
- **Steps**:
  1. Assess whether your buyer prefers self-education or expects a sales process.
  2. Evaluate time-to-value: if users can get value in days, PLG is more viable.
  3. Determine whether the product needs customization, context, or handholding to be understood.
  4. Delay sales hiring if the product has not yet earned enough resonance to justify the cost.
  5. Consider hybrid roles like sales engineers to guide users without overcommitting to traditional sales.
- **Example**: She noted that developer tools often work well with PLG, while categories like HR may require sales earlier; she also described companies engaging sales only after usage thresholds are hit.

### Cross-company growth lever alignment
- **When to use**: When pursuing major growth moves like moving upmarket, adding products, entering new segments, or expanding globally.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the growth move as a company strategy, not a single department initiative.
  2. Align product roadmap, marketing, sales, support, and research around the same target.
  3. Fund the initiative appropriately across functions.
  4. Use executive alignment or offsites/OKRs to formalize the shift.
- **Example**: She described enterprise expansion as requiring not just more leads, but enterprise features, enterprise-capable support, customer research, and sales talent.

### Press release first product planning
- **When to use**: When product and marketing are misaligned on what a launch actually means to customers.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start by writing the press release for the product or feature before building it.
  2. Define the customer outcome and why it matters.
  3. Use that narrative to test whether the concept is compelling enough.
  4. Align product and marketing on the theme before development proceeds.
- **Example**: She described this as a way to avoid handing marketing “a bag of doorknobs” instead of a coherent product story.

## Core Advice
- **When you want to reach the executive level**: Do extra learning, take on responsibilities outside your formal role, and deliberately understand adjacent functions — because executive roles require systems thinking, strategic judgment, and credibility beyond one specialty.
- **When the job market is weak or you’ve been laid off**: Preserve endurance, use your network aggressively, keep building skills, and avoid settling for a bad company if you can — because downturns are survivable, but poor company choices can compound into weaker future opportunities.
- **When choosing where to work early in your career**: Join a high-momentum company with strong growth and strong people — because fast-growing companies create larger scope, better logos, stronger networks, and faster career growth.
- **When pressure builds to spend heavily on brand advertising**: Only spend if the product is already strong and the campaign can stay focused and consistent — because advertising amplifies product reality, it rarely fixes weak product experience.
- **When trying to create hypergrowth**: Strengthen product love, user-to-user spread, and leadership capacity for the next scale stage — because you cannot buy hypergrowth efficiently; durable hypergrowth comes from product pull, virality, and organizational readiness.
- **When deciding whether to hire sales for a PLG business**: Don’t hire sales too early; first confirm users can reach value quickly and that the product resonates on its own — because sales is expensive and can mask product problems.
- **When a product is almost self-serve but still confusing**: Use sales engineers or technically capable guides instead of traditional SDRs — because this extends PLG while helping users overcome complexity without forcing a heavy sales motion.
- **When pursuing a major growth move like upmarket or a new segment**: Treat it as a company-wide strategic shift with aligned OKRs, roadmap, support, and GTM changes — because big growth levers fail when treated as a marketing or sales problem alone.
- **When you want more word of mouth**: Invest in expert-led thought leadership, useful content, and customer communities — because users trust practitioners and peers more than company messaging.
- **When building early products**: Do things that don’t scale, including deep direct observation of customers using the product — because close customer contact reveals the real friction and emotional journey that later drives retention and product-market fit.
- **When considering bundling in PLG**: Lead with a single product for initial adoption, then expand later — because bundling increases evaluation complexity and slows self-serve conversion.
- **When anxiety or uncertainty spikes**: Convert worry into urgency and focus only on what you can control next — because action on controllable variables restores momentum.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: You can buy growth with enough paid marketing and advertising. → **Their view**: Hypergrowth cannot be purchased sustainably; it must be driven by product love, inbound demand, and virality — because paid channels are too expensive to support hypergrowth economics at scale.
- **Conventional**: Hiring sales earlier is the safest way to accelerate revenue. → **Their view**: Hiring sales too early can become a costly crutch that hides product weaknesses — because if the product lacks resonance, sales adds burn without solving adoption.
- **Conventional**: Bundling more products helps you win faster because customers get a fuller suite. → **Their view**: In PLG, bundling is often a poor land strategy; a single-product entry works better — because multiple-product evaluation slows self-serve decisions.
- **Conventional**: Growth problems usually belong to marketing or sales. → **Their view**: The biggest growth levers are company strategy problems, not departmental problems — because moves like upmarket expansion require coordinated changes across the company.
- **Conventional**: Economic downturns are periods when careers stall. → **Their view**: Downturns can be some of the best times to accelerate your career — because ambitious people can take on neglected work and expand scope.
- **Conventional**: Brand advertising is a major lever for fixing growth. → **Their view**: Advertising mostly amplifies what is already true about the product — because weak product experience overwhelms brand spend.
