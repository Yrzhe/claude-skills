# Matt Mochary

**CEO coach** | **Independent / Mochary Method** | Expertise: leadership, executive coaching, organizational design, startup operations

## Bio
Matt Mochary is a full-time executive coach for founders and CEOs, with deep experience coaching leaders at companies like OpenAI, Coinbase, Reddit, Rippling, and Notion. His perspective matters because it is unusually practical: he has spent years testing management practices in real companies and turning what works into repeatable playbooks.

He is especially valuable when leaders need help with hard people decisions, emotional regulation, operating cadence, and building high-performing teams without unnecessary complexity.

## Signature Frameworks

### Fear and anger give bad advice
- **When to use**: When a leader feels stuck, defensive, avoidant, or overly reactive in a high-stakes decision.
- **Steps**:
  1. Notice when fear or anger may be driving the reaction.
  2. Assume the emotion is distorting judgment and exaggerating outcomes.
  3. Check the decision with someone who is not in fear.
  4. Test the feared prediction against reality, often by making a lower-stakes bet or taking a small action first.
  5. Once proven, use the reminder “I perceive you to be in fear/anger” to help the person regain clarity.
- **Example**: A CEO feared telling the board about a business problem. Matt predicted the opposite outcome, the CEO disclosed transparently, and the board responded with increased trust.

### Humane firing and layoff process
- **When to use**: When letting someone go individually or running a larger layoff.
- **Steps**:
  1. Deliver the news one-on-one, ideally from the direct manager.
  2. Warn the person that the conversation will be difficult so the surprise does not trigger a stronger emotional reaction.
  3. State the decision clearly and briefly.
  4. Invite the person to express fear, anger, sadness, or other emotions.
  5. Actively listen and make them feel heard.
  6. Become their agent by actively helping them find a role that fits their strengths and passions.
  7. For larger layoffs, communicate separately to the stay team, explain the rationale, and hold one-on-ones to process emotions.
- **Example**: Matt described a layoff where each manager delivered the news in a one-on-one, then later helped the affected people find their next role.

### Decision vs implementation
- **When to use**: When making hard people decisions or any choice that affects multiple stakeholders.
- **Steps**:
  1. Separate the decision from the implementation.
  2. Identify the primary stakeholder the decision should optimize for, usually the customer.
  3. Make the decision based on that stakeholder’s best interest.
  4. Then map who will be hurt by implementation.
  5. For each affected party, ask what they actually want.
  6. Design the implementation to help each party get closer to what they want.
- **Example**: In firing someone, the decision may be to remove a non-top performer for the customer’s benefit, while the implementation focuses on helping the employee find a better-fitting job and helping the team process the loss.

### Feeling heard
- **When to use**: When giving feedback, resolving conflict, or helping someone process a difficult change.
- **Steps**:
  1. If in a group, have people write their thoughts first and read them asynchronously.
  2. Repeat back the person’s words to confirm basic understanding.
  3. Go deeper by reflecting the underlying thoughts they may be censoring or softening.
  4. Ask whether your reflection is accurate or directionally right.
  5. After they feel heard, decide whether you accept the feedback.
  6. If you do not accept it, explain your perspective and what is happening in your world.
- **Example**: Matt would say, “I think what I heard you say is...” and then push deeper until the other person felt accurately understood.

### Top goal
- **When to use**: When your day gets consumed by other people’s requests and you need to protect strategic progress.
- **Steps**:
  1. Choose one priority that matters most.
  2. Block dedicated time every day for that priority.
  3. Treat that time as non-negotiable.
  4. Use an accountability partner if you struggle to stay focused.
  5. Repeat consistently until the habit sticks.
- **Example**: Matt recommends 30 minutes to two hours per day on the top goal, and he sometimes has someone sit with him to keep him on task.

### Energy audit and zone of genius
- **When to use**: When you want to redesign your calendar around what creates energy and value.
- **Steps**:
  1. Review a representative two weeks of your calendar.
  2. Mark each hour green if it gave you more energy and red if it drained you.
  3. Look for patterns in the red blocks.
  4. For each draining activity, decide whether to eliminate it, delegate it, or improve it.
  5. For unavoidable work, redesign the process so it becomes exquisite and less draining.
  6. Repeat until your calendar is mostly green.
- **Example**: Matt suggests turning a three-hour exec meeting into a 45-minute meeting by requiring pre-read updates and pre-routed issues.

### Small-team innovation inside a large company
- **When to use**: When a mature company wants to build new products without slowing down the core business.
- **Steps**:
  1. Create a small, founder-mentality team.
  2. Keep the team outside the core product/engineering/design hierarchy.
  3. Report the team directly to the CEO or another outside-of-EPD leader.
  4. Decouple the new product from the core codebase and brand.
  5. Optionally create a separate legal entity for the new product.
  6. Run parallel approaches, such as an engineering-heavy team and a customer/manual-solution team, to see which moves faster.
- **Example**: Wei Deng reportedly created multiple C corps for new products and ran two independent teams per idea to accelerate experimentation.

## Core Advice
- **When you feel fear before a hard decision**: assume your brain is giving you distorted advice and check the decision with someone who is not afraid — because fear exaggerates downside and makes people avoid the exact action that may be necessary.
- **When you are about to tell investors bad news**: disclose transparently and early rather than hiding the problem — because Matt has repeatedly seen transparency increase trust rather than destroy it.
- **When you notice yourself getting angry**: pause immediately and do not act until you have shifted out of anger — because anger destroys relationships and often covers underlying pain.
- **When you need to tell someone difficult news**: warn them first that the conversation will be hard and give them a few seconds to prepare — because surprise is a major trigger for the amygdala.
- **When someone is upset and you want them to feel heard**: reflect back both their words and the stronger thoughts you think they may be holding back — because people usually feel most understood when you capture the underlying thought, not just the polite version.
- **When you are making a firing decision**: separate the decision from the implementation and optimize the decision for the customer — because the right decision can still be implemented humanely for the employee and the team.
- **When you let someone go**: actively help them find their next role instead of offering only a passive reference — because being their agent reduces harm, preserves dignity, and often leads to a better outcome for everyone.
- **When you are running a layoff**: have each manager deliver the news one-on-one to their own reports — because a personalized conversation is the clearest marker of a humane layoff.
- **When your company is growing and coordination is breaking down**: consider reducing headcount or keeping the team smaller rather than defaulting to more hiring — because every additional person adds coordination overhead, context-sharing burden, and morale friction.
- **When your calendar is full of work that drains you**: eliminate, delegate, or redesign the work until your calendar is mostly green — because you create more value when you spend more time in your zone of genius.
- **When you need to protect strategic progress**: block daily top-goal time and use an accountability partner if necessary — because without protected time, urgent requests will consume the day.
- **When you want to innovate inside a large company**: spin up a small, autonomous team outside the core hierarchy and let it move fast — because large-company processes, code review, and brand risk slow experimentation.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: More people means more capacity and better performance. → **Their view**: In many companies, fewer people can make the organization perform better — because each additional human adds coordination overhead, information-flow friction, and morale management.
- **Conventional**: The best way to handle layoffs is to communicate broadly and quickly via email or group announcements. → **Their view**: The most important factor is a one-on-one delivery from the manager — because group delivery feels dehumanizing and prevents safe emotional processing.
- **Conventional**: Equity is the main thing that motivates people to build new products inside a large company. → **Their view**: Autonomy, ownership, and seeing work used in the world matter more than equity — because real decision-making power is often more motivating than upside alone.
- **Conventional**: Fear is a useful signal that should be respected. → **Their view**: Fear often gives bad advice and points you away from the right action — because it exaggerates downside and blocks necessary moves.
- **Conventional**: A manager should simply give feedback and a reference when someone is let go. → **Their view**: A humane manager should actively become the person’s agent and help them land their next role — because passive support is not enough.
- **Conventional**: To innovate in a large company, you should use the existing org structure and processes. → **Their view**: The new product should often be structurally separated, sometimes even as its own C corp — because decoupling lets the team move at startup speed.
