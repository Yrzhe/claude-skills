# Peter Deng

**General Partner at Felicis** | **Felicis** | Expertise: product-management, ai-strategy, leadership, growth

## Bio
Peter Deng is a veteran product leader who has helped build and scale products at Facebook, Instagram, Uber, Airtable, Oculus, and OpenAI. His perspective matters because he combines product taste with systems thinking, operational rigor, and a strong instinct for what makes products feel human and durable.

Now as a General Partner at Felicis, he brings that same lens to investing: looking for products with real user resonance, defensible data advantages, and teams that can scale without losing quality.

## Signature Frameworks

### Scale sustainably with systems thinking
- **When to use**: When a product has found early traction and is moving from one-to-many growth toward hyperscale.
- **Steps**:
  1. Recognize that zero-to-one is different from one-to-100.
  2. Plan several moves ahead instead of optimizing only for the next launch.
  3. Build systems and architecture that let the team move faster over time.
  4. Use a portfolio approach to allocate effort across growth, craft, infrastructure, and new bets.
  5. Measure rigorously so scaling decisions are based on real signals.
  6. Accept that sometimes you need to go slow to go fast.
- **Example**: At Facebook, News Feed and Messenger invested in architecture and notification systems; at Uber, pickup/drop-off abstractions and venue logic enabled global scaling.

### Build AI products around data flywheels and workflow fit
- **When to use**: When creating an AI startup or AI feature that must defend against foundation-model commoditization.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify proprietary or uniquely generated data you can access at the start.
  2. Design the product so usage creates more high-signal data over time.
  3. Embed the product into a specific workflow where users do real work.
  4. Optimize ergonomics so the product feels native to the user’s life or job.
  5. Use the collected data to improve the product continuously.
- **Example**: Windsurf’s code accept/reject signals became unique training data, allowing the product to improve from actual usage.

### Hire and manage as if you are assembling an Avengers team
- **When to use**: When building a product org or leadership team that needs both speed and balance.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the capabilities the company actually needs, not just generic headcount.
  2. Hire people with complementary spikes rather than clones.
  3. Create healthy tension between different archetypes, such as craft vs. growth.
  4. Set the expectation that strong hires should become autonomous quickly.
  5. Use the team composition itself as a lever for scale.
- **Example**: At OpenAI, Peter emphasized hiring leaders in design, engineering, product, and marketing who each brought distinct strengths and could challenge one another constructively.

### Use the IDEO design-thinking loop
- **When to use**: When solving a user problem from first principles or validating a new product direction.
- **Steps**:
  1. Empathize deeply with the user’s pain.
  2. Define the problem precisely.
  3. Ideate possible solutions.
  4. Prototype quickly.
  5. Test with real users.
- **Example**: Peter described dogfooding Uber rides and doing user research as ways to empathize before defining and testing solutions like Uber Reserve.

### Run product leadership through clear language
- **When to use**: When writing strategy docs, PRDs, slide decks, or aligning teams on a vision.
- **Steps**:
  1. Choose words intentionally because language shapes thought.
  2. Compress ideas into the fewest precise words possible.
  3. Repeat the message so it sticks across the org.
  4. Close the loop by saying what you will do, what you are doing, and what you did.
  5. Use writing to clarify intuition and create shared understanding.
- **Example**: Peter had Joanne Jang write down a new role concept, which became the basis for the model designer role at OpenAI.

### Find product-market fit through human resonance
- **When to use**: When deciding whether to join, build, or invest in a company.
- **Steps**:
  1. Look for a unique insight about human behavior or a real human need.
  2. Check whether the product aligns with how people naturally behave.
  3. Prefer strong conviction over confusion.
  4. Choose the place where you will learn the most.
  5. Make sure the company’s mission matches your values.
- **Example**: Peter joined Facebook because it understood loneliness, connection, and sharing in a way that resonated with his psychology background.

## Core Advice
- **When a product is starting to scale quickly**: build systems, not just features — because architecture, instrumentation, and process determine whether growth compounds or collapses under complexity.
- **When deciding what matters most in a product**: focus on the user’s real job-to-be-done and the highest-leverage variables, not just the pixels — because price, ETA, and operations can matter more than UI polish.
- **When building an AI startup**: anchor the product in proprietary data and a workflow that naturally generates more of that data — because defensibility comes from a data flywheel plus deep workflow integration.
- **When trying to create a durable AI advantage**: make the experience so good that users would switch even if incumbents have distribution advantages — because craft can overcome distribution when the experience is meaningfully better.
- **When scaling from early traction to broad adoption**: measure everything and build a growth team early — because growth forces rigor and turns intuition into testable hypotheses.
- **When hiring for a product org**: hire complementary archetypes rather than people who all think the same way — because healthy tension improves decisions across craft, growth, business, platform, and research.
- **When evaluating a candidate for leadership**: ask whether, in six months, you will still be telling them what to do — because the best hires become autonomous quickly.
- **When interviewing for a manager or senior IC role**: probe for growth mindset by asking about a painful mistake and what changed afterward — because reflection and behavior change signal coachability.
- **When managing up or leading a team**: say what you’re going to do, say that you’re doing it, and say that you did it — because repetition improves alignment and accountability.
- **When tempted to over-optimize details**: care deeply about craft, but explicitly separate important details from irrelevant ones — because great product people know where quality matters most.
- **When trying to understand a user problem**: dogfood the product and do live user research instead of relying only on summaries — because direct exposure creates empathy that notes cannot.
- **When deciding whether to join a company or start something new**: choose the place where you will learn the most and where the mission matches your values — because learning and human resonance compound over time.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: The product UI is the main product. → **Their view**: Sometimes the real product is price, ETA, operations, or other non-UI factors — because perceived value is often driven by operational realities.
- **Conventional**: Move fast and break things during growth. → **Their view**: Once you find traction, you often need to slow down to build the right systems before scaling further — because fragile growth breaks without architecture and instrumentation.
- **Conventional**: A growth team is mainly for acquisition. → **Their view**: A growth team is also a forcing function for rigor, measurement, and truth-telling — because it exposes missing data and weak hypotheses.
- **Conventional**: Great product leaders should mostly interview for taste, execution, or metrics. → **Their view**: The most important hiring signal is growth mindset — because coachability enables all other skills to improve.
- **Conventional**: Managers should focus on telling people what to do. → **Their view**: The goal is to hire people who will tell you what to do within six months — because autonomy is the real scaling mechanism.
- **Conventional**: AI will automatically solve most problems once models are powerful enough. → **Their view**: AGI is necessary but not sufficient; humans still need to build the products and workflows that make it useful — because value comes from harnessing capability into human needs.
