## Signature Frameworks

### Core-first expansion strategy
- **When to use**: When a company has a strong core product and is considering adjacent product lines or new categories.
- **Steps**:
  1. Keep out-investing competitors in the core product.
  2. Use profits from the core to fund new ventures.
  3. Avoid moving people away from the core to build adjacent products.
  4. Only expand when the core is healthy and you are best-in-class where it matters.
  5. Be willing to cut secondary products if they are only mild successes and distract from the core.
- **Example**: Mixpanel stopped spreading engineering across product analytics, messaging, and data infrastructure, then refocused the whole team on closing core analytics gaps.

### Two-phase product recovery: table stakes first, then architecture
- **When to use**: When a product is losing to competitors because it lacks basic functionality and also has UX/design fragmentation.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the table-stakes gaps causing churn or competitive losses.
  2. Optimize for speed and ship the missing core capabilities quickly.
  3. Once the fire is out, step back and assess the product architecture holistically.
  4. Define the core building blocks and how users discover and connect them.
  5. Invest in consistency and reusable system design so new features have high reach.
- **Example**: Mixpanel first shipped core analytics features like cohorts and advanced funnels, then launched a design-led effort to make visualizations consistent across the product.

### Problem-led planning with collaborative bets
- **When to use**: When planning product work over a medium horizon and you want alignment without over-prescribing solutions.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with a leadership strategy memo that sets annual direction and pillars.
  2. Have teams synthesize quantitative and qualitative customer context.
  3. Turn that context into bets framed as problem, hypothesis, plan to win, and success metric.
  4. Collaborate directly with teams during solution discovery instead of only reviewing finished proposals.
  5. Publish the bets in a shared artifact and roadshow them to the broader company for feedback.
- **Example**: Mixpanel uses a Notion bets database, a one-slide-per-bet presentation, and an execution plan to align product, design, and engineering.

### RICE with delayed confidence and effort scoring
- **When to use**: When prioritizing a backlog that includes both incremental work and potentially high-upside, ambiguous bets.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start by evaluating reach and impact first.
  2. Do not let confidence and effort prematurely kill ambitious ideas.
  3. Spend time with engineers and designers to explore the idea space.
  4. After exploration, estimate confidence and effort more accurately.
  5. Re-rank with full RICE and ensure the portfolio includes innovative, incremental, and debt-reduction work.
- **Example**: A team at Mixpanel spent a week exploring high-reach, high-impact ideas before scoring them fully, which surfaced better solutions and more realistic effort estimates.

### Appetite-based scoping
- **When to use**: When estimating work that is hard to size upfront and you want to force sharper product thinking.
- **Steps**:
  1. Set a time appetite instead of asking for a precise estimate.
  2. Explore what would change if the project had less or more time.
  3. Use those scenarios to find the efficient frontier of cost and impact.
  4. At the end of the time box, check whether new information justifies continuing.
  5. Be honest about whether you are still on the high-value path or just in the long tail.
- **Example**: For a project, the team might compare what they would do in four weeks versus eight weeks to determine the smallest complete solution.

### Open customer-feedback loop
- **When to use**: When you want product, design, and engineering to stay close to real customer pain without PMs becoming bottlenecks.
- **Steps**:
  1. Pipe raw customer feedback into a shared Slack channel.
  2. Include feedback from sales, customer success, NPS, Twitter, and win/loss notes.
  3. Let engineers and designers read the raw feed directly.
  4. Encourage them to email customers and ask follow-up questions.
  5. Enrich the data with account context so people can route sensitive issues appropriately.
- **Example**: At Mixpanel, engineers react with an email emoji in Slack, then contact the customer directly to ask the five whys and improve the product.

## Core Advice
- **When you face a new idea and your instinct is to say no immediately**: Try to make yes work first, then document why it does or does not work before deciding — because a hard no can kill promising directions too early.
- **When engineers are evaluating a request that feels risky or annoying**: Spend 10 minutes sincerely considering how the idea could work before rejecting it — because this balances engineering scar tissue with product empathy.
- **When your core product is losing customers to competitors**: Refocus the whole team on closing core gaps before expanding into adjacent products — because spreading people across new bets weakens the main product.
- **When you are tempted to launch adjacent products because they seem naturally related**: Only expand if the new category can materially move growth — because “good enough” in a secondary category rarely justifies the distraction.
- **When you need to recover quickly from a product fire**: Optimize for speed and ship table-stakes fixes first — because parity matters more than elegance when you are behind.
- **When a team is shipping many tactical features but the product feels fragmented**: Pause and invest in system architecture, consistency, and reusable building blocks — because each feature should have high reach.
- **When design is only being brought in at the end of projects**: Give design dedicated time to work on product architecture and consistency — because late-stage polish is a poor use of design talent.
- **When prioritizing a backlog with both obvious and ambiguous bets**: Delay confidence and effort scoring until after exploration — because high-upside ideas often look weak before they are explored.
- **When estimating work that is hard to size upfront**: Use appetite-based scoping and compare what changes at different time boxes — because it forces the smallest complete solution.
- **When you want product teams to stay close to customers**: Expose raw customer feedback directly to engineers and designers, and let them follow up themselves — because direct access builds empathy and reduces PM bottlenecks.
- **When your company is collecting feedback from many sources**: Centralize feedback in a shared system and enrich it with account context before routing action — because open access without context creates noise.
- **When setting up analytics for a product**: Default to server-side event tracking instead of client-side SDK tracking — because server-side is more reliable, cross-platform, and easier to maintain.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Product analytics should primarily be implemented with client-side SDKs in web and mobile apps. → **Their view**: Server-side tracking should be the default, with client-side context added only when needed — because client-side tracking drops events and creates inconsistent semantics.
- **Conventional**: A product team should keep expanding into adjacent categories to capture more customer problems. → **Their view**: Expansion can weaken the core and create only marginal revenue gains — because secondary products often become low-ranked versions of existing categories.
- **Conventional**: Prioritization should score confidence and effort early to filter ideas quickly. → **Their view**: Delay those scores for high-upside ideas until after exploration — because early scoring can bury innovative bets too soon.
- **Conventional**: Design should mainly polish features after engineering has finished building them. → **Their view**: Design should shape architecture and consistency, not just visuals — because that increases feature reach and avoids end-stage scope creep.
- **Conventional**: Product teams should rely on precise upfront estimates to plan work. → **Their view**: Use appetite and time-boxes instead — because estimates are unreliable before the problem is understood.
