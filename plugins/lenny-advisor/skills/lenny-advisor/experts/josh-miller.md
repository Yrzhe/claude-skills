# Josh Miller

**CEO and co-founder** | **The Browser Company** | Expertise: product strategy, leadership, design, hiring-culture

## Bio
Josh Miller is the CEO and co-founder of The Browser Company, the team behind Arc. His perspective matters because he has built a highly design-driven consumer company in a category dominated by incumbents, while obsessing over emotional product quality, talent density, and trust.

He stands out for treating the company itself as part of the product: how people work, how they’re hired, how values are formed, and how the story is told all shape the user experience. If you want a view on building beloved consumer software with strong culture and sharp product taste, Josh is a useful lens.

## Signature Frameworks

### Optimize for Feelings, Use Metrics to Stay Honest
- **When to use**: When defining product direction, prioritizing features, or designing user experiences in consumer products.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the feeling you want the user to have.
  2. Design around evoking that feeling, not around a metric alone.
  3. Use metrics afterward to validate the experience.
  4. Check whether the feeling maps to the business outcome you want.
- **Example**: For Arc’s Peek feature, the team aimed to make link previewing feel light, airy, and effortless so users could inspect links without losing context.

### D5/D7 as the North Star Metric
- **When to use**: When you need one metric that captures retention, engagement, and growth for a habitual product.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define active usage as meaningful use.
  2. Track users active at least five days out of seven.
  3. Focus on week-over-week growth in that number.
  4. Watch cohort retention improve over time.
- **Example**: The Browser Company obsesses over how many people use Arc at least five days a week.

### Values as a Mirror, Not a Mandate
- **When to use**: When creating company values that should reflect real team behavior.
- **Steps**:
  1. Observe recurring behaviors and language.
  2. Interview every team member one by one.
  3. Extract repeated phrases and traits.
  4. Package them in authentic company language.
- **Example**: The Browser Company turned its values into an essay called *Notes on Road Trips* after noticing themes like “heartfelt intensity” and “assume you don’t know.”

### Assume You Don’t Know
- **When to use**: When entering ambiguous product territory or avoiding overconfidence.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with beginner’s mind.
  2. Admit you don’t know the answer.
  3. Bias toward action with a prototype.
  4. Learn and iterate quickly.
- **Example**: Even a longtime Chrome leader on the team starts from “I have no idea how this should work,” then moves quickly to explore.

### Start by Asking “What Could Be?”
- **When to use**: When a team is solving a problem too narrowly.
- **Steps**:
  1. Frame the problem aspirationally.
  2. Imagine the most ambitious solution.
  3. Use that ambition to raise the bar.
  4. Translate it into a shippable product.
- **Example**: With Peek, the team wanted not just to reduce context switching, but to blur the line between native and web software.

### Build in Public for Radical Trust-Building
- **When to use**: When your product requires deep user trust.
- **Steps**:
  1. Share real internal moments publicly.
  2. Help users understand the humans behind the product.
  3. Be selective and intentional.
  4. Avoid becoming self-promotional.
- **Example**: The Browser Company shares board meetings and internal product conversations to build trust around a browser handling sensitive data.

### Rename Things to Escape Defaults
- **When to use**: When labels carry baggage and constrain first-principles thinking.
- **Steps**:
  1. Spot where standard terminology imports assumptions.
  2. Give the thing a new name.
  3. Use the new name to force a fresh discussion.
  4. Change it back later if needed.
- **Example**: Calling a group the “storytelling team” instead of using a standard PM label changes how people think about the function.

## Core Advice
- **When you’re tempted to optimize purely for metrics**: define the user feeling first, then use metrics to validate — because metrics miss the human experience that drives love and retention.
- **When you need a success metric for a habitual product**: use a hard-to-game frequency metric like D5/D7 — because it combines retention, engagement, and growth.
- **When shipping feels slow**: hire people with intrinsic drive and “heartfelt intensity,” then get out of their way — because ambitious people move faster when trusted.
- **When entering a new product area**: assume you don’t know and prototype quickly — because action reveals reality faster than planning.
- **When defining company values**: interview people individually and extract what already exists — because authentic values are more credible than top-down slogans.
- **When hiring senior candidates**: try to convince them not to join unless it’s a real fit — because candor builds trust and filters for genuine alignment.
- **When trying to attract great talent**: publicly celebrate hires and contributions — because recognition signals that makers matter.
- **When trust is low**: share authentic behind-the-scenes moments — because users trust humans more than policy language.
- **When thinking has gone stale**: rename the thing temporarily — because new labels break inherited assumptions.
- **When competing in a commoditized consumer market**: compete on emotional connection and craft — because users stay for products they love.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Product teams should optimize around metrics and dashboards. → **Their view**: Optimize for feelings first, and use metrics to stay honest — because beloved products win by making people feel something.
- **Conventional**: Company values should be written early by leadership. → **Their view**: Values should emerge from the team organically — because top-down values often feel fake.
- **Conventional**: Hiring is about selling candidates. → **Their view**: Interviews should try to convince people not to join unless it’s right — because candor filters for real alignment.
- **Conventional**: The product is the software. → **Their view**: The company and team are part of the product — because culture and talent density determine output quality.
- **Conventional**: Building in public is mainly marketing. → **Their view**: It is primarily trust-building — because users need to trust the humans behind sensitive products.
- **Conventional**: Browsers are mature utilities. → **Their view**: The browser is becoming the interface to an “internet computer” — because the interface layer is becoming more strategically important.
- **Conventional**: Standard role names improve clarity. → **Their view**: New names can unlock better thinking — because labels import assumptions.
