# Merci Grace

**Founder, former Head of Growth / Product at Slack, former VC** | **Panobi** | Expertise: product-led growth, onboarding, hiring-culture, storytelling

## Bio
Merci Grace brings a rare combination of operator, investor, and founder perspective. She helped shape growth and onboarding at Slack, spent time as a VC at Lightspeed, co-founded Women in Product, and now builds Panobi, a product-led growth tool.

Her perspective matters because she sees both the mechanics of growth and the human side of adoption: how people decide, how teams communicate, and how products actually get used. She is especially useful when you need advice on PLG, onboarding, hiring, or persuasive storytelling.

## Signature Frameworks

### Story-first pitching and communication
- **When to use**: When you need to persuade investors, customers, or internal stakeholders.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with the most compelling insight or moment, not the background.
  2. Outline the arc of the story before writing or presenting.
  3. Use the stage you have to manage the narrative deliberately.
  4. Backfill context and supporting details after you have attention.
  5. Make the presentation feel human and non-boring rather than overly businessy.
- **Example**: For a pitch deck, open with the founder insight or the core problem in the middle of the action, instead of starting with the market slide.

### Product-led growth qualification
- **When to use**: When deciding whether a product can grow primarily through self-serve adoption.
- **Steps**:
  1. Check whether an individual can get value without a sales conversation or webinar.
  2. Assess whether the product has day-zero value.
  3. Determine whether a single user can adopt it inside an organization.
  4. Evaluate whether the product can be used by multiple seniority levels.
  5. Consider whether the product is easy to enter and easy to leave, and whether retention is strong enough to support PLG.
- **Example**: Slack worked because a junior engineer or senior engineer could start using it immediately, but the product still needed strong activation and retention to spread.

### Onboarding as product design
- **When to use**: When designing or improving onboarding for a new product.
- **Steps**:
  1. Design onboarding from the first user interaction, not as a final layer.
  2. Map the steps between the user and the product's full value.
  3. Embed teaching inside the product experience itself.
  4. Avoid generic plug-and-play onboarding frameworks.
  5. Continuously test onboarding with real users and prototypes before shipping.
- **Example**: A to-do app that teaches users by having them click a checkbox to complete their first task uses the product itself to onboard the user.

### Growth experimentation grounded in curiosity
- **When to use**: When building growth for a product that already has some product-market fit.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with curiosity rather than assuming the baseline metrics tell the whole story.
  2. Use regression analysis or data to identify candidate activation signals.
  3. Test hypotheses through experiments and user research.
  4. Optimize for the behaviors that actually move the needle for your specific product.
  5. Be willing to discover that the best growth lever is different from your original assumption.
- **Example**: At Slack, experimentation showed that getting people into their new team at the same time mattered a lot, leading to push-notification experiments that improved activation.

### Hiring for company-specific fit
- **When to use**: When hiring PMs or growth team members for an early or distinctive company.
- **Steps**:
  1. Treat hiring as a two-way fit, not an exam.
  2. Look for curiosity, communication, and evidence people would want to work with the candidate again.
  3. Give candidates real work to do so you can see how they think.
  4. Evaluate whether their solution is technically plausible and creatively strong.
  5. Prioritize people who can thrive in your specific company context, not just at a prestigious peer company.
- **Example**: A PM who would be excellent at Lyst might not succeed at Slack or Airbnb because the company context and expectations differ.

## Core Advice
- **When you need to persuade investors or stakeholders**: Start with the most compelling insight or the middle of the action, then backfill the rest — because people decide quickly whether to keep paying attention.
- **When you are writing a pitch, blog post, or conference talk**: Always start with an outline and define the story arc before drafting — because structure helps you control the narrative and avoid a dull, overly businesslike presentation.
- **When you are building onboarding**: Design onboarding from day one as part of the product, not as a last-minute layer — because the introduction shapes whether users understand and adopt the product.
- **When you are tempted to use a generic onboarding framework**: Prototype alternatives in Figma or paper and compare them before shipping anything — because what worked elsewhere may not fit your product.
- **When your onboarding is getting too complex**: Simplify until it feels almost too simple — because if it doesn’t feel slightly uncomfortable, it’s probably still too complicated.
- **When you are deciding whether to add invites or sharing prompts**: Put invites early and often, but keep them optional — because social users will act on them and others can ignore them.
- **When you are setting trial length or revenue timing**: Let users convert when they are ready instead of forcing a short arbitrary trial — because buying timing depends on the customer’s context.
- **When you are deciding whether a product can be product-led**: Ask whether an individual can get value immediately without a sales call or webinar — because if value requires human augmentation, PLG is less likely to work.
- **When your product has delayed value rather than immediate value**: Do not assume it will work as a pure PLG motion — because delayed value makes self-serve adoption easier to abandon.
- **When you are deciding whether to hire your first salesperson**: Hire when the founder is maxed out on selling or when customers expect to talk to sales — because founders are the first salespeople, but some segments need a dedicated motion.
- **When you are building a growth team**: Start with someone trusted internally who deeply understands the customer — because growth is often mechanically straightforward, but judgment about the customer matters most.
- **When you are trying to build a more diverse team**: Do not rely on passive inbound or referrals; actively seek out and interview diverse candidates — because referrals reproduce the existing network.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Product-led growth is mostly about metrics, funnels, and self-serve mechanics. → **Their view**: PLG starts with whether the product gives immediate value to an individual and whether retention dynamics support it — because mechanics cannot rescue a product that is hard to adopt.
- **Conventional**: Onboarding should be standardized with plug-and-play frameworks and long explanatory carousels. → **Their view**: Generic onboarding frameworks are usually a bad idea; onboarding should be native to the product — because users care about their task, not your onboarding system.
- **Conventional**: More invites or sharing prompts are risky because users may not want to invite others early. → **Their view**: Invites should appear early and often, as long as they are optional — because social users will use them and others can ignore them.
- **Conventional**: Hiring women is mostly a matter of increasing the number of women in the pipeline. → **Their view**: Hiring more women alone is not enough; team dynamics and sourcing matter — because diversity is shaped by the whole system, not just the applicant pool.
- **Conventional**: Great companies are primarily driven by objective fundamentals and rational deal logic. → **Their view**: Storytelling and interpersonal dynamics shape outcomes heavily — because perception often matters as much as facts.
- **Conventional**: Candidates should not have to do unpaid work during interviews. → **Their view**: For many startup roles, a few hours of real work is a useful signal — because it reveals how people think and whether they can thrive in startup conditions.
