# Nabeel S. Qureshi

**Founder, writer, researcher, and engineer; former forward deployed engineer and PM at Palantir** | **Independent / ex-Palantir** | Expertise: enterprise software, data platforms, product strategy, AI strategy

## Bio
Nabeel Qureshi spent nearly eight years at Palantir, where he worked directly inside customer environments on public health, defense-adjacent, and industrial problems before moving into product management. That background gives him a rare perspective on how enterprise products are actually born: not from abstract roadmaps, but from messy operational reality, tight customer feedback loops, and repeated translation between technical systems and human incentives.

He now writes, researches, and builds companies, with a strong focus on product leverage, mission-driven teams, and how AI changes the economics of building software. His perspective is especially valuable when a team is trying to turn a hard, real-world problem into a reusable product.

## Signature Frameworks

### Forward Deployed Product Loop
- **When to use**: When you need to solve a high-value enterprise problem that is too messy to understand from the outside.
- **Steps**:
  1. Go into the customer's environment and work alongside them in person.
  2. Learn the business deeply enough to speak the customer's language and understand their incentives.
  3. Identify the real operational bottleneck, not just the stated software request.
  4. Build a solution quickly, even if it requires creating new software from scratch.
  5. Show it to users within days, gather feedback, and iterate repeatedly.
  6. Fold the reusable parts back into the core product so the next customer benefits.
- **Example**: At Airbus, Palantir engineers worked on production ramp-up for the A350 by mapping factory stations, work orders, and parts into a human-readable system that later informed Foundry's ontology.

### Murder Board Planning
- **When to use**: When starting a new project and you want to stress-test assumptions before committing resources.
- **Steps**:
  1. Write a concise two-page plan covering vision, goals, tactics, and principles.
  2. Invite smart people who are not familiar with the project.
  3. Ask them to tear apart the plan and challenge every assumption.
  4. Use the feedback to sharpen the principles and identify weak spots.
  5. Choose principles that are actually debatable, not generic slogans.
- **Example**: Nabeel said “move fast” is a bad principle because nobody can reasonably disagree with it; a good principle should provoke disagreement.

### Data Platform Abstraction Ladder
- **When to use**: When turning messy enterprise data into a reusable product.
- **Steps**:
  1. Ingest data from many sources into one system.
  2. Map raw tables and fields to human concepts that users understand.
  3. Provide tools for cleaning, joining, and normalizing data.
  4. Expose multiple interaction modes such as SQL, notebooks, point-and-click analytics, maps, and graphs.
  5. Build a unified ontology so users can ask business questions without knowing the underlying schema.
- **Example**: Airbus data stored in SAP tables was mapped to concepts like aircraft, station, part, and work order, enabling a unified view of factory operations.

### Mission-Driven Hiring Filter
- **When to use**: When building a small team that needs unusually high ownership and intensity.
- **Steps**:
  1. Screen for people who care deeply about the mission for personal reasons.
  2. Look for evidence of extra effort in past work, not just competence.
  3. Prefer candidates with strong independent judgment and willingness to push back.
  4. Use cultural signals that attract the right people and repel the wrong ones.
  5. Hire for the extra 20% of motivation that makes people go beyond the job description.
- **Example**: For Nabeel's health-focused startup, he looks for people who have personal experience with the healthcare system or family experiences with serious illness.

### Product Leverage Metric
- **When to use**: When evaluating whether a company is becoming a product company versus a consulting company.
- **Steps**:
  1. Measure how much revenue or impact each engineer can create.
  2. Track whether new customer problems are solved by the existing product or require bespoke work.
  3. Reduce the number of people needed per customer over time.
  4. Increase product reuse so each new deployment requires less custom engineering.
- **Example**: At Palantir, the goal was to move from five to ten engineers per customer in early days toward one person supporting multiple customers as the product matured.

## Core Advice
- **When a customer problem looks like a feature request but may be the core pain point**: Treat the request as a blank slate and be willing to pivot the product around the bigger problem — because founders often anchor to their original vision and miss the real opportunity.
- **When deciding whether to do forward-deployed work**: Only do it if the customer ticket size is large enough to justify heavy investment and in-person work — because the model is expensive and only works when the economics support deep engagement.
- **When you want to know if you're building a product or just consulting**: Ask whether the work creates reusable product leverage or just one-off delivery — because repeated bespoke engineering means you are not compounding software value.
- **When hiring for an early startup**: Prioritize people who will work the extra 20% and care about outcomes, not just people who are smart or technically strong — because motivation and ownership matter more than raw skill in the first 20 hires.
- **When trying to attract exceptional talent**: Be explicit about who the company is not for and use a distinctive mission signal — because polarizing clarity attracts the right people and repels the wrong ones.
- **When building enterprise software for messy organizations**: Spend time understanding the political and operational incentives around data access and ownership — because the hardest part is often not analysis but getting usable data across teams.
- **When designing a team for customer-facing technical work**: Make sure the people in the field are real builders who can create new product, not just solutions architects — because the best field teams invent software, not merely deploy it.
- **When trying to create a strong product culture**: Use fast iteration cycles with real customers and ask for money early — because rapid feedback reveals whether the problem is real and worth solving.
- **When building for healthcare, defense, or other complex domains**: Learn the domain's business model and incentives before designing the workflow — because what looks intuitive from the outside often makes no sense operationally.
- **When choosing between staying in your original market or pivoting**: Use customer pain intensity, not your original category label, as the deciding factor — because adjacent pain can be much more valuable than your intended niche.
- **When building in an AI era**: Reassess your workflows every couple of months and adopt tools that make you a “cyborg” with AI — because the cost of experimentation is changing quickly.
- **When deciding whether to work on hard, messy sectors**: Lean into the messy real world instead of avoiding it — because the biggest opportunities often live where operations are painful but economically important.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Good companies should be broadly inclusive and avoid signaling who does not belong. → **Their view**: Strong companies should clearly define who they are not for, because that helps attract the right people — because a distinctive signal creates mission alignment.
- **Conventional**: Product managers should come from traditional PM backgrounds. → **Their view**: The best PMs can be former forward deployed engineers — because field experience builds customer empathy, urgency, and trust with engineers.
- **Conventional**: Titles and promotion ladders motivate people and create clarity. → **Their view**: Titles can create unproductive competition and Goodhart’s-law behavior, so removing them can improve meritocracy — because people optimize for status instead of outcomes.
- **Conventional**: Enterprise software should be sold as a standardized product with minimal custom work. → **Their view**: Deeply customized in-person work can be the fastest path to a reusable product — because one customer’s mission-critical problem can reveal platform primitives.
- **Conventional**: Disengaging from controversial sectors is the safest moral choice. → **Their view**: In many cases, being in the room is better — because working inside hard sectors can improve safety, targeting, and outcomes.
- **Conventional**: College is optional if you can start working early and learn on the job. → **Their view**: College is usually worth it — because it is a rare period for deep friendships, exploration, and self-discovery.
