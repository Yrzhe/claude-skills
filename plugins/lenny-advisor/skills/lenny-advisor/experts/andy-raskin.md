# Andy Raskin

**Strategic narrative consultant and advisor to CEOs** | **Independent / AndyRaskin.com** | Expertise: positioning, go-to-market, strategy, leadership

## Bio
Andy Raskin helps CEOs build a **strategic narrative**: a simple, repeatable story that aligns sales, marketing, product, fundraising, recruiting, and leadership around the same market shift. He’s best known for his analysis of Zuora’s deck in *“The Greatest Sales Deck I’ve Ever Seen”* and for helping B2B companies like Gong and 360Learning turn positioning into a company-wide operating system.

His perspective matters because he doesn’t treat messaging as copywriting. He treats it as a strategic tool for defining the market, creating urgency, and helping the whole company make better decisions.

## Signature Frameworks

### Strategic Narrative
- **When to use**: When a company needs a unifying story to explain why its product matters, differentiate beyond features, align teams, guide product decisions, or scale founder-led selling.
- **Steps**:
  1. Name the shift from the old game to the new game in concise, memorable terms.
  2. Name the stakes by showing why this shift matters now, often by highlighting winners already playing the new game and the downside of staying in the old one.
  3. Name the object of the new game: the rallying cry or buyer mission the audience is trying to achieve.
  4. Show why winning the new game is hard by identifying the obstacles standing in the way.
  5. Explain how your product helps overcome those obstacles, supported by proof and success stories.
- **Example**: Zuora framed the shift as moving from transactions to the subscription economy, showed that winning companies were already adopting subscriptions, posed the goal of turning customers into subscribers, outlined new challenges like measuring lifetime value and changing preferences, and then positioned Zuora as the system to make that possible.

### Old Game to New Game Framing
- **When to use**: When your current pitch sounds like feature comparison or problem-solution selling and you need to create urgency and a sense of movement.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the old worldview your buyers are still operating in.
  2. Define the new worldview that is emerging.
  3. Give both worlds short, sticky names.
  4. Use those names consistently across sales, marketing, and product communication.
- **Example**: Salesforce framed the market as software versus cloud, rather than saying it was just a better CRM than Siebel.

### Narrative Testing in Sales Calls
- **When to use**: When you have a draft narrative and want to validate whether it resonates before rolling it out broadly.
- **Steps**:
  1. Draft the narrative using the strategic narrative structure.
  2. Test it in a small number of live sales conversations rather than launching it company-wide immediately.
  3. Listen for whether prospects engage with the shift and stakes, for example by saying they are seeing the same change.
  4. Ask directly whether the framing matches their reality.
  5. Refine based on qualitative reactions before broader rollout.
- **Example**: Andy suggests salespeople ask prospects, “Am I crazy or are you seeing this?” after describing the shift, then use the response to judge resonance.

### Narrative Development Process
- **When to use**: When a CEO wants to build a strategic narrative collaboratively without losing clarity.
- **Steps**:
  1. Form a small strategic narrative team of up to four people, often including leaders from marketing, sales, product, and sometimes finance.
  2. Run a kickoff session to gather ideas for the shift, stakes, object, and obstacles.
  3. Interview customers to learn how they describe the change and what language resonates.
  4. Have the CEO work one-on-one to turn the raw material into a first draft.
  5. Review the first draft with the team, expect strong criticism, and use that feedback to improve the narrative.
  6. Iterate until the story is clean, memorable, and usable.
- **Example**: Andy warns that the second session is often painful because the first draft necessarily discards many good ideas, but that “a shit draft is a million times more valuable than having all these great ideas.”

## Core Advice
- **When your pitch sounds like feature bragging or a standard problem-solution deck**: Reframe the conversation around a market shift from an old game to a new game, then position your product as helping buyers win the new game — because movement creates urgency and differentiation.
- **When you struggle to differentiate in a crowded B2B category**: Name the old and new game in concise, memorable language and build your story around that contrast — because sticky labels make the shift easy to repeat across the company.
- **When buyers seem apathetic or slow to decide**: Raise the stakes by showing the downside of staying in the old game and the upside of joining the new one — because people act when the future feels meaningfully split.
- **When it’s unclear what your company helps customers achieve**: Define the object of the new game as a simple buyer mission or rallying cry — because a clear destination is more motivating than a tool description.
- **When product teams are overwhelmed by feature requests**: Use the strategic narrative as a prioritization filter — because it helps decide whether a feature actually helps customers win the new game.
- **When you need to scale beyond founder-led selling**: Invest in a narrative that sales, marketing, product, recruiting, and fundraising can all repeat — because shared context becomes the company’s operating system.
- **When you’re unsure whether new messaging works**: Test it in a few sales calls first and listen for natural engagement — because real prospect reactions are the fastest signal of resonance.
- **When the first draft feels messy or incomplete**: Keep going and expect criticism — because clarity comes from iteration, not from trying to get it perfect in your head.
- **When you’re tempted to copy another company’s deck**: Use principles, not templates — because the structure is reusable, but the story must fit your market and audience.
- **When slides are hard to follow**: Make each slide title the takeaway — because the audience should instantly know the point of each slide.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: A strong pitch should follow problem-solution format and explain why your product is better than alternatives. → **Their view**: Start with a shift in the world and define a movement, not just a problem and your treatment — because movement-based stories create a bigger reason to change.
- **Conventional**: Differentiation comes mainly from category naming and category creation. → **Their view**: The story behind the category matters more than the label itself — because the narrative gives the category meaning.
- **Conventional**: Business storytelling is mostly about becoming a better storyteller in general. → **Their view**: The key is getting one core company story structurally right — because one strategic narrative can guide the whole company.
- **Conventional**: Messaging is mainly a sales and marketing concern. → **Their view**: Strategic narrative should be a north star for product, fundraising, recruiting, and leadership — because it shapes decisions across the business.
- **Conventional**: Templates are the best way to scale good decks. → **Their view**: Avoid rigid templates; use principles and tailor the narrative — because copying structure without the underlying story usually fails.
