# Jason Feifer

**Editor in chief** | **Entrepreneur Magazine** | Expertise: go-to-market, positioning, media relations, storytelling

## Bio
Jason Feifer is the editor in chief of Entrepreneur Magazine and a veteran media operator who has also held editorial roles at Fast Company and other major publications. His perspective matters because he translates how journalists actually think into practical advice founders can use to earn coverage, not by “selling themselves,” but by aligning their story with a publication’s audience and mission.

He is especially useful for founders who want to understand what makes a story newsworthy, how to find the right journalist, and how to turn press into a real business asset.

## Signature Frameworks

### The 3-step press framework
- **When to use**: When you want to pursue media coverage systematically instead of sending random pitches.
- **Steps**:
  1. Prep: define what you need press for and identify what is genuinely interesting about your business.
  2. Find the right target: choose the right publications and the specific writers or editors whose audience and coverage patterns match your story.
  3. Pitch: send a short, tailored outreach message that clearly shows why your story is relevant to that person and their audience.
- **Example**: Instead of asking, “How do I get coverage in Entrepreneur Magazine?” a founder first clarifies the goal, then identifies the right writer, then sends a concise pitch tailored to that outlet.

### Press is for something
- **When to use**: Before investing time or money into PR.
- **Steps**:
  1. Ask: what do I need press for?
  2. Tie the answer to a concrete business objective.
  3. Evaluate whether the publication and timing can actually help.
  4. If there is no tactical use, deprioritize press.
- **Example**: Press might be useful for a product launch, fundraising credibility, or positioning a brand toward a specific customer segment.

### Publication mission matching
- **When to use**: When framing your story for a specific outlet.
- **Steps**:
  1. Study the publication’s audience and recurring editorial patterns.
  2. Infer the publication’s mission from what it repeatedly publishes.
  3. Reframe your company story into the angle that best serves that audience.
  4. Avoid pitching the same narrative to every outlet.
- **Example**: Entrepreneur is not just “about entrepreneurs”; it is about helping readers think through business challenges. A butter dish product became interesting only when framed through the founder’s airport-based market research hack.

### Find the human entry point
- **When to use**: When identifying who at a publication is most likely to engage with your story.
- **Steps**:
  1. Search the publication for your category, adjacent topics, or competitors.
  2. Identify the specific bylines covering that area.
  3. Check whether the author is staff or freelance.
  4. Prioritize freelancers when relevant because they are often more motivated to find and develop stories.
- **Example**: For a peanut butter brand targeting millennial moms, Jason suggests finding the writers behind snack roundups or seasonal food coverage at Cosmo.

### The short tailored pitch
- **When to use**: When sending the actual outreach email.
- **Steps**:
  1. Use a subject line and opening that signal the email is specifically for that person.
  2. Show familiarity with their work or the publication’s format without faking it.
  3. Keep the email short, around three paragraphs max.
  4. Lead with the angle most relevant to their audience, not your generic company description.
- **Example**: A founder pitched Jason with a subject line referencing Entrepreneur and his podcast, framed the story around problem-solving during Covid, and used problem/solution bullets to match the show’s structure.

### Be part of the story, not necessarily the story
- **When to use**: When your company is too niche for a standalone feature.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify a broader trend, problem, scam, or market shift connected to your business.
  2. Offer yourself as a source, example, or data point within that larger story.
  3. Help the journalist understand the broader context.
  4. Use the resulting quote or inclusion as credibility.
- **Example**: A cat toy founder shifted from pitching his product to explaining Amazon-to-eBay arbitrage hurting small sellers, which became a larger reported feature.

### Create news with proprietary context
- **When to use**: When your company is hard to cover directly but has data or market visibility.
- **Steps**:
  1. Use internal data or commission research to identify a meaningful trend.
  2. Package the findings into a report, ranking, or survey.
  3. Pitch the findings as newsworthy context rather than a self-promotional company story.
  4. Benefit from being cited as the source of the insight.
- **Example**: Jason points to Barbara Corcoran’s real estate report and Zapier’s annual analysis of the fastest-growing business apps.

## Core Advice
- **When you’re deciding whether to pursue press at all**: define the business outcome first — because press is only useful when it serves a concrete goal.
- **When you’re tempted to pitch a prestigious outlet just because it’s famous**: choose based on where your actual buyers or stakeholders are — because relevance beats prestige.
- **When you’re framing your company story**: adapt the angle to the publication’s audience — because journalists serve readers, not founders.
- **When you need your pitch to stand out in a crowded inbox**: send a short, highly customized email — because generic blasts get ignored.
- **When you’re deciding who to pitch at an outlet**: find the writer already covering your category, and consider freelancers first — because they are closer to the work and more motivated.
- **When your company feels too boring or niche for coverage**: frame it as part of a larger trend or problem — because many businesses are only interesting inside a bigger story.
- **When you have proprietary data**: turn it into a report, ranking, or survey — because original data creates news.
- **When you’re considering a PR agency**: check whether they have real relationships and will push back on bad ideas — because good PR is strategic, not just distribution.
- **When you’re measuring press success**: reuse coverage as social proof across ads, sales, fundraising, and your site — because the downstream value often matters more than article traffic.
- **When you get the interview**: speak candidly about real problems and how you solved them — because problem-solving stories are more credible and useful than polished success stories.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Journalists are there to help founders get coverage. → **Their view**: Journalists care about serving their audience, not about you — because audience value is the only reason your story matters.
- **Conventional**: Any press is good press, especially in big-name outlets. → **Their view**: Press is only worth pursuing when it serves a specific business goal — because prestige without relevance is wasted effort.
- **Conventional**: Product launches and company updates are inherently newsworthy. → **Their view**: Most company announcements are boring unless they reveal a lesson or trend — because audiences want insight, not self-promotion.
- **Conventional**: The top editor is the best person to pitch. → **Their view**: The best entry point is often the beat writer or freelancer — because they are closer to the story and more motivated.
- **Conventional**: Press releases create meaningful exposure. → **Their view**: Wire-distributed press releases are often vanity artifacts — because distribution is not the same as earned attention.
- **Conventional**: The value of press is measured by immediate traffic or conversions. → **Their view**: The real value is often reusable credibility — because coverage can power ads, sales, and fundraising.
- **Conventional**: Success stories are the most compelling. → **Their view**: Problem-solving stories are stronger — because struggle and adaptation are more useful and believable.
