# Adriel Frederick

**VP of Product Management, Reddit X** | **Reddit** | Expertise: product-management, growth, leadership, marketplace, ai-strategy, pricing, user-research

## Bio
Adriel Frederick is a product leader who has worked on growth at Facebook, marketplace and pricing at Lyft, and new product incubation at Reddit. His perspective matters because he combines systems thinking with a strong human-centered instinct: he cares about algorithms, but only as tools for amplifying human judgment and product intent.

He is especially useful to seek out when you’re building algorithmic products, trying to improve activation or retention, or leading innovation inside a larger company. His advice is grounded in real operating experience across growth, pricing, and incubation—not just abstract product theory.

## Signature Frameworks

### Human-in-the-loop algorithm design
- **When to use**: When building products that rely heavily on machine learning, optimization, pricing, ranking, or other algorithmic systems.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the product intent and strategic goals before optimizing anything.
  2. Decide explicitly what decisions the algorithm should make versus what decisions humans should make.
  3. Identify the constraints and judgment calls algorithms cannot reliably infer.
  4. Design interfaces and tools that give humans the right information to make strategic decisions.
  5. Use the algorithm to amplify human intent at scale rather than operate as an unchecked black box.
  6. Continuously observe outcomes and refine the division of responsibility.
- **Example**: At Lyft, pricing systems needed people in the loop because local teams had to respond to snowstorms, fuel price changes, taxes, or competitive moves in specific cities.

### Marginal user analysis
- **When to use**: When trying to improve activation, signup conversion, onboarding, or growth in products with large funnels.
- **Steps**:
  1. Find the user segment just on the cusp of succeeding but currently struggling.
  2. Inspect the worst-case user experience to reveal the full set of failures.
  3. Observe real users directly instead of relying only on funnel data.
  4. List all friction points, including issues not visible in metrics.
  5. Prioritize barriers that are important and realistically fixable.
  6. Make the product “stupid easy” for that user to succeed.
- **Example**: At Facebook, he looked at users in high-traffic, low-conversion countries using feature phones and found issues like wrong language defaults, phone number formatting problems, and latency to data centers.

### Core growth loop simplification
- **When to use**: When a product has demand but struggles to turn new users into retained users.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the few core actions that unlock value.
  2. Make it easy to find the product.
  3. Make it easy to get into the product.
  4. Make it extremely easy to find friends, collaborators, or relevant connections.
  5. Reinforce the habit of returning by reminding users there is something valuable waiting.
  6. Keep grinding on fundamentals rather than chasing clever hacks.
- **Example**: At Facebook, the team focused on easier signup, helping users find first friends, and building return habits instead of relying on one-off growth tricks.

### R&D team integration framework
- **When to use**: When setting up an incubation, innovation, or long-horizon product team inside a larger company.
- **Steps**:
  1. Tie the team clearly to the company’s core mission.
  2. Make success feel shared across the organization.
  3. Prevent the perception that all innovation only happens in the special team.
  4. Position the team as taking on work the broader org needs but lacks capacity for.
  5. Build cultural alignment so the rest of the company sees the team as part of the same body.
- **Example**: At Reddit X, he emphasized that an R&D team must be seen as core to Reddit’s mission or it risks “organ rejection.”

### Experiment portfolio balancing
- **When to use**: When deciding how much effort to put into incremental experiments versus larger product bets.
- **Steps**:
  1. Separate work into small “lead bullets” and larger “cannonballs.”
  2. Avoid filling the roadmap with only easy, incremental experiments.
  3. Invest heavily in a few fundamental changes when the product stage calls for it.
  4. Use smaller experiments to refine and support the larger bets.
  5. Adjust the mix based on product maturity.
  6. Remember experimentation has a cost in time and organizational focus.
- **Example**: At Facebook growth, the team balanced many small optimizations with major investments like phone-number signup, global SMS delivery, and better friend recommendations.

## Core Advice
- **When facing criticism, controversy, or bad press**: do direct user immersion and stay focused on the underlying customer problem — because headlines amplify conflict, but users reveal the truth.
- **When working on algorithmic or ML-driven products**: define what humans decide, what algorithms decide, and how the interface connects them — because product value comes from judgment plus scale.
- **When growth or activation is slow**: grind on the core actions that unlock value — because durable growth comes from making the product easier to discover, enter, and use.
- **When a funnel is confusing**: study marginal users and worst-case users — because data shows where people drop off, but not always why.
- **When building a diverse team**: treat diversity as a business advantage and build a culture that uses it — because diverse perspectives make teams faster and better.
- **When moving into senior leadership**: focus on organization design and empathy — because impact at scale comes from alignment, incentives, and trust.
- **When a launched system is failing**: admit it, identify the missing first-order requirement, and rebuild if needed — because patching a fundamentally flawed system wastes time.
- **When pressured to run many experiments**: use a portfolio approach with a few big bets and a smaller set of tests — because too many small experiments can create the illusion of progress.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: If you feed enough data into an algorithm, it will make the best decisions. → **Their view**: Algorithms should not run the product alone; humans must define judgment, intent, and constraints — because models miss context and long-term effects.
- **Conventional**: Growth comes from clever hacks. → **Their view**: Durable growth comes from fundamentals — because users eventually notice when the product lacks real value.
- **Conventional**: Funnels and data tell you what to build next. → **Their view**: Data is only a clue; you still need direct user observation — because hidden problems often sit outside the funnel.
- **Conventional**: Innovation teams should be separate and protected. → **Their view**: R&D teams must feel core to the mission — because isolation creates resentment and “organ rejection.”
- **Conventional**: The best individual contributors naturally become the best leaders. → **Their view**: Senior leadership is mostly organization design and empathy — because scaling impact means aligning people, not just being brilliant.
