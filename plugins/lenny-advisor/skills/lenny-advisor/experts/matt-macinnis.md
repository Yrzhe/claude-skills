# Matt MacInnis

**Chief Product Officer; former longtime Chief Operating Officer** | **Rippling** | Expertise: leadership, product management, operations, hiring-culture

## Bio
Matt MacInnis is the Chief Product Officer at Rippling and previously served as its longtime COO. He brings a blunt, systems-oriented view of leadership shaped by years at Apple, founding Inkling, and helping scale Rippling into a large, fast-growing business.

His perspective matters because he thinks across product, operations, and hiring as one system. He is especially useful when you need someone who will challenge soft thinking, diagnose org problems from first principles, and push for standards that hold under real competitive pressure.

## Signature Frameworks

### Extraordinary effort for extraordinary outcomes
- **When to use**: When leading teams in highly competitive markets where outsized results are the goal.
- **Steps**:
  1. Set the expectation that comfort is a sign something is wrong.
  2. Treat sustained effort as necessary, not optional, for top-decile outcomes.
  3. Use small escalations and Friday-night emergencies as proof points of team quality.
  4. Keep the organization on the front foot so competitors cannot exploit slack.
- **Example**: At Rippling, Matt frames Friday-night escalations and bug triage as the moments that separate great teams from good ones.

### Understaff deliberately, but not recklessly
- **When to use**: When allocating people to projects in uncertain, emergent work.
- **Steps**:
  1. Assume you cannot know the perfect staffing level in advance.
  2. Bias toward understaffing rather than overstaffing.
  3. Avoid overstaffing that creates politics and work on lower-priority items.
  4. Continuously reassess so you do not under-understaff critical work.
- **Example**: Matt says Rippling deliberately understaffs projects so teams stay focused on the highest-priority work.

### Alpha-beta management
- **When to use**: When deciding how much process, structure, or volatility a team or function should have.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify whether the area needs high alpha (outsized upside) or low beta (low volatility).
  2. Use less process where creativity and risk-taking matter.
  3. Use more process where reliability and predictability matter.
  4. Apply process only to the extent it lowers volatility without suppressing upside.
- **Example**: Matt wants payroll to be low beta with more process, while zero-to-one product work should preserve alpha.

### Product quality list (PQL) as a cultural vessel
- **When to use**: When you need to create a durable quality standard or cultural meme across a large organization.
- **Steps**:
  1. Create a memorable, idiosyncratic label for the standard.
  2. Define a lightweight checklist of non-negotiable quality expectations.
  3. Use the checklist in factory-inspection-style reviews before shipping.
  4. Iterate the checklist as you learn from misses and defects.
- **Example**: Rippling’s PQL added a rule that a product may ship with only one feature flag governing the entire product.

### First-principles product diagnosis
- **When to use**: When entering a function you do not deeply know or when an org feels locally optimized but globally incoherent.
- **Steps**:
  1. Go into the mess instead of judging from the outside.
  2. Study the system bottom-up from specific observed problems.
  3. Break imported ideas into constituent parts before applying them.
  4. Only then decide which metrics, processes, or standards matter.
- **Example**: When Matt moved into product, he realized foundational standards like test coverage and product-quality checks were missing before adoption metrics could matter.

### Hiring by decoded intuition
- **When to use**: When evaluating candidates for leadership or product roles.
- **Steps**:
  1. Trust your intuition about the candidate.
  2. Translate that intuition into explicit traits others can evaluate.
  3. Use SPOTAC to structure the assessment.
  4. Match the candidate’s alpha/beta profile to the role’s needs.
- **Example**: Matt uses SPOTAC and alpha-beta fit to decide whether a candidate belongs in a high-alpha product area or a low-beta operational role.

## Core Advice
- **When you face a competitive market and want exceptional outcomes**: Do not let the team settle into comfort; push for sustained, exhausting effort — because extraordinary outcomes require extraordinary effort.
- **When you are allocating headcount to a project**: Deliberately understaff first, then add people only when the need is clear — because overstaffing creates politics, wasted work, and attention on lower-priority tasks.
- **When a function needs reliability more than novelty**: Add process to lower volatility and make outputs predictable — because process exists to reduce beta.
- **When a function needs creativity or breakthrough upside**: Keep process light so you do not suppress alpha — because too much process kills upside.
- **When you join a new function or inherit a messy org**: Go see the work yourself and diagnose from first principles before prescribing fixes — because executives cannot solve what they have not studied bottom-up.
- **When you think a product team should focus on adoption metrics**: First ensure the basics are in place: quality standards, test coverage, and stable shipping discipline — because higher-level metrics are premature without execution fundamentals.
- **When you are hiring for a role**: Use intuition, but force yourself to explain the intuition in concrete traits like SPOTAC — because this makes gut feel communicable and less vague.
- **When you are evaluating a candidate for a specific team**: Match the person’s alpha/beta profile to the role’s needs rather than hiring generic talent — because fit matters more than abstract star power.
- **When you are tempted to hide feedback because it feels uncomfortable**: Give the feedback anyway and escalate issues quickly — because withholding useful feedback is selfish and harmful to team performance.
- **When customers or teammates bring you problems**: Treat escalations as gifts and use them to find root causes, not just patch symptoms — because escalations reveal system flaws.
- **When your organization starts to feel relaxed or “chill”**: Increase intensity publicly and model urgency yourself — because intensity decays as management layers increase.
- **When you are building or investing in a startup and it is not clearly working**: Be willing to quit, reset, and start fresh instead of clinging to a failing path — because “never quit” is often VC propaganda.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Great leaders should protect teams from intensity and avoid overloading them. → **Their view**: Leaders should preserve and mirror intensity, not buffer it away. — because intensity decays through management layers and relaxation invites entropy.
- **Conventional**: More staffing is usually better because it speeds execution. → **Their view**: Deliberate understaffing is often better than overstaffing. — because overstaffing creates politics and wasted effort.
- **Conventional**: You learn primarily from mistakes. → **Their view**: You often learn more from success than from failure. — because seeing how things work when done right is more informative.
- **Conventional**: Founders should never quit and should keep pivoting until they win. → **Their view**: Quitting can be the rational move, and “never quit” is often VC propaganda. — because most startups never reach product-market fit.
- **Conventional**: Launches, marketing, and hype can create product-market fit. → **Their view**: Marketing cannot create demand if the market does not already want the product. — because awareness is not the same as underlying pull.
- **Conventional**: Product managers are broadly necessary and universally valuable. → **Their view**: He is anti-shitty product managers, not anti-product managers. — because great PMs add leverage, while bad ones slow teams down.
