## Signature Frameworks

### Punch Above Your Weight
- **When to use**: When you're a small startup trying to look credible to larger customers, candidates, or the market.
- **Steps**:
  1. Add polish to every outward-facing touchpoint: emails, landing pages, visuals, and sample content.
  2. Use details that signal you understand the customer's world, not generic placeholders.
  3. Create a point of view in public communications that connects your product to a broader movement or trend.
  4. Use low-cost but high-signal credibility markers when needed, such as targeted billboards or premium swag.
  5. Make sure someone owns quality and checks the small things consistently.
- **Example**: At Airtable, the team used polished emails, industry-specific sample content, and even targeted billboards in New York to signal legitimacy to enterprise buyers.

### Champion-Led Growth
- **When to use**: When your product is horizontal, hard to explain, or spreads best through internal evangelists.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the psychographic profile of likely champions, not just job titles.
  2. Talk to early users directly to learn what makes them successful and what use cases resonate.
  3. Overinvest in helping the first champion succeed so they can help others internally.
  4. Use those learnings to create templates, messaging, and campaigns that match real patterns.
  5. Encourage champions to evangelize both inside and outside their company.
- **Example**: Airtable used early customer success conversations to identify “tinkerer” champions, then built templates and outreach around those patterns so users could spread Airtable within their organizations.

### Customer Success as a Growth Engine
- **When to use**: When your product requires education, workflow design, or trust before it can scale.
- **Steps**:
  1. Treat customer success as a source of product insight, not just support.
  2. Help customers build their first successful workflow or setup.
  3. Track outcomes that matter, such as promotions or internal adoption, not just tickets closed.
  4. Convert repeated customer patterns into templates, blog posts, and reusable assets.
  5. Feed those assets back into marketing and sales motions.
- **Example**: At Airtable, customer success helped users build bases, then the team turned those patterns into templates and content that made later acquisition and expansion easier.

### Minimum Viable Infrastructure
- **When to use**: When you need to move quickly in a high-stakes or rapidly changing environment.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with the smallest possible system that can create real value.
  2. Use lightweight tools and manual processes before building complex software.
  3. Let the first version teach you what actually matters.
  4. Prune aggressively as the environment changes.
  5. Only add complexity when the simpler version proves insufficient.
- **Example**: VaccinateCA began as phones, Discord, Airtable, and spreadsheets, and that was enough to create a national vaccine information clearinghouse.

### Mission-Driven Mobilization
- **When to use**: When you need to recruit volunteers, employees, or community support quickly.
- **Steps**:
  1. State the mission in a way that is instantly understandable.
  2. Show people exactly how they can help.
  3. Repeat the mission relentlessly across channels and meetings.
  4. Give contributors a sense of agency and impact.
  5. Keep the message concise and emotionally resonant.
- **Example**: VaccinateCA’s message was essentially “pick up phone, help save lives,” which made it easy for hundreds of volunteers to join and stay motivated.

## Core Advice
- **When you're a small startup and customers may be nervous about trusting you**: Invest in polish on every touchpoint: reread emails, use decent visuals, and make sample content feel tailored to the customer's world — because small details signal that the team thought about the customer specifically, which builds trust and makes people root for you.
- **When your product is hard to explain or has many possible use cases**: Use templates to narrow the surface area and help users connect their problem to your product — because templates reduce ambiguity and help users see a concrete path to value faster.
- **When you need to grow a horizontal product inside companies**: Focus on champions first, not buyers; help one person succeed deeply so they can spread the product internally — because the person who loves the product is often different from the budget holder, and internal evangelists drive expansion.
- **When your product requires education before it can be adopted**: Pair customer success with marketing and turn customer learnings into templates, blog posts, and reusable assets — because customer success reveals real workflows and objections, and marketing can scale those insights.
- **When you are tempted to build a large, complex first version**: Start with the laughably smallest MVP that can still create value — because a tiny version teaches you what actually matters and keeps you agile as conditions change.
- **When your team is broad or distributed and people may miss context**: Repeat the same core message constantly in multiple formats — because people fill in blanks with incorrect assumptions unless you keep reinforcing the same priorities.
- **When you want to look more credible to enterprise buyers**: Use high-signal credibility markers like targeted billboards, premium swag, or other visible proof points — because these signals reduce perceived risk and help buyers believe you're a legitimate company.
- **When you're deciding whether to sponsor events**: Skip flashy sponsorships unless the event is truly where your buyers already congregate — because most sponsorships are expensive and weak at generating meaningful leads or trust.
- **When you're planning PR for an early-stage company**: Use PR for credibility, hiring, or improving cold outbound response rates—not as a direct acquisition channel — because PR rarely drives users directly, but it can improve trust in contexts where you proactively reach out.
- **When you need to talk to customers more often but don't have a system**: Create a simple weekly process: write a template email, pick a customer segment, and send a few interview requests every week — because unstructured customer conversations produce better insight than surveys and are easy to systematize.
- **When you want to build a strong brand on a small team**: Choose a person in the company to own quality and detail, and give them a lightweight checklist for recurring outputs — because quality doesn't happen by accident; it needs an explicit owner and repeatable process.
- **When you are considering category creation as a marketing strategy**: Prefer elevating a profession or identity over inventing a new software category — because people fight for identities and careers, but a category is just a budget line item.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Move fast and ship as quickly as possible; polish can wait. → **Their view**: Even early-stage companies should invest in small details like reread emails, decent visuals, and tailored sample content. — because those details build trust and make customers feel the product was built for them, which matters especially when you're small.
- **Conventional**: Templates are mainly a top-of-funnel acquisition play. → **Their view**: Templates are often more valuable for education, adoption, and expansion inside companies than for SEO-driven acquisition. — because at Airtable, templates helped users understand the product and spread it internally, but they were not a major traffic engine.
- **Conventional**: Big PR launches are a key growth lever for startups. → **Their view**: PR is usually not a good acquisition channel; it is better used for credibility, hiring, or cold outbound support. — because most readers won't convert directly from a press piece, so the asset needs a clear downstream use case.
- **Conventional**: Creating a new category is the pinnacle of product marketing. → **Their view**: Category creation is often a huge waste of time in B2B SaaS; elevating a profession is usually more effective. — because jobs and identities motivate people far more than abstract software categories, and communities form around careers.
- **Conventional**: Customer success is a support function separate from marketing. → **Their view**: Customer success and marketing are fundamentally doing the same job and should be tightly connected, if not one team. — because both identify needs, reduce friction to value, and turn customer insights into scalable resources.
- **Conventional**: Sophisticated technology is required to solve big problems. → **Their view**: Simple tools and manual processes can be enough to create massive impact when the problem is urgent and the system is broken. — because VaccinateCA used phones, spreadsheets, Discord, and Airtable to fill a critical infrastructure gap and save lives.
