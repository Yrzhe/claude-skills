# Jessica Lachs

**VP of Analytics and Data Science** | **DoorDash** | Expertise: leadership, metrics, strategy, product-management, growth, operations

## Bio
Jessica Lachs leads Analytics and Data Science at DoorDash, where she has spent over a decade building a highly respected analytics organization inside a complex marketplace business. Her perspective matters because she has scaled analytics from a reporting function into a strategic decision-making engine that directly shapes product, operations, and growth.

She is especially strong on how to structure data teams, how to define metrics that actually drive behavior, and how to make analytics useful in businesses where outcomes are lagging, messy, and cross-functional.

## Signature Frameworks

### Centralized Analytics Pod Model
- **When to use**: When building a data organization that needs strong talent standards, shared methodologies, and deep partnership with product, engineering, marketing, and operations without becoming a ticket-taking service team.
- **Steps**:
  1. Keep analytics in a centralized reporting structure under a dedicated analytics leader.
  2. Organize the team into pods that mirror partner functions like product, engineering, marketing, and operations.
  3. Align analytics goals directly with partner team goals so incentives are shared.
  4. Expect analytics to have a seat at the table and contribute proactive insights, not just answers.
  5. Use the central structure to maintain a consistent hiring bar, common metrics, shared tooling, and career mobility across domains.
- **Example**: At DoorDash, marketing analytics reports through Jessica's central analytics org, but the team is structured into pods that map to marketing and other partner teams so they operate as embedded thought partners while preserving centralized standards and culture.

### Proxy Metrics for Long-Term Outcomes
- **When to use**: When the ultimate business outcome is too slow-moving or lagging to be useful for day-to-day experimentation and team goaling.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the long-term outcome you care about, such as retention.
  2. Break it down into short-term input metrics that plausibly drive that outcome.
  3. Choose a metric that teams can influence in the near term.
  4. Validate through experimentation that movement in the proxy metric leads to the desired long-term result.
  5. Use the proxy metric for team goals and iteration cadence.
- **Example**: Jessica argues that retention is a poor direct goal because it is hard to move meaningfully in the short term, so teams should instead identify and measure the short-term inputs that actually drive retention.

### Simple Metrics Over Composite Scores
- **When to use**: When teams are tempted to create weighted composite metrics that are mathematically elegant but hard to understand, communicate, or act on.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with the business outcome you want to improve.
  2. Avoid combining too many inputs into a single weighted score unless it is truly interpretable.
  3. Identify the few most important underlying drivers.
  4. Set separate goals on those drivers if needed rather than hiding them in one index.
  5. Prioritize the highest-impact driver first and move sequentially.
- **Example**: DoorDash experimented with a merchant health composite score that mixed factors like active hours, images, and menu completeness, but the resulting score was hard to interpret. They replaced it with simpler metrics such as percentage of new merchants getting an order in the first seven days, photo coverage, and accurate open hours.

### Common Currency Trade-off Framework
- **When to use**: When multiple teams across a marketplace or complex business need to compare very different levers and decide where to invest time or money.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define a common business currency, such as GOV or order volume.
  2. Quantify the expected impact of each lever in that common currency.
  3. Estimate trade-offs across functions, such as pricing, logistics, marketing, supply growth, or selection.
  4. Compare options on a like-for-like basis over the relevant time horizon.
  5. Allocate resources to the mix of actions with the best expected return.
- **Example**: DoorDash translates changes like lowering price by a dollar, reducing delivery time by a minute, improving login conversion, onboarding more dashers, or signing a Thai restaurant in Sacramento into expected GOV and volume impact so teams can compare investments across the business.

### Deep-Dive Hackathon Discovery Process
- **When to use**: When teams are overloaded with reactive requests and need protected time to uncover hidden opportunities, root causes, or strategic insights.
- **Steps**:
  1. Intentionally carve out time for self-directed exploratory work.
  2. Choose an area where averages or surface metrics may be masking something important.
  3. Investigate deeply using both data and hands-on product experience.
  4. Look at distributions, segments, and edge cases rather than just averages.
  5. Turn findings into concrete recommendations and roadmap changes.
- **Example**: In a DoorDash hackathon, the team investigated referral performance, referred each other, attempted referral fraud, and discovered a bimodal distribution: high-quality genuine referrals and low-quality fraud-driven referrals. This led to recommendations like stronger fraud checks and referral caps.

### Trade-off-Based Prioritization for Analytics Requests
- **When to use**: When analytics teams are flooded with ad hoc asks and need a principled way to say no without damaging relationships.
- **Steps**:
  1. Anchor the team on shared goals with partner functions.
  2. Make current priorities explicit on a weekly or monthly basis.
  3. When a new request arrives, compare it against existing commitments.
  4. Ask whether the new request is more important than the current top priorities.
  5. Reprioritize transparently if needed, or defer the request.
  6. Occasionally do quick low-cost asks to build goodwill.
- **Example**: Jessica advises teams to respond to new requests by saying, in effect, “If you want this extra thing, one of these other priorities has to drop. Is it more important than the three things we already aligned on?”

### Extreme Ownership in Analytics
- **When to use**: When analytics teams need to move beyond narrow role definitions and solve ambiguous business problems end-to-end.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the team's job as figuring out what is happening and what to do next.
  2. Encourage people to go beyond standard analytics tasks when needed.
  3. Use qualitative methods like customer calls when quantitative data is insufficient.
  4. Treat ownership of outcomes as more important than staying inside role boundaries.
  5. Reinforce this behavior culturally and through leadership expectations.
- **Example**: When an affordability initiative underperformed, DoorDash data scientists did not stop at segment analysis. They made phone calls to customers directly to understand motivations and inform the next experiment.

## Core Advice
- **When you face pressure to embed analytics fully inside business units**: keep reporting lines centralized while structuring analytics pods to map tightly to partner teams and share their goals — because this preserves embedded partnership while maintaining a consistent hiring bar, shared methodologies, stronger culture, and better career growth.
- **When you face a data team becoming a reactive service desk**: position analytics as a business-impact function with a seat at the table — because teams that only answer questions rarely shape strategy.
- **When you face endless inbound requests that crowd out strategic work**: create protected time for exploratory deep dives — because the highest-leverage insights often come from self-directed investigation.
- **When you face difficulty saying no to ad hoc asks**: make trade-offs visible against current priorities — because refusal becomes collaborative prioritization when the opportunity cost is explicit.
- **When you face hiring for analytics roles**: screen for curiosity and self-motivated investigation, not just technical skill — because the best analysts uncover higher-value insights without being told.
- **When you face evaluating soft skills in interviews**: use real business cases with ambiguity and moments where candidates must react to being wrong — because this reveals adaptability and structured thinking better than polished technical screens.
- **When you face choosing a team metric like retention**: use a short-term proxy metric that teams can influence — because lagging outcomes are too slow for iteration.
- **When you face a proposal for a sophisticated composite metric**: simplify it into a few understandable drivers — because metrics only work if people can understand and act on them.
- **When you face cross-functional trade-off decisions in a complex business**: convert levers into a common business currency like volume or GOV — because shared units make investment decisions comparable.
- **When you face metrics that look healthy on average**: inspect distributions, edge cases, and fail states — because averages can hide costly problems.
- **When you face a product issue that data alone cannot explain**: supplement quantitative analysis with direct customer conversations — because qualitative context often unlocks the “why.”
- **When you face building a team in a specialized field but candidates have nontraditional backgrounds**: hire for technical capability plus complementary backgrounds — because business judgment and cross-functional empathy matter a lot.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Analytics teams should be embedded directly inside each business unit for maximum alignment and responsiveness. → **Their view**: A centralized analytics org is superior, as long as it is structured into pods aligned to partner teams and shares their goals — because centralization improves standards, culture, and career paths without sacrificing partnership.
- **Conventional**: Analytics is primarily a service function that answers questions and builds dashboards. → **Their view**: Analytics should drive business impact and shape decisions — because the highest-value work is “what do we do now?”
- **Conventional**: Retention is a great north-star metric to goal teams on. → **Their view**: Retention is a terrible direct goal — because it is too slow-moving to guide short-term iteration.
- **Conventional**: More sophisticated composite metrics are better. → **Their view**: Simple metrics are usually better — because interpretability beats mathematical elegance in day-to-day execution.
- **Conventional**: Rare edge cases are not worth much attention. → **Their view**: Rare fail states deserve dedicated focus — because they can be disproportionately costly.
- **Conventional**: Data scientists should stay in their lane. → **Their view**: They should do whatever is necessary, including customer calls — because ownership matters more than role purity.
- **Conventional**: You need a traditional analytics background to lead a world-class data org. → **Their view**: Nontraditional backgrounds can be an advantage — because they can produce stronger business orientation.
