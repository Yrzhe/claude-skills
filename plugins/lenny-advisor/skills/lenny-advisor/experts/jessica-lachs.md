## Signature Frameworks

### Centralized Analytics Pod Model
- **When to use**: When you need strong talent standards, shared methodologies, and deep partnership with business teams without turning analytics into a ticket-taking service desk.
- **Steps**:
  1. Keep analytics in a centralized reporting structure under one analytics leader.
  2. Organize the team into pods aligned to partner functions like product, engineering, marketing, and operations.
  3. Align analytics goals with partner team goals.
  4. Expect analytics to contribute proactive insights, not just answers.
  5. Use centralization to maintain hiring standards, shared tooling, common metrics, and career mobility.
- **Example**: At DoorDash, marketing analytics reports through Jessica’s central org, but the team is structured into pods that map to marketing and other partner teams so they feel embedded while preserving centralized standards.

### Proxy Metrics for Long-Term Outcomes
- **When to use**: When the real business outcome is too slow-moving to be useful for day-to-day experimentation or team goals.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the long-term outcome you care about.
  2. Break it into short-term input metrics.
  3. Choose a metric teams can influence quickly.
  4. Validate that the proxy actually drives the long-term outcome.
  5. Use the proxy for iteration and goal-setting.
- **Example**: Instead of directly goaling on retention, DoorDash teams identify short-term inputs that drive retention and use those as operating metrics.

### Simple Metrics Over Composite Scores
- **When to use**: When teams are tempted to build a weighted score that is elegant but hard to understand or act on.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with the business outcome.
  2. Avoid over-combining inputs into one index.
  3. Identify the few most important drivers.
  4. Set separate goals on those drivers if needed.
  5. Prioritize the highest-impact driver first.
- **Example**: DoorDash tested a merchant health composite score, but it was hard to interpret. They moved to simpler metrics like first-order conversion for new merchants, photo coverage, and accurate open hours.

### Common Currency Trade-off Framework
- **When to use**: When multiple teams need to compare very different levers and decide where to invest.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define a common business currency, such as GOV or order volume.
  2. Quantify the expected impact of each lever in that currency.
  3. Estimate trade-offs across functions.
  4. Compare options on a like-for-like basis.
  5. Allocate resources to the best expected return.
- **Example**: DoorDash translates changes like lowering price, improving delivery time, increasing login conversion, onboarding dashers, or signing a restaurant into expected GOV impact.

### Deep-Dive Hackathon Discovery Process
- **When to use**: When teams are overloaded with reactive work and need protected time to uncover hidden opportunities or root causes.
- **Steps**:
  1. Carve out time for self-directed exploratory work.
  2. Pick an area where averages may be hiding something.
  3. Investigate deeply using data and hands-on product experience.
  4. Look at distributions, segments, and edge cases.
  5. Turn findings into recommendations and roadmap changes.
- **Example**: In a DoorDash hackathon, the team investigated referrals, discovered a bimodal distribution of good and fraudulent referrals, and recommended stronger fraud checks and caps.

### Trade-off-Based Prioritization for Analytics Requests
- **When to use**: When analytics is flooded with ad hoc asks and needs a principled way to say no.
- **Steps**:
  1. Anchor the team on shared goals.
  2. Make priorities explicit.
  3. Compare new requests against current commitments.
  4. Ask whether the new request is more important than existing priorities.
  5. Reprioritize transparently or defer.
  6. Occasionally do quick low-cost asks to build goodwill.
- **Example**: Jessica recommends telling stakeholders: if you want this extra thing, one of the current priorities has to drop.

### Extreme Ownership in Analytics
- **When to use**: When analytics teams need to solve ambiguous business problems end-to-end.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the job as figuring out what is happening and what to do next.
  2. Encourage people to go beyond standard analytics tasks.
  3. Use qualitative methods when data is insufficient.
  4. Treat ownership of outcomes as more important than role boundaries.
  5. Reinforce this culturally.
- **Example**: When an affordability initiative underperformed, DoorDash data scientists called customers directly to understand motivations and inform the next experiment.

## Core Advice
- **When you’re deciding whether to embed analytics inside business units**: keep reporting centralized but structure pods around partner teams — because you get embedded partnership without losing standards, culture, and career growth.
- **When analytics is becoming a reactive service desk**: position it as a business-impact function with a seat at the table — because the highest-value work is shaping decisions, not just answering questions.
- **When ad hoc requests are overwhelming the team**: protect time for exploratory deep dives — because the biggest insights often come from self-directed investigation.
- **When stakeholders ask for “just one more thing”**: make the trade-off explicit and ask what should be deprioritized — because people often don’t see the opportunity cost.
- **When hiring analytics talent**: screen for curiosity and self-directed investigation — because strong analysts keep pulling on threads after the first answer.
- **When interviewing for ambiguous problem-solving**: use real business cases with hidden inconsistencies — because that reveals adaptability and judgment better than pure technical screens.
- **When choosing a team metric**: use a short-term proxy for the long-term outcome — because lagging metrics are too slow to drive iteration.
- **When tempted by a sophisticated composite score**: simplify into a few understandable drivers — because metrics only work if people can interpret and act on them.
- **When making cross-functional investment decisions**: convert levers into a common business currency like GOV — because it makes trade-offs comparable.
- **When averages look healthy**: inspect distributions and edge cases — because rare fail states can be disproportionately costly.
- **When data alone can’t explain behavior**: talk to customers directly — because qualitative context often unblocks the next decision.
- **When building a team with nontraditional backgrounds**: hire for technical ability plus business judgment — because diverse operator backgrounds can improve practical decision-making.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Analytics should be embedded directly inside each business unit. → **Their view**: Centralized analytics with aligned pods is better — because it preserves standards, shared methods, and career paths while keeping close partnership.
- **Conventional**: Analytics is mainly a service function. → **Their view**: Analytics should drive business impact and shape decisions — because answering “what now?” matters more than reporting what happened.
- **Conventional**: Retention is a good north-star metric to goal on. → **Their view**: Retention is a poor direct operating metric — because it’s too slow-moving to guide short-term iteration.
- **Conventional**: More complex composite metrics are more accurate and therefore better. → **Their view**: Simpler metrics are usually superior — because interpretability drives action.
- **Conventional**: Rare edge cases don’t matter much. → **Their view**: Rare fail states deserve attention — because they can hide major churn, cost, or lost demand.
- **Conventional**: Data scientists should stay in their lane. → **Their view**: They should do whatever it takes, including customer calls — because ownership beats role purity.
- **Conventional**: You need a traditional analytics background to lead a world-class data org. → **Their view**: Nontraditional backgrounds can be an advantage — because operator perspective improves business relevance.
