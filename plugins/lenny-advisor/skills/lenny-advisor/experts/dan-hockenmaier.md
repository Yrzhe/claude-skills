# Dan Hockenmaier

**Head of Strategy and Analytics** | **Faire** | Expertise: marketplace strategy, growth modeling, analytics, go-to-market

## Bio
Dan Hockenmaier is a marketplace and growth strategy leader who helped scale Thumbtack in its early years, later advised dozens of growth-stage startups through Basis One, and now leads strategy and analytics at Faire. His perspective matters because he combines first-principles modeling with deep marketplace intuition, especially around liquidity, retention, and how to allocate resources in complex businesses.

He is particularly valuable when a team needs to understand what really drives growth, how to evaluate marketplace health beyond GMV, or how to decide where to expand next.

## Signature Frameworks

### Growth Model Framework
- **When to use**: When you need an analytical representation of how a business grows in order to assess opportunities, compare initiatives, and improve planning.
- **Steps**:
  1. Map acquisition channels and define the core inputs for each, such as traffic, spend, and conversion rate.
  2. Model activation and retention over time, including cohort survival or repeat behavior.
  3. Model monetization, such as subscription revenue, transaction frequency, average order value, or take rate.
  4. For transactional businesses, add unit economics like COGS or contribution margin.
  5. For marketplaces, add supply acquisition and retention, plus assumptions about how supply and demand interact.
  6. Add non-linear loops where relevant, such as virality or reinvestment of contribution margin into paid acquisition.
  7. Use the model for opportunity sizing and tradeoff decisions, not as a precise finance forecast.
- **Example**: At Thumbtack, building the growth model revealed that the business was highly sensitive to repeat usage and cross-category repeat rate, which led the team to shift resources away from top-of-funnel SEO and conversion work toward lifecycle and cross-sell efforts.

### Dual-Level Modeling: Company Model + Team Mini-Models
- **When to use**: When a company is too complex for one master model and individual teams need clarity on the levers that drive their own north star metrics.
- **Steps**:
  1. Build a simple high-level model that explains the core mechanics of the whole business.
  2. Identify the major functional areas or product pods.
  3. For each team, create a mini-model that maps the inputs to that team's north star metric.
  4. Use the company model to understand broad leverage points and the team models to guide execution.
  5. Avoid overfitting one giant stitched-together model if the business has too many interacting parts.
- **Example**: A marketplace quality team may need a different model than a growth funnel team: instead of optimizing a linear funnel, they model the tension between adding more supply for short-term GMV and preserving quality for long-term retention.

### Marketplace Health Metrics Framework
- **When to use**: When evaluating whether a marketplace is healthy, scalable, and defensible beyond just top-line GMV.
- **Steps**:
  1. Track GMV or transactions as the core output metric tying supply and demand together.
  2. Understand unit economics deeply, especially in early stages when they may be weak or negative.
  3. Define and measure liquidity as marketplace reliability from the user's perspective.
  4. Express liquidity in a customer-relevant form, such as wait time, conversion rate, or search-to-fail rate.
  5. Measure share of wallet on one or both sides of the marketplace to understand depth and defensibility.
- **Example**: For Uber or Lyft, liquidity can be measured by wait time, with a key threshold around four to five minutes. For commerce marketplaces, Dan suggests conversion rate or search-to-fail metrics.

### Marketplace Expansion Prioritization Framework
- **When to use**: When deciding which adjacent markets, categories, or geographies a marketplace should expand into after establishing an initial foothold.
- **Steps**:
  1. Ignore TAM as the primary decision variable once all options are already large enough to matter.
  2. Prioritize adjacency to the current business as a proxy for execution feasibility.
  3. Assess whether the expansion strengthens the existing network effect by reusing supply or serving overlapping demand.
  4. Ensure product quality and customer experience can support the expansion before scaling go-to-market.
  5. Expand from a strong, retained, high-liquidity core rather than chasing broad GMV.
- **Example**: Instacart expanding into convenience stores is operationally adjacent to its core, while Uber Eats is a strong adjacency because it can leverage overlapping drivers and customer demand.

### Marketplace Evolution Continuum
- **When to use**: When thinking about how marketplaces evolve over time and whether they remain marketplaces or become more vertically integrated businesses.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify whether the marketplace is primarily aggregating demand, creating trust, or taking over more of the value chain.
  2. Observe whether commission rates rise as the company does more work for the transaction.
  3. Assess whether the business is moving toward owning supply or removing direct supplier-demand interaction.
  4. Evaluate whether the category depends on supplier creativity or on standardized, commoditized experiences.
  5. Predict whether the business will remain a marketplace or evolve into a more integrated commerce or service model.
- **Example**: Zillow and HomeAdvisor are earlier lead-gen style marketplaces, Airbnb and Etsy are trust-building managed marketplaces, and DoorDash, Instacart, and Faire are more heavily managed marketplaces that own logistics or underwriting. Opendoor is an example of a company that has effectively moved beyond the marketplace model.

## Core Advice
- **When you face uncertainty about what actually drives growth in your business**: Do the work to build a growth model in a spreadsheet from first principles, ideally with a strong analyst or finance partner — because the process forces clarity on how acquisition, retention, monetization, and unit economics fit together.
- **When you face annual or quarterly planning decisions across multiple teams**: Do scenario modeling to convert each team's proposed metric movement into a common business currency using the growth model — because it makes otherwise incomparable initiatives tradeable against one another.
- **When you face a choice between improving top-of-funnel metrics and improving retention or repeat behavior**: Do the modeling first and expect retention or repeat rate to be more valuable than intuition suggests — because retention compounds through referrals, contribution margin, and future monetization.
- **When you face poor retention and are tempted to fix it with emails, push notifications, or resurrection campaigns**: Do deeper work on the early user experience and core product value instead of relying on re-engagement tactics — because retention is the output of the whole product experience.
- **When you face inconsistent early experiences for new users or suppliers**: Do analysis to find variability in the first week or month and smooth out below-average early experiences — because users infer long-term value from their first experience.
- **When you face the classic marketplace question of whether to focus on supply or demand**: Do your prioritization through the lens of demand and only invest in supply to the extent that it measurably improves customer experience — because demand is the true currency of a marketplace.
- **When you face marketplace acquisition decisions and are unsure how to judge balance between the two sides**: Do dual-sided ROI modeling that includes the CAC of the side you're acquiring plus the loaded CAC of the other side needed to support it — because simple balance ratios can be misleading.
- **When you face pressure to expand your marketplace into adjacent categories or geographies**: Do expansion into the most adjacent opportunities that strengthen your existing network effect, and keep product and go-to-market in lockstep — because adjacency predicts execution feasibility better than raw TAM.
- **When you face the temptation to add a SaaS product on top of a marketplace**: Do it only after you have a liquid, high-performing marketplace and a clear customer pain point that software can solve — because SaaS add-ons are often pursued too early or for margin reasons.
- **When you face decisions about changing core marketplace incentives, pricing, or mechanisms**: Do small, careful changes and tread lightly — because marketplace systems have delayed and hard-to-predict second- and third-order effects.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Growth teams should focus heavily on top-of-funnel acquisition and conversion because those are the most visible levers. → **Their view**: Retention and repeat behavior are often much more important than intuition suggests — because small retention gains compound across referrals, monetization, and acquisition economics.
- **Conventional**: If retention is weak, you should focus on churn prevention, resurrection campaigns, and re-engagement messaging. → **Their view**: The highest-leverage retention work usually happens in the earliest user experience — because by the time users churn, the product has often already lost them.
- **Conventional**: Marketplace founders should focus primarily on supply because supply is the hard side and the product itself. → **Their view**: Demand is ultimately the only thing that matters — because supply should be acquired only insofar as it improves demand-side experience.
- **Conventional**: Marketplace balance ratios are the main way to decide whether to invest in supply or demand. → **Their view**: Balance metrics matter less than robust dual-sided ROI models — because you need to internalize the cost of supporting both sides.
- **Conventional**: When expanding a marketplace, the biggest opportunity should be the one with the largest TAM. → **Their view**: Adjacency and network-effect reinforcement matter more than TAM — because execution feasibility is the real constraint.
- **Conventional**: Unbundling large horizontal platforms into vertical marketplaces is a broadly reliable startup strategy. → **Their view**: Unbundling is overhyped and works less often than people think — because scaled horizontal platforms often have stronger LTV and network effects than founders assume.
