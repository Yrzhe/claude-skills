# Ravi Mehta

**Founder and product advisor; formerly Chief Product Officer at Tinder** | **Outpace** | Expertise: product strategy, product leadership, startup building, coaching

## Bio
Ravi Mehta is a veteran product leader who has held senior roles at Microsoft, Tripadvisor, Facebook, and Tinder, and now runs Outpace, a coaching company focused on making expert coaching more accessible with product and AI. His perspective matters because he combines deep operator experience with unusually practical frameworks for strategy, PM development, feedback, and leadership.

He is especially useful when teams need clarity: what strategy actually means, how to set the right kind of goals, how to coach PMs, and how to lead without drifting into either chaos or overcontrol.

## Signature Frameworks

### Product Strategy Stack
- **When to use**: Use when defining, debugging, or aligning product strategy across company, product, roadmap, and goals.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the company mission: the qualitative, aspirational change the company wants to bring to the world.
  2. Define the company strategy: the rigorous logical plan for achieving the mission.
  3. Define the product strategy: the connective tissue between company strategy and day-to-day product work.
  4. Translate product strategy into a roadmap: the sequence of product bets and initiatives.
  5. Translate the roadmap into goals: measurable targets that reflect the roadmap and strategy.
  6. Debug bottom-up when needed: if goals are off, inspect roadmap; if roadmap is off, inspect product strategy; if product strategy is off, inspect company strategy and mission.
- **Example**: At Tinder vs. Hinge, both apps had similar goals around meaningful conversations, but different missions and product strategies led to different roadmaps and features.

### Frontier of Understanding
- **When to use**: Use when setting goals in uncertain environments, especially when the team does not yet know what truly moves a metric.
- **Steps**:
  1. Assess whether the team understands the levers that move the metric.
  2. If not, set an understanding goal first to learn what drives the metric.
  3. If the levers are understood but resources or tools are missing, set a dependency goal to remove blockers.
  4. If the team has the hypothesis and resources, set an execution goal to validate the plan.
  5. If the hypothesis is wrong, treat that as strategic risk and revisit the strategy.
- **Example**: If a team wants to improve retention but does not know why users stay, the right goal may be to learn what drives retention before committing to a retention target.

### PM Competency Model
- **When to use**: Use for PM hiring, career development, leveling, self-assessment, and feedback.
- **Steps**:
  1. Product execution: functional specification, product delivery, product quality.
  2. Customer insight: fluency with data, voice of the customer, user experience design.
  3. Product strategy: owning business outcomes, product vision and roadmapping, strategic impact.
  4. Leadership: stakeholder inclusion, team leadership, managing up.
- **Example**: Tripadvisor used this framework in its rotational program to develop PMs from entry level into senior product managers.

### Selective Micromanagement
- **When to use**: Use when a team is heading in the wrong direction and hands-off leadership would let them continue off course.
- **Steps**:
  1. Diagnose whether the team lacks direction or confidence in the right direction.
  2. Intervene tactically and temporarily with more detailed guidance.
  3. Use the intervention to teach the framework and decision logic behind the desired direction.
  4. Pull back once the team can make aligned decisions independently.
- **Example**: Ravi argues that if a team is going the wrong way, the answer is not to be hands-off; it is to micromanage briefly, then restore autonomy.

### Exponential Feedback
- **When to use**: Use when giving performance feedback or coaching PMs and leaders.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with a quick self-rating across competencies to surface gaps.
  2. Compare manager and self-ratings to identify alignment and disagreement.
  3. Move from symptoms to root causes by mapping feedback to underlying competencies.
  4. Focus feedback on behaviors and patterns that compound over time, not just tactical fixes.
- **Example**: A manager and PM each rate the PM as needs focus, on track, or outperforming across competencies, then discuss the mismatches in depth.

### Strategy-to-Wireframe Alignment
- **When to use**: Use when defining product strategy for visual products or when teams interpret strategy differently.
- **Steps**:
  1. Write the strategy clearly in words.
  2. Convert the strategy into low-fidelity wireframes or sketches.
  3. Use the visuals to align stakeholders on what the product will actually look like.
  4. Use the limited screen real estate to force trade-offs and clarify priorities.
- **Example**: At Tripadvisor, the strategy doc for trip planning was not considered complete until it included wireframes.

## Core Advice
- **When moving from a big company into a startup**: optimize for latency, not just velocity — because startups win by shortening the time from idea to test to learning.
- **When tempted to use experiments too early at a startup**: use conviction-based decision-making until you have enough users and data for valid experiments — because small samples can mislead.
- **When you want to start a company someday while working at a big tech company**: build an early-stage network now, not later — because founders, angels, indie hackers, and early operators are the most useful startup contacts.
- **When you are a PM trying to define strategy**: start with mission and strategy before goals and roadmap — because goals without context can optimize the wrong thing.
- **When your strategy is only written in words**: add wireframes or sketches to the strategy doc — because visuals reduce interpretation drift and make trade-offs concrete.
- **When you do not have a designer available**: sketch the experience yourself or use a fast wireframing tool like Balsamiq — because PMs should think visually, not wait on design.
- **When you are setting quarterly goals but do not yet understand the metric**: set an understanding goal first instead of forcing an outcome goal — because learning what moves the metric comes before optimizing it.
- **When you know the lever but lack the tools or resources to move it**: set a dependency goal to remove blockers before expecting metric movement — because the constraint may be access, tooling, or cross-functional support.
- **When you have a strong hypothesis and the team can execute**: set an execution goal that proves the team can run the needed experiments or delivery process — because execution quality itself is often the bottleneck.
- **When you are a new manager worried about micromanaging**: do not swing to hands-off leadership; use temporary, selective micromanagement when the team is off track — because brief intervention can reset direction without permanently reducing autonomy.
- **When your manager is not giving you useful feedback**: ask them to rate you across a competency framework and explicitly invite real-time feedback — because managers often have intuitive judgments that become clearer when structured.
- **When you are evaluating a coach or mentor**: ask them about the client they are most proud of helping and how they helped — because that reveals their values and working style.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Startups are always faster than large companies. → **Their view**: Startups are not faster in velocity; they are faster in latency — because they can go from idea to test to learning much more quickly.
- **Conventional**: Product teams should be highly experimental and data-driven as early as possible. → **Their view**: At startups, conviction should often come before experimentation — because early sample sizes are too small for reliable experiments.
- **Conventional**: Goals should come first, then roadmap and execution. → **Their view**: Goals should come after strategy and roadmap in the strategy stack — because goals without strategic context can misdirect the team.
- **Conventional**: Good leadership means avoiding micromanagement. → **Their view**: Selective micromanagement is sometimes the right move — because a team heading the wrong way needs temporary guardrails.
- **Conventional**: PMs should focus on outcomes, not outputs, at all times. → **Their view**: Sometimes the right goal is understanding or execution, not an immediate business outcome — because the team may first need to learn what actually moves the metric.
- **Conventional**: A big-company network is the best foundation for founding a startup. → **Their view**: The most useful startup network is often founders, indie hackers, angels, investors, and early-stage operators — because startup work requires different instincts than big-company work.
