# Kenneth Berger

**Executive coach; former founder and first product manager at Slack** | **Independent / Ask for What You Want** | Expertise: leadership, product-management, executive-coaching, startup-culture

## Bio
Kenneth Berger coaches startup leaders on how to avoid burnout, stay in integrity, and ask for what they want with clarity and humility. He spent 20+ years in tech, including as the first PM at Slack, and now helps people build more sustainable, fulfilling careers and relationships.

His perspective matters because he combines product judgment, startup operating experience, and coaching depth. He is especially useful when someone is stuck in ambiguity, avoiding conflict, or trying to influence without formal authority.

## Signature Frameworks

### Ask for What You Want
- **When to use**: When you feel stuck, frustrated, conflicted, or unsure how to move toward a goal in work or life.
- **Steps**:
  1. Articulate what you want as clearly as possible.
  2. Ask for it intentionally and directly.
  3. Accept the response, especially the no, without overreacting or dismissing it.
  4. Try again using the new information you got from the response.
- **Example**: At Slack, Kenneth clearly proposed staying in product instead of being moved to user research, and management quickly reconsidered.

### Dream Behind the Complaint
- **When to use**: When you know something feels wrong but can’t yet name what you actually want.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with a complaint or frustration.
  2. Ask what better future that complaint implies.
  3. Check whether the implied dream feels inspiring, credible, and big enough.
  4. Refine the dream until it becomes something worth pursuing.
- **Example**: If someone complains that meetings are boring, the deeper dream might be a team that makes decisions efficiently and respects everyone’s time.

### Hell Yes / Whole-Body Yes
- **When to use**: When negotiating commitments, deadlines, or alignment and you want real buy-in rather than polite agreement.
- **Steps**:
  1. Treat anything short of enthusiastic consent as a no.
  2. Ask what would be required to get to a true hell yes.
  3. Look for full-body alignment, not just verbal agreement.
  4. Use the answer to adjust the ask or the plan.
- **Example**: If a CTO says they can maybe deliver by May 1st, Kenneth would treat that as a no and ask what date would be a hell yes.

### Clear Ask + Humility
- **When to use**: When you need to influence someone but don’t have formal authority, or when you want to avoid sounding entitled.
- **Steps**:
  1. State your preference or disagreement clearly.
  2. Acknowledge that it may not be your call.
  3. Show respect for the other person’s perspective and autonomy.
  4. Invite reconsideration or collaboration.
- **Example**: A PM might say: “I really disagree with this product decision. It’s not my call, but it matters to me. Are you willing to reconsider?”

### Relationship Design Conversation
- **When to use**: When joining a new company, especially as a first PM or early employee, or when expectations with a founder are unclear.
- **Steps**:
  1. Discuss what success looks like for both sides.
  2. Clarify expectations, working style, and decision rights.
  3. Identify how each person prefers to communicate and collaborate.
  4. Co-create the best way to work together given the current team and context.
- **Example**: Kenneth recommends founders do this with their first 10–20 hires so they don’t assume the job description alone is enough.

## Core Advice
- **When you feel stuck in the same problem week after week**: Treat it as a signal that your current way of asking, deciding, or relating is not working and try a different approach — because repeated stuckness usually means you are not getting new data or changing behavior.
- **When you’re not sure what you want**: Start with your complaints and infer the dream behind them — because complaints are often easier to access than aspirations, and every complaint points to a desired better future.
- **When you default to saying “I’m fine” or “it’s fine”**: Check for subtle dissatisfaction, annoyance, or nervousness and name it more honestly — because suppressing wants preserves short-term calm but creates long-term misalignment.
- **When your goal sounds vague or too small**: Pressure-test whether it is actually inspiring enough to motivate you through fear and uncertainty — because a dream that feels “meh” won’t compete with the resistance that comes with asking for it.
- **When your ask feels embarrassing or unrealistic**: Refine it until it becomes credible and possible, not just a fantasy of total control or universal agreement — because overreaching can hide a deeper, more human desire.
- **When you’re afraid to ask because you might get a no**: Reframe no as useful data rather than rejection or failure — because a no tells you what to try next; it is not a verdict on your worth.
- **When you get a lukewarm yes**: Treat it as a no unless it is a true hell yes — because polite agreement often fails later when the person was never fully committed.
- **When you need buy-in on a deadline or commitment**: Ask, “What would it take to get to a hell yes?” — because it shifts the conversation from passive acceptance to explicit conditions for real commitment.
- **When you are tempted to rely only on data**: Also state your informed judgment or gut sense out loud — because in product and leadership decisions, expert opinion and relationship context matter too.
- **When you are a people pleaser and avoid conflict**: Practice saying what you want directly, even if it feels uncomfortable — because avoiding the ask creates long-term frustration and self-betrayal.
- **When you are in authority and tend to be controlling**: Ask with humility instead of issuing commands — because influence depends on trust and respect, and coercion reduces follow-through.
- **When fear is driving your behavior**: Name the fear, validate the scared part of you, and then choose based on vision rather than threat — because fear is often a protective story, while vision is more sustainable motivation.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Avoid upsetting people and ask in a way that guarantees no conflict. → **Their view**: There is no way to ask for what you want without risk — because trying to eliminate all discomfort keeps you stuck.
- **Conventional**: A yes is a yes if someone verbally agrees. → **Their view**: Anything short of a hell yes should be treated as a no — because lukewarm agreement often fails in execution.
- **Conventional**: Being nice and avoiding hard feedback is the best way to lead. → **Their view**: Respecting people means being clear about expectations and consequences — because adults can handle consequences, and ambiguity weakens accountability.
- **Conventional**: Fear is necessary to stay motivated. → **Their view**: Fear is often a poor long-term motivator; vision and joy are more sustainable — because fear may create bursts of effort but not durable performance.
- **Conventional**: If you don’t have data, you shouldn’t speak up. → **Their view**: Your informed judgment and lived context are also valuable data — because leadership decisions are never purely quantitative.
- **Conventional**: Discipline and a drill-sergeant mindset make people successful. → **Their view**: Sustainable success comes from wanting the thing — because intrinsic motivation lasts longer than pressure.
