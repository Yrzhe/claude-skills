# Nir Eyal

**Author, speaker, and behavioral design expert** | **NirAndFar** | Expertise: focus, behavioral design, product psychology, habit formation

## Bio
Nir Eyal is the author of *Hooked* and *Indistractable*, where he applies behavioral science to both sides of the attention problem: how products create habits, and how people break unwanted habits like distraction. His perspective matters because he treats distraction not as a moral failure or a tech problem, but as a design and emotion-regulation problem that can be solved with systems, precommitments, and intentional planning.

## Signature Frameworks

### The 4-step Indistractable framework
- **When to use**: When you want to reduce distraction and regain control over attention in work or life.
- **Steps**:
  1. Master internal triggers: identify the uncomfortable feeling you are trying to escape.
  2. Make time for traction: time-box your values and priorities on your calendar.
  3. Hack back external triggers: reduce or redesign pings, dings, meetings, and interruptions.
  4. Prevent distraction with pacts: add precommitments that create friction or consequences for going off track.
- **Example**: Nir schedules deep work, family time, exercise, and even social media on his calendar, uses do-not-disturb, and sets a router timer that shuts off internet at 10:00 P.M.

### The 10-minute rule
- **When to use**: When you feel the urge to procrastinate or switch to an easier task instead of doing hard work.
- **Steps**:
  1. Notice the internal trigger and name the discomfort.
  2. Set a timer for 10 minutes.
  3. Tell yourself “not yet” instead of “no.”
  4. During the 10 minutes, return to the task or surf the urge until it peaks and subsides.
  5. Use self-talk to reinforce that discomfort is part of progress.
- **Example**: When tempted to check email while writing, Nir sets a 10-minute timer and repeats, “This is what it feels like to get better.”

### Time-boxed calendar planning
- **When to use**: When to-do lists are causing overwhelm, procrastination, or poor prioritization.
- **Steps**:
  1. Convert values into scheduled time blocks for self, relationships, and work.
  2. Schedule reactive work and reflective work separately.
  3. Plan the day the day before or during a weekly review.
  4. Use the calendar as the source of truth for what counts as traction.
  5. Adjust time boxes based on how long tasks actually take.
- **Example**: Nir blocks time for writing, exercise, bedtime, family time, meetings, and even social media so each has an intentional place.

### Schedule syncing / managing your manager
- **When to use**: When your boss, team, or family keeps interrupting your focus time or overloading your schedule.
- **Steps**:
  1. Print or show your time-boxed calendar.
  2. Explain what you are working on and where your time is already allocated.
  3. Show the list of requests that do not fit.
  4. Ask the other person to help prioritize rather than saying no.
  5. Re-sync schedules regularly at work or home.
- **Example**: Nir recommends saying, “Here’s my calendar, here’s the list of things you’ve asked me to do—help me prioritize.”

### The three pact types
- **When to use**: When you need a last-line-of-defense mechanism to keep yourself from giving in to distraction.
- **Steps**:
  1. Use a price pact: attach a financial penalty to failing to do the intended behavior.
  2. Use an identity pact: define yourself as the kind of person who does the behavior.
  3. Use an effort pact: add friction so distraction takes more work than staying on task.
- **Example**: Nir uses a $10 outlet timer to shut off his router at 10:00 P.M., Forest to make phone distraction costly, and Focusmate for accountability.

### Reimagining internal triggers
- **When to use**: When you keep procrastinating because the task feels unpleasant, hard, or emotionally uncomfortable.
- **Steps**:
  1. Reimagine the task as a step toward growth rather than a burden.
  2. Reimagine the trigger by recognizing the feeling as temporary.
  3. Reimagine your temperament by rejecting the belief that you are limited or powerless.
  4. Use self-talk and identity to reduce the emotional charge of discomfort.
- **Example**: Nir’s mantra, “This is what it feels like to get better,” reframes discomfort as progress.

## Core Advice
- **When you sit down to do important work and immediately reach for email, Slack, or social media**: pause and identify the internal feeling you are trying to escape before touching any app — because Nir argues that most distraction starts from within, not from notifications.
- **When a task feels too hard to start**: use the 10-minute rule and tell yourself “not yet” — because short postponement lowers resistance and proves you can tolerate discomfort.
- **When work feels emotionally unpleasant**: surf the urge instead of fighting it — because emotions are temporary waves, not permanent states.
- **When your to-do list keeps growing and makes you feel behind**: replace it with a time-boxed calendar — because a calendar creates constraints and feedback, while a to-do list encourages easy-task bias.
- **When you are tempted to treat work-related checking as “productive” distraction**: only do work tasks during the time you intentionally scheduled for them — because anything not on the calendar is distraction, even if it feels urgent.
- **When your day gets hijacked by notifications, meetings, or family interruptions**: hack back external triggers with do-not-disturb and visible boundaries — because reducing interruptions protects attention and sets expectations.
- **When coworkers or family keep interrupting your focus blocks**: use a visible signal that you are unavailable unless it is urgent — because clear norms reduce accidental interruptions.
- **When you know you can technically ignore your plan but keep doing it anyway**: add an effort pact or price pact — because precommitments create friction between impulse and action.
- **When you want a recurring focus habit like bedtime, exercise, or deep work**: make it part of your identity and routine — because identity-based commitments are more durable than willpower alone.
- **When your manager keeps assigning work faster than you can absorb it**: show your calendar and ask them to help prioritize — because managers are responsible for trade-offs, and the calendar makes them explicit.
- **When you want better focus at home with a partner or kids**: synchronize schedules weekly — because many conflicts come from unsynchronized expectations, not bad intentions.
- **When you think you are “bad at willpower” or “addicted” to distraction**: treat it as a skill and impulse-control problem, not a character flaw — because most people need tools and practice, not shame.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Distraction is mainly caused by technology, notifications, and social media. → **Their view**: The root cause is usually internal discomfort; technology is only the trigger about 10% of the time — because boredom, anxiety, uncertainty, fatigue, and loneliness drive most distraction.
- **Conventional**: The opposite of distraction is focus. → **Their view**: The opposite of distraction is traction — because traction means intentional action aligned with values.
- **Conventional**: To be productive, you should just say no more often. → **Their view**: Saying no is often bad advice; ask for help prioritizing and use your calendar as the decision tool — because most people can’t simply refuse their boss.
- **Conventional**: To-do lists are a core productivity tool. → **Their view**: To-do lists are often harmful because they have no constraints — because they reward box-checking instead of time management.
- **Conventional**: Willpower is a limited resource that gets depleted during the day. → **Their view**: Willpower depletion is largely a belief effect — because people who believe willpower is limited experience it most.
- **Conventional**: Everyone is addicted to social media and digital products. → **Their view**: Most people are not addicted; only a small minority are pathologically addicted — because ordinary distraction is different from compulsive harmful use.
