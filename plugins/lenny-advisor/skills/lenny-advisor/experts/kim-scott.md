# Kim Scott

**Author, executive coach, and leadership advisor** | **Radical Candor** | Expertise: leadership, feedback, management, workplace culture

## Bio
Kim Scott is the author of *Radical Candor* and *Radical Respect*, and has coached leaders at Dropbox, Qualtrics, Twitter, and Google. She also led teams at Google, YouTube, AdSense, and DoubleClick, so her advice comes from both the manager’s seat and the coach’s seat.

Her perspective matters because she focuses on the hard middle ground most leaders miss: being both kind and direct. She is especially useful when you need to improve feedback, build trust, or create a culture where people can tell the truth without fear.

## Signature Frameworks

### Radical Candor 2x2
- **When to use**: When giving praise or criticism, or diagnosing why a conversation is going badly.
- **Steps**:
  1. Assess whether you are showing care personally.
  2. Assess whether you are challenging directly.
  3. If high care + high challenge, you are in Radical Candor.
  4. If low care + high challenge, you are in Obnoxious Aggression.
  5. If high care + low challenge, you are in Ruinous Empathy.
  6. If low care + low challenge, you are in Manipulative Insincerity.
  7. Use the framework as a compass for specific conversations rather than as a moral judgment.
- **Example**: Telling Bob his work was not good enough after 10 months would have been Radical Candor if it had been done with clear care and directness; instead, Kim describes her delayed, vague approach as ruinous empathy mixed with manipulative insincerity.

### HHIIPP / CORE feedback delivery
- **When to use**: When giving feedback or praise in a way that is clear, humane, and actionable.
- **Steps**:
  1. Be humble: treat the conversation as a dialogue, not a monologue.
  2. Be helpful: explicitly state that your intent is to help.
  3. Do it immediately rather than waiting for a “better time.”
  4. Do it in person or synchronously; avoid email, text, and Slack for feedback.
  5. Praise in public and criticize in private.
  6. Avoid talking about personality; focus on behavior and impact.
  7. Use CORE: Context, Observation, Result, Next step.
- **Example**: In the “um every third word” example: context was the meeting, observation was the repeated ums, result was sounding stupid, and next step was to work with a speech coach.

### Soliciting feedback order of operations
- **When to use**: When you want to build trust and make it easier to both give and receive feedback.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start by soliciting criticism from the other person.
  2. Ask a specific, authentic question that fits your voice.
  3. Stay silent long enough for the other person to answer.
  4. Listen to understand, not to respond.
  5. Ask follow-up questions to clarify what they mean.
  6. Reward candor by acting on it and showing the change.
  7. If you disagree, acknowledge the useful part, explain respectfully, and follow up later.
- **Example**: Kim recommends asking, “What could I do or stop doing that would make it easier to work with me?” and then closing your mouth and counting to six.

### Career conversation framework
- **When to use**: When managing direct reports or helping someone grow in their role.
- **Steps**:
  1. Have a conversation about the person’s past and life story.
  2. Have a separate conversation about their future and dreams.
  3. Translate those goals into a career action plan.
  4. Identify skills to build, people to meet, and opportunities to pursue.
  5. Tweak the job where possible so the person gains relevant experience.
- **Example**: Kim suggests three 45-minute conversations: one about the past, one about the future, and one to build a concrete action plan.

### Handling defensive reactions in feedback conversations
- **When to use**: When the other person seems sad, mad, or dismissive after receiving feedback.
- **Steps**:
  1. Notice emotional signals in real time.
  2. If they seem sad, move up on care personally without backing away from the issue.
  3. If they seem mad, get curious rather than furious.
  4. Keep the challenge direct while adjusting tone and empathy.
  5. Do not retreat into ruinous empathy or escalate into obnoxious aggression.
- **Example**: When Kim’s daughter said she wished Kim were “the lady who minded her own business,” Kim asked a follow-up question instead of assuming the meaning.

## Core Advice
- **When you want feedback from someone and they are likely to brush you off**: Ask a specific question like, “What could I do or stop doing that would make it easier to work with me?” — because generic requests usually get a polite no-answer.
- **When you are about to give feedback**: Go in humble and frame it as your perspective, not absolute truth — because candor works as a dialogue, not a verdict.
- **When you have praise or criticism to share**: Do it immediately instead of waiting for a better moment — because delay usually becomes silence.
- **When you need to give feedback**: Use a synchronous channel, ideally in person or on the phone, not Slack, text, or email — because you need to read the reaction and adjust in real time.
- **When you are giving feedback in a meeting or one-on-one**: Focus on behavior and impact, not personality — because personality attacks are less actionable and more defensive.
- **When you want your feedback to be actionable**: Use Context, Observation, Result, and Next step — because structure makes the message concrete.
- **When you are managing people**: Praise in public and criticize in private — because public praise reinforces good behavior and private criticism protects dignity.
- **When you are soliciting feedback from direct reports, peers, or your boss**: Make it a recurring habit, especially at the end of one-on-ones — because regular solicitation normalizes honesty.
- **When someone gives you feedback**: Stay silent for a few seconds, then ask follow-up questions — because people often need space to say the real thing.
- **When you disagree with feedback**: Acknowledge the part you agree with, then say you want to think about the rest and follow up later — because respectful disagreement preserves trust.
- **When someone takes the risk to tell you something hard**: Reward the candor by fixing the issue and showing the change — because people stop being honest if nothing happens.
- **When you are too busy to have feedback conversations**: Build slack into your calendar or be willing to be late to the next meeting — because these short conversations prevent much bigger problems later.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Being direct and blunt is the same as being candid or honest. → **Their view**: Directness without care is not candor; it is obnoxious aggression. — because people shut down when they feel attacked.
- **Conventional**: Being nice and protecting feelings is the best way to be a good leader. → **Their view**: Excessive niceness often becomes ruinous empathy, which is actually unkind in the long run. — because it withholds growth-producing truth.
- **Conventional**: You should wait for the right moment to give feedback. → **Their view**: Waiting usually means never; feedback should happen immediately unless emotions are too hot. — because timeliness prevents small issues from becoming big ones.
- **Conventional**: Feedback should be given through written channels because they are efficient. → **Their view**: Slack, text, and email are bad for feedback; synchronous conversation is better. — because you need real-time emotional signals.
- **Conventional**: The best way to build a strong culture is to be relentlessly tough and public about mistakes. → **Their view**: Public pile-ons and personality-based criticism are harmful, not high standards. — because they humiliate people and create fear.
- **Conventional**: Great leadership means choosing between being a jerk and being nice but ineffective. → **Their view**: You do not have to choose; you can be successful and kind at the same time. — because care plus direct challenge produces better results.
