# Dr. Becky Kennedy

**Clinical psychologist, author, and CEO of Good Inside** | **Good Inside** | Expertise: leadership, hiring-culture, strategy, design

## Bio
Dr. Becky Kennedy is a clinical psychologist and the founder/CEO of Good Inside, a parenting education company that spans books, a podcast, an app, and community. Her perspective matters because she translates child psychology into practical tools for leadership, communication, boundaries, resilience, and relationship repair—skills that apply just as much in families as in teams and organizations.

She is especially useful when you need a framework for handling conflict without shame, setting limits without power struggles, and helping people grow without over-rescuing them.

## Signature Frameworks

### Repair
- **When to use**: After you damage trust through yelling, harshness, cutting someone off, or handling a moment in a way you are not proud of.
- **Steps**:
  1. Go back to the person after the moment.
  2. Take responsibility for your part without defensiveness.
  3. Acknowledge the impact on them.
  4. If relevant, provide context without using it as an excuse.
  5. State what you will try to do differently next time.
- **Example**: “Hey, earlier in the meeting, I totally cut you off. I used a really harsh tone. Honestly, I did disagree with what you're saying, but it's no excuse for me to talk to you the way I did... I'm sorry. I'm going to work on that.”

### Connect Before Correct
- **When to use**: When you need cooperation, behavior change, or alignment from a child, colleague, or partner.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with a mindset of being present without an agenda, even briefly.
  2. Acknowledge the other person's reality and priorities.
  3. Show that you see them as a full human, not an obstacle.
  4. Form a bridge through connection before asking for action or correction.
- **Example**: Instead of “We have to do our taxes” followed by blame, say: “You look like you're just settling in to that book... can we get on top of this together? I know we're on the same team.”

### Good Inside: Separate Behavior from Identity
- **When to use**: When someone is underperforming, acting out, being late, defensive, or otherwise behaving badly and you need a productive conversation.
- **Steps**:
  1. Explicitly separate who the person is from what they did.
  2. Lead with the assumption that they are a good person.
  3. Name the behavior clearly without collapsing it into identity.
  4. Invite curiosity about what is driving the behavior.
  5. Work together on the underlying issue and expectations.
- **Example**: For chronic lateness: “I know you're a good person... we both know we need to start meetings on time... it's been happening consistently, which lets me know something is going on that I want to get to the bottom of with you.”

### MGI (Most Generous Interpretation)
- **When to use**: When you catch yourself telling a negative story about someone's motives or character and need a more useful lens.
- **Steps**:
  1. Notice your default least generous interpretation.
  2. Ask: what is the most generous interpretation of this behavior?
  3. Choose a plausible explanation rooted in unmet needs, fear, or missing skills.
  4. Use that interpretation to shape a more effective intervention or conversation.
- **Example**: If someone is belaboring a point in meetings, instead of assuming vanity, consider that they may not feel heard—and address that cycle directly in private.

### Curiosity Over Judgment
- **When to use**: When someone's behavior is frustrating, confusing, or triggering and judgment would shut down progress.
- **Steps**:
  1. Notice your judgmental reaction.
  2. Replace it with a question about what is happening internally for them.
  3. Assume there is a reason beneath the behavior.
  4. Investigate the missing skill, fear, or unmet need.
  5. Coach the underlying issue rather than only punishing the symptom.
- **Example**: She reframes bad behavior as “feelings that overpower skills,” whether it's a child hitting or an adult reacting poorly at work.

### Sturdy Leadership
- **When to use**: When others are upset, anxious, protesting, or looking to you for direction during turbulence.
- **Steps**:
  1. Acknowledge that their emotional reaction makes sense.
  2. Do not shame them for having feelings.
  3. Do not collapse into their feelings or hand over leadership.
  4. Hold your own point of view and decision-making authority.
  5. Communicate confidence that the group can get through the difficulty.
- **Example**: The pilot metaphor: “I hear everyone screaming back there, that makes sense... I know what I'm doing... I'll see you when we land in Los Angeles.”

### Boundary Setting
- **When to use**: When you need to enforce a limit without power struggles, threats, or depending on the other person to comply voluntarily.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the boundary as what you will do, not what they must do.
  2. Make sure it requires the other person to do nothing.
  3. State it clearly and calmly in advance if possible.
  4. Follow through physically or operationally if needed.
  5. Allow their upset without changing the boundary.
- **Example**: Instead of “Don't press the elevator buttons,” say: “When we go into the elevator, I'm going to stand between you and the button... I'm not going to let you press the buttons.”

### I Believe You / I Believe in You
- **When to use**: When someone is anxious, discouraged, overwhelmed, or resisting a hard task.
- **Steps**:
  1. Validate their experience: “I believe you.”
  2. Normalize the feeling: “It makes sense.”
  3. Hold confidence in their capability: “I believe in you.”
  4. Offer support without taking over.
  5. Frame the hard thing as a path to capability and pride.
- **Example**: “This is a hard project... it makes sense you're nervous... and the reason we think you can take on this project is because we know you can figure this out.”

### Resilience Over Happiness
- **When to use**: When deciding whether to remove discomfort for someone or help them build capacity through it.
- **Steps**:
  1. Recognize that optimizing for short-term comfort can create long-term fragility.
  2. Allow disappointment, frustration, and discomfort to exist.
  3. Support the person through the hard experience rather than erasing it.
  4. Reinforce their ability to cope and recover.
  5. Treat difficult moments as opportunities to build capability.
- **Example**: Instead of doing a child's puzzle to stop whining, say: “There's no better feeling in the world than watching yourself work on something... I will not take that feeling away from you.”

## Core Advice
- **When you face a trust rupture after speaking harshly or mishandling a conversation**: Do a repair quickly: own your part, acknowledge impact, apologize, and say what you'll do differently next time — because repair reestablishes trust and reduces defensiveness.
- **When you face resistance or poor cooperation from a teammate or child**: Do connection before correction by first acknowledging their reality and showing you see them as a person — because connection creates the bridge for cooperation.
- **When you face frustrating behavior like lateness, rambling, or defensiveness**: Do not collapse behavior into identity; explicitly frame them as a good person with a problem behavior — because identity attacks trigger defensiveness.
- **When you face a story in your head that someone is lazy, manipulative, dramatic, or impossible**: Do an MGI check and ask for the most generous interpretation before acting — because it leads to better interventions.
- **When you face someone else's big emotions during a decision or change**: Do sturdy leadership: validate their feelings, keep your footing, and hold the decision if it is yours to make — because people feel safest when leaders don’t panic.
- **When you face repeated boundary violations**: Do boundaries as statements of what you will do, not requests for what they should do — because real boundaries preserve your agency.
- **When you face someone who is anxious about a hard assignment or setback**: Do both halves: say “I believe you” and “I believe in you” — because people need validation and confidence.
- **When you face the temptation to make things easier just to stop discomfort quickly**: Do less rescuing and more coaching through the hard thing — because capability grows through struggle.
- **When you face uncertainty about how you're showing up as a leader or parent**: Ask, “If I could do one thing different this week to be better for you, what would it be?” — because the question itself builds trust and reveals useful feedback.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Great relationships are built by getting things right all the time and avoiding mistakes. → **Their view**: Secure relationships are built less by perfection and more by repair after inevitable mistakes — because humans will mess up repeatedly.
- **Conventional**: Bad behavior should be handled mainly through rewards, punishments, and consequences. → **Their view**: Bad behavior is usually feelings overpowering skills, so the real intervention is teaching missing skills while holding boundaries.
- **Conventional**: A good parent or leader should keep people happy and minimize discomfort. → **Their view**: Optimizing for short-term happiness creates long-term fragility; resilience matters more.
- **Conventional**: Validating feelings means letting those feelings drive decisions. → **Their view**: Validation and boundaries are separate jobs; you can fully validate feelings while still holding the line.
- **Conventional**: Boundaries are telling other people what they need to stop doing. → **Their view**: Most “boundaries” are actually requests; a boundary is what you will do and requires the other person to do nothing.
- **Conventional**: If someone is acting badly at work, the fastest route is to call out the behavior directly and bluntly. → **Their view**: The quickest way to make a conversation unproductive is to lose sight of the person's “good inside.”
