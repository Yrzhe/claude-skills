# Sarah Tavel

**Partner** | **Benchmark** | Expertise: consumer startups, marketplaces, product strategy, growth

## Bio
Sarah Tavel is a Benchmark partner focused on early-stage consumer and marketplace companies. Before venture, she was the first product manager at Pinterest, where she helped shape discovery surfaces and developed a product-led view of engagement, retention, and network effects.

Her perspective matters because she thinks in systems: what truly signals user value, what creates durable retention, and how marketplaces tip from fragile early traction into compounding advantage. She is especially useful when you need to separate vanity growth from real product-market fit.

## Signature Frameworks

### Hierarchy of Engagement
- **When to use**: When building or optimizing a consumer product, especially consumer social products, and you need a clear hierarchy of what to measure and improve.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the core action that signals a user truly understands and values the product, and use that as the primary active-user metric.
  2. Make the product more retentive by ensuring the core action improves the experience over time and creates more to lose by leaving.
  3. Build self-perpetuating loops so user activity improves the product for others and drives organic growth or re-engagement.
- **Example**: Pinterest’s core action was pinning; the product became more personalized the more users pinned, and pins also created re-engagement and network effects.

### Hierarchy of Marketplaces
- **When to use**: When building or scaling a marketplace and you need to decide where to focus first, when to expand, and how to create durable dominance.
- **Steps**:
  1. Focus on a constrained thimble of the market and achieve strong product-market fit and happy transactions there.
  2. Tip the market by creating growth loops and happiness loops that make the marketplace easier and better as it scales.
  3. Dominate the market by expanding into adjacent markets, use cases, or geographies once the playbook is proven.
- **Example**: DoorDash started in the suburbs, where competition was low and the use case was focused, then expanded from a constrained wedge into broader delivery markets.

### Top-down and Bottom-up Core Action Discovery
- **When to use**: When you are unsure what the North Star metric or activation event should be for a consumer product.
- **Steps**:
  1. Bottom-up: Measure all candidate actions and rank them by how strongly they predict return behavior.
  2. Top-down: Ask what the product is fundamentally for and whether users who do not do the action truly understand the product.
  3. Choose the action that both predicts retention and best represents the product’s purpose.
  4. Use that action as the basis for experiments, activation, and product prioritization.
- **Example**: At Pinterest, the team analyzed actions like liking, following, clicking through, time on site, pinning, and repinning, and concluded pinning was the core action.

### Tipping Loop Design
- **When to use**: When a marketplace has reached early traction and you want to accelerate growth without relying only on paid acquisition.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify growth loops where one user brings in another user or another side of the market.
  2. Identify happiness loops that improve matching quality and reward the best suppliers.
  3. Increase the strength of loops by removing friction and adding incentives where needed.
  4. Use the loops to move from manual sales to organic inbound demand and supply.
- **Example**: REKKI turned restaurant orders into supplier-facing order forms, which caused suppliers to ask REKKI to onboard their customers.

### Current-Based Market Selection
- **When to use**: When evaluating a market opportunity and deciding whether it is worth entering now.
- **Steps**:
  1. Look for a market current: a change in behavior, technology, regulation, or economics that creates momentum.
  2. Prefer markets where the current pulls the company forward rather than requiring heavy force to make progress.
  3. Assess whether the current is strong enough to make the founder’s job easier and create enduring value.
- **Example**: Sarah contrasted a stagnant market with one where a current is already pulling the company forward, and cited stablecoins as a market with a strong current.

## Core Advice
- **When you face a consumer product with lots of possible metrics**: Pick one core action that best represents real engagement and optimize around that, not MAUs or downloads — because vanity metrics can rise while the product still fails to create enduring value.
- **When your new-user experience is not converting signups into real users**: Design onboarding to teach the product’s mental model and drive users to the core action as quickly as possible — because activation should mean doing the thing that creates engagement, not just signing up.
- **When you are choosing a core action**: Choose an action that scales to enough users and that would shape the roadmap in the right direction if you optimized for it — because the wrong core action can distort product decisions.
- **When you want to increase retention**: Make the product better the more it is used and increase the user’s cost of leaving — because retentive products accumulate value over time.
- **When your product has weak retention because users do the core action once and disappear**: Use the core action itself to improve personalization, utility, or stored value over time — because the strongest retention comes when the product gets more useful as users contribute more.
- **When you want organic growth in a consumer or marketplace product**: Build loops where user activity creates value for other users, and remove friction so the loop spins faster — because self-perpetuating loops are more durable than paid-only growth.
- **When your product relies on push notifications or real-time prompts to stay alive**: Be careful; if notifications become overwhelming, the loop can break and users will start ignoring the product — because noisy engagement can collapse.
- **When you are building a marketplace and want to get to scale**: Start with a very constrained market wedge and prove the playbook there before expanding — because focus helps you reach tipping faster.
- **When you are measuring marketplace success**: Track happy GMV, not just GMV — because raw volume can hide poor transaction quality and weak retention.
- **When you are deciding whether to expand a marketplace**: Expand only after you see retention, organic growth, and a tipping signal in the initial market — because expansion without a tipping core usually makes the problem harder.
- **When you are building a marketplace with limited supply-side differentiation**: Avoid markets where supply is too homogeneous to create a meaningfully better buyer experience — because the marketplace cannot build a strong flywheel without differentiated value.
- **When you are tempted to chase growth too broadly in a marketplace**: Put more wood behind fewer arrows and win one market decisively before planting flags everywhere — because diffuse resources make it easier for competitors to outspend you.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Market size is the primary thing investors should care about. → **Their view**: What matters more is the market’s current and momentum. — because a smaller market with a strong current can be easier to win and more durable than a large stagnant one.
- **Conventional**: Consumer startups should optimize for MAUs, signups, downloads, or other top-line growth metrics. → **Their view**: Those are often vanity metrics; the real question is whether users complete the core action that signals true engagement. — because superficial growth can mask weak retention.
- **Conventional**: Marketplace founders should go broad early to maximize GMV and prove demand everywhere. → **Their view**: Start in a thimble-sized market and get extremely focused before expanding. — because broad early expansion diffuses attention and prevents tipping.
- **Conventional**: More GMV is always better for a marketplace. → **Their view**: GMV can be a vanity metric if it is not happy GMV. — because volume without great transactions does not create durable value.
- **Conventional**: The biggest marketplace opportunity is often the most obvious market. → **Their view**: Underestimated or small-looking markets can be best. — because they are less competitive and easier to tip.
- **Conventional**: Anonymity is a growth hack for social products because it lowers friction. → **Their view**: Pure anonymity often undermines retention and community quality. — because persistent identity helps users accumulate value and discourages bad behavior.
