# Melissa Perri

**Founder and CEO** | **The Product Institute** | Expertise: product-management, product-operations, agile-transformation, strategy

## Bio
Melissa Perri is a product management leader, author of *Escaping the Build Trap* and *Product Operations*, and founder of The Product Institute. She has trained product teams and leaders across many Fortune 500 companies, helping large enterprises move beyond rigid agile rituals toward real product strategy and outcome-driven product management.

Her perspective matters most when an organization has “done agile” but still struggles to build the right things, measure impact, or develop strong product leaders. She is especially valuable for large companies trying to modernize product development without confusing process adoption with true transformation.

## Signature Frameworks

### Product operating model for large organizations
- **When to use**: When a company wants to improve how it builds products at scale, especially during a digital or agile transformation.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define product strategy and tie it to company goals.
  2. Organize around products, not just projects or components.
  3. Build product operations so teams can access data, customers, and decision-making infrastructure.
  4. Set culture and incentives around outcomes and value, not shipping volume.
  5. Create transparency into team work and business alignment.
  6. Establish career paths and leadership roles for product management at multiple levels.
- **Example**: Large banks and companies like Capital One improved by adding product management on top of agile practices.

### Outcome-driven product discovery
- **When to use**: When teams are building features but are not sure they are solving the right problem.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with a product vision and business goal.
  2. Talk to customers and gather qualitative insight.
  3. Use market research and data to validate the problem.
  4. Form a hypothesis about the desired outcome.
  5. Break the vision into smaller deliverables.
  6. Instrument the work to measure whether the release moved the intended metric.
  7. Inspect results and adapt the next iteration.
- **Example**: Ask, “What do we hope will happen when we release this?” then measure whether the metric actually changes.

### Inspect-and-adapt agile
- **When to use**: When a team is using Scrum or another agile method and the process has become rigid or bloated.
- **Steps**:
  1. Use the framework as a starting point, not a religion.
  2. Regularly ask whether each ceremony or rule serves the team.
  3. Remove meetings and rituals that do not add value.
  4. Keep the parts that help the team talk, learn, and ship faster.
  5. Adjust sprint length, cadence, and collaboration patterns to fit the team.
- **Example**: Her team at OpenSky found two-week sprints too long and moved to weekly shipping while skipping daily standups.

### Transformation through capability building, not process adoption
- **When to use**: When a company is trying to modernize product development and is tempted to buy a framework as a shortcut.
- **Steps**:
  1. Assess missing skills in product, leadership, and execution.
  2. Bring in experienced leaders who have done the work before.
  3. Train existing employees, but do not rely on training alone.
  4. Baseline the organization, then let people practice the role.
  5. Identify who can grow into product leadership and who should move elsewhere.
  6. Codify roles, responsibilities, and career paths after the work is understood.
- **Example**: At Athenahealth, she helped train hundreds of product people, and some moved into operations, data, or user research instead of product management.

### Product owner to product manager progression
- **When to use**: When an organization has product owners who want to become true product managers.
- **Steps**:
  1. Move beyond backlog management and user stories.
  2. Get exposure to customers and discovery work.
  3. Learn to translate customer needs into strategy and feature direction.
  4. Understand metrics, outcomes, and business value.
  5. Own a problem end-to-end, not just delivery tasks.
  6. Use a career ladder such as associate PM, PM, senior PM.
- **Example**: “I prioritized the backlog” becomes “I led the work around the login API to meet regulatory requirements and save the bank millions.”

## Core Advice
- **When a rigid agile process is slowing the team down**: Inspect each ceremony and remove anything that is not helping the team learn, collaborate, or ship value — because agile should accelerate value delivery, not create overhead.
- **When adopting Scrum or SAFe as a plug-and-play solution**: Treat the framework as a development operating model, not a full product strategy or leadership system — because delivery cadence is not the same as product thinking.
- **When improving product outcomes in a large enterprise**: Build product operations so teams can access customers, data, and decision-making support — because good product work is impossible without the surrounding infrastructure.
- **When planning a feature or release**: Ask, “What do we hope will happen when we release this?” and define the metric you expect to change — because outcomes matter more than output.
- **When building without enough discovery**: Do customer research and hypothesis validation before committing to a quarter of work — because assumptions often lead teams to build the wrong thing.
- **When a product owner is stuck in tactical backlog work**: Push to join customer research and strategy discussions so you can own the problem, not just the tickets — because product management is about outcomes, not ceremony.
- **When your team has no feature work ready for developers**: Let developers work on tech debt and architecture improvements instead of forcing filler — because system improvements can create more value than artificial backlog items.
- **When product career paths are unclear**: Ask leadership to define a product ladder and clarify what comes next — because strong people leave or stagnate without growth.
- **When moving from product owner to product manager**: Rewrite your resume around outcomes, customer impact, and business value — because hiring managers want evidence of problem-solving, not Scrum participation.
- **When certifications are treated as a shortcut**: Be skeptical of short certifications and prioritize real experience and coaching — because judgment and skill come from practice.
- **When leading a transformation without experienced product leaders**: Mix internal upskilling with experienced hires who can model what good looks like — because people learn faster from strong practitioners.
- **When stuck in a weak product environment**: Move laterally to a stronger team or leave for an organization that does product management well — because proximity to good leadership accelerates growth.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: SAFe is the best way to scale agile across a large organization. → **Their view**: SAFe is usually overkill and often becomes a crutch. — because it focuses on process and release trains, not strategy, discovery, or leadership.
- **Conventional**: A product owner is basically the same as a product manager. → **Their view**: Product owner is a Scrum-specific tactical role, not the same as product management. — because PM requires customer research, strategy, experimentation, and outcome ownership.
- **Conventional**: More process and more meetings will make teams more productive. → **Their view**: Too much process can destroy productivity. — because teams end up doing work about work instead of solving customer problems.
- **Conventional**: A certification can make someone ready for product management. → **Their view**: Short certifications do not make someone capable of doing the job. — because PM requires judgment and repeated practice.
- **Conventional**: Agile transformation is mainly a process rollout. → **Their view**: Real transformation is an operating-model and capability change. — because strategy, org design, product ops, culture, incentives, and leadership all matter.
- **Conventional**: If developers are not fully booked with feature work, the team is underutilized. → **Their view**: Unused capacity can go to tech debt, architecture, and system improvements. — because forcing constant feature output creates artificial work.
