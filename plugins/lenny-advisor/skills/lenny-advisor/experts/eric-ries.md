# Eric Ries

**Founder and executive chairman of the Long-Term Stock Exchange** | **Long-Term Stock Exchange (LTSE)** | Expertise: lean startup, product strategy, experimentation, governance, AI strategy

## Bio
Eric Ries is the creator of the Lean Startup methodology and author of *The Lean Startup*, one of the most influential books in modern product and startup thinking. His perspective matters because he helped define how founders should learn under uncertainty, and he now applies that same systems-thinking to corporate governance and long-term value creation through LTSE.

What makes his viewpoint unique is the combination of startup experimentation and institutional design: he cares not just about building products faster, but about building companies that stay aligned with their mission over time.

## Signature Frameworks

### Lean Startup / Build-Measure-Learn
- **When to use**: When building new products or startups under high uncertainty, especially when you do not yet know what customers truly want.
- **Steps**:
  1. State the core hypothesis or leap-of-faith assumption behind the product.
  2. Build the smallest test that can validate or invalidate that assumption.
  3. Measure customer behavior and outcomes, not just opinions.
  4. Learn whether the hypothesis is supported.
  5. Decide whether to persevere, iterate, or pivot.
- **Example**: At IMVU, the team shipped a crude teleport-style movement system instead of building full avatar walking technology. Customers perceived it as more advanced than competitors because it removed waiting.

### Minimum Viable Product (MVP)
- **When to use**: When you need to test whether a product idea, feature, or value proposition is viable before investing heavily in a full build.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the specific hypothesis you want to test.
  2. Determine the minimum quality bar required by that market or customer.
  3. Strip scope aggressively to the smallest version that can still test the hypothesis.
  4. Expose it to a small number of real customers.
  5. Use the feedback and behavior to refine the next version.
- **Example**: Ries advised industrial teams to test demand with a high-quality brochure and pre-orders instead of spending years and millions building physical equipment.

### Pivot
- **When to use**: When the current strategy is not working, but the broader vision or underlying insight still seems worth pursuing.
- **Steps**:
  1. Acknowledge clearly that the current approach is not working.
  2. Preserve what has been learned from the current effort.
  3. Change strategy while maintaining fidelity to the broader vision.
  4. Run focused experiments on the new direction.
  5. Repeat until a productive path emerges or shut down if none does.
- **Example**: LTSE spent years pursuing a partnership-based path that failed after SEC approval was withdrawn. That failure enabled a pivot into the structure that eventually got approved.

### Innovation Accounting / Establishing a Baseline
- **When to use**: When teams need objective evidence of progress in uncertain product development rather than vanity metrics or intuition.
- **Steps**:
  1. Measure the current baseline performance, even if it is poor.
  2. Track learning metrics tied to the hypothesis being tested.
  3. Run experiments to improve from that baseline.
  4. Compare results over time to determine whether progress is real.
  5. Use the evidence to decide whether to continue or change direction.
- **Example**: Ries described wanting customers to react negatively to an early product because that establishes a baseline from which the team can measure whether later changes actually improve outcomes.

### Leap-of-Faith Assumption Mapping
- **When to use**: When a team is struggling to define what to test first or keeps building too much before learning.
- **Steps**:
  1. List the assumptions that must be true for the business to work.
  2. Identify which assumptions are most critical and most uncertain.
  3. Choose a learning metric for each key assumption.
  4. Design an MVP specifically to test the highest-risk assumption.
  5. Prioritize experiments based on what would most quickly reveal whether the business can work.
- **Example**: For industrial equipment, Ries pushed teams to realize the real assumption was not “can we build the machine” but “will customers buy based on these specifications,” which could be tested with a brochure.

### Fixed-Time Pivot Decision Process
- **When to use**: When founders suspect the company is stuck and need a practical way to decide whether to persevere, pivot, or shut down.
- **Steps**:
  1. Admit that the current path may not be working.
  2. Set a fixed period of time to try one decisive effort on the current strategy.
  3. Focus the whole team or a dedicated person on the one thing that matters most.
  4. If it still does not work, ask each team member what they would build if starting over today.
  5. Choose the most compelling new direction and test it for a fixed period.
  6. If no direction feels worth pursuing, consider shutting down or changing leadership.
- **Example**: If a startup has only a few months of runway left, Ries suggested allocating one month per promising new direction rather than drifting indefinitely.

### Governance for Human Flourishing
- **When to use**: When founders want to ensure their company remains aligned with its mission and values as it scales, raises capital, or goes public.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the harms or mission failures you want the company structurally protected against.
  2. Ask legal counsel what governance mechanisms could prevent those outcomes.
  3. Adopt structures such as public benefit corporation status, mission pledges, foundations, or mission-aligned SPVs.
  4. Embed responsibility for stakeholder outcomes into board and ownership structures.
  5. Continuously align governance, management, operations, and culture rather than treating them as separate.
- **Example**: Ries described companies creating foundations tied to indigenous stakeholders or requiring board members and investors to commit contractually to supporting the mission.

## Core Advice
- **When you face uncertainty about what customers actually want**: Do a real-world test with the smallest viable version of the product instead of building the full thing first — because most startup waste comes from building high-quality products that customers do not want.
- **When you face pressure to make your MVP polished and feature-rich**: Cut your planned feature set in half, then in half again, and build that — because founders usually overestimate what is necessary by one or two orders of magnitude.
- **When you face a market with high quality expectations**: Raise quality only where the market truly requires it, while keeping scope extremely narrow — because MVP means minimum needed to test the hypothesis, not low quality.
- **When you face fear that a rough early product will damage your reputation**: Separate product launch from marketing launch and test quietly with a small number of customers — because the downside of a small failed test is usually much lower than founders imagine.
- **When you face a long, expensive build cycle for a physical or enterprise product**: Test demand with artifacts like brochures, specs, mockups, or pre-orders before building the full product — because customers often buy based on perceived value and specifications.
- **When you face a product that is beautifully crafted but not growing**: Establish a baseline and run a series of experiments before concluding the value proposition is wrong — because one failed launch can have many causes.
- **When you face a decision about whether to pivot**: Give yourself a fixed amount of time to make one decisive push on the current strategy, then reassess honestly — because time-boxing prevents endless drift.
- **When you face a stuck startup and the team is demoralized**: Ask everyone, “If we started over today, what would we build?” and compare answers — because teams often already know the current path is wrong.
- **When you face a zombie company that is alive but no longer meaningful to you**: Ask yourself whether, if you could start fresh today, you would choose to build this same company — because inertia and ego can trap founders in misaligned businesses.
- **When you face pressure to compromise your principles at work**: Be willing to get fired for doing the work the way you believe is right — because principled conviction often leads to better long-term opportunities.
- **When you face the challenge of building a company you can still trust after fundraising or IPO**: Address governance early with your lawyer instead of assuming you can fix it later — because governance changes are “always too early until it’s too late.”
- **When you face AI uncertainty and do not know which future scenario to optimize for**: Choose actions that are ethical and useful across many possible futures, such as increasing transparency and building better governance — because robust actions are safer than betting on one forecast.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Startup failure is the worst outcome for a founder. → **Their view**: Building or staying in a zombie company you hate can be far worse than failing — because survival without meaning can be emotionally and morally costly.
- **Conventional**: MVP means shipping something cheap, low-quality, and bare-bones. → **Their view**: MVP is about the most efficient way to test a hypothesis in a given market — because some markets require high quality even in the first test.
- **Conventional**: Visionary founders should trust their instincts rather than experiment. → **Their view**: Even strong vision benefits from experimentation and double-checking assumptions — because conviction does not remove uncertainty.
- **Conventional**: Craft and design excellence are at odds with Lean Startup. → **Their view**: True craft can coexist with Lean Startup — because if you insist on high standards in one area, you should be even more rigorous about testing the rest.
- **Conventional**: Shareholder primacy and standard corporate governance maximize returns. → **Their view**: Mission-aligned governance can outperform financially and create more durable companies — because long-term structures reduce destructive short-term pressure.
- **Conventional**: AI risk is mainly about the technology itself. → **Their view**: The bigger issue is corporations and institutions deploying AI without aligned values and governance — because AI reflects the organizations that build and use it.
