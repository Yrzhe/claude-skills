# Annie Pearl

**Chief Product Officer** | **Calendly** | Expertise: product strategy, product management, go-to-market, leadership

## Bio
Annie Pearl is the Chief Product Officer at Calendly, where she leads product management, design, research, and product operations through a major shift from pure product-led growth to a combined PLG and sales-led motion. She previously served as CPO at Glassdoor and held product leadership roles at Box, giving her deep experience scaling product organizations and sharpening strategy in both consumer-like and enterprise settings.

Her perspective matters because she is unusually strong on the intersection of strategy, execution, and GTM. She is especially valuable when a company needs to focus its product bets, align cross-functional teams, or evolve from startup-style product work into a more mature operating model.

## Signature Frameworks

### Playing to Win product strategy framework
- **When to use**: When a company has broad demand but needs sharper prioritization, clearer market focus, and a coherent product strategy.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the winning aspiration.
  2. Choose where to play: markets, segments, and personas.
  3. Decide how to win with the target audience.
  4. Use those choices to determine what not to build.
  5. Allocate resources across strategic horizons based on current maturity and future bets.
- **Example**: At Calendly, the aspiration is to become the best place to schedule, prepare for, and follow up on external meetings. The company focused on core ICPs like sales, recruiting, and customer success teams, and deprioritized requests like a Venmo integration because it did not fit those personas.

### Three-horizon resource allocation
- **When to use**: When balancing core business improvements with adjacent growth opportunities and longer-term bets.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define horizon 1, horizon 2, and horizon 3 in relation to the company vision.
  2. Allocate resources by horizon based on business stage.
  3. Rebalance annually as the company matures and confidence increases.
  4. Reduce investment in mature horizons and increase investment in emerging growth areas.
- **Example**: Calendly initially allocated roughly 70% to horizon 1 and 30% to horizon 2, with 0% to horizon 3. In year two it shifted to 50/50, and by year three to about 30% horizon 1, 60% horizon 2, and 10% horizon 3.

### Product development lifecycle: Discovery → Solutioning → Build → Launch/Measure/Iterate
- **When to use**: When teams need more realistic planning and should avoid overcommitting to delivery dates before enough learning has happened.
- **Steps**:
  1. Discovery: research the problem space and determine whether it is worth pursuing.
  2. Solutioning: explore solutions and test them with users.
  3. Build: estimate and implement once the problem and solution are clear.
  4. Launch, measure, and iterate: release, evaluate outcomes, and refine.
- **Example**: Calendly commits only to dates within its control. For example, it can commit to finishing discovery in a quarter, but not to shipping a feature six months out before discovery and solutioning are complete.

### OKR maturity progression
- **When to use**: When a company is evolving from ad hoc execution to aligned cross-functional planning.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify whether work lacks strategic clarity and measurable success criteria.
  2. Introduce team-level OKRs tied to strategy.
  3. Elevate to company-level OKRs with a small number of top priorities.
  4. Map dependencies across functions so plans are integrated, not siloed.
  5. Track annual objectives with quarterly milestones.
- **Example**: Calendly progressed from having little OKR discipline, to product-only OKRs, to company-wide OKRs with tightly integrated planning across departments.

### Transitioning into product management
- **When to use**: When someone from a non-PM or non-technical background wants to break into product management.
- **Steps**:
  1. Apply to formal APM programs.
  2. Look for junior PM roles, especially via internal job boards.
  3. Shadow or partner closely with a PM and take on product work informally.
  4. Join a subject matter expert program to embed with a product squad.
  5. At an early-stage startup, take on PM-like responsibilities as part of a broader role.
- **Example**: Annie says the most successful path she has seen is internal transfer: people make their interest known, partner closely with PMs, and demonstrate PM skills before formally getting the role.

## Core Advice
- **When you face broad customer demand and constant prioritization conflicts**: define your target market, segments, and personas, and use that to say no to requests that do not fit — because focus enables better prioritization and stronger products for the customers who matter most.
- **When you face pressure to commit to feature delivery dates too early**: plan in phases and only commit to dates for the phase directly in front of you — because you cannot estimate accurately before understanding the problem and validating a solution.
- **When you face siloed planning and OKRs that do not drive execution**: use company-level OKRs with tightly integrated cross-functional plans and explicit dependency mapping — because OKRs work best when they connect top-level objectives to departmental work.
- **When you face the transition from PLG to adding a sales-led motion**: hire for the actual motion and buyer you have now, not the one you may have later — because early PLG-to-sales buyers are often department heads, not CIOs.
- **When you face tension between product and sales teams**: treat sales and go-to-market teams as a major source of customer insight — because they speak with far more customers than PMs can directly reach.
- **When you face the challenge of transitioning into product management from another function**: do an internal transfer if possible, make your interest explicit, and partner closely with a PM — because this builds credibility and proof of skill before the title change.
- **When you face awkwardness sending a scheduling link**: use a softer handoff, invite availability first, then offer your Calendly link and suggested times — because it reduces social friction while keeping scheduling efficient.
- **When you need to understand competitors without distracting the whole team constantly**: run structured competitive war-gaming with small groups studying one competitor deeply — because concentrated expertise creates repeatable learning.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: More customer requests mean more opportunities, so product teams should try to serve as many use cases as possible. → **Their view**: Narrow the target market and personas, even if that means saying no to real demand — because trying to build for everyone leads to mediocre products.
- **Conventional**: Product teams should keep sales at arm’s length to avoid becoming reactive. → **Their view**: Sales and GTM teams can be one of product’s greatest assets — because they talk to many more customers and surface real problems.
- **Conventional**: Roadmaps should include firm delivery dates far in advance. → **Their view**: Commit only to dates for work within current control, like discovery or solutioning — because early estimates are unreliable.
- **Conventional**: To add enterprise revenue, hire classic enterprise sales reps early. → **Their view**: In early PLG-to-sales transitions, hire for inbound/PQL-driven selling and department-head buyers — because the motion is not yet a mature enterprise motion.
- **Conventional**: Starting with outsourced development is usually a mistake for startups. → **Their view**: It can work, and in Calendly’s case it helped seed the first users — because early distribution can emerge from unexpected channels.
