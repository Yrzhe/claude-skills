# Pete Kazanjy

**Founder, author, and sales leader** | **Atrium** | Expertise: founder-led sales, B2B sales, sales operations, sales management

## Bio
Pete Kazanjy is the author of *Founding Sales* and the founder of Atrium, a data-driven sales management software company. He came from product marketing and product management, learned sales by necessity while founding TalentBin, and later built Modern Sales Pros, a major community for sales operations and leadership professionals.

His perspective matters because he treats sales as a learnable, systemizable craft rather than a mystical talent. He is especially useful for founders and early sales leaders who need to build a repeatable B2B sales motion from scratch.

## Signature Frameworks

### Founder-led sales loop
- **When to use**: When you are a B2B founder trying to find product-market fit and establish a repeatable sales motion before hiring a sales team.
- **Steps**:
  1. Validate the customer problem through direct customer development and research.
  2. Build the minimum viable product to address that problem.
  3. Sell the product yourself to real prospects and ask for money.
  4. Use each customer interaction to refine messaging, objections, and product direction.
  5. Only after the motion is repeatable, package the learnings into scripts, slides, and templates.
  6. Hand the packaged motion to additional reps and scale by adding more sellers.
- **Example**: Pete described TalentBin and Atrium as examples where he had to personally sell early customers, learn what resonated, and only then teach the motion to others.

### Sales motion as an iterative software system
- **When to use**: When you want to improve a sales process rather than treat selling as a one-off art.
- **Steps**:
  1. Run a small cohort of prospects through the motion.
  2. Observe where prospects get stuck or object.
  3. Update collateral, scripts, discovery questions, and demos based on what you learned.
  4. Re-run the improved motion on the next cohort.
  5. Repeat continuously until the motion is reliable and repeatable.
- **Example**: If a prospect asks an objection you cannot answer, Pete recommends creating a slide that handles it and then using that updated material in the next round of conversations.

### Leading-indicator sales management
- **When to use**: When hiring, ramping, or coaching sales reps and you need to know quickly whether they are on track.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the funnel stages for your specific sales motion.
  2. Track leading indicators such as first meetings, second meetings, proposals, and activity volume.
  3. Compare stage conversion rates against expected benchmarks.
  4. Diagnose whether problems are due to targeting, messaging, coaching, or rep behavior.
  5. Make decisions early instead of waiting for lagging revenue outcomes.
- **Example**: Pete gave the example of measuring whether 10 first meetings turn into 7 second meetings and eventually 2-3 wins, rather than waiting months to see only closed revenue.

### ICP and persona targeting
- **When to use**: When deciding who to sell to and how to structure discovery in B2B.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the ideal account characteristics that make a company a good fit.
  2. Define the personas involved in the buying process.
  3. Map which persona is the user, which is the technical evaluator, and which owns budget.
  4. Prioritize opportunities in the white-hot center of the ICP.
  5. Avoid spending time on accounts that do not have the problem or buying capacity.
- **Example**: For Amplitude, Pete described the ICP as software companies with multiple product managers, with product managers as users and VP of Product or CTO as budget owners.

### Ramp reps with tight feedback loops
- **When to use**: When onboarding junior sellers or SDRs in an early-stage company.
- **Steps**:
  1. Sit close to the rep and observe calls frequently.
  2. Give immediate feedback after calls.
  3. Use mock discovery conversations and mock demos.
  4. Have the rep shadow you and then practice the motion.
  5. Monitor leading indicators weekly or monthly to see if the rep is progressing.
- **Example**: Pete argued that sitting side-by-side with junior reps allows corrections to happen immediately, which is much faster than reviewing calls asynchronously once a day.

## Core Advice
- **When you are a founder who thinks sales is beneath you or too scary**: Do the selling yourself early, even if you are uncomfortable, and treat it as a learnable skill — because you will learn what problem you are really solving, how to position it, and what a future salesperson needs to know.
- **When you are deciding whether it is time to hire your first salesperson**: Wait until you can reliably close roughly 15%-25% of qualified first meetings into customers yourself — because that level suggests the motion is repeatable enough to package and transfer.
- **When you are tempted to run a big, unstructured sales experiment**: Run small cohorts of prospects and iterate between cohorts instead of testing everything at once — because small batches reveal which questions, slides, and demos work.
- **When your sales conversations are not progressing**: Track leading indicators like second meetings, third meetings, proposal rate, and activity volume rather than only closed revenue — because lagging revenue data arrives too late.
- **When prospects keep objecting to the same thing**: Turn the objection into a reusable asset by adding a slide, script, or FAQ to your sales motion — because each objection is a signal that your current materials are incomplete.
- **When you are hiring your first sales reps**: Hire a couple of early-stage sellers, not a seasoned VP of Sales first — because early-stage selling requires someone close to the work who can help build the motion.
- **When you are recruiting sales talent for an early-stage startup**: Look for deputies or early sellers from similar companies and similar sales motions — because people who sold a comparable product to a similar persona are more likely to succeed.
- **When a new rep is ramping and you need to know quickly if they will work out**: Use the earliest possible indicators, such as whether they are booking first meetings within a month — because if they cannot generate meetings early, the problem is likely fundamental.
- **When you are building a B2B company and considering a fully self-serve motion**: Do not assume product-led growth eliminates the need for sales; add sales when the business needs larger deals or enterprise expansion — because many PLG companies stagnate until they layer in sales.
- **When you are selling to a user who lacks budget authority**: Use the user to create demand, then sell upward to the budget owner or decision-maker — because the user may love the product but still need help navigating the buying process.
- **When you are trying to improve your own selling ability quickly**: Practice rapid rapport building with strangers and ask direct, even uncomfortable, questions — because sales requires fast trust-building and surfacing real pain.
- **When you are working with junior sellers in an early-stage startup**: Keep them in close physical proximity if possible and coach them in real time — because fast feedback loops accelerate learning.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Sales is a magical talent that only natural-born sellers can do well. → **Their view**: Sales is a learnable set of behaviors; founders and product people can get good at it. — because Pete learned it himself without a sales background.
- **Conventional**: Founders can outsource sales early and let a hired salesperson figure it out. → **Their view**: Founders must do the first sales themselves because they hold the product knowledge and need the feedback loop. — because a third party cannot learn the product and market as deeply as the founder.
- **Conventional**: You should hire a VP of Sales early to professionalize the function. → **Their view**: Early-stage companies should usually hire a couple of frontline sellers first, not a senior sales leader. — because the startup still needs someone building the motion, not managing it from afar.
- **Conventional**: If a product is self-serve or PLG, sales is optional. → **Their view**: Most B2B companies eventually need sales; the question is when, not if. — because enterprise growth and larger contracts usually require human selling.
- **Conventional**: Remote work is equally fine for all sales roles. → **Their view**: Remote work is especially harmful for junior sellers because they need fast coaching and observation. — because early reps learn best through immediate feedback loops.
- **Conventional**: The goal of sales is to persuade anyone to buy. → **Their view**: Good sales is consultative and should focus on people who actually have the problem and fit the solution. — because the seller should help the right buyer recognize a real problem.
