# Scott Belsky

**Chief Strategy Officer, Executive Vice President of Design and Emerging Products at Adobe** | **Adobe** | Expertise: product strategy, product design, consumer products, AI strategy

## Bio
Scott Belsky is a product leader, founder, investor, and author of *The Messy Middle*. He founded Behance, helped shape product at Adobe, and now leads strategy, design, corporate development, and emerging products.

His perspective matters because he combines founder-level product intuition with enterprise-scale experience. He is especially strong on how great products actually get adopted: through ruthless simplification, deep customer empathy, and a practical view of how AI changes product creation.

## Signature Frameworks

### First-mile product design
- **When to use**: When improving activation, onboarding, or early retention for a product.
- **Steps**:
  1. Assume users are lazy, vain, and selfish in the first 30 seconds.
  2. Remove learning curves, tours, and unnecessary friction.
  3. Optimize defaults, orientation, and the path back home/help.
  4. Get users to a quick success moment as fast as possible.
  5. Only after activation, expand into the product's deeper value and mission.
- **Example**: Photoshop’s onboarding had to evolve as the product became cheaper and attracted more pragmatic users with less tolerance for friction.

### Empathy-first product sense
- **When to use**: When trying to understand what to build or why users are struggling.
- **Steps**:
  1. Spend time shoulder to shoulder with customers in their real context.
  2. Observe how the product fits into the rest of their day and environment.
  3. Use that context to interpret data and identify hidden constraints.
  4. Let empathy shape the solution rather than starting from a favored solution.
  5. Build as if you are the customer.
- **Example**: Watching customers navigate meetings, pings, kids, and distractions reveals constraints that interviews and demos miss.

### Reduce to the core crank
- **When to use**: When a product has too many features, too many options, or unclear focus.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the single core action that drives the business.
  2. Remove or defer features that distract from that action.
  3. Replace additions with removals when possible.
  4. Test what happens when nonessential elements are killed.
  5. Keep only what helps users reach the core value faster.
- **Example**: At Behance, killing Tip Exchange and Groups increased project publishing, proving publishing was the core metric.

### Optimize for the problems you want to have
- **When to use**: When deciding what to include in an MVP or early product release.
- **Steps**:
  1. Ship only what removes brick walls to activation and value.
  2. Avoid building features users will ask for only after they are already successful.
  3. Focus on signup, account connection, login, and basic usability.
  4. Defer platform requests, sharing features, and advanced capabilities until they become real problems.
- **Example**: Make sure users can sign up and connect their account, but don’t build later-stage feature requests too early.

### Golden gut for product decisions
- **When to use**: When making micro-decisions in UX, flow design, and simplification.
- **Steps**:
  1. Explore many possible versions of a flow.
  2. Narrow down to a few strong candidates.
  3. Get feedback and refine further.
  4. Use instinct to reduce cognitive load and simplify.
  5. Prefer fewer steps, fewer choices, and presumptive defaults when they speed most users through.
- **Example**: Removing portfolio color controls in Behance and using a presumptive default made the experience cleaner and more focused.

### AI as surface-area expansion
- **When to use**: When using AI for product, design, or creative exploration.
- **Steps**:
  1. Use AI to generate more possibilities, not final answers.
  2. Ask it to explore scenarios, alternatives, and edge cases.
  3. Treat outputs as a way to see what you would not do.
  4. Use the expanded option set to make better human decisions.
  5. Let AI accelerate exploration, not replace judgment.
- **Example**: A Hollywood director uses ChatGPT to generate five story scenarios, not to write the script, just to widen the surface area.

## Core Advice
- **When you face a struggling startup or product and are unsure whether to continue**: Reassess your conviction in the problem and solution with full knowledge of what you've learned; if conviction is gone, quit or pivot — because entrepreneurship requires conviction, and learning should strengthen it, not erode it.
- **When building a new product and deciding what to ship first**: Ship only the minimum needed to remove major friction and let users reach value; defer feature requests that come after success — because you want users to get far enough to ask for more, not get blocked before they care.
- **When your team is tempted to add more features, options, or complexity**: Try removing things first and see whether the core metric improves — because deleting nonessential features often clarifies the product and improves the main behavior.
- **When you want to improve onboarding or activation**: Design for the first 30 seconds as if users are lazy, vain, and selfish — because early users want speed, status, and immediate success, not tours or learning curves.
- **When you need better product intuition**: Spend time with customers in context, not just in interviews or product demos — because the full environment reveals constraints and motivations isolated usage misses.
- **When your product is becoming harder to understand or navigate**: Simplify the object model so users can answer: how did I get here, what do I do now, and what do I do next? — because clear breadcrumbs and orientation are essential for confident movement.
- **When you're building a consumer product and want people to talk about it**: Create surprise, delight, or a small mystery rather than merely meeting expectations — because people talk about what surprises them.
- **When you're working in a resource-constrained environment**: Refactor, automate, and use AI before adding headcount or throwing money at the problem — because resourcefulness builds durable capability.
- **When your team is in the messy middle and morale is low**: Short-circuit the reward system with micro-goals and visibly merchandise progress — because people need proof of progress to endure uncertainty.
- **When you're trying to stay current with AI as a PM or product leader**: Play with the tools constantly and use them for curiosity-driven experiments — because hands-on play is the fastest way to understand what’s possible.
- **When you're a product leader trying to improve cross-functional execution**: Bring design upstream and collapse functional boundaries early — because better intuition and better interfaces come from shared ownership.
- **When you're deciding whether to keep a founder, team, or product effort alive**: Use conviction as the test: if the team still believes more deeply after learning the hard truths, keep going; if not, move on — because conviction is the fuel for surviving the volatile middle.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: A CPO should mainly oversee product, and design/engineering should remain separate functions. → **Their view**: Those boundaries are artificial and often constraining — because the user experience, not org charts, determines whether products win.
- **Conventional**: More features and options make a product more complete and valuable. → **Their view**: You usually only need half of what you think you need — because removing features often improves the core metric.
- **Conventional**: Consumer products need to be specialized, lightweight, and narrowly focused to win. → **Their view**: Modern users can handle more complexity, and big platforms can absorb useful features quickly — because standalone simplicity is no longer the only path to adoption.
- **Conventional**: AI will mainly replace jobs and reduce the need for people. → **Their view**: AI will increase human ambition and expand what teams can build — because productivity gains usually raise expectations, not just reduce headcount.
- **Conventional**: Great products are defined by doing exactly what users expect very well. → **Their view**: People talk about products that surprise them — because unexpected behavior creates conversation and delight.
- **Conventional**: Founders should never quit and should keep pushing through any downturn. → **Their view**: If conviction in the problem and solution has genuinely faded, quitting or pivoting is the right move — because lost conviction means lost fuel.
