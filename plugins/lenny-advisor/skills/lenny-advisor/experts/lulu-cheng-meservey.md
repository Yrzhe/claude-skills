# Lulu Cheng Meservey

**Communications leader / strategic comms advisor** | **Substack (former head of comms); previously Activision Blizzard** | Expertise: communications, PR, messaging, go-to-market, positioning, growth

## Bio
Lulu Cheng Meservey is a sharp, high-leverage communications strategist known for helping underdog companies punch above their weight. Her perspective matters because she treats comms as a business system, not a branding exercise: the goal is not to sound polished, but to make the right people care, believe, and act.

She is especially strong on founder-led communication, audience targeting, and building owned distribution. Her style is direct, memorable, and often contrarian: narrow the audience, speak like a human, and optimize for real business outcomes instead of vanity metrics.

## Signature Frameworks

### Cultural Erogenous Zones
- **When to use**: When you need a message to resonate with a specific audience instead of trying to change what they care about.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify what the audience already cares about or is emotionally activated by.
  2. Accept that some people are not your natural audience and should not be targeted.
  3. Find the bridge between their existing interests and your product, mission, or message.
  4. Shape the message to fit their worldview rather than asking them to adopt yours.
  5. Use a concrete connection that makes them feel, “If I care about X, I should care about Y.”
- **Example**: Connecting K-12 reading standards to national security by pointing out that Army recruits need a 10th-grade reading level.

### Concentric Circles Distribution
- **When to use**: When launching a message, product update, or company narrative and you need to control spread through trusted layers.
- **Steps**:
  1. Get crystal clear on the message yourself.
  2. Share it with the inner circle first: co-founders, executives, employees, investors.
  3. Then move to power users and early believers.
  4. Then expand to adjacent influencers, media, and broader audiences.
  5. Do not skip circles, because each outer circle looks to the inner circle for validation.
- **Example**: Rolling out product updates first to employees and power writers, then to broader writer influencers and media covering writing.

### Comms Math Formula
- **When to use**: When you want communications tied to a real business outcome instead of vanity metrics.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with a business goal, not a communications goal.
  2. Identify the specific people who must take action to achieve that goal.
  3. Define what those people need to believe in order to act.
  4. Map where those people spend time intellectually and which voices they trust.
  5. Deliver the message through those channels with a clear call to action.
- **Example**: For sneaker sales, identify the target buyers, determine they must believe their feet are uncomfortable and the new technology is real, then reach them through the media and accounts they already follow.

### Decrease Surface Area, Increase Pressure
- **When to use**: When you are an underdog startup or have limited time, money, or attention.
- **Steps**:
  1. Choose a narrow audience instead of trying to appeal to everyone.
  2. Sharpen the message to fit that audience precisely.
  3. Concentrate effort on a small number of diehards.
  4. Build deep relationships with those people so they become evangelists.
  5. Expand outward only after dominating the small initial niche.
- **Example**: Focusing on diehard readers and podcast audiences rather than doing a traditional broad book tour.

### Go Direct with a Human Voice
- **When to use**: When a founder or executive needs to build trust, defend the company, or explain something uniquely well.
- **Steps**:
  1. Have the founder or senior leader speak in first person.
  2. Use an authentic human voice rather than corporate language.
  3. Show vulnerability and mistakes where appropriate.
  4. Build a direct audience before you need it in a crisis.
  5. Use the direct channel alongside, not necessarily instead of, media.
- **Example**: A founder staying a person publicly rather than becoming a generic corporate chief, which makes people interested in the company through them.

## Core Advice
- **When you want an idea to spread**: Make it memorable and give people a reason to say it voluntarily — because people share things that help them look interesting, express identity, make others laugh, or bring joy.
- **When you are crafting a message**: Turn it into a joke, analogy, or vivid image — because repeatable phrasing and mental imagery are far stickier than abstract explanation.
- **When your draft sounds clever only to you**: Rewrite it so a second-grader could understand it — because reducing cognitive burden makes the message instantly graspable and shareable.
- **When you are tempted to use a poetic or obscure reference**: Avoid inside jokes and esoteric metaphors unless the audience will immediately get them — because a dead spot in the message breaks momentum.
- **When you are deciding whether to take a comms risk**: Prefer mistakes of commission over mistakes of omission — because visible mistakes can be learned from quickly, while doing nothing lets the status quo win.
- **When you are an underdog startup**: Build your own distribution from day one — because you usually won’t have the resources or relationships to win on incumbents’ terms.
- **When you have too many possible messages or audiences**: Pick one to three core facts and rank your audiences by influence — because internal knowledge creates dilution unless you force focus.
- **When you are launching content or a direct channel from zero**: Prepare a backlog and post consistently from the start — because early momentum and cadence help audience formation.
- **When you are choosing a platform for founder-led communication**: Pick the medium that matches the founder’s natural style — because authenticity and fit matter more than forcing a bad channel.
- **When your company is small and niche**: Focus on a tiny corner of the market and dominate it before expanding — because a small, well-defined niche is easier to own.
- **When you want product attention from the right people**: Give the product away for free to people who are both likely to love it and influential — because the right early users become powerful messengers.
- **When you are deciding where to spend comms effort**: Spend time, money, and credibility on the smallest set of people who matter most — because focused effort creates more pressure and better ROI.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Good messaging should try to persuade everyone and broaden appeal as much as possible. → **Their view**: Narrow the audience and target the people who already care — because trying to appeal to everyone waters the message down.
- **Conventional**: Comms should be cautious, polished, and low-risk. → **Their view**: Startups should take more risks and prefer mistakes of commission — because doing nothing lets the status quo win.
- **Conventional**: Corporate communications should sound like a serious institution. → **Their view**: Founders and executives should speak as humans in first person — because people trust people, not faceless corporations.
- **Conventional**: To grow attention, be active on every major platform. → **Their view**: Pick one channel that fits the spokesperson and go deep — because spreading effort across too many channels leads to mediocrity.
- **Conventional**: Viral growth is the goal. → **Their view**: Viral spikes are often low-value if they attract the wrong audience — because numbers without fit don’t create real business value.
- **Conventional**: Traditional media should be the primary way to get attention. → **Their view**: Direct channels and owned distribution matter more for many startups — because you can’t rely on institutions to carry your story.
