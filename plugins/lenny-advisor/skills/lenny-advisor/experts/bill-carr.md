# Bill Carr

**Co-author of *Working Backwards*; founder of Working Backwards, LLC** | **Working Backwards, LLC** | Expertise: leadership, product-management, metrics, operations

## Bio
Bill Carr spent 15 years at Amazon, where he helped lead major businesses including digital media, Amazon Music, Prime Video, and Amazon Studios. He is best known for codifying Amazon’s operating mechanisms—especially working backwards, single-threaded leadership, input/output metrics, and Bar Raiser hiring—into a practical system for building and scaling companies.

His perspective matters because he translates Amazon’s often-misunderstood operating model into repeatable management tools. If you need a disciplined way to choose products, organize teams, hire well, or manage by metrics, Carr offers a highly structured, execution-oriented point of view.

## Signature Frameworks

### Working Backwards / PR-FAQ
- **When to use**: When deciding what new product, feature, or initiative to build, especially in growing companies that need a repeatable product development process.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with the customer, not internal constraints like revenue targets, org structure, or current technical limitations.
  2. Write an internal press release that clearly defines who the customer is, what problem they have, and what solution you will deliver.
  3. Use the FAQ to flesh out assumptions, details, tradeoffs, and likely objections.
  4. Iterate through concentric-circle reviews: author first, then small group, then wider stakeholders, then senior leadership as needed.
  5. Use the process as a funnel to compare many ideas and select only the strongest ones for the roadmap.
- **Example**: A team drafting a PR/FAQ for a new streaming feature writes three crisp PR paragraphs: who the customer is, what pain they have, and how the feature solves it. Weak ideas get dropped early, before they ever reach executives.

### Single-Threaded Leadership
- **When to use**: When a company becomes complex and cross-functional resource contention slows execution, especially around engineering, design, and product resources.
- **Steps**:
  1. Create a team with one clear leader accountable for a specific program or domain.
  2. Dedicate the necessary cross-functional resources to that team, either through direct or dotted-line reporting.
  3. Shift from project orientation to program orientation so the team continuously owns an area rather than swarming temporarily on projects.
  4. Align the team’s plan with senior leadership through rigorous review upfront.
  5. Let the team run autonomously against its roadmap and metrics after alignment.
  6. Add countermeasures to preserve functional excellence, such as centralized standards, promotion panels, and expert reviews.
- **Example**: Instead of a temporary project team to improve search, create a permanent search team that owns search quality, page load time, and click-through on top results.

### Input vs. Output Metrics
- **When to use**: When teams are over-fixated on lagging business outcomes like revenue and need a better way to drive long-term performance.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the key outputs you care about, such as revenue, active customers, or free cash flow.
  2. Map the end-to-end customer experience to find controllable drivers of those outputs.
  3. Define measurable input metrics tied to customer experience and operational levers.
  4. Review plans and business performance primarily through those input metrics.
  5. Continuously refine the metrics as you learn which ones are truly causal.
- **Example**: Rather than scrambling with promotions at quarter-end to hit revenue, a team tracks inputs like selection, low prices, speed, and customer experience quality, trusting outputs to follow.

### Disagree and Commit
- **When to use**: When an important decision is being made and team members have differing views that need to be surfaced without causing endless conflict.
- **Steps**:
  1. If you disagree, voice your perspective and provide new information, data, or a different point of view.
  2. Escalate if necessary until you are confident your view has been heard and understood.
  3. Once the decision-maker demonstrates they understand your reasoning and has weighed it, stop re-litigating the issue.
  4. Commit fully to the chosen direction by understanding the core logic behind it and helping execute it.
- **Example**: If you disagree with a leader’s call, you explain your reasoning clearly, then after the decision is made you look for the “kernel” of what they saw and help make the plan work.

### Bar Raiser Hiring Process
- **When to use**: When scaling hiring quickly and trying to maintain quality, culture fit, and consistent standards across many interviewers and managers.
- **Steps**:
  1. Assign one trained Bar Raiser to each interview loop who is independent of the hiring manager.
  2. Use objective criteria for evaluation, such as leadership principles and behavioral interviewing.
  3. Have the Bar Raiser run the debrief meeting to ensure the process is followed and evidence is used consistently.
  4. Let the hiring manager remain the final decision-maker, while the Bar Raiser acts as a quality control and anti-urgency counterweight.
  5. Pilot the process in one department first with people who care deeply about hiring and have high standards.
- **Example**: Amazon created Bar Raisers because “new people [were] hiring new people hiring new people,” which risked diluting standards during hypergrowth.

## Core Advice
- **When you face pressure to decide what product or feature to build next**: Do a PR/FAQ-style writeup that crisply defines the customer, the problem, and the solution before committing resources — because it exposes weak ideas early and makes comparison systematic.
- **When you face constant cross-functional resource fights and slow execution**: Shift toward single-threaded teams with one accountable leader and dedicated resources — because ownership and autonomy reduce bottlenecks.
- **When you face late-quarter panic because revenue is behind plan**: Focus on controllable input metrics instead of last-minute promotional scrambling — because inputs compound while fire drills usually just pull demand forward.
- **When you face disagreement with a leader’s decision**: Disagree clearly and with evidence, then commit once your perspective has been heard and understood — because decision quality improves without sacrificing execution speed.
- **When you face the challenge of preserving functional excellence inside autonomous cross-functional teams**: Create countermeasures like centralized standards, promotion criteria, expert review panels, and cross-team mentorship — because autonomy without craft guardrails can degrade quality.
- **When you face a desire to encourage innovation but employees fear being punished for failed bets**: Align compensation and performance systems around long-term company outcomes and quality of contribution, not short-term bonuses tied to immediate business results — because people take smarter risks when failure on a worthwhile initiative is not automatically punished.
- **When you face a need to improve hiring quality during rapid growth**: Pilot a Bar Raiser-style process in one function with trained interviewers independent from the hiring manager — because it reduces urgency bias and preserves standards.
- **When you face the temptation to summarize performance with one blended score**: Avoid compound metrics and manage the important drivers separately — because blended metrics hide causality.
- **When you face implementation of a new operating process**: Commit fully for several months rather than dipping your toes in halfway — because these systems are hard at first and often fail before teams become fluent.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Manage the business primarily by watching revenue and other top-line outcomes. → **Their view**: Focus primarily on customer-centric input metrics and treat outputs as consequences. — because revenue is lagging and often drives reactive behavior.
- **Conventional**: Cross-functional collaboration and centralized planning are the best way to allocate scarce resources. → **Their view**: Autonomous single-threaded teams with dedicated resources often work better. — because centralized planning creates bureaucracy and slows execution.
- **Conventional**: If people disagree with a decision, they should keep pushing until the decision changes or else silently comply. → **Their view**: Disagree vigorously up front, then stop re-litigating and commit once understood. — because the point of disagreement is better information, not endless conflict.
- **Conventional**: To move fast, teams should skip documentation and just start building. → **Their view**: Slow down up front with rigorous writing and thinking. — because clarity prevents wasted effort later.
- **Conventional**: If a product fails, the team probably just executed poorly. → **Their view**: Many failures come from solving no meaningful customer problem in the first place. — because starting with technology instead of customer need is a common trap.
