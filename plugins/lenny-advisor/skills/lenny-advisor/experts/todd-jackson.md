## Signature Frameworks

### Four Levels of Product-Market Fit
- **When to use**: Use when you need to diagnose where your B2B startup is on the path to product-market fit and what to optimize next.
- **Steps**:
  1. **Level 1: Nascent PMF** — find 3 to 5 customers with an important, urgent problem and deliver a satisfying solution.
  2. **Level 2: Developing PMF** — move from 5 to 25 satisfied customers by making demand more repeatable and scalable.
  3. **Level 3: Strong PMF** — grow from 25 to 100+ customers while improving efficiency and maintaining satisfaction and demand.
  4. **Level 4: Extreme PMF** — sustain demand, satisfaction, and efficiency at scale, then expand TAM with new products or markets.
- **Example**: Vanta started at level 1 by manually solving SOC 2/compliance pain for a few design partners, then expanded into a broader trust management platform at higher levels.

### The Four Ps Pivot Framework
- **When to use**: Use when growth stalls or customers are not responding strongly enough and you need to decide what to change.
- **Steps**:
  1. **Persona**: change who you are targeting.
  2. **Problem**: change the pain point you are solving.
  3. **Promise**: change how you position or describe the value.
  4. **Product**: change the actual solution or implementation.
- **Example**: Lattice kept the same persona (HR leaders) but changed the problem, promise, and product when it pivoted from OKR software to performance management.

### Dollar-Driven Customer Discovery
- **When to use**: Use in early-stage B2B when you want to validate not just interest, but real willingness to pay.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify extreme value by asking about the customer's top goals and pain points without leading them.
  2. Test whether the customer already has a budget, is buying a competing tool, or is building an internal solution.
  3. Understand the decision process for third-party tools and who approves spend.
  4. Probe willingness to pay with concrete price questions such as fair, expensive, and prohibitively expensive price points.
  5. Look for behavioral signals of interest, such as asking for a follow-up or wanting to show the idea to teammates.
- **Example**: Todd asked Lenny what his top goals were for the newsletter and podcast, then pitched a hypothetical service; Lenny said he would pay a lot for it, which signaled strong value.

### Marginal Customer Test
- **When to use**: Use to assess whether product-market fit is strengthening over time.
- **Steps**:
  1. Track how hard it is to acquire the next customer.
  2. Track how hard it is to satisfy the next customer.
  3. Expect the marginal customer to become easier to win and serve as PMF improves.
- **Example**: At level 3, Todd said the marginal customer should feel like they are coming more naturally through referrals, inbound, or a repeatable channel.

### Design Partner to Paying Customer Path
- **When to use**: Use when you are pre-product or early product and need to turn a few high-signal customers into revenue.
- **Steps**:
  1. Find a small number of high-pain design partners.
  2. Manually solve the problem if needed to prove value.
  3. Use the early solution to unlock a concrete business outcome for the customer.
  4. Convert the design partner into a paying customer once the value is clear.
- **Example**: Vanta manually filled out compliance spreadsheets for early customers like Segment, Front, and Figma, helping them land enterprise deals and become paying customers.

## Core Advice
- **When you are pre-seed or seed stage and still searching for the right problem**: Focus on finding 3 to 5 customers with an important, urgent problem and deliver a solution that makes them deeply satisfied — because early satisfaction matters more than efficiency.
- **When you are early and unsure whether customers truly need your product**: Ask whether they would be disappointed if your product disappeared and whether a cheaper competitor would make them switch — because this reveals whether you are in the “friend zone” or a necessity.
- **When you are trying to validate a startup idea before building too much**: Sell the idea before you build it whenever possible — because customer signal should guide what you build.
- **When you have a few happy customers but growth is not becoming repeatable**: Change the persona, problem, promise, or product rather than just pushing harder on the same approach — because stagnation usually means one of the four Ps is misaligned.
- **When customers say your product is “interesting” but do not take action**: Treat “interesting” as a polite no and look for stronger signals like a follow-up request, a deck share, or a willingness to meet again — because real interest shows up in behavior, not compliments.
- **When you are doing customer discovery**: Ask about goals, pain points, budgets, and current workarounds instead of pitching your solution too early — because customers are better at describing problems than predicting what they will buy.
- **When you are unsure whether a problem is worth solving**: Look for evidence that the customer already spends money on a competing tool or is trying to build the solution internally — because existing spend or failed internal attempts are strong indicators of budget and urgency.
- **When you are at level 2 and trying to move from 5 to 25 customers**: Build a scalable demand source such as cold outreach, content, community, or events while preserving customer satisfaction — because founder grit alone is not enough.
- **When your sales cycle is long and your ACV is low**: Reconsider the market or positioning — because slow sales plus low ACV is a dangerous economics quadrant.
- **When you are at level 3 and growth is slowing**: Find a new scalable channel or improve efficiency rather than assuming the current channel will keep working — because channels saturate and competition rises.
- **When you are tempted to keep a weak product alive for too long**: Be willing to make a real pivot or even return the money if you have spent years without market pull — because a breakthrough becomes unlikely after years of no traction.
- **When you are choosing what to optimize first in the earliest stage**: Spend disproportionate time on picking the market, persona, and problem, not just building — because the initial choice sets the constraints for years.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Product-market fit is a binary state: you either have it or you don't. → **Their view**: PMF is a progression through distinct levels, and each level has different goals and metrics — because nascent, developing, strong, and extreme PMF require different trade-offs across satisfaction, demand, and efficiency.
- **Conventional**: Efficiency can wait until later; early startups should only focus on growth and customer love. → **Their view**: Efficiency is part of the definition of true PMF, and ignoring it can create fake traction that does not scale — because demand and satisfaction alone are not enough.
- **Conventional**: Founders should create a new category to win. → **Their view**: It is often easier to win by entering an existing category and expanding its definition — because buyers already understand the budget and the problem.
- **Conventional**: Customer interviews are enough to validate demand. → **Their view**: You need dollar-driven discovery that tests willingness to pay, budget, and decision process — because customers are polite and often bad at predicting behavior.
- **Conventional**: If a startup is making revenue, it must be progressing toward PMF. → **Their view**: A company can have meaningful revenue and still be stuck at level 1 or 2 — because repeatability and efficiency matter as much as revenue.
- **Conventional**: You should keep pushing the same product harder if growth stalls. → **Their view**: A meaningful pivot across the four Ps is often the right move, and a 10% tweak is usually not enough — because founders often need a much bigger change than they expect.
