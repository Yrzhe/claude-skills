# Karri Saarinen

**Co-founder and CEO** | **Linear** | Expertise: design, product-management, leadership, go-to-market

## Bio
Karri Saarinen is the co-founder and CEO of Linear, the fast-growing issue tracking and project management tool for software teams. Before Linear, he was a founding designer at Coinbase and a principal designer at Airbnb, which shaped his belief that great products come from taste, focus, and strong opinions—not endless customization.

His perspective matters because he bridges design excellence, product strategy, and startup execution. He’s especially useful when you need to build a product people love in a crowded category, or when your team needs sharper focus and better judgment.

## Signature Frameworks

### The Linear Method
- **When to use**: When building modern software products that need strong focus, high quality, and clear product opinions rather than excessive process or customization.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start from a clear understanding of the customer problem through direct user contact and research.
  2. Design opinionated defaults instead of maximizing flexibility for every edge case.
  3. Move into building quickly rather than over-polishing designs upfront.
  4. Ship early internally and to small opt-in customer groups to learn in production.
  5. Polish heavily before general release.
  6. Use team judgment and taste to make decisions, supported by selective data rather than driven by metrics targets.
- **Example**: Linear ships new features to internal users first, then to a few design-partner customers like Vercel, and only later polishes them for broad release.

### Opinionated Software
- **When to use**: When building productivity or company software where the goal is to reduce user decision fatigue and help teams work effectively.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the most effective default workflow for the target user.
  2. Avoid designing for everyone, which leads to generic and overly flexible products.
  3. Encode strong product opinions into the workflow and UI.
  4. Let users focus on their actual work instead of configuring the tool.
- **Example**: Productivity software should provide good defaults so teams do not spend time figuring out 10 different ways to use the same feature.

### Cycles
- **When to use**: When teams face an infinite backlog and need a lightweight way to preserve focus over short time windows.
- **Steps**:
  1. Choose a fixed time window such as one or two weeks.
  2. Commit to a small set of priorities for that cycle.
  3. Ignore the rest of the backlog during the cycle unless something urgent changes.
  4. If priorities change, use the original cycle plan as the baseline for explaining trade-offs.
  5. Automate the cadence so the team does not manually reset the process each time.
- **Example**: Cycles are like sprints, but less performative—meant simply to help teams focus on a few things instead of the infinite list.

### Cohort-Based Beta Rollout
- **When to use**: When launching an early B2B product and you want high-signal feedback without overwhelming the team with duplicate issues.
- **Steps**:
  1. Collect a waitlist with qualifying information such as company size, current tools, and motivation.
  2. Invite a small cohort of users that best match the current product capabilities.
  3. Gather feedback and identify the biggest blockers or bugs.
  4. Fix those issues before inviting the next cohort.
  5. Repeat until the product is stable enough for broader release.
- **Example**: Linear invited only around 10 people a week at first from its waitlist, fixed the issues that cohort found, and then invited the next group.

### Main Quest vs. Side Quests
- **When to use**: When a startup is tempted by many worthwhile ideas but needs to preserve focus on what actually advances the business.
- **Steps**:
  1. Ask whether the task is important now or can wait until later.
  2. Determine whether it directly helps customers or improves the core product.
  3. Label non-essential but attractive work as a side quest.
  4. Say no to side quests until the main quest is meaningfully advanced.
- **Example**: Delaying SOC 2 until it was truly needed, or rejecting distractions like making T-shirts if they did not help customers or the product.

### Paid Work Trial Hiring
- **When to use**: When hiring for a startup that values ownership, judgment, and real-world collaboration more than interview performance alone.
- **Steps**:
  1. Run a standard interview loop for baseline fit and skills.
  2. Bring finalists in as paid mini-contractors for a short trial.
  3. Give them a vague but realistic problem to scope and solve.
  4. Let them work with the actual team, tools, and codebase.
  5. Evaluate how they think, communicate, make progress, and handle ambiguity.
  6. Let the candidate also assess the company and working style.
- **Example**: Engineering candidates at Linear get access to the codebase, build a scoped feature, and present their work at the end of the trial.

## Core Advice
- **When you face pressure to ship polished features quickly**: Do early internal and limited-customer releases first, then polish before general availability — because you learn faster in the real product while preserving a high-quality public experience.
- **When you face a crowded market with many similar products**: Do invest heavily in design quality and brand from the beginning — because in mature categories, users choose what feels most trustworthy, polished, and differentiated.
- **When you face endless incoming requests and backlog churn**: Do work in short cycles with a fixed set of priorities — because cycles create focus, reduce context switching, and make trade-offs explicit.
- **When you face uncertainty about what to build next**: Do talk to customers directly and make that exposure a company-wide habit — because shared customer understanding improves judgment and reduces dependence on metrics as a substitute for thinking.
- **When you face the temptation to add flexibility everywhere**: Do choose strong defaults and build opinionated workflows — because too much flexibility pushes complexity onto users.
- **When you face hiring decisions for a small, high-talent startup**: Do hire for breadth of judgment, not just narrow functional skill — because small teams need people who can take broader scope and overlap across functions.
- **When you face difficulty evaluating candidates through interviews alone**: Do use a paid work trial with a realistic, ambiguous problem — because it reveals how candidates actually operate and collaborate.
- **When you face questions about whether you have product-market fit**: Do evaluate fit by customer segment and double down where pull is strongest — because PMF is segment-specific and often appears first in a narrow wedge.
- **When you face many attractive opportunities as a founder**: Do ask whether each task advances the main quest or is just a side quest — because focus on the few highest-leverage actions compounds faster.
- **When you face pressure to move fast on every decision**: Do slow down enough to think clearly before acting — because thoughtful decisions often save time by avoiding rework.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Great craft means polishing extensively before anyone sees the feature. → **Their view**: Craft should be combined with very early shipping to internal users and select customers — because you understand the right solution better once it exists in the product.
- **Conventional**: Product teams need PMs deeply embedded in every project and decision. → **Their view**: A small number of PMs can work if engineers and designers own more product thinking — because the builders notice opportunities in the details.
- **Conventional**: The best product orgs are highly metrics-driven and constantly A/B test. → **Their view**: Judgment, taste, and customer understanding can be better guides — because complex B2B problems are often about solving the customer problem well, not moving a narrow metric.
- **Conventional**: Flexible software is better because every team can adapt it. → **Their view**: Productivity software should be opinionated and provide strong defaults — because flexibility creates configuration work and decision fatigue.
- **Conventional**: Fast-growing startups should hire aggressively to scale output. → **Their view**: Smaller teams of unusually strong people often build faster and better — because coordination overhead rises quickly with headcount.
- **Conventional**: Product-market fit is binary. → **Their view**: PMF is segment-specific and exists on a spectrum — because a product can fit one wedge deeply before it fits the broader market.
