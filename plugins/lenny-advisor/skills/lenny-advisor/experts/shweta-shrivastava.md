# Shweta Shrivastava

**Senior Director of Product Management** | **Waymo** | Expertise: autonomous vehicles, product management, AI systems, commercial scaling

## Bio
Shweta Shrivastava leads product across some of the hardest problems in technology: onboard autonomous driving software, simulation and validation tooling, and the commercialization of Waymo’s ride-hailing business. Her perspective matters because she operates at the intersection of safety-critical AI, real-world operations, and customer trust—where product decisions have to be both technically rigorous and commercially viable.

She previously held product leadership roles at Nauto, Amazon Web Services, and Cisco. That background gives her a rare blend of deep technical fluency, customer-centric product thinking, and long-horizon execution discipline.

## Signature Frameworks

### Work backwards from the customer problem
- **When to use**: When building any product, especially in complex or highly technical environments where it is easy to optimize for technology instead of user value.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with the user or customer problem, not the technology.
  2. Define who the product is for and what outcome it should create.
  3. Write the launch narrative or press release before building to clarify the value proposition.
  4. Use that narrative to sharpen prioritization and scope.
- **Example**: At Amazon, PMs write a press release for the finished product before building it; Shweta said Waymo uses its own version of this principle.

### Build trust through a holistic experience
- **When to use**: When designing products that users must trust quickly, especially in safety-critical or unfamiliar experiences.
- **Steps**:
  1. Make the system feel credible, predictable, and natural.
  2. Expose transparency cues so users understand what the system is doing.
  3. Provide human fallback/support when needed.
  4. Tune behavior based on real user expectations, not just technical correctness.
  5. Combine many small details rather than relying on one trust-building feature.
- **Example**: Waymo shows riders what the car sees on a monitor, calls riders if they are not wearing a seatbelt, and adjusted downhill behavior in San Francisco because riders expected the car to slow down more naturally.

### Measure autonomous systems with dual lenses: commercial and system behavior
- **When to use**: When operating a product that is both a real business and a complex technical system.
- **Steps**:
  1. Track commercial metrics such as trips, active users, funnel conversion, and operating cost.
  2. Track system behavior metrics such as safety, road-rule compliance, progress, and rescue interventions.
  3. Benchmark performance against human behavior where relevant.
  4. Balance safety with forward progress so the system is not overly cautious.
- **Example**: Waymo measures collisions per 100,000 miles against human-driver benchmarks and also tracks stops, strands, and how often the vehicle needs rescue help.

### Disrupt yourself before someone else does
- **When to use**: When working in a large company or market leader that risks becoming incremental and complacent.
- **Steps**:
  1. Question whether the current product strategy is becoming too safe or too incremental.
  2. Create new offerings that challenge the company’s existing model.
  3. Invest in transformational bets before a competitor forces the change.
  4. Use internal innovation to avoid external disruption.
- **Example**: Shweta led Honey Code at Amazon, a no-code application development platform in a space Amazon had not previously entered.

### Earn promotion through impact, not promotion-chasing
- **When to use**: When trying to grow in your career or get promoted.
- **Steps**:
  1. Focus on the highest-impact work for the business.
  2. Make your ambition known to your manager.
  3. Avoid optimizing your behavior for the promotion itself.
  4. Let visible business impact create the promotion signal.
- **Example**: She advised people to tell leaders they want stretch work, but not maneuver too much for title advancement.

## Core Advice
- **When a complex product problem seems too easy or everyone agrees too quickly**: proactively challenge assumptions and invite contention — because lack of pushback often means the real issues are still hidden.
- **When building in a highly technical domain**: go deep enough technically to understand system details and tradeoffs — because PMs in complex products need more technical nuance than in typical software.
- **When launching a safety-critical product**: set a much higher MVP bar and do not cut corners on safety — because the cost of failure is fundamentally different.
- **When users are hesitant to trust a new system**: design for credibility, predictability, and transparency from day one — because trust is built through consistent behavior and visibility.
- **When a product is technically correct but feels unnatural**: tune behavior to match human expectations and social norms — because users judge systems by how they feel, not only by correctness.
- **When measuring progress in a real-world product**: track both business metrics and system-quality metrics — because commercial success and technical quality can diverge.
- **When evaluating autonomous or AI performance**: benchmark against human behavior, not just internal targets — because the real question is whether the system is better than the status quo.
- **When a system is too cautious**: optimize for adequate forward motion, not just safety — because a safe product that doesn’t move is not useful.
- **When trying to keep leadership committed to a long-term bet**: show meaningful progress, especially in real commercial deployment — because momentum sustains confidence.
- **When tempted to build for every customer request**: be crisp about what you are not building — because focus is what creates a coherent product direction.
- **When working across teams with different incentives**: listen first, empathize, and understand constraints before pushing your view — because influence without authority depends on alignment.
- **When discussion is stuck in email**: move to a live call or in-person huddle after a few back-and-forths — because ambiguity resolves faster in real time.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: MVPs should be launched quickly and iterated on, even if imperfect. → **Their view**: In autonomous driving, MVP still exists, but the safety bar is dramatically higher and you cannot cut corners — because safety is the product.
- **Conventional**: The goal of self-driving is full Level 5 autonomy everywhere. → **Their view**: Level 4 autonomy in real commercial environments may solve most important use cases — because useful value can be delivered without universal autonomy.
- **Conventional**: The best autonomous system should simply maximize safety and obey rules rigidly. → **Their view**: The system also has to feel natural and socially aware — because driving is social and context-dependent.
- **Conventional**: Career growth comes from aggressively optimizing for promotion. → **Their view**: The best way to get promoted is to focus on impact and not want the promotion too badly — because business results are the real signal.
- **Conventional**: Large companies should mostly protect their market position and iterate incrementally. → **Their view**: Market leaders need to disrupt themselves before a startup does it for them — because complacency creates openings for upstarts.
