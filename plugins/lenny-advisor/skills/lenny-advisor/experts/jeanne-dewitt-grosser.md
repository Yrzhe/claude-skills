# Jeanne DeWitt Grosser

**COO** | **Vercel** | Expertise: go-to-market, sales strategy, product-led growth, AI-enabled revenue operations

## Bio
Jeanne DeWitt Grosser is the COO of Vercel, where she runs marketing, sales, customer success, revenue operations, and field engineering. She previously helped build Stripe’s early sales organization and held leadership roles at Google and Stripe, giving her a rare perspective on how to design world-class, product-centric B2B go-to-market systems.

Her viewpoint matters because she treats GTM as an operating system, not a set of disconnected functions. She is especially valuable when a company needs to scale revenue without losing product quality, customer trust, or operational rigor.

## Signature Frameworks

### Go-to-Market as a Product
- **When to use**: When redesigning the customer journey in markets where product differentiation is narrowing and experience becomes a competitive advantage.
- **Steps**:
  1. Map the full lifecycle from awareness to expansion and retention.
  2. Identify every touchpoint across marketing, sales, support, success, and partnerships.
  3. Design each touchpoint intentionally, not transactionally.
  4. Add value at every interaction, even before a purchase.
  5. Iterate using customer feedback and observed friction.
- **Example**: At Stripe, the first qualified sales call became a collaborative whiteboarding session where the customer drew their payments architecture. Stripe learned the stack and pain points, while the customer left with a useful artifact.

### GTM Engineer Workflow Automation
- **When to use**: When a sales or revenue workflow is repeatable enough to encode into AI-assisted processes.
- **Steps**:
  1. Shadow the best performer in the function.
  2. Document tools, tabs, decisions, and heuristics.
  3. Separate deterministic from non-deterministic tasks.
  4. Build an agent for the parts AI can do well.
  5. Keep a human in the loop for QA at first.
  6. Measure against the same KPIs as human performance.
- **Example**: At Vercel, one GTM engineer spent 6 weeks building an inbound lead agent, reducing the SDR workflow from 10 SDRs to 1 human reviewer while keeping lead-to-opportunity conversion flat.

### Segmentation Graph
- **When to use**: When deciding where to focus sales and marketing resources or building a repeatable GTM motion.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with company size.
  2. Add a second dimension tied to economic value.
  3. Layer in product-relevant attributes like business model or workload type.
  4. Use segments to prioritize accounts and tailor messaging.
  5. Revisit based on revenue and win-rate data.
- **Example**: At Stripe, Jeanne segmented by size and growth potential, then by business model such as B2B, B2C, platform, or marketplace. At Vercel, the team uses size, growth potential, traffic rank, and workload type.

### AI-Powered Deal Review Loop
- **When to use**: When improving win rates, diagnosing losses, or enabling a GTM team quickly.
- **Steps**:
  1. Aggregate calls, emails, Slack, and CRM data.
  2. Run an agent over lost deals to find root causes.
  3. Feed insights into product, marketing, and sales leadership.
  4. Move from retrospective analysis to real-time coaching.
  5. Fix recurring issues in weekly sprints.
- **Example**: A deal marked “lost on price” was actually lost because the team never engaged the economic buyer and failed to articulate ROI. The insight changed how Vercel quantified value.

### Founder-to-First-Sales Handoff
- **When to use**: When transitioning from founder-led sales to the first salesperson.
- **Steps**:
  1. Stay founder-led until there is repeatability, often around $1M ARR.
  2. Confirm customers are similar enough to define an ICP.
  3. Document what works: discovery, objections, content, process.
  4. Hire only once the motion is teachable.
  5. Stay close to customers after the handoff.
- **Example**: If the first million in ARR comes from wildly different customer types, Jeanne would not call that a repeatable sales motion yet.

## Core Advice
- **When you need to improve GTM efficiency without hiring a large team**: audit your best reps’ workflows and build AI agents around the most repeatable tasks — because the easiest automation wins are structured and measurable.
- **When inbound leads are piling up and response times are slow**: use AI-assisted qualification and response drafting with human QA — because speed boosts conversion and AI handles scale well.
- **When deal losses are unclear**: analyze calls, emails, and notes with an agent instead of relying on rep-reported reasons — because reps often misdiagnose the real blocker.
- **When competitors’ products look similar**: invest more in the buying experience — because experience becomes a stronger differentiator as technical gaps narrow.
- **When founder-led sales feels hard to delegate**: codify ICP, discovery, objections, and process before hiring — because people can only scale what is teachable.
- **When reps pitch too quickly**: slow down and ask more questions, including five whys — because strong discovery reveals pain, power, and risk.
- **When you’re unsure which customers to target**: segment by 2–3 variables that change buying behavior and value — because segmentation sharpens prioritization and messaging.
- **When PLG is working but growth is flattening**: start building a sales motion before the ceiling hits — because enterprise and outbound motions take time to mature.
- **When pricing feels off**: treat pricing like a product and revisit packaging — because pricing shapes both customer experience and monetization.
- **When sales and engineering are misaligned**: train salespeople to think like product managers — because product depth turns sales into a source of real product signal.
- **When hiring a first GTM engineer**: look for someone with GTM context and technical ability — because the role needs both workflow intuition and software-building skill.
- **When outbound is weak**: improve account data and personalization — because context-aware outreach performs better than generic prospecting.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Go-to-market mostly means sales and marketing. → **Their view**: GTM is the full customer lifecycle across every function that touches revenue — because fragmented ownership creates inconsistent experiences.
- **Conventional**: Sales automation should be bought from vendors. → **Their view**: many high-value GTM agents should be built internally — because company-specific workflows are too unique for generic tools.
- **Conventional**: PLG can be the long-term growth engine on its own. → **Their view**: PLG usually has a ceiling and needs sales to unlock full scale — because large deals rarely happen self-serve.
- **Conventional**: Great salespeople are pure closers. → **Their view**: top GTM teams should think like product managers and R&D — because customer conversations are a source of product insight.
- **Conventional**: Customers buy mainly for upside. → **Their view**: customers often buy to avoid pain and reduce risk — because enterprise buying is frequently a career-risk decision.
- **Conventional**: Annual comp plans are always good behavior-shaping tools. → **Their view**: rigid comp plans can reduce flexibility — because fast-changing markets require faster pivots.
- **Conventional**: SDR work is a necessary apprenticeship. → **Their view**: AI should eliminate much of repetitive SDR work — because humans should move sooner into higher-value selling.
