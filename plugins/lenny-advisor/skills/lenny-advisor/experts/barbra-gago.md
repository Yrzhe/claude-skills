## Signature Frameworks

### Category Creation Decision Framework
- **When to use**: When deciding whether to create a new category or compete within an existing one.
- **Steps**:
  1. Assess the size and ambition of the market you want to serve, and whether the current category is too narrow.
  2. Check whether buyers already have budget allocated for the problem area.
  3. Listen to how customers describe their pain points and the language they naturally use.
  4. Evaluate whether your product is meaningfully differentiated from existing categories.
  5. Consider whether competition exists or is likely to emerge, since a category is only truly validated when multiple companies occupy it.
  6. If the new category language does not stick, fall back to the existing category and elevate its value instead.
- **Example**: At Miro, “online whiteboard” felt too small for the company’s ambition, so they developed “visual collaboration” as a broader category. At Greenhouse, they tried “recruiting optimization platform” but abandoned it because buyers still budgeted for and described the product as an ATS.

### Category Creation Execution Playbook
- **When to use**: When you have decided to seed and build a new category in the market.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with extensive customer conversations to understand pain points, use cases, and language.
  2. Define the unique differentiators and value propositions that justify a new category.
  3. Test the positioning through PR and external messaging to see whether it resonates.
  4. Work with analysts and directory sites like Gartner, Forrester, and G2 to get the category recognized.
  5. Invest heavily in thought leadership and content marketing to educate buyers.
  6. Teach the market not only what the category is, but why it deserves budget allocation.
- **Example**: For Miro’s “visual collaboration” category, the team met with G2 to explain how the category differed from adjacent ones and used content marketing to educate buyers on why this new category mattered.

### Middle-Ground Positioning Strategy
- **When to use**: When your product fits an existing budget line today but you want to shift the market toward a new category over time.
- **Steps**:
  1. Sell into the existing category buyers already understand and budget for.
  2. Use the established category in directory sites and procurement contexts.
  3. Introduce a new concept in parallel through messaging and thought leadership.
  4. Gradually educate the market on why the new framing better captures the product’s value.
  5. Increase investment in the new category only as traction and resonance grow.
- **Example**: At Pando, Barbra plans to sell into the established performance management category while simultaneously introducing the longer-term concept of “employee progression.”

### Rebrand as Product Process
- **When to use**: When a company has product-market fit and the current name or brand no longer matches the scale of its ambition.
- **Steps**:
  1. Determine whether the current name and brand can support the company’s future scale.
  2. Rebrand early enough that brand equity is still manageable to transition.
  3. Preserve some recognizable elements from the old brand to maintain continuity.
  4. Run the rebrand cross-functionally like a product development effort with sprint teams and clear owners.
  5. Involve customers and internal stakeholders through feedback loops.
  6. Coordinate legal, domain, product, website, sales collateral, and launch execution tightly.
- **Example**: Miro’s rebrand from RealtimeBoard preserved the yellow visual cue from the original Post-it logo, involved customer feedback, and was executed in a compressed sprint-based process leading up to a South by Southwest launch.

### Values-to-Brand System
- **When to use**: When building a brand that feels authentic, scalable, and consistent across customer touchpoints.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define a small set of memorable company values.
  2. Translate those values into visual and verbal brand elements such as shapes, colors, language, and behavior.
  3. Ensure the brand system can scale across product, marketing, and company communications.
  4. Embed values into how employees behave so the brand shows up naturally in every interaction.
  5. Use the brand to create emotional connection, not just recognition.
- **Example**: At Miro, each letterform and shape in the brand system represented different characteristics tied to company values, including agility, helping the brand express the company’s identity visually.

### Opinionated Software Design Framework
- **When to use**: When software should enforce best practices rather than merely offer flexible tooling.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify a process that either lacks good software support or is commonly done poorly.
  2. Define the principles or best practices that should govern that process.
  3. Build workflows, rules, and constraints into the product to reinforce those practices.
  4. Use the product to teach customers a better way of working.
  5. Accept reduced flexibility when it leads to better outcomes and less bias or inconsistency.
- **Example**: Greenhouse enforced structured recruiting workflows to reduce bias and improve transparency. Pando is being built to require more transparency around levels, expectations, and accountability in employee progression.

## Core Advice
- **When deciding whether to create a new category**: Do customer research first and validate whether buyers have budget, use the language naturally, and see your product as meaningfully different from existing categories — because category creation only works when it matches real buyer behavior and market structure.
- **When a category has bad reputation but strong buyer awareness**: Do not force a new category if customers still buy through the old one; instead elevate the value of the existing category — because if the market already has budget and vocabulary for the old category, reframing may fail.
- **When your product spans many use cases and adjacent categories**: Do keep optimizing for existing category entry points while introducing a broader umbrella category in parallel — because this captures current demand without giving up the chance to shape a larger market narrative over time.
- **When launching a new category**: Do invest in PR, analyst relations, directory-site classification, thought leadership, and content marketing — because a category only becomes real when external market actors recognize it and buyers are educated enough to budget for it.
- **When a company has outgrown its name**: Do rebrand after product-market fit but before the old brand accumulates too much equity and operational complexity — because early rebrands are easier to execute and less risky.
- **When running a major rebrand**: Do run it like a product launch with sprint teams, clear owners, cross-functional coordination, and stakeholder feedback — because rebrands touch legal, product, sales, web, events, and customer perception.
- **When building a startup brand**: Do treat brand as a core company system—logo, colors, voice, values, and behavior—not as a cosmetic afterthought — because a strong brand compounds when it is authentic and consistent.
- **When defining company values**: Do use a structured workshop: brain dump ideas, group them into themes, vote on priorities, then have a strong writer draft the final wording — because this balances broad input with clarity.
- **When flexibility creates poor outcomes or bias**: Do build opinionated software that enforces best practices and transparency — because constraints can improve consistency, fairness, and quality.
- **When company values are long or vague**: Do simplify them so they are specific but easy to recall — because values only shape behavior if employees can remember and use them.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: A category is strongest when you have no competitors and own it alone. → **Their view**: A category is not really a category until other companies also occupy it — because competition validates that the market exists.
- **Conventional**: If your current category has a bad reputation, you should rename the category and escape it. → **Their view**: Sometimes you should stay in the existing category and raise its value instead of inventing a new one — because buyers already understand and budget for it.
- **Conventional**: Category creation is a clean binary choice: either you are in an existing category or you create a new one. → **Their view**: You can sell into an existing category while gradually building a new one in parallel — because this captures current demand while shaping future perception.
- **Conventional**: Good software should maximize flexibility and let customers configure everything. → **Their view**: In some domains, software should be opinionated and enforce a better way of working — because constraints can improve outcomes.
- **Conventional**: Brand is mostly a marketing layer added after the product is built. → **Their view**: Brand should be considered as early as possible and built as deeply as the product itself — because product and brand together drive acquisition and loyalty.
