# Asha Sharma

**Corporate Vice President of Product for Microsoft’s AI Platform** | **Microsoft** | Expertise: ai-strategy, product-management, go-to-market, leadership

## Bio
Asha Sharma leads Microsoft’s AI platform across infrastructure, foundation models, agent toolchains, applied engineering, responsible AI, and growth. She brings a rare operator’s view of AI from both product and company-building roles, including COO at Instacart and product leadership at Meta.

Her perspective matters because she thinks about AI not as a feature layer, but as a full system: models, data, workflows, org design, and business outcomes. She is especially useful when teams need to turn AI hype into durable product advantage.

## Signature Frameworks

### Product as Organism
- **When to use**: When building AI-native products that improve through interaction data, post-training, and continuous optimization rather than static feature shipping.
- **Steps**:
  1. Treat the product as a living system rather than a shipped artifact.
  2. Continuously ingest user and system interaction data.
  3. Design reward models around desired outcomes such as price, performance, or quality.
  4. Run multiple optimization tracks in parallel, including synthetic data generation, fine-tuning, and A/B testing.
  5. Use the resulting feedback loops as the core product moat and IP.
- **Example**: Microsoft’s Dragon medical product improved from roughly 30–60% character acceptance to about 83% after expert annotation of 600,000 patient-physician interactions and continuous optimization.

### AI Adoption Maturity Path
- **When to use**: When rolling out AI across an enterprise and trying to move from experimentation to measurable business impact.
- **Steps**:
  1. Make everyone AI fluent by embedding copilots or AI tools into daily workflows.
  2. Apply AI to improve an existing process, such as support, fraud, or operations.
  3. Measure the impact and connect it to P&L or intrinsic business value.
  4. Use proven wins to drive growth, retention, customer experience, or entirely new categories.
- **Example**: A company starts by using AI in day-to-day work, then improves customer support or fraud resolution time, and only then uses AI to inflect growth.

### Build for the Slope, Not the Snapshot
- **When to use**: When planning in fast-moving technology environments where tools, models, and capabilities change too quickly for fixed long-term assumptions.
- **Steps**:
  1. Avoid overcommitting to a single tool or vendor in a rapidly changing stack.
  2. Choose a platform or app-server layer that lets you swap technologies in and out.
  3. Plan around directional change and capability trajectories rather than current point-in-time constraints.
  4. Leave slack in the system for disruption and emerging opportunities.
- **Example**: With tens of thousands of enterprise AI tools launching in a year, the right move is to avoid being beholden to any one technology.

### Seasons-Based Planning
- **When to use**: When roadmap planning is unstable because the underlying AI landscape changes every few months.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the current “season” based on major secular shifts in technology and customer needs.
  2. Align the organization on the ethos of that season, including customer problems, what winning looks like, and the north star metric.
  3. Set loose quarterly OKRs that support the season.
  4. Have squads operate on shorter 4–6 week goals.
  5. Preserve slack for unplanned changes and strategic disruption.
- **Example**: Earlier seasons were early AI prototyping and then models/reasoning models; the current season is the rise of agents.

### Loop, Not Lane
- **When to use**: When building AI products requires tighter integration across product, engineering, design, evaluation, and operations.
- **Steps**:
  1. Optimize for the full feedback loop rather than narrow functional ownership.
  2. Blur traditional role boundaries so builders understand system efficiency, reward design, UI/UX, and agent behavior.
  3. Make observability and continuous feedback part of the culture.
  4. Use cross-functional iteration to improve throughput and product quality.
- **Example**: In AI-native organizations, PM, engineering, and design increasingly converge because speed depends on mastering the loop end-to-end.

### Maximize Option Value
- **When to use**: For personal and career decisions when long-term compounding matters more than minimizing short-term regret.
- **Steps**:
  1. Prioritize choices that increase future flexibility and compounding returns.
  2. Invest in health, family, trust, relationships, and skills as long-term assets.
  3. Avoid trading away foundational assets for short-term output.
  4. Evaluate life by future adventures and capability, not just past regrets.
- **Example**: After starting a family, she shifted from a “minimize regret” mindset to maximizing option value.

## Core Advice
- **When you face pressure to “do AI” across the company**: Do not launch many disconnected AI projects at once; first make the organization AI fluent, then improve one real process, then scale into growth use cases — because this creates understanding, proof of value, and a repeatable blueprint.
- **When you face a rapidly changing AI tooling landscape**: Build on a flexible platform layer that lets you swap models and tools in and out — because the ecosystem changes too quickly to lock into one architecture.
- **When you face the challenge of building differentiated AI products**: Invest in post-training loops using your own data, synthetic data, reward design, and rigorous A/B testing — because foundation models are commoditizing and advantage comes from adaptation.
- **When you face slow product development caused by too many handoffs**: Create more full-stack builders and organize around end-to-end loops rather than narrow functional lanes — because AI product development moves too fast for heavy handoff chains.
- **When you face roadmap uncertainty because model capabilities keep changing**: Plan in seasons, set loose quarterly OKRs, and run teams on 4–6 week goals with slack built in — because this preserves alignment while staying adaptable.
- **When you face the temptation to overfocus on UI in AI products**: Spend more time on composability, agent readability, and how systems interact through text and code-native interfaces — because leverage often comes from programmability, not just polish.
- **When you face the need to improve model quality in a specialized domain**: Use high-quality expert-labeled domain data rather than relying only on synthetic fine-tuning — because expert annotation can materially improve performance in high-stakes workflows.
- **When you face scaling work through agents**: Invest early in observability, evals, self-healing systems, and review mechanisms — because the bottleneck shifts from execution to monitoring and quality control.
- **When you face platform prioritization decisions**: Prioritize invisible fundamentals like reliability, privacy, safety, performance, and data residency before adding many features — because trust and consistency drive durable adoption.
- **When you face choosing between one model and many**: Use a model system or ensemble approach, selecting different models for different tasks and constraints — because different models optimize for different tradeoffs.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Products are mostly static artifacts: define features, ship them, and monitor dashboards. → **Their view**: AI products should be treated as organisms that think, learn, and improve continuously through feedback loops — because the moat is in optimization, not just shipping.
- **Conventional**: The main value in AI comes from pre-training ever-larger foundation models. → **Their view**: Post-training will become as important as, and eventually more important than, pre-training — because product value comes from adapting models to specific outcomes.
- **Conventional**: Great product building depends on clear functional specialization and org-chart-defined ownership. → **Their view**: The future belongs to full-stack polymaths organized around loops, not lanes — because AI compresses workflows and punishes handoffs.
- **Conventional**: Software interfaces will remain primarily GUI-driven. → **Their view**: Many important AI products will shift toward code-native, composable interfaces that work better with LLMs and agents — because text and code compose more naturally with models.
- **Conventional**: Org charts are the enduring structure for coordinating work. → **Their view**: As agents proliferate, the org chart starts to become the work chart — because task routing matters more than hierarchy.
- **Conventional**: Platform success is driven mainly by visible features. → **Their view**: Invisible infrastructure qualities often matter more than feature count — because reliability, privacy, and controls drive trust and retention.
