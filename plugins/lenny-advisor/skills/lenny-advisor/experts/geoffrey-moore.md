# Geoffrey Moore

**Author, advisor, and go-to-market strategist** | **Independent / Crossing the Chasm framework** | Expertise: go-to-market, strategy, positioning, product-market-fit

## Bio
Geoffrey Moore is the author of *Crossing the Chasm*, one of the most influential books ever written on technology go-to-market strategy. His work matters because it explains why many promising products stall after early traction—and what to do differently to reach mainstream buyers.

He is especially valuable when you need to understand how disruptive products move from visionary early adopters to pragmatic enterprise customers. His frameworks are widely used for segment selection, positioning, and choosing the right sales motion for each stage of market adoption.

## Signature Frameworks

### Technology Adoption Lifecycle
- **When to use**: Use when deciding how to sell and scale a disruptive technology based on the market’s stage of adoption.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify whether you are selling to technology enthusiasts and visionaries in the early market.
  2. Recognize the chasm between visionaries and pragmatists, where references stop transferring.
  3. Use the bowling alley playbook to win pragmatist customers in a narrow beachhead segment.
  4. Shift to the tornado playbook when budgets become broadly established and the category goes horizontal.
  5. Operate on Main Street when the product is commoditized and service/expansion becomes the main innovation lever.
- **Example**: Before the chasm, customers say, “We believe what you believe.” After the chasm, pragmatists say, “We need what you have.”

### Beachhead Segment Selection
- **When to use**: Use when choosing the first market segment to target after winning an initial marquee customer.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with a segment that is big enough to matter.
  2. Ensure it is small enough to lead.
  3. Make sure it is a strong fit with your company’s crown jewels.
  4. Define the segment narrowly by same geography, same industry, same profession, and a compelling use case.
  5. Aim for a fish-to-pond ratio where you could realistically reach 30–50% share in that segment within a couple of years.
- **Example**: Instead of targeting “the Fortune 500,” choose a narrow segment where several peer companies can all choose you and establish you as the standard.

### Bowling Alley Strategy
- **When to use**: Use after the early market, when you need to cross the chasm by winning pragmatist customers one adjacent segment at a time.
- **Steps**:
  1. Pick one narrow beachhead segment with a compelling reason to buy.
  2. Overcommit to solving that segment’s problem with a complete solution.
  3. Win multiple reputable customers in that segment so peers can reference one another.
  4. Expand only to adjacent segments.
  5. Use either the same customer with a new use case or the same use case in a new customer base to drive adjacency.
- **Example**: Documentum started in pharmaceuticals for FDA drug approval documents, then expanded to chemicals, petrochemicals, oil and gas leases, and eventually Wall Street.

### Compelling Reason to Buy
- **When to use**: Use when selling to pragmatists who will only adopt if the pain is severe enough to overcome inertia and risk aversion.
- **Steps**:
  1. Lead with the customer’s problem, not your product demo.
  2. Diagnose the pain with probing questions.
  3. Confirm the problem is urgent, worsening, or costly enough to force action.
  4. Position your offering as a commitment to remove that problem, not just as interesting technology.
  5. Avoid pitching until the customer has fully articulated their own pain.
- **Example**: Moore compares this to a doctor visit: the patient does not want a demo of the operation; they want the doctor to understand the pain in their side.

### Four Go-to-Market Playbooks
- **When to use**: Use when aligning your sales motion to the current market phase so you do not apply the wrong operating model.
- **Steps**:
  1. Early market: sell the disruptive vision to a visionary executive sponsor using a project model.
  2. Bowling alley: sell a complete solution to a pragmatist with a compelling reason to buy.
  3. Tornado: scale broad sales coverage and standardize the product to capture market share.
  4. Main Street: focus on land-and-expand, service innovation, and low-risk adoption in a mature market.
- **Example**: Qualifying on existing budget is smart in tornado/Main Street but a mistake in early market and bowling alley, where budget must be created or redirected.

### Marquee Customer / Radiating Reference
- **When to use**: Use in the early market to establish credibility before attempting to cross the chasm.
- **Steps**:
  1. Find a visionary executive sponsor at a recognizable company.
  2. Deliver extraordinary support to make the project successful.
  3. Ensure the customer is willing to talk publicly or semi-publicly about the win.
  4. Use that reference to become visible in the market.
  5. Then separately identify a pragmatist beachhead segment for crossing the chasm.
- **Example**: AWS being used by the CIA is the kind of lighthouse customer that puts a company on the map.

## Core Advice
- **When you face pressure to sell to anyone who will buy during the chasm**: stay narrowly focused on one beachhead segment and hold your effort in one place until references compound — because pragmatists buy what their peers buy.
- **When you face uncertainty about whether your ICP is too broad**: define the segment by same geography, same industry, same profession, and one compelling use case — because references transfer best inside a real peer group.
- **When you face the challenge of getting your startup noticed early on**: win a marquee customer with a visionary executive sponsor who will talk about the project — because recognizable proof creates credibility you cannot manufacture.
- **When you face pragmatist buyers in sales conversations**: do not start with a demo; start by diagnosing their problem — because they care about risk reduction, not your vision.
- **When you face a decision about whether to discount to win early customers**: use value pricing instead of discounting — because price cuts do not solve perceived risk and can weaken commitment.
- **When you face pressure to scale before the product is stable**: keep doing project-style work with visionaries until the core product is reliable enough for pragmatists — because pragmatists will not tolerate instability.
- **When you face the question of whether to raise venture capital**: bootstrap to cash-flow break-even if possible — because crossing the chasm can be done narrowly, and funding is only necessary when the market window or capital intensity demands it.
- **When you face the need to validate a beachhead market hypothesis**: run a focused campaign for one suspected use case and try to win two more similar deals quickly — because repeatability is the real test.
- **When you face hiring for chasm-crossing sales**: hire someone more like a sales engineer than a classic enterprise salesperson — because the job is diagnostic and domain-specific.
- **When you face product-led growth success and assume it will carry you into the enterprise**: add a sales motion when risk-bearing enterprise decisions appear — because PLG alone does not solve enterprise risk.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: If growth is slow, broaden your target market and take any customer you can get. → **Their view**: Narrow your focus even more and dominate a tiny beachhead first — because concentrated wins create the references and ecosystem support needed to cross the chasm.
- **Conventional**: A great product demo and compelling pitch are the key to winning customers. → **Their view**: For pragmatists, shut the laptop and diagnose first — because they buy to reduce risk and solve urgent pain.
- **Conventional**: Discounting helps startups win early customers. → **Their view**: Discounting is a mistake when crossing the chasm — because the barrier is perceived risk, not price.
- **Conventional**: Once you have a famous early adopter, you can use that customer as your beachhead to reach the mainstream. → **Their view**: Your marquee visionary customer usually is not your beachhead market — because visionaries are atypical and not credible peer references.
- **Conventional**: Crossing the Chasm applies broadly to all startups, including consumer apps. → **Their view**: The framework is primarily optimized for B2B markets with federated, high-risk buying decisions — because enterprise adoption is reference-driven.
- **Conventional**: Product-led growth can carry a company through every stage of adoption. → **Their view**: PLG is powerful for low-risk land-and-expand, but it does not by itself cross the enterprise chasm — because enterprise purchases require sponsorship and risk reduction.
