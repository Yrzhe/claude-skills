# Lauren Ipsen

**Executive recruiter / talent advisor** | **General Catalyst (consumer and crypto portfolio); formerly Daversa Partners** | Expertise: executive search, product leadership hiring, talent strategy, relationship-based recruiting

## Bio
Lauren Ipsen is a veteran executive recruiter who has placed roughly 85 executives across tech, with especially deep experience hiring product, engineering, and growth leaders. Her perspective matters because she combines external market knowledge with hands-on startup talent work, so she understands both how to assess senior candidates and how hiring decisions should change with company stage.

She started in broadcast, moved into executive search at Daversa Partners, later built talent in-house at a startup, and now advises General Catalyst portfolio founders while staying active in the market. That mix makes her especially useful when a company needs to hire a senior leader without over-indexing on brand names or generic recruiting advice.

## Signature Frameworks

### Hire for the role, stage, and mandate — not the biggest name
- **When to use**: When hiring a first or early senior product leader and you need the right fit for the company’s current stage.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the concrete outcome the hire must deliver.
  2. Clarify the mandate for the next 90 days, 12 months, and 2 years.
  3. Decide what the role should major and minor in: design, core product, platform, growth, monetization, or vision.
  4. Map the org chart around the hire so you know what they own and what they should not own.
  5. Choose the candidate whose current strengths match the company’s immediate needs, even if they are not the most famous person in the market.
- **Example**: Instead of hiring a former Google or YouTube CPO to “own product,” hire someone closer to the work who can operate like a senior PM while also building the leadership layer the company needs now.

### Market mapping before the search
- **When to use**: When you know you will hire later and want to build a strong candidate pipeline in advance.
- **Steps**:
  1. Keep a constant pulse on which companies are thriving.
  2. Identify the people who are well known, well referenced, and doing great work.
  3. Talk to benchmark candidates before you have an opening.
  4. Use those conversations to calibrate what good looks like.
  5. Keep warm relationships through advising, product involvement, or occasional check-ins.
- **Example**: Lauren described a seven-month courtship for a VP of engineering where the candidate first advised on org design and talent before eventually joining.

### Relationship-first recruiting
- **When to use**: When sourcing, networking, or engaging candidates for executive roles.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with genuine curiosity and no immediate ask.
  2. Find common ground and build rapport.
  3. Listen carefully and remember candidate preferences and life events.
  4. Follow up at the right time based on what they told you.
  5. Only then introduce relevant opportunities.
- **Example**: If a candidate says they do not want crypto or are waiting for a vest date, Lauren notes it and reaches out later with context instead of pushing immediately.

### Back-channel validation over resume reading
- **When to use**: When evaluating senior candidates whose resumes may not reveal the full truth.
- **Steps**:
  1. Use provided references as a baseline, not the final answer.
  2. Ask hard questions about weaknesses and reasons not to hire.
  3. Ask whether the reference would hire or report to the candidate again.
  4. Compare provided references with back-channel references.
  5. Look for consistency across multiple people who worked closely with the candidate.
- **Example**: Lauren said she prefers asking, “Why would I not hire this person?” and “Would you report to this person?” to surface real signal.

### Career breadth as a leadership accelerator
- **When to use**: When a PM or product leader wants to grow into a broader leadership role.
- **Steps**:
  1. Rotate across different product surfaces or functions.
  2. Take on both entrepreneurial and more established environments over time.
  3. Build experience across platform, core product, UX, growth, or monetization.
  4. Collect evidence of impact that others can speak to.
  5. Avoid becoming only a specialist if the goal is product leadership breadth.
- **Example**: Lauren suggested that someone aiming for head of product should have touched multiple components rather than only one narrow specialty.

## Core Advice
- **When you face an early-stage product leadership hire**: Do not default to the biggest brand-name executive; hire the person closest to the work who can both execute and lead — because big-company leaders are often too far from the tactical work and may not match the company’s immediate needs.
- **When you are kicking off a search for a senior product leader**: Write down exactly what this person will do in the first 30, 90, and 365 days — because specificity clarifies whether you need a PM operator, a design-heavy leader, a vision setter, or a team builder.
- **When you are unsure whether you need a head of product at all**: Work backward from the business problem and the org chart before opening the role — because many searches start from a board directive rather than a real company need.
- **When you are deciding between product archetypes**: Choose the bucket that matches the immediate gap: platform, core/consumer product, or specialist (growth/monetization/design) — because these are materially different jobs.
- **When you are hiring for a role that may evolve quickly**: Use the title that matches the stage, and keep it simple early on — because startups are title-agnostic and overpromising a C-level title can create problems later.
- **When you know you will need a senior hire in the future**: Keep a live map of the market and maintain warm relationships with standout people now — because the best hires often come from long-term relationship building, not last-minute sourcing.
- **When you are trying to recruit someone you admire**: Reach out with no agenda and ask to learn from them rather than immediately pitching a job — because people are more open when they feel respected and not poached.
- **When you are spending time on hiring**: Spend your time on high-signal networking and referrals, not blind LinkedIn outreach — because the best candidates are often not active on LinkedIn, and trusted introductions produce better signal.
- **When you are evaluating a candidate with short stints**: Look for patterns, but let the candidate explain the context and verify it through references — because short tenures can be legitimate in volatile markets.
- **When you are interviewing for a product role**: Do not blame other functions for your misses; tell a balanced story with ownership and specifics — because strong candidates show accountability and cross-functional maturity.
- **When you are building your product career**: Seek breadth across products, teams, and company stages instead of collecting logos — because breadth makes you a better product leader and gives you more options later.
- **When you are considering a move for title or prestige**: Do not leave a role you like just for a shinier title or logo — because impact, learning, and growth matter more than prestige.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Hire the most impressive executive you can get, especially if they come from a famous company. → **Their view**: Hire the person best suited to this specific role at this specific stage, even if they are less famous — because early-stage companies often need operators closer to the work.
- **Conventional**: A head of product, VP of product, and CPO are meaningfully different and should be treated as distinct career milestones. → **Their view**: At many startups, those titles mostly mean the same thing relative to stage — because breadth and depth vary more by company stage and org design than by title alone.
- **Conventional**: More sourcing time and more LinkedIn outreach will produce better hiring outcomes. → **Their view**: Quality of relationships and market knowledge matter far more than raw hours or volume outreach — because trusted referrals outperform cold volume.
- **Conventional**: A polished resume and provided references are enough to evaluate senior talent. → **Their view**: Back-channel references and real-world reputation are much more reliable — because provided references are curated.
- **Conventional**: Short tenures are automatically bad. → **Their view**: Short stints can be completely rational in volatile markets if the context is honest and the candidate can explain the impact — because roles change, companies pivot, and layoffs happen.
- **Conventional**: Career success comes from optimizing for the best title or the most prestigious logo. → **Their view**: Optimizing for impact, learning, and fulfillment is usually better than chasing prestige — because title collecting can leave people with a strong resume but little real growth.
