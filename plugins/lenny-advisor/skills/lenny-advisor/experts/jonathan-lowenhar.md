# Jonathan Lowenhar

**Co-founder of Enjoy The Work** | **Enjoy The Work** | Expertise: leadership, hiring-culture, go-to-market, strategy, operations

## Bio
Jonathan Lowenhar helps founders make the hardest transition in startups: becoming effective CEOs. His perspective matters because he translates messy founder problems—hiring, planning, sales, company building, and acquisitions—into practical frameworks that are easy to use and hard to ignore.

He is especially useful for founders who are past the invention stage and need to build a real company around what they’ve created. His lens is blunt, operational, and unusually focused on the difference between startup instinct and executive craft.

## Signature Frameworks

### Founder vs. CEO
- **When to use**: When a founder is struggling to scale themselves as the company grows beyond the invention stage.
- **Steps**:
  1. Recognize that being a founder is a state of being: grit, courage, instinct, and tenacity.
  2. Recognize that being a CEO is a craft: hiring, planning, selling, financial management, organizing people, and capital strategy.
  3. Audit the gap between the CEO you are and the CEO your company needs.
  4. Deliberately build the missing skills instead of treating operational leadership as beneath the founder role.
  5. Learn to calibrate when to go deep as a founder and when to operate from the cockpit as a CEO.
- **Example**: A founder who excels at product intuition but avoids planning and people management may still be a great founder, but they will become the bottleneck unless they learn CEO craft.

### Two Phases of Startup Building
- **When to use**: When founders over-focus on product creation and underinvest in company building.
- **Steps**:
  1. Phase 1: Build something people want to buy.
  2. Phase 2: Build a company around that thing people want to buy.
  3. Shift attention from invention to repeatability, demand generation, operations, and cash flow.
  4. Treat company building as a learnable discipline, not an accidental byproduct of product success.
- **Example**: A startup may have strong product-market fit, but without systems for sales, hiring, and cash management, it cannot scale into a durable business.

### CEO Failure Modes
- **When to use**: When diagnosing recurring leadership problems in a founder-CEO.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the dominant pattern: robot, pleaser, perfectionist, angry, laissez-faire, micromanager, ready-fire-aim, brake-rider, or accelerator-rider.
  2. Name the behavior clearly so it becomes discussable.
  3. Trace the business consequences of that behavior.
  4. Install a counter-practice, such as planning, accountability rituals, emotional awareness, or delegation boundaries.
  5. Reassess over time since most CEOs are a mix of multiple failure modes.
- **Example**: The “ready, fire, aim” CEO keeps making bets without enough planning, which can work early but becomes dangerous at scale.

### Working Backwards from the Real Goal
- **When to use**: When planning company priorities, hiring, or resource allocation.
- **Steps**:
  1. Choose the actual top-of-mountain outcome: exit, next fundraise, profitability, or winding down.
  2. Define what must be true qualitatively and quantitatively to reach that outcome.
  3. Translate those requirements into actions, cadence, owners, and resources.
  4. Create accountability rituals and short feedback loops.
  5. Use the plan to drive hiring and execution rather than reacting ad hoc.
- **Example**: For fundraising, define what must be true to unlock the next round, then build the milestones and operating cadence around that target.

### Magic Box Paradigm
- **When to use**: When an early-stage startup wants to maximize acquisition outcomes without running a traditional “for sale” process.
- **Steps**:
  1. Learn the fantasy: identify a specific champion at a potential acquirer and understand the future they imagine if they buy you.
  2. Prove the fantasy: create lightweight evidence that your product or technology can make that imagined future real.
  3. Quantify the fantasy: build a forward-looking model of how the acquirer’s business changes if the acquisition happens.
  4. Navigate the stakeholder map: champion, advocates, blockers, buyer, and sometimes Corp Dev.
  5. Avoid acting “for sale”; instead, entice and preserve momentum while protecting the fantasy.
- **Example**: Instagram’s sale to Facebook is a classic Magic Box case: the price was justified by a future business model, not historical revenue.

### Magic Box Stakeholder Map
- **When to use**: When managing an acquisition conversation inside a larger company.
- **Steps**:
  1. Find the champion who has fallen in love with the idea and will fight for you internally.
  2. Identify advocates who may not take political risk but can provide useful intel.
  3. Anticipate blockers such as legal, procurement, or security who can stop the deal.
  4. Understand the buyer, usually a committee or executive group, that cares about business-case math.
  5. Use Corp Dev for process momentum, but avoid negotiating live with them.
- **Example**: In a buyer’s product org, a champion may see how your startup’s technology could transform their core product and become your internal advocate.

### Hiring by Outcomes, Not Job Descriptions
- **When to use**: When hiring for any role, especially in startups where every hire must materially change the business.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start 12 months in the future and define what success would look like if the hire were amazing.
  2. Document the business changes you expect from the role.
  3. Look for candidates who have already done that kind of work before.
  4. Check whether they have a history of being “pulled” into new roles by former colleagues or managers.
  5. Evaluate whether they embody the company’s core values through a dedicated culture interview.
- **Example**: Instead of writing a generic sales leader job description, define the business outcomes you want 12 months from now and hire for that change.

### Architect / Optimizer / Scaler
- **When to use**: When hiring senior functional leaders and deciding what kind of executive the company needs next.
- **Steps**:
  1. Determine whether the function needs an architect, optimizer, or scaler.
  2. Architect: create the first playbook from founder intuition.
  3. Optimizer: improve performance and efficiency once the playbook exists.
  4. Scaler: create leverage and expand the function dramatically.
  5. Match candidates to the actual stage need rather than the title on the org chart.
- **Example**: In sales, an architect codifies the founder’s early sales magic; an optimizer improves it for a team; a scaler builds leverage for much larger growth.

### Four-Part Go-to-Market Framework
- **When to use**: When founders need to turn ad hoc selling into a repeatable go-to-market motion.
- **Steps**:
  1. Ideal Customer Profile: define who you really want to sell to, their qualifications, discovery questions, and kill criteria.
  2. Marketing and Positioning: define your uncommon denominator versus competitors or the status quo.
  3. Demand Generation: choose channels and run experiments to find the right customers.
  4. Sales: codify the playbook for discovery, objections, demos, and closing.
- **Example**: A founder who sells to anyone interested will waste time and money; a tight ICP creates focus and better learning.

### Brain Writing
- **When to use**: When a founder wants better ideation and less distortion from hierarchy in meetings.
- **Steps**:
  1. State the problem or prompt clearly.
  2. Have participants write ideas independently and asynchronously before discussion.
  3. Hide authorship if possible to reduce status effects.
  4. Let everyone review the combined input first.
  5. Only then discuss and debate as a group.
- **Example**: Instead of live brainstorming, team members submit ideas in writing first so the founder’s presence doesn’t dominate the room.

## Core Advice
- **When you face the transition from founder to CEO**: do an honest skills audit and deliberately learn the craft of CEO work — because scaling requires hiring, planning, financial management, and people leadership, not just founder instinct.
- **When you face chaotic execution**: work backward from one real company goal — because a concrete destination makes priorities, resources, and accountability clearer.
- **When you face a possible acquisition opportunity**: don’t put up a for-sale sign; find a champion and build their fantasy — because early-stage exits are often driven by future vision, not historical metrics.
- **When you face Corp Dev negotiations**: move the conversation async and ask for terms in writing — because live negotiation favors the buyer’s specialists.
- **When you face a hiring decision**: define success 12 months out before writing the role — because companies hire for business change, not titles.
- **When you face a candidate who looks impressive on paper**: check whether they were repeatedly pulled into new roles by former colleagues — because that’s a strong signal of trust and performance.
- **When you face the need to hire a senior leader**: decide whether you need an architect, optimizer, or scaler — because stage fit matters more than seniority.
- **When you face weak go-to-market traction**: tighten your ICP and define explicit kill criteria — because chasing every interested prospect destroys focus.
- **When you face a brainstorming session**: use brain writing instead — because hierarchy distorts live ideation.
- **When you face a major founder decision**: get quiet enough to hear your intuition — because fear often masks what you already know.
- **When you face annual revenue planning**: build the plan from actual funnel reality upward — because back-solving from a target creates fantasy math.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Founder mode is a superior alternative to management. → **Their view**: Founders must learn the craft of CEO work — because scaling a company requires skills beyond invention.
- **Conventional**: To sell a startup, run a standard sale process and tell buyers you’re open. → **Their view**: The best exits happen when you are never explicitly for sale — because seduction and internal championing create better leverage.
- **Conventional**: Hiring starts with a job description. → **Their view**: Hiring should start with 12-month success outcomes — because the business needs change, not titles.
- **Conventional**: Brainstorming in a room is the best way to generate ideas. → **Their view**: Brain writing is better — because founder presence and hierarchy suppress honest input.
- **Conventional**: Start revenue planning from the target and make the funnel work. → **Their view**: Start from actual funnel performance — because realistic planning exposes the true gap.
