# Ami Vora

**Chief Product Officer** | **Faire** | Expertise: product-management, go-to-market, leadership, design

## Bio
Ami Vora is the Chief Product Officer at Faire, where she helps shape product strategy for a B2B marketplace connecting independent retailers and brands. She previously spent about 15 years at Facebook/Meta, leading major product efforts including the Facebook developer platform, Facebook Ads, Instagram ads, and product/design for WhatsApp.

Her perspective matters because she combines deep product craft with executive-level operating experience across consumer, platform, and marketplace businesses. She is especially strong on how to make good decisions in large organizations without turning leadership into a bottleneck.

## Signature Frameworks

### Curiosity-over-ego disagreement framework
- **When to use**: When you strongly disagree with someone in a meeting or decision discussion.
- **Steps**:
  1. Notice the visceral reaction and pause before responding.
  2. Reframe disagreement as a signal that the other person has information you do not.
  3. Ask with genuine curiosity why they think that way.
  4. Optimize for the best outcome, not for being personally right.
  5. Reinforce the habit by noticing better outcomes and stronger relationships.
- **Example**: Instead of pushing back defensively, say: “Fascinating, you have to tell me more why you think that.”

### Product review: recommendation + principles, not information dump
- **When to use**: When preparing or running product reviews with executives or cross-functional leaders.
- **Steps**:
  1. Clarify the problem the review is meant to solve and the timeline.
  2. Bring only the information needed to support a clear recommendation.
  3. Assume leaders have limited bandwidth and cannot absorb every detail.
  4. Use the review to align on principles, trade-offs, and decision criteria.
  5. Leave with reusable principles so teams can decide faster next time.
- **Example**: A team presents one recommendation, a few key facts, and the trade-offs, rather than a 40-slide dump of every option.

### My manager owns context, I own the recommendation
- **When to use**: When working with senior leaders and deciding how to split decision-making responsibilities.
- **Steps**:
  1. Do the deep analysis yourself.
  2. Form a recommendation and stand behind it.
  3. Ask your manager for broader context and pattern matching.
  4. Treat the manager’s role as complementary, not superior.
  5. Use that context to sharpen your recommendation.
- **Example**: A product lead brings a clear proposal to a VP, who adds company-level constraints and historical patterns.

### Emotion-first product metaphor design
- **When to use**: When trying to create a coherent product feel across teams.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the feeling you want users to have.
  2. Identify a real-life moment that creates that feeling.
  3. Turn that into a shared metaphor or analogy.
  4. Use the metaphor to guide design and trade-offs.
  5. Let teams make decentralized decisions that still feel consistent.
- **Example**: At WhatsApp, the product was anchored on the feeling of face-to-face communication, so typing indicators and read receipts became tiny signals of presence.

### Hill climb / local vs. global optimum
- **When to use**: When considering a major strategic shift that requires short-term pain for long-term gain.
- **Steps**:
  1. Recognize when you are in a local optimum.
  2. Identify the better future state.
  3. Accept that reaching it may require a valley.
  4. Set expectations that the dip is temporary.
  5. Keep the team focused on the summit.
- **Example**: Moving from desktop to mobile meant sacrificing parts of a strong existing business to reach a much larger opportunity.

### Execution-first strategy loop
- **When to use**: When teams are over-investing in strategy and under-investing in shipping.
- **Steps**:
  1. Get to a good-enough strategy.
  2. Invest heavily in execution.
  3. Put the product in customers’ hands.
  4. Use market feedback to refine strategy.
  5. Repeat until strategy and execution compound.
- **Example**: A team ships quickly, learns from customer behavior, and adjusts direction based on real evidence rather than endless planning.

### Goal detangling to avoid “toddler soccer”
- **When to use**: When multiple teams are chasing the same top-line metric and crowding each other.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start from the company outcome and customer journey.
  2. Break the top-line metric into input metrics or surfaces.
  3. Assign distinct goals to different teams.
  4. Make sure each goal ladders up to the company outcome.
  5. Check that metrics still reflect real customer value.
- **Example**: Instead of every team chasing GMV, one team owns visits, another conversion, another reorder behavior.

### Career choice by “putting on the coat of the job”
- **When to use**: When evaluating a new role or company.
- **Steps**:
  1. Do the rational analysis if needed.
  2. Imagine waking up in the job and living it day to day.
  3. Ask what problems you would solve and who you would work with.
  4. Notice whether the place feels like home.
  5. Choose the environment where you feel lucky to be there.
- **Example**: She asks what she’d think about on her commute, who she’d have lunch with, and whether the role feels like a fit emotionally.

## Core Advice
- **When you face strong disagreement in a meeting**: pause and ask what the other person knows that you do not yet know — because curiosity surfaces missing information and reduces defensiveness.
- **When you face a product review with senior leaders**: bring a concise recommendation, not a giant information dump — because leaders add context and pattern matching, not exhaustive analysis.
- **When you face repeated escalations for small decisions**: use reviews to align on principles and trade-offs — because that builds organizational decision-making capacity.
- **When you face the challenge of creating a coherent product experience**: define the feeling first, then anchor the team on a metaphor — because shared narrative enables consistent decentralized decisions.
- **When you face a major strategic transition**: explicitly name the valley between local and global optimum — because teams persist better when temporary pain is expected.
- **When you face pressure to spend lots of time on strategy**: do enough strategy, then spend most energy on execution and learning — because shipped work creates feedback and customer value.
- **When you face teams all chasing the same metric**: decompose the metric and assign distinct goals — because it reduces overlap and improves accountability.
- **When you face cross-functional tension**: assume good intent and re-center on the shared outcome — because disagreement often reflects different information, not dysfunction.
- **When you face career decisions**: choose the role that feels like home, not just the one that looks best on paper — because trust and belonging predict great work.
- **When you face feedback to “be more strategic”**: talk to customers more directly — because customer intimacy makes strategy concrete and grounded.
- **When you face a new senior role**: expect 60–120 days of learning before major impact — because context, vocabulary, and relationships matter more than people admit.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: The best leaders are the ones who are right most often and can shut down bad ideas quickly. → **Their view**: Leaders should optimize for curiosity, not being right — because other people often hold critical information.
- **Conventional**: Product reviews are for executives to make decisions after teams present all the information. → **Their view**: Reviews should be lightweight forums for recommendations and principles — because decision-making should be distributed, not centralized.
- **Conventional**: Senior leaders should mostly do strategy and vision. → **Their view**: Senior leaders should still spend most of their time on execution at the system level — because customers only experience shipped products.
- **Conventional**: Perfect strategy is the key to winning. → **Their view**: Good-enough strategy plus excellent execution wins — because execution creates learning and market feedback.
- **Conventional**: Disagreement signals politics or dysfunction. → **Their view**: Disagreement is often healthy tension from different information — because cross-functional roles naturally see different parts of the problem.
- **Conventional**: Women need a mentor/sponsor to unlock success. → **Their view**: That framing can become another burden; what matters is using the support already around you — because formal labels can create unnecessary deficiency.
- **Conventional**: There is a best org design if you think hard enough. → **Their view**: Senior leadership is usually choosing among suboptimal options — because real organizations involve unavoidable trade-offs.
