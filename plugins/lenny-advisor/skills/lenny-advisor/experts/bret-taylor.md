# Bret Taylor

**Co-founder and CEO** | **Sierra** | Expertise: product-management, ai-strategy, go-to-market, leadership

## Bio
Bret Taylor is a veteran product and technology leader whose career spans Google Maps, FriendFeed, Facebook, Salesforce, and OpenAI’s board. He now leads Sierra, an AI agent company focused on customer experience, giving him a rare perspective across consumer product design, enterprise software, platform shifts, and AI commercialization.

What makes his viewpoint especially valuable is that he thinks in systems: how products win, how companies scale, how teams should allocate attention, and how AI changes both product and business models. He is especially useful when you need a practical lens on AI strategy, GTM, pricing, and leadership under uncertainty.

## Signature Frameworks

### Most Impactful Thing Today
- **When to use**: When leading across changing roles, especially as a founder or executive deciding where to spend time.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start each day by asking what the most impactful thing you could do is.
  2. Define impact in terms of the company or team's success, not your personal preferences or prior identity.
  3. Be willing to shift between functions such as recruiting, product, engineering, sales, or partnerships.
  4. Reassess frequently as the company's needs change.
- **Example**: Taylor said Sheryl Sandberg helped him realize that as Facebook CTO he was trying to shape the job around what he liked doing, instead of asking what would most help the mobile and platform teams succeed.

### Differentiate, Don't Digitize
- **When to use**: When building a new product in a category that already exists offline or in older digital forms.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify whether the current product is just a digital copy of an existing workflow.
  2. Ask why a customer should use this new product instead of the incumbent behavior.
  3. Reassemble the experience around what the new platform uniquely enables.
  4. Add a compelling hook that creates initial pull, while ensuring enduring value remains strong.
- **Example**: Google Local underperformed because it was essentially a digital Yellow Pages. The breakthrough came when the team made the map the primary canvas, which led to Google Maps; later, satellite imagery provided the “sizzle” that drove viral adoption.

### Interrogate the Story You're Telling Yourself
- **When to use**: When diagnosing why a deal was lost, growth stalled, or a product is underperforming.
- **Steps**:
  1. Write down the explanation you currently believe.
  2. Assume your first explanation may be biased toward your own skill set or comfort zone.
  3. Test alternative explanations with partners from other functions.
  4. Seek intellectually honest input from people close to the problem and from outside advisors.
  5. Orient strategy only after pressure-testing the root cause.
- **Example**: Taylor warned that a team might conclude a customer said the product was “too expensive,” when the deeper issue was lack of product differentiation; optimizing pricing in that case would miss the real problem.

### Advice Evaluation via “Why, Why, Why”
- **When to use**: When collecting advice from mentors, board members, or operators and deciding what to trust.
- **Steps**:
  1. Ask not only what someone recommends, but why they believe it.
  2. Trace the advice back to the experiences and assumptions behind it.
  3. Look for repeated patterns across multiple advisors rather than treating one anecdote as a rule.
  4. Build your own first-principles framework from the underlying reasoning.
- **Example**: Taylor said advice often comes from one memorable experience and can sound like a universal rule; by repeatedly asking why, you can separate anecdote from durable principle.

### Choose GTM Based on Buyer-User Dynamics
- **When to use**: When deciding how to sell a software or AI product.
- **Steps**:
  1. Map who uses the product and who buys it.
  2. If engineers can independently adopt it, consider developer-led growth.
  3. If the user and buyer are the same person, consider product-led growth.
  4. If the buyer is a line-of-business leader or procurement function, use direct sales.
  5. Validate the purchasing and evaluation process before committing to a GTM motion.
- **Example**: Taylor contrasted developer-led products like Stripe and Twilio with products sold to finance teams, where self-serve PLG breaks because the end user is not the budget holder.

### Outcome-Based Pricing for Agents
- **When to use**: When your AI product autonomously completes a measurable business task.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the business outcome your product can complete without human intervention.
  2. Define a measurable success event tied to customer value.
  3. Price based on that outcome rather than seats, tokens, or generic usage.
  4. Align internal operations so you only win when the customer gets the result.
- **Example**: At Sierra, if an AI agent resolves a customer issue without requiring a human call-center agent, Sierra charges based on that successful resolution because it directly maps to avoided support cost.

### Root-Cause AI Productivity Improvement
- **When to use**: When AI tools like coding assistants are producing inconsistent or low-quality output.
- **Steps**:
  1. Treat bad AI output as a systems problem, not just a one-off annoyance.
  2. Investigate what context the model lacked when it made the mistake.
  3. Improve the surrounding system, such as context delivery, review layers, or tooling.
  4. Use AI to supervise AI where possible.
  5. Repeat until the system reliably produces better output.
- **Example**: Taylor described Sierra having an engineer focused on the model context protocol setup for Cursor, with the goal of understanding what context was missing so the tool would generate better code next time.

## Core Advice
- **When you face a new role or company stage that demands skills outside your comfort zone**: do the work that is most impactful for the business, even if it is not the work you most identify with — because leaders should optimize for company success, not personal identity.
- **When you face a product that feels like a better version of an existing category leader**: redesign around what the new platform uniquely enables — because polished copies often fail to give users a compelling reason to switch.
- **When you face a stalled deal or weak adoption and think you know the reason**: do a root-cause review that challenges your first explanation — because your functional bias may be hiding the real bottleneck.
- **When you face conflicting advice from mentors, investors, or operators**: ask “why” repeatedly until you understand the framework behind the recommendation — because advice is often anecdotal and overgeneralized.
- **When you face uncertainty about whether learning to code still matters in the AI era**: still learn computer science and systems thinking — because the scarce skill shifts from syntax to architecture, constraints, and operating complex systems.
- **When you face the question of where to build in AI as a startup founder**: focus on applied AI agents or enabling tools rather than frontier models — because foundation models are becoming a hyperscaler-scale game.
- **When you face pricing decisions for an AI product that autonomously completes work**: price on measurable outcomes whenever possible — because it aligns incentives and better reflects value.
- **When you face pressure to choose a go-to-market motion for your AI product**: choose GTM based on who buys, who uses, and how the product is evaluated — because the wrong motion can block growth even with a strong product.
- **When you face disappointing results from AI coding tools**: improve the surrounding system with context and review layers — because current gains come from system design, not magic model improvements.
- **When you face the challenge of helping kids or students prepare for an AI-rich future**: encourage AI as a learning utility and personalized tutor — because it can increase agency and adapt to different learning styles.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: AI companies should mostly price by seats, subscriptions, or usage metrics like tokens. → **Their view**: AI agents should increasingly be priced on outcomes. — because autonomous software can directly complete measurable work.
- **Conventional**: The best way for startups to win is to build a better product than incumbents. → **Their view**: A better copy often loses; you need a fundamentally new experience. — because platform shifts reward reassembled workflows, not incremental digitization.
- **Conventional**: Product-led growth is the modern default for software startups. → **Their view**: Many AI companies should lean more on direct sales. — because buyer-user separation makes PLG less effective in enterprise settings.
- **Conventional**: Learning to code may become obsolete because AI will write software. → **Their view**: Studying computer science remains highly valuable. — because systems thinking becomes more important, not less.
- **Conventional**: Founders should double down on their strengths to solve company problems. → **Their view**: Your instinctive solution is often biased by your background and should be questioned. — because functional blind spots distort diagnosis.
- **Conventional**: The biggest AI opportunity is building foundation models. → **Their view**: Most startups should avoid frontier models and focus on applied AI or tooling. — because model building is capital-intensive and increasingly dominated by hyperscalers.
