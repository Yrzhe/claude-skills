# Alex Hardiman

**Chief Product Officer** | **The New York Times** | Expertise: product-management, leadership, go-to-market, design

## Bio
Alex Hardiman leads product and design at The New York Times across news, cooking, games, audio, advertising, and more. Her perspective matters because she has helped steer product through some of the hardest problems in modern media: balancing mission and business, scaling trust-sensitive products, and turning editorial judgment into product systems.

She is especially valuable when you need to build in ambiguity, operate under crisis, or grow a subscription business without losing the core product magic.

## Signature Frameworks

### Functions and Missions Org Model
- **When to use**: When structuring a large product organization that needs both strong craft standards and cross-functional execution against business goals.
- **Steps**:
  1. Create functional homes for disciplines like product and design to manage craft excellence, career growth, promotion processes, and community of practice.
  2. Create cross-functional missions aligned to major company outcomes.
  3. Group missions into categories such as consumer, monetization, and platform.
  4. Embed the right domain experts into each mission, including editors where the product directly shapes journalism.
  5. Let mission teams pursue shared goals while functions maintain standards and talent development.
- **Example**: At The New York Times, product and design are managed as functions, while execution happens through cross-functional missions like consumer products, subscriber growth, digital advertising, and shared platforms.

### Editorial Judgment + Algorithmic Scale
- **When to use**: When building recommendation or ranking systems in domains where content quality and trust matter more than pure engagement optimization.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with expert human curation to identify what is most important and trustworthy.
  2. Translate editorial expertise into structured signals, such as importance scores.
  3. Train algorithms on those editorial signals rather than only behavioral engagement data.
  4. Use the resulting system to scale expert judgment to large audiences.
  5. Still optimize for outcomes like reach, engagement, and conversion without losing quality standards.
- **Example**: On the Times home screen, editors curate important stories first, and algorithms are trained on editorial importance scores so recommendations reflect journalistic judgment rather than just engagement.

### One-off to Platform Storytelling Pipeline
- **When to use**: When creative, high-impact content formats emerge ad hoc and you want to turn the best ones into scalable product capabilities.
- **Steps**:
  1. Allow autonomous editorial, design, and engineering teams to create one-off storytelling experiments for major stories.
  2. Observe which formats resonate strongly with readers and journalists.
  3. Have a dedicated storytelling product team identify patterns and test product-market fit for reusable formats.
  4. Build tooling for both content creation and consumer-facing experiences.
  5. Scale successful formats across the report while preserving room for future experimentation.
- **Example**: The Times creates bespoke interactive experiences for major stories, then its storytelling product team turns winning ideas into scalable formats like live updates, visuals, and richer article experiences.

### Wartime Product Management
- **When to use**: When external events create urgent, high-stakes user needs and existing roadmaps no longer match reality.
- **Steps**:
  1. Acknowledge that the operating context has changed and explicitly reset priorities.
  2. Blow up or pause the existing roadmap.
  3. Share clear context with the entire organization about the new mandate.
  4. Pull talent from across teams to focus on the most important user problems.
  5. Ship fast, mission-critical products and information access points.
  6. Be explicit about what will stop so teams can focus.
- **Example**: During COVID, the Times redirected teams to build a public COVID data set, launch local infection and vaccination tools, and make critical public-safety coverage free.

### Essential Subscription Strategy
- **When to use**: When building a subscription business that needs to expand beyond a single product into a durable daily-use bundle.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the largest addressable market where your brand can provide daily value.
  2. Anchor the bundle around the core product that creates trust and top-of-funnel demand.
  3. Add adjacent products that share the same brand DNA and solve recurring user needs.
  4. Connect the products into a discoverable family so users can move across them easily.
  5. Use the bundle to increase frequency, retention, and perceived indispensability.
- **Example**: The New York Times uses news as the “sun” and builds surrounding products like games, cooking, sports, Wirecutter, and audio into a connected subscription bundle.

### Thoughtful Acquisition Integration
- **When to use**: When acquiring a beloved consumer product whose core experience users are emotionally attached to.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the core magic of the product and commit not to breaking it.
  2. Improve infrastructure only where it clearly adds user value.
  3. Migrate the product into your stack carefully and incrementally.
  4. Preserve continuity for users, especially identity, history, and social currency.
  5. Communicate transparently when migration issues or unexpected incidents occur.
- **Example**: After acquiring Wordle, the Times rewrote it in its tech stack and connected it to free NYT accounts so users could preserve stats and streaks across devices without changing the core gameplay.

## Core Advice
- **When you face chaotic, high-pressure product problems with no clear playbook**: structure the chaos, separate what is true from what is uncertain, and rally the team around one shared goal — because Hardiman sees ambiguity as a core product leadership skill.
- **When a crisis changes user needs overnight**: do a hard reset on priorities, pause the old roadmap, and redirect teams to the few mission-critical problems that matter now — because speed and clarity matter more than roadmap fidelity.
- **When building products in trust-sensitive domains like news or information**: do not optimize only for engagement; incorporate expert judgment directly into product and algorithm design — because engagement alone can reward low-quality or misleading content.
- **When your organization creates lots of bespoke, creative work**: let one-off experiments happen, then productize the patterns that prove valuable — because innovation often starts close to the problem, not in a platform roadmap.
- **When integrating an acquired product with a passionate user base**: preserve the core user experience and only change infrastructure in ways that clearly protect or enhance user value — because continuity is part of the product’s value.
- **When burnout is rising across teams**: reduce context switching by being explicit about the small number of things that must get done and the many things that can stop — because focus is one of the few levers leaders can directly control.
- **When defining product impact in a mission-driven company**: connect business metrics to mission outcomes rather than treating metrics as the end goal — because teams work better when they understand the larger purpose.
- **When deciding whether to acquire or expand into adjacent products**: ask whether the new product strengthens a daily-use bundle and whether your company is actually ready to integrate it well — because strategic fit is not enough without operational readiness.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Business goals and metrics should drive product strategy, and mission is secondary. → **Their view**: At mission-driven companies, business goals should be in service of the mission, not the other way around — because growth is a means to a larger societal purpose.
- **Conventional**: Algorithms should optimize for engagement because user behavior is the best signal. → **Their view**: Algorithms in information products should be trained on expert editorial judgment, not just engagement data — because engagement alone cannot distinguish trustworthy information.
- **Conventional**: Product teams should avoid the most chaotic, politically charged, or urgent areas of the business. → **Their view**: The hardest, most chaotic problems are often where product managers can add the most value — because ambiguity and prioritization are core PM strengths.
- **Conventional**: One-off custom work is inefficient and should be minimized in favor of platformization. → **Their view**: Some of the best product innovations begin as one-off editorial experiments and should be protected before being scaled — because creativity often precedes standardization.
- **Conventional**: A news company should focus narrowly on news products. → **Their view**: A modern news company can become an essential daily subscription by expanding into adjacent life-need categories like games, cooking, shopping, sports, and audio — because daily relevance drives retention.
