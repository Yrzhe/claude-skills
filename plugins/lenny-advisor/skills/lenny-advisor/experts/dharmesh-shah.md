# Dharmesh Shah

**Co-founder and CTO** | **HubSpot** | Expertise: product strategy, hiring-culture, leadership, go-to-market, operations, AI strategy

## Bio
Dharmesh Shah is the co-founder and CTO of HubSpot, where he helped grow the company from startup to a public company worth roughly $30B. His perspective matters because he combines first-principles product thinking with unusually strong opinions on simplicity, culture, and how to build companies that stay coherent as they scale.

He is especially valuable when you need a view on whether to go broad or narrow, how to design culture intentionally, or how to make decisions without creating organizational drag. Shah’s style is pragmatic, contrarian, and deeply systems-oriented.

## Signature Frameworks

### High-conviction, low-consensus bets
- **When to use**: When deciding whether to pursue a strategy that most people disagree with but you believe strongly is right.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify a belief you hold that most of the market, investors, or peers disagree with.
  2. Validate that the belief is grounded in a real customer problem, not just contrarianism.
  3. Commit to the bet for a long enough time horizon that it can compound.
  4. Use the bet to drive downstream product, market, and organizational decisions consistently.
- **Example**: HubSpot stayed focused on SMB for 18 years despite constant pressure to explain “the path to enterprise.”

### Problem-first breadth vs. depth framework
- **When to use**: When deciding whether a startup should focus narrowly on one product or build a broader all-in-one solution.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the actual customer problem, not just the product you want to build.
  2. Talk to customers to determine whether one tool solves the problem or whether integration across multiple tools is the real pain point.
  3. If breadth is required, accept that you cannot be best-in-class in every subcategory.
  4. Set constraints so broadness does not create runaway complexity.
- **Example**: HubSpot built SEO, blogging, analytics, CMS, and more because SMB customers struggled to stitch together many point solutions.

### Fight for simplicity
- **When to use**: When a growing company starts accumulating complexity in product, org design, pricing, or operations.
- **Steps**:
  1. Assume complexity will naturally increase unless actively resisted.
  2. Start with the simplest possible rule or system that might work.
  3. Add complexity only when the simple version truly breaks.
  4. Create guardrails and constraints that systematically limit complexity.
- **Example**: HubSpot used rules like full transparency by default, equal starting salaries, and seat lotteries to reduce politics and overhead.

### Culture is a product
- **When to use**: When defining, scaling, or evolving company culture beyond vague values statements.
- **Steps**:
  1. Treat employees as customers of the culture product.
  2. Describe the existing culture before trying to prescribe a new one.
  3. Gather regular feedback using surveys such as employee NPS and open comments.
  4. Publish the feedback transparently and identify culture “bugs.”
  5. Fix some issues, explicitly defer others, and iterate continuously.
- **Example**: Shah created HubSpot’s Culture Code by asking what variables would predict a new hire’s success, then later ran recurring culture surveys and reviewed the results like product feedback.

### Flash tags
- **When to use**: When leaders want to communicate opinions clearly without people overreacting to every comment as a mandate.
- **Steps**:
  1. Use `#fyi` for information sharing with no response expected.
  2. Use `#suggestion` for ideas worth considering, still with no response expected.
  3. Use `#recommendation` when you’ve thought deeply and want a response if the team disagrees.
  4. Use `#plea` when you feel strongly and want the team to seriously consider acting, while preserving autonomy.
- **Example**: Shah created flash tags because people would treat a founder’s casual comment like gospel and spend months acting on it.

### Debate, decide, unite
- **When to use**: When teams need to make decisions without endless alignment-seeking or post-decision fragmentation.
- **Steps**:
  1. Encourage open debate before the decision is made.
  2. Assign a directly responsible individual to make the decision.
  3. Use data to inform the decision, but do not let data pretend to decide on its own.
  4. After the decision, unite around it even if you argued for a different option.
- **Example**: HubSpot adapted “disagree and commit” into “debate, decide, unite” to feel more collaborative and culturally aligned.

### Decision-calorie proportionality
- **When to use**: When deciding how much time and process to spend on a decision.
- **Steps**:
  1. Estimate the consequences and reversibility of the decision.
  2. Spend more time on decisions that are costly to reverse or have large impact.
  3. Spend less time on low-stakes or easily reversible decisions.
  4. Avoid over-processing small decisions and under-processing major ones.
- **Example**: Shah argues the calories spent on a decision should match its consequences, extending the one-way-door vs. two-way-door idea.

## Core Advice
- **When you’re unlikely to become great at a role**: design your role around your strengths — because people tend to be good at what they enjoy and enjoy what they’re good at.
- **When you feel pressure to run the company like everyone else**: question defaults and shape the company around your principles — because many norms are conventions, not laws.
- **When deciding whether to go narrow or broad**: start from the customer problem — because breadth only works when integration is the real pain point.
- **When complexity starts compounding**: start with the simplest rule that might work — because complexity creates politics, slower decisions, and maintenance burden.
- **When feature requests keep piling up**: account for long-term complexity cost, not just build cost — because every feature adds engineering, marketing, analytics, and decision overhead.
- **When culture feels vague or hard to scale**: treat culture like a product — because it can be measured, debugged, and iterated.
- **When founder communication gets over-interpreted**: use explicit opinion labels like `#fyi` and `#plea` — because clarity preserves speed and autonomy.
- **When the team disagrees on a decision**: debate first, assign one owner, then unite — because data informs but does not decide.
- **When evaluating startup ideas**: score upside, probability, passion/proximity, and prowess — because low-probability ideas can still be high-expected-value.
- **When learning a new technology like AI**: solve a real problem with it — because concrete goals create faster learning loops.
- **When improving a weak skill**: break it into sub-skills and measure them — because most skills are learnable if decomposed.
- **When tempted to say yes to everything**: default to no unless you know what you’ll stop doing — because time is fixed.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Startup founders should focus on one thing and become world-class at that single product. → **Their view**: Sometimes the right move is to build a broader all-in-one product from the start — because the real customer pain is stitching together multiple tools.
- **Conventional**: Senior executives should manage teams directly and have a traditional org chart. → **Their view**: A founder or CTO can be highly effective with zero direct reports — because role design should match strengths.
- **Conventional**: Going public ends founder freedom and startup energy. → **Their view**: Going public can be positive and still support long-term thinking — because liquidity and transparency can coexist with principles.
- **Conventional**: Company culture should be preserved as the company scales. → **Their view**: Culture should be iterated like a product — because employee needs change over time.
- **Conventional**: Culture is soft and hard to operationalize. → **Their view**: Culture is a product, and employees are its customers — because that makes it measurable and improvable.
- **Conventional**: Leaders should avoid sharing half-formed thoughts. → **Their view**: Leaders should share often, but label the strength of the opinion — because speed and clarity can coexist.
- **Conventional**: If a module becomes top-three in its category, that’s clearly a win. → **Their view**: In an all-in-one product, that can signal over-investment — because the goal is the integrated outcome.
- **Conventional**: SMB software is unattractive because it’s hard to sell and scale. → **Their view**: SMB can be a great market precisely because others abandon it — because the economics and feedback loops can be strong once you solve simplicity.
