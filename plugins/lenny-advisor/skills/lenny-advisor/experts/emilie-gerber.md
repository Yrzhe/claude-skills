# Emilie Gerber

**Founder and CEO** | **Six Eastern** | Expertise: public relations, go-to-market, positioning, B2B communications

## Bio
Emilie Gerber is the founder and CEO of Six Eastern, a PR agency that has supported more than 100 tech companies, from stealth startups to public companies. She previously led PR for Uber’s business development and B2B programs and worked on product communications at Box, giving her a highly tactical view of how startups can earn coverage that actually helps sales, recruiting, and credibility.

Her perspective matters because she treats PR as a practical go-to-market lever, not a vanity exercise. She is especially useful when you need to turn a messy company story into something reporters immediately understand and want to cover.

## Signature Frameworks

### Funding announcement pitch framework
- **When to use**: When announcing a startup launch or funding round and trying to secure exclusive coverage, especially with TechCrunch or other deal-focused outlets.
- **Steps**:
  1. Lead with exactly what the news is: startup launch or funding round.
  2. Offer it as an exclusive and be explicit that you will not pitch others if they take it.
  3. State the amount raised, the company category/space, and the investors.
  4. Add one concise line for why this specific round matters.
  5. Target the most relevant beat reporter based on their coverage area.
  6. Follow up respectfully after 3-4 days with new context or relevance.
  7. If there is no traction, move to the next most relevant reporter.
- **Example**: “We’re reaching out with a startup launch or funding. We’re offering this to you as an exclusive. Here’s the amount raised, here’s the space they’re in, and here are the investors.”

### Publication-to-story matching
- **When to use**: When deciding which media outlet to target based on the type of story you have.
- **Steps**:
  1. Classify the story type: funding, product launch, partnership, AI news, founder journey, future-of-work, local story, or awards/lists.
  2. Map the story to the outlet’s editorial appetite.
  3. Avoid pitching outlets with stories they do not typically cover.
  4. Use adjacent properties from the same brand when relevant, such as podcasts, conferences, or lists.
- **Example**: TechCrunch is strong for funding and some product news, Axios is highly deals-focused, VentureBeat is strong for AI, Fast Company is strong for future-of-work and design, and Business Insider can work well for pitch-deck and founder-journey stories.

### Simple, direct reporter outreach
- **When to use**: When cold pitching reporters, podcast hosts, or newsletter writers without relying on prior relationships.
- **Steps**:
  1. Use a clear subject line that states the intent and relevance.
  2. Keep the pitch extremely short, ideally around three sentences.
  3. State what you are offering and why it fits that person’s beat or column.
  4. Use the reporter’s frame of reference, including incumbents or known categories.
  5. If following up, acknowledge their inbox is busy and add new information.
  6. Try multiple channels: email, X/Twitter, DMs, and sometimes LinkedIn.
- **Example**: “Reaching out with this Martech company that’s raised $8M from X investors. Really interesting approach, they’re taking on X incumbent. Do you want the exclusive?”

### Incumbent-based positioning for PR
- **When to use**: When a startup’s product is hard to understand quickly or when category-creation language is too abstract for press.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the best-known incumbent or familiar product in the space.
  2. Describe the startup as taking on or improving on that incumbent.
  3. Explain the specific differentiator in plain language.
  4. Use that framing in subject lines and pitches to give reporters instant context.
- **Example**: For Ramp’s bill pay launch, Six Eastern framed the story as Ramp taking on Bill.com rather than as a generic product launch, helping secure CNBC coverage.

### Find the nugget and double down
- **When to use**: When a company does not have obviously headline-worthy news and needs a stronger angle.
- **Steps**:
  1. List all possible story threads: founder story, customer impact, partnership, trend relevance, product capability, or market problem.
  2. Identify the one thread that connects to a broader issue people already care about.
  3. Build the pitch around that single strongest angle instead of the full company narrative.
  4. Choose the publication or event that best matches that angle.
- **Example**: For Column Tax, they focused on helping low-income families file taxes through Propel, tying the story to TurboTax criticism and the broader free-filing conversation.

### Plain-language messaging test
- **When to use**: When a press release, blog post, or pitch sounds overly marketing-heavy or confusing.
- **Steps**:
  1. Answer basic questions verbally: what does the product do, who is it for, who are the customers, why should people care, and what is new now.
  2. Read the draft out loud.
  3. Strip out jargon and phrases that cannot be explained simply.
  4. Replace abstract marketing language with direct, human wording.
  5. Keep only the minimum detail needed for understanding.
- **Example**: She contrasted a confusing release full of “workflow automation,” “AI assistance,” and “multiplayer experience” with TechCrunch’s clearer framing: a startup trying to “eat Zapier’s lunch.”

### Modern announcement asset strategy
- **When to use**: When preparing launch materials and deciding between a traditional press release and a more flexible format.
- **Steps**:
  1. Create a blog post or custom asset instead of defaulting to a wire press release.
  2. Include all core news details in one place.
  3. Use visuals, customer proof points, and concise investor information.
  4. Make the asset easy to share socially and useful for reporters.
  5. Only share the full asset after a reporter shows interest, since email details are on the record.
- **Example**: For Gamma’s launch, the team created a Gamma presentation instead of a standard press release, which helped land TechCrunch coverage.

## Core Advice
- **When PR does not seem to drive direct signups**: do PR anyway if you can use coverage as third-party validation in sales, recruiting, partnerships, and credibility-building — because B2B PR often pays off in second-order effects.
- **When announcing funding**: do a concise, direct exclusive pitch that clearly states the amount raised, investors, company category, and why the round matters — because deal reporters pattern-match fast.
- **When your pitch feels too long**: keep it to roughly three sentences and remove jargon — because attention spans are short and clarity wins.
- **When you lack warm reporter relationships**: invest in strong cold outreach rather than overvaluing relationships — because story quality matters more than familiarity.
- **When your product is hard to explain**: frame it against a known incumbent or category leader — because reporters need an immediate mental model.
- **When a launch feels boring**: find the one angle tied to a broader market issue, customer impact, or cultural conversation — because media responds to relevance, not generic updates.
- **When building a podcast or newsletter list**: ask customers what they consume, then qualify outlets by audience, guest mix, and activity — because audience fit matters more than popularity.
- **When hosts are unresponsive**: start with “Are you open to guest ideas?” — because a lightweight ask is easier to answer than a full pitch.
- **When messaging sounds polished but confusing**: read it out loud and rewrite it in plain conversation language — because teams often hide clarity behind jargon.
- **When choosing between a press release and a blog post**: prefer a blog post or custom launch asset for most startup announcements — because it is more flexible, human, and shareable.
- **When using growth stats**: share concrete context instead of vague percentage increases from a tiny base — because credibility matters.
- **When launch timing is fixed internally**: optimize for the reporter’s timing and flexibility — because the right coverage is usually more valuable than a rigid date.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: PR is mostly not worth it because it rarely drives direct signups. → **Their view**: PR can be highly valuable even without immediate growth — because its biggest value is often credibility, sales enablement, recruiting, and validation.
- **Conventional**: Warm relationships with reporters are the key to coverage. → **Their view**: Cold outreach done well is often just as effective — because editorial interest comes from story quality and relevance.
- **Conventional**: Startups should position themselves as category creators. → **Their view**: Comparing yourself to a known incumbent usually works better — because it gives reporters instant context.
- **Conventional**: A professional press release is the default best announcement format. → **Their view**: A blog post or custom asset is often better — because it is more modern, flexible, and shareable.
- **Conventional**: Big-name PR agencies are safer for startups. → **Their view**: Scrappier specialists are often a better fit — because startups need speed, experimentation, and hands-on pitching.
- **Conventional**: Brand social accounts should lead company social presence. → **Their view**: Executive-led social is usually more effective — because people follow people more naturally than brands.
