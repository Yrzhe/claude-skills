# Chandra Janakiraman

**Chief Product Officer and Executive Vice President** | **VRChat** | Expertise: product strategy, product management, leadership, go-to-market, user research, growth

## Bio
Chandra Janakiraman is a product executive who has led teams at VRChat, Meta, Headspace, Zynga, and Amazon. His perspective matters because he has repeatedly built and applied a practical, operator-friendly strategy process that helps teams align, choose, and execute with clarity.

What makes him distinctive is that he treats strategy as a learnable craft, not a mystical talent. He is especially useful when a team needs a repeatable way to turn messy inputs into a focused roadmap and stronger business outcomes.

## Signature Frameworks

### Small-s Product Strategy
- **When to use**: Use when you already have a product or business area and need a focused 18–24 month strategy to solve the most important current problems.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define strategy as the layer between mission/vision and roadmap.
  2. Form a cross-functional working group with product, engineering, design, and data.
  3. Run a preparation phase to synthesize behavioral data, UXR, leadership interviews, competitive analysis, adjacent roadmaps, and direct user observation.
  4. Hold a strategy sprint to cluster problems and convert them into opportunity areas.
  5. Score opportunities on impact, certainty, clarity of levers, and uniqueness.
  6. Select about three strategic pillars and explicitly name what will not be prioritized.
  7. Turn each pillar into “How might we” questions.
  8. Write a two-year winning aspiration.
  9. Run a design sprint to make the strategy tangible.
  10. Roll out the strategy through pre-reads, stakeholder reviews, and team roadshows.
- **Example**: At Meta, this approach helped shape growth work for Oculus and Portal, including initiatives like Oculus referrals and Portal memories.

### Five-Phase Strategy Development Process
- **When to use**: Use when you want a repeatable, time-boxed process for building strategy with strong alignment.
- **Steps**:
  1. Preparation phase: gather and synthesize inputs over about 4 weeks.
  2. Strategy sprint: align on problems, cluster them, prioritize opportunities, and define pillars over 3–5 days.
  3. Design sprint: create illustrative concepts in about 1 week.
  4. Document writing: PM writes a 3–4 page strategy doc plus appendix over 1–2 weeks.
  5. Rollout: pre-flight with gatekeepers, align stakeholders, and roadshow over 2–3 weeks.
- **Example**: Chandra says the full process usually takes 8–12 weeks, which is worth it because the resulting strategy can guide work for a couple of years.

### Problem-to-Opportunity Clustering
- **When to use**: Use when you have many scattered user and business issues and need to turn them into a small set of strategic choices.
- **Steps**:
  1. Capture all observed problems from research, analytics, leadership input, and user observation.
  2. Cluster related problems into 10–15 themes.
  3. Rename each cluster as a positively framed opportunity area.
  4. Evaluate each opportunity against shared criteria.
  5. Down-select to the top three opportunities as strategic pillars.
- **Example**: “People don’t know where to find things” becomes “discovery,” and “users see irrelevant content” becomes “relevance.”

### Winning Aspiration / Newspaper Headline Exercise
- **When to use**: Use after selecting strategic pillars to define success in simple, motivating language.
- **Steps**:
  1. Ask the team to imagine a newspaper article written two years in the future.
  2. Have each person write a headline.
  3. Review the headlines for common words and themes.
  4. Blend them into one winning aspiration statement.
- **Example**: For a privacy effort, the aspiration might center on a headline about Facebook moving the needle on consumer trust.

### Big-S Strategy
- **When to use**: Use when the company needs a 5–10 year view of the future, not just a plan to fix current problems.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start from mission and vision.
  2. Research long-term cultural, social, competitive, and technological trends.
  3. Interview leaders about future behavior and why the world is better in 5–10 years.
  4. Cluster inputs into about three future scenarios.
  5. Build concept-like prototypes for those futures.
  6. Test prototypes with users.
  7. Extract winning components and push them into live experiments.
  8. Merge big-S and small-s insights into one roadmap.
- **Example**: He described future travel scenarios like autonomous travel, extreme-speed travel, and virtual travel.

### Leadership Interview Pre-Flight
- **When to use**: Use early in strategy work to avoid late-stage surprises and misalignment with senior stakeholders.
- **Steps**:
  1. Interview leaders before drafting the strategy.
  2. Ask what success looks like, what failure looks like, and what metrics matter.
  3. Ask what principles should guide the work.
  4. Ask for their favorite or “better” ideas directly.
  5. Use these inputs as constraints during strategy formation.
- **Example**: His “fruit” analogy: instead of bringing a mango, then an apple, then a banana to a reviewer, ask upfront what they actually want.

## Core Advice
- **When you need strategy fast**: do it as an 8–12 week process, not a rushed solo exercise — because preparation and synthesis are what make the output durable.
- **When the roadmap feels arbitrary**: define explicit focus areas and non-focus areas — because strategy is the bridge from mission to roadmap.
- **When inputs are messy**: synthesize analytics, research, customer feedback, leadership views, and competitive signals first — because patterns emerge only after meta-analysis.
- **When senior reviews keep derailing work**: interview leaders early — because pre-flighting reduces surprise objections.
- **When there are too many priorities**: score opportunities on impact, certainty, levers, and uniqueness — because shared criteria make tradeoffs defensible.
- **When the strategy list is too long**: narrow it to about three pillars — because focus creates organizational clarity.
- **When strategy feels abstract**: run a design sprint — because concrete concepts make the direction legible.
- **When strategy and roadmap are getting mixed together**: keep them separate — because strategy is about choices, roadmap is about sequencing and resourcing.
- **When you’re unsure if the strategy is good**: treat it as a hypothesis and test it in the market — because strategy only matters if it produces results.
- **When the team needs a future vision**: run a parallel big-S process — because present-day problem solving alone can be too incremental.
- **When the process is political**: use a low-ego integrator — because the best strategy lead synthesizes rather than dominates.
- **When research work is repetitive**: use AI to accelerate synthesis — because humans should spend more time on judgment and choice.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Strategy is an innate talent. → **Their view**: Strategy is a learnable process. — because structure, synthesis, and collaboration can reliably produce strong strategy.
- **Conventional**: A strong PM should write the strategy alone. → **Their view**: Strategy should be facilitated cross-functionally. — because shared creation improves ideas and buy-in.
- **Conventional**: Strategy should mainly say what the company will do. → **Their view**: Strategy must also say what it will not do. — because choice requires exclusion.
- **Conventional**: Good strategy can be written in a couple of weeks. → **Their view**: Real strategy usually needs 8–12 weeks. — because prep and alignment are the hard part.
- **Conventional**: Don’t bother leaders until the strategy is polished. → **Their view**: Interview leaders early. — because early constraints prevent late-stage rejection.
- **Conventional**: Strategy should include resourcing from the start. → **Their view**: Keep resourcing in roadmap planning. — because strategy should first define direction.
- **Conventional**: Strategy is valuable once approved. → **Their view**: Strategy has value only if execution works. — because business results are the real test.
- **Conventional**: Strategy is mostly about current problems. → **Their view**: You also need future-backward exploration. — because transformative opportunities come from imagining new futures.
