# Jonathan Becker

**Founder** | **Thrive Digital** | Expertise: growth, go-to-market, metrics, ai-strategy

## Bio
Jonathan Becker is the founder of Thrive Digital, a performance marketing agency that manages roughly $500M in annual ad spend for brands like Uber, Asana, and Tempur-Pedic. His perspective matters because he works at the intersection of paid acquisition, attribution, creative testing, and scalable growth systems across channels.

He is especially useful when you need a practical view of what actually drives revenue in modern performance marketing, not just what looks good in a dashboard. He combines operator-level rigor with a strong bias toward experimentation, diversification, and using AI to increase leverage.

## Signature Frameworks

### Diversified Channel Mix
- **When to use**: When building a growth strategy and deciding how much to rely on paid acquisition versus other channels.
- **Steps**:
  1. Treat marketing spend like capital allocation with expected returns.
  2. Avoid concentrating all budget in a single paid channel.
  3. Diversify across paid and non-paid channels such as SEO, email, direct mail, and offline media.
  4. Continuously rebalance based on volatility, performance, and changing market conditions.
- **Example**: A brand that depends entirely on Meta ads is exposed to platform changes and CPC swings; Becker would push them to spread spend across paid search, SEO, email, and offline channels.

### Creative Testing Funnel
- **When to use**: When paid social or display performance depends heavily on creative quality and message sequencing.
- **Steps**:
  1. Map creative to funnel stages, from top-of-funnel intent generation to bottom-of-funnel intent capture.
  2. Avoid using one homogeneous message for every audience and stage.
  3. Create controlled tests within the ad platform using one audience and two nearly identical creatives.
  4. Change only one variable at a time, such as copy or image.
  5. Use leveling metrics like click-through rate or impressions-to-conversion to compare ads fairly.
  6. Feed learnings back to the creative team and iterate repeatedly.
- **Example**: For a furniture company, switching from polished room shots to photos of a dog on the couch dramatically improved paid social performance, roughly doubling or tripling ROAS.

### Lead Scoring for B2B Paid Acquisition
- **When to use**: When payback is delayed and you need to optimize B2B or lead-gen campaigns beyond cheap top-of-funnel leads.
- **Steps**:
  1. Stop optimizing only for low cost per lead.
  2. Pipe CRM revenue data into a database using connectors or ETL tools.
  3. Join CRM outcomes with channel and audience data.
  4. Build a lead scoring model that predicts which leads are most likely to become high-revenue customers.
  5. Bid more aggressively on audiences with higher predicted downstream value.
- **Example**: Thrive uses tools like Supermetrics or internal ETL systems to connect CRM revenue back to ad audiences, then optimizes toward high-value customers instead of the cheapest leads.

### Attribution as Ongoing Investigation
- **When to use**: When evaluating whether paid campaigns are actually driving revenue, especially in a privacy-constrained environment.
- **Steps**:
  1. Accept that there is no single source of truth.
  2. Choose attribution lenses based on business goals such as profitability versus growth.
  3. Use multiple methods, including platform attribution, surveys, statistical validation, and media mix modeling.
  4. Continuously test whether campaign conclusions hold up under different validation methods.
  5. Be willing to conclude that some campaigns do not work and reallocate budget.
- **Example**: Becker notes that even sophisticated campaigns his firm built were later invalidated by MMM or other validation methods, leading to the conclusion that those campaigns should not continue receiving budget.

### AI-Assisted Creative and Operations Workflow
- **When to use**: When trying to increase output and strategic leverage in performance marketing teams without scaling manual work linearly.
- **Steps**:
  1. Use language models to draft copy variants, RFP responses, and first-pass strategic materials.
  2. Use image generation tools to create rough creative mockups quickly.
  3. Have humans focus on prompting, iteration, validation, and strategic judgment.
  4. Use AI outputs as rough drafts rather than final deliverables.
  5. Reinvest saved time into higher-level modeling, testing, and creative direction.
- **Example**: Thrive uses ChatGPT to draft new RFP responses from prior examples and uses DALL-E and MidJourney to generate creative mockups in about 1% of the previous time.

## Core Advice
- **When you are deciding whether paid acquisition is right for your company**: do not scale paid aggressively until you have evidence of product-market fit and proof that your product already resonates through another channel — because paid amplifies existing demand better than it creates demand for an unproven product.
- **When you feel pressure to make paid your main growth engine**: diversify across channels and avoid putting all of your budget into one platform or tactic — because single-channel dependence creates volatility and platform risk.
- **When paid social is underperforming**: build a rigorous creative testing process that isolates one variable at a time and maps messages to funnel stages — because creative is now one of the biggest remaining levers.
- **When a B2B lead-gen program looks efficient on CPL but weak on revenue**: optimize for lead quality and downstream revenue, not just cheap leads — because low CPL often hides poor customer value.
- **When attribution gets messy after privacy changes**: use multiple validation methods instead of trusting one dashboard — because no single source of truth exists anymore.
- **When choosing between SEO and paid search**: do both if possible rather than treating them as mutually exclusive — because paid gives immediate feedback while SEO creates durable returns.
- **When hiring your first performance marketer**: prioritize technical aptitude, problem-solving, and channel fluency over years of experience — because performance marketing is fundamentally a systems and data problem.
- **When deciding agency versus in-house for paid growth**: start with an internal marketing owner and pair them with an agency — because the best setup combines context with specialized expertise.
- **When evaluating TikTok for D2C or e-commerce**: actively test it, especially with founder-led or influencer-style creative — because the channel rewards authentic, high-volume creative production.
- **When repetitive manual work is slowing creative or proposal output**: use AI for rough drafts, copy variants, and mockups, then have humans refine and validate — because AI compresses low-leverage work and frees time for strategy.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Paid growth is a bad channel because it becomes an addictive drug for startups. → **Their view**: Paid growth is only dangerous when you become over-reliant on one channel; used as part of a diversified mix, it is a powerful growth lever — because the real issue is concentration risk, not paid itself.
- **Conventional**: The best way to improve paid performance is through better bidding, targeting, and platform tactics. → **Their view**: Creative is now one of the biggest remaining drivers of performance — because platforms increasingly automate the mechanics.
- **Conventional**: For lead generation, lower cost per lead means better performance. → **Their view**: Cheap leads can be the wrong target; higher-cost leads may produce much better ROI — because downstream revenue matters more than vanity efficiency.
- **Conventional**: There should be a single source of truth for attribution. → **Their view**: Attribution is an ongoing investigation using multiple imperfect methods — because privacy and cross-channel effects make any one model incomplete.
- **Conventional**: Companies should choose between in-house and agency for paid growth. → **Their view**: The best setup is usually both — because agencies bring depth and pattern recognition while internal teams provide context and alignment.
