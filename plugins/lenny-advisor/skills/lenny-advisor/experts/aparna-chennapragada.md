# Aparna Chennapragada

**Chief Product Officer** | **Microsoft** | Expertise: ai-strategy, product-management, design, go-to-market

## Bio
Aparna Chennapragada leads AI product strategy at Microsoft, with a focus on productivity tools and agents. She has also held product leadership roles at Google and Robinhood, giving her a rare perspective on zero-to-one product creation, interface design, and how new technologies reshape behavior and business models.

Her perspective matters because she thinks about AI not just as a model problem, but as a product, design, and organizational change problem. She is especially useful when you need to figure out what a true AI agent is, how to design for it, and when a new product idea is actually ready to exist.

## Signature Frameworks

### Agent product principles: autonomy, complexity, natural interaction
- **When to use**: When defining what qualifies as a true AI agent and designing agent experiences.
- **Steps**:
  1. Design for increasing autonomy so users can delegate goals, not just commands.
  2. Support complex, multi-step tasks rather than one-shot outputs.
  3. Create natural interaction loops so users can inspect, redirect, and collaborate with the agent.
- **Example**: A work researcher agent analyzes meeting participants’ views and suggests the right persuasion pitch for an upcoming leadership meeting.

### NLX is the new UX
- **When to use**: When building conversational or natural-language AI products where the interface is chat, voice, or agent interaction.
- **Steps**:
  1. Treat natural language as a designed interface, not an undesigned byproduct of the model.
  2. Define new UI constructs such as prompts, editable plans, follow-up suggestions, and progress visibility.
  3. Calibrate how much of the model’s work to show so users feel informed without being overwhelmed.
  4. Design follow-up paths that guide users toward successful outcomes.
- **Example**: Editable plans become a new interface construct for agents, with careful tradeoffs between verbose “thinking aloud” and terse progress updates.

### Demos before memos / prompt sets are the new PRDs
- **When to use**: When evaluating new product ideas, features, or concepts in the AI era.
- **Steps**:
  1. Prototype the idea instead of relying only on written specs.
  2. Include prompt sets alongside product requirements to express intended behavior.
  3. Use prototypes as a high-bandwidth communication tool across product, design, and engineering.
  4. Iterate quickly based on what the prototype reveals.
- **Example**: New projects and features should come with prototypes and prompt sets; if you are not prototyping to see what you want to build, you are doing it wrong.

### Solve before scale
- **When to use**: When building zero-to-one products before product-market fit is established.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify whether you are in solve mode or scale mode.
  2. In solve mode, allow wide exploration and major pivots instead of premature optimization.
  3. Avoid locking onto a local maximum too early.
  4. Only shift into scaling once the core user value is clearly solved.
- **Example**: Google Lens explored use cases from plant detection to foreign-language translation before narrowing in on what truly worked.

### Two-out-of-three inflection test
- **When to use**: When assessing whether it is the right time to build a new zero-to-one product or startup.
- **Steps**:
  1. Check for a step-function technology shift.
  2. Check for a meaningful consumer behavior shift.
  3. Check for a business model shift or new monetization unlock.
  4. Proceed when at least two of the three are true.
- **Example**: Google Lens combined deep learning with a behavior shift in how people used phone cameras; Robinhood combined mobile behavior with a business model shift around zero-fee trading.

### Frontier program / live one year in the future
- **When to use**: When trying to expose teams or customers to cutting-edge AI capabilities without waiting for full enterprise-wide rollout readiness.
- **Steps**:
  1. Create a sandbox or experimental environment for early adopters.
  2. Simulate a future operating model with advanced AI tools and agents.
  3. Observe how individuals and teams work differently in that environment.
  4. Use those learnings to inform broader product and organizational change.
- **Example**: At Microsoft, she described a “fake company” where people can try cutting-edge science projects, deep research agents, and advanced work agents.

## Core Advice
- **When you face a new product idea in the AI era**: Do a prototype and create prompt sets before relying on a traditional PRD — because prototypes are a faster, higher-bandwidth way to communicate intent, test feasibility, and accelerate iteration across teams.
- **When you face uncertainty about whether AI can help with a task**: Pause and explicitly ask how AI could do the task before doing it manually — because people’s priors about AI are outdated and many tasks once impossible are now feasible.
- **When you face enterprise AI rollout challenges**: Do not hold back early adopters while waiting for the whole organization to be ready — because AI adoption and change management move at different speeds, and frontier users create critical learning.
- **When you face a zero-to-one product problem**: Do solve before scale and stay open to large pivots early on — because premature optimization can trap teams on the wrong hill.
- **When you face pressure to define metrics very early for a new product**: Do not over-index on mature product metrics too soon; use qualitative signals and identify the few core jobs the product must nail — because early metrics can create false precision.
- **When you face a flood of ideas and prototypes enabled by AI**: Do strengthen the editorial and taste-making function in product leadership — because AI increases the supply of ideas, making judgment more valuable.
- **When you face skepticism that coding or computer science no longer matters**: Do keep learning computer science and computational thinking, even as tools abstract away implementation details — because understanding systems remains a durable advantage.
- **When you face the question of whether now is the right time to build a startup or new product**: Do test for at least two of three inflections: technology, behavior, and business model — because strong products usually emerge when multiple shifts align.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: With AI, the model eats the product, so interface design matters less. → **Their view**: Natural language interfaces still require rigorous product and interaction design — because conversations have structure, grammar, and invisible UI elements that shape success.
- **Conventional**: AI will make PMs obsolete because anyone can build now. → **Their view**: AI raises the importance of product judgment, editing, and taste-making — because cheaper idea generation makes deciding what deserves to exist more valuable.
- **Conventional**: Coding is dead, so studying computer science matters less. → **Their view**: Coding is moving up the abstraction stack, not disappearing — because people still need to reason about computation and instruct systems.
- **Conventional**: Once you have a promising idea, you should quickly optimize for scale and metrics. → **Their view**: In zero-to-one work, solve before scale and be wary of mature metrics too early — because early metrics can create false precision.
- **Conventional**: Enterprise AI products should wait for full governance and broad readiness before exposing advanced capabilities. → **Their view**: Create controlled frontier environments for early adopters instead — because AI cycles are too compressed for traditional rollout models.
