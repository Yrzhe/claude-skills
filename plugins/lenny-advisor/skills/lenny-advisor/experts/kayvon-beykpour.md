# Kayvon Beykpour

**Former Head of Product and GM of Consumer at Twitter** | **Twitter** | Expertise: product-management, leadership, design, growth

## Bio
Kayvon Beykpour is the former longest-tenured head of product at Twitter and the founder of Periscope, one of the earliest breakout live-streaming products. His perspective matters because he has lived through both sides of consumer product building: startup invention and large-company transformation.

He is especially valuable when you need to rethink a stagnant product culture, balance core optimization with bold new bets, or build consumer products with strong taste and conviction. He’s also unusually candid about how organizations can accidentally optimize themselves into irrelevance.

## Signature Frameworks

### Sacred Cows as Roadmap
- **When to use**: When a product organization is stagnant, risk-averse, and constrained by long-held assumptions about what cannot be changed.
- **Steps**:
  1. List the product assumptions or norms the company treats as untouchable.
  2. Identify which sacred cows are causing customer pain or blocking innovation.
  3. Turn those assumptions into explicit product opportunities to test.
  4. Use early experiments to expose cultural resistance and build momentum for broader change.
  5. Repeat the process to normalize challenging old constraints.
- **Example**: At Twitter, sacred cows included the reverse chronological timeline, 140-character constraints, and the idea that users should not control replies on their own tweets. Kayvon used these as starting points for features like ranked timelines and hide replies.

### Portfolio of Core Improvements and Speculative Bets
- **When to use**: When a company needs to keep near-term growth going while also evolving the product with new capabilities.
- **Steps**:
  1. Continue investing in proven growth levers such as onboarding, recommendations, and notifications.
  2. In parallel, define a set of more ambitious bets that may not pay off immediately.
  3. Protect speculative bets from being judged solely by short-term core metrics.
  4. Balance resources across both categories rather than overcommitting to one.
  5. Adjust the portfolio as evidence emerges.
- **Example**: Twitter kept improving recommendations and onboarding while also investing in Spaces, Communities, and Community Notes, even though some of those bets could hurt DAU or revenue in the short term.

### Founder-Led Acquihire Incubation
- **When to use**: When a large company wants to accelerate innovation in new product areas that need urgency, conviction, and startup-style leadership.
- **Steps**:
  1. Acquire small teams or founders with strong entrepreneurial drive.
  2. Give them ownership of a meaningful new initiative.
  3. Create a protected silo or startup-like environment around the project.
  4. Ensure leadership shields the team from organizational antibodies and premature metric pressure.
  5. Integrate successful bets into the broader product over time.
- **Example**: Twitter used acquihired founders to lead major bets: Communities, Fleets, Community Notes/Birdwatch, and creator monetization efforts like Super Follows.

### Belief-Based Staffing
- **When to use**: When building risky or speculative products that require unusual persistence and creativity.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the people with the right functional skills.
  2. Filter for people who genuinely believe in the idea and want to pursue it.
  3. Avoid staffing projects based only on availability or org-chart convenience.
  4. Watch for signs of passive resistance or lack of conviction.
  5. Build teams whose members are motivated enough to will the product into existence.
- **Example**: Kayvon described how projects like hide replies struggled when staffed with people who did not believe in the idea, including an engineer warning a PM that working on it would be bad for her career.

### Frameworks with Judgment, Not Religion
- **When to use**: When teams rely heavily on frameworks like Jobs-to-be-Done or OKRs and risk losing product judgment.
- **Steps**:
  1. Use frameworks to sharpen thinking, not replace thinking.
  2. Check whether the framework is leading to decisions that feel obviously bad for users.
  3. Look for cases where the framework blocks promising long-term bets.
  4. Reintroduce product taste and leadership judgment into trade-off decisions.
  5. Modify incentives or evaluation criteria when the framework distorts behavior.
- **Example**: Kayvon criticized Twitter's overreliance on DAU and frameworks that justified hostile user experiences, such as forcing users back into the ranked timeline even when they explicitly preferred reverse chronological order.

## Core Advice
- **When you face a stagnant product culture that is afraid to ship**: Do a systematic review of your product's sacred cows and turn them into your experimentation roadmap — because the things an organization believes it cannot change often reveal the biggest opportunities for customer impact and cultural renewal.
- **When you face resistance to changing culture from inside a large organization**: Do repeated internal storytelling around the vision, the bets you're making, and why they matter — because culture change requires more than plans; people need to hear a compelling narrative over and over until it becomes shared direction.
- **When you face a team split between believers and skeptics on a new initiative**: Do a clear assessment of who is on the wagon and who is off the wagon, then either align them quickly or move them off the project — because ambitious product bets are hard enough without internal disbelief.
- **When you face pressure to optimize only short-term metrics**: Do a portfolio approach that protects speculative bets from being judged only by immediate DAU or revenue impact — because some important new capabilities initially hurt core metrics but create long-term value.
- **When you face staffing decisions for a risky new product**: Do staffing based on conviction and obsession, not just availability or formal qualifications — because people who believe in the idea work harder and persist through friction.
- **When you face an acquihire or founder acquisition that could get lost inside a big company**: Do give the acquired founder real ownership, a small protected team, and enough latitude to operate like a startup — because urgency and founder-style leadership get diluted in bureaucracy.
- **When you face a framework that everyone follows rigidly**: Do step back and ask whether the resulting decisions are obviously bad for users or blocking important bets — because frameworks are useful only insofar as they improve judgment.
- **When you face the challenge of getting better at consumer product sense**: Do become a voracious user of products and try many new tools, even ones that seem silly at first — because repeated exposure sharpens taste and intuition.
- **When you face a decision about whether to copy or borrow from another product**: Do take inspiration shamelessly when it serves customers, but adapt it thoughtfully to your own product's mechanics and context — because great ideas spread, but taste is in the adaptation.
- **When you face a new product area inside a large company that conflicts with existing teams**: Do unify the product and technical vision early instead of letting multiple teams build competing versions internally — because fragmentation slows execution and helps competitors.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: The safest path to growth is to keep refining the core product and avoid risky new bets. → **Their view**: Refining the core can restart growth, but if taken too far it calcifies the organization and kills ambition — because you need both optimization and bold new bets.
- **Conventional**: Frameworks like Jobs-to-be-Done or OKRs make product decisions more rigorous and therefore better. → **Their view**: Any framework followed too religiously becomes harmful — because product judgment and taste matter more than strict adherence.
- **Conventional**: You should staff projects with whoever is available and functionally qualified. → **Their view**: For speculative bets, belief in the idea matters more than availability — because disbelief creates drag and slows execution.
- **Conventional**: A standalone live-video app can become a durable mass-market consumer platform. → **Their view**: Generalized live-only consumer products are not durable on their own — because they need asynchronous social scaffolding around them.
- **Conventional**: Copying another product is a sign of weak product thinking. → **Their view**: Borrowing ideas is normal and often smart — because the key is whether you do it with taste and adapt it to your product.
