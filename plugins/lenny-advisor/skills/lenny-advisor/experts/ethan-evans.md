# Ethan Evans

**Former Vice President; executive coach and course creator** | **Amazon** | Expertise: leadership, career development, executive growth, innovation

## Bio
Ethan Evans spent 15 years at Amazon, where he helped build businesses including Prime Video, the Amazon Appstore, Prime Gaming, and Twitch Commerce, and eventually became a vice president. His perspective matters because he has seen career growth, leadership, and innovation from the inside at a company known for high standards, fast execution, and strong leadership principles.

Today, he coaches leaders on how to advance their careers and operate at higher levels. He is especially useful when you want practical, system-based advice on promotions, executive presence, recovering from failure, or becoming more inventive.

## Signature Frameworks

### The Magic Loop
- **When to use**: When you want to grow your career, earn promotion, change roles, or gain better opportunities—even if your manager is mediocre or too busy to proactively develop you.
- **Steps**:
  1. Do your current job well enough that your manager sees you as solid and reliable.
  2. Ask your manager how you can help them.
  3. Do the work they ask for, even if it is not the most glamorous assignment.
  4. After building trust, ask for work that both helps your manager and advances your own goal.
  5. Repeat the cycle to compound trust, opportunity, and growth.
- **Example**: An entry-level employee used this loop to secure major raises and promotions, and an Amazon engineer used it over about eight years to grow from SDE II to director leading hundreds of people.

### Advanced Magic Loop / Proactive Leadership Mode
- **When to use**: When you are more senior and have already built trust with leadership; especially useful at senior manager, director, and executive levels.
- **Steps**:
  1. Move from asking what to do toward identifying important problems yourself.
  2. Suggest opportunities or fixes your manager may value.
  3. Once trust is high, act proactively on important issues without waiting for explicit permission.
  4. Keep your leader informed rather than seeking detailed direction.
- **Example**: At executive levels, leaders are expected to notice problems, start solving them, and keep their manager in the loop rather than explicitly asking, “How can I help?”

### Systematic Invention
- **When to use**: When you want to become more inventive as a PM or leader instead of waiting for inspiration to strike.
- **Steps**:
  1. Build enough expertise in the domain you want to invent in.
  2. Set aside dedicated time to think deeply without devices or distractions.
  3. Start with known components or ideas rather than trying to create something from nothing.
  4. Combine two existing concepts in a novel way.
  5. Spend the long tail of effort refining and expressing the idea over time.
- **Example**: He described inventing a drone-delivery concept by combining drones and an aircraft carrier, which led to the idea of a truck acting as a mobile drone base.

### Failure Recovery Playbook
- **When to use**: When a launch, project, or major initiative fails publicly and senior leadership has lost trust.
- **Steps**:
  1. Own the failure directly instead of deflecting blame.
  2. Communicate a clear recovery plan and provide proactive updates on a fixed cadence.
  3. Use communication to prevent panic and micromanagement from cascading leaders.
  4. Pull in help aggressively and work urgently to fix the issue.
  5. Rebuild trust over time through reliability, learning, and visible behavior change.
  6. If possible, address the relationship in person to reduce escalation and restore humanity.
- **Example**: After the Amazon Appstore launch failed on a feature Jeff Bezos had highlighted, Evans took ownership, sent hourly updates, brought in AWS experts, fixed the issue, and later rebuilt trust enough to be promoted to VP.

### Senior Manager to Director/Executive Transition
- **When to use**: When a strong functional leader is stuck at senior manager and needs to break through to director or VP.
- **Steps**:
  1. Recognize that promotion depends both on your readiness and on whether the organization has a need at that level.
  2. Shift from functional execution toward influence, strategy, and cross-team coordination.
  3. Practice next-level behaviors before you formally have the title.
  4. Take on strategic projects and show inventiveness.
  5. Make yourself the obvious choice when a scarce opportunity opens.
- **Example**: In flatter organizations, leaders often need patience, but can improve their odds by becoming the person leadership trusts to absorb more scope when peers leave or teams are consolidated.

## Core Advice
- **When your manager is not developing you**: Do The Magic Loop: perform well, ask how to help, deliver on that help, then align future work with your own goals — because career growth is your responsibility and trust creates opportunity.
- **When promotion criteria feel vague**: Share your target explicitly and ask for work that closes the gap — because managers often assume you want something different unless you say it clearly.
- **When you want director-level growth**: Do the next-level job before you have the title by taking strategic, cross-functional work — because leaders promote people they have already seen operating at that level.
- **When invention feels mysterious**: Do a monthly deep-thinking session of about two hours in a domain you know well — because invention is systematic, not magical.
- **When a launch fails publicly**: Own it, set a recovery plan, communicate regularly, and keep working until it is fixed — because visible reliability rebuilds trust.
- **When you are tempted to hide after a mistake**: Meet leaders in person rather than avoiding them — because face-to-face interaction can soften escalation and restore humanity.
- **When interviewing**: Show enthusiasm, present professionally, and explain why your work mattered — because interviewers hire for energy, fit, and impact, not just task completion.
- **When interviewing on Zoom**: Use a stable, professional setup with camera on and strong presence — because remote interviews still reward body language and engagement.
- **When speed is important**: Move fast, but keep reputational downside in mind — because some mistakes are costly enough to warrant extra caution.
- **When a junior person makes a painful mistake**: Offer emotional support and make clear the system, not just the person, is responsible — because people can internalize blame and lose confidence.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Career growth should mostly come from having a good manager who notices your work and develops you. → **Their view**: You should assume your career is your responsibility and actively engineer growth rather than waiting for managerial support. — because many managers are too busy or not skilled enough to coach consistently.
- **Conventional**: Invention requires lots of time and many breakthrough ideas. → **Their view**: You only need occasional dedicated thinking time and a small number of strong ideas to be highly inventive. — because one good idea can be refined for years.
- **Conventional**: Returning to the office is the safer long-term bet. → **Their view**: Remote work has more long-term upside because it is still early and under-optimized. — because offices have had centuries of refinement, while remote collaboration is still young.
- **Conventional**: Serious business should be heavily contract-driven and legally protected. → **Their view**: More business should be done on trust and handshake-style commitments. — because over-suspicion creates friction and social cost.
- **Conventional**: A major public failure can permanently derail your career. → **Their view**: Even very visible failures can be recovered from if you own them, learn, and rebuild trust. — because Evans’ own Appstore failure did not end his trajectory.
