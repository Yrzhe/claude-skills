## Bio
Alisa Cohn is a top-ranked executive coach who works with startup founders, C-suite leaders, and Fortune 500 executives on leadership, communication, and organizational effectiveness. Her perspective matters because she translates messy people problems into clear, usable scripts and operating principles—especially for hard conversations, team alignment, and founder conflict.

She is especially valuable when leaders need to be both compassionate and direct. Her style is practical, grounded, and unusually specific: she focuses on what to say, when to say it, and how to keep conversations tied to performance and outcomes.

## Signature Frameworks

### Difficult feedback conversation script
- **When to use**: When you need to give constructive performance feedback about behavior, collaboration, or skill gaps.
- **Steps**:
  1. Get your mindset right: clarify that your goal is to help the person improve, not to vent or punish.
  2. Open neutrally and matter-of-factly rather than making it dramatic.
  3. State observable facts or what you've heard from credible sources.
  4. Tie the feedback to expectations and what success in the role requires.
  5. Invite their perspective without losing clarity on the issue.
  6. End with a concrete expectation for what needs to change.
- **Example**: “I wanted to have a conversation with you about some things I’ve been hearing from your peers...” followed by specific missed deadlines or communication issues, ending with a clear expectation for how they will keep peers and the team in the loop.

### Pause-and-reset for defensiveness
- **When to use**: When the other person becomes defensive, heated, upset, or too emotional to productively continue a feedback conversation.
- **Steps**:
  1. Pause the conversation explicitly.
  2. Name the shift in energy or emotional temperature without escalating.
  3. Reassure them of your intent: you are trying to help their career and success.
  4. Assess whether to continue now or take a break.
  5. Recommit to having the conversation later if needed, rather than abandoning it.
- **Example**: “Let’s just pause for a second because I’m feeling the energy has changed... I have no intention of upsetting you. I just want to be able to talk to you about the things that are going to help you in your career.”

### No-promotion conversation with hope for the future
- **When to use**: When someone expected a promotion but will not receive it.
- **Steps**:
  1. Lead with the disappointing decision quickly and clearly.
  2. Explain the reasoning behind the decision with specific criteria.
  3. Avoid vague platitudes; give the real factors involved.
  4. Offer hope for the future and reaffirm your investment in their growth.
  5. If appropriate, set up a follow-up conversation after they’ve had time to process.
- **Example**: Explain that the company is hiring externally because it needs someone who has done the role multiple times and has specialized expertise, then add that you want to help the employee build skills and succeed in their career there.

### Pre-termination clarity conversation
- **When to use**: When performance issues are serious enough that continued employment is at risk, but before firing someone.
- **Steps**:
  1. State clearly that this is a difficult conversation.
  2. Reference prior feedback conversations and the repeated pattern.
  3. Describe the unresolved issues with specific evidence.
  4. Set a clear timeline for improvement.
  5. State the consequence directly: failure to improve means parting ways.
  6. Reinforce that you believe they can change while making the stakes unmistakable.
- **Example**: Tell someone that after six months of repeated conversations, they must fix coordination and communication issues within 30 days or the company will have to part ways.

### Three questions to end every meeting
- **When to use**: At the end of any decision-making or working meeting to ensure alignment and follow-through.
- **Steps**:
  1. Ask: What did we decide here?
  2. Ask: Who needs to do what by when?
  3. Ask: Who else needs to know?
  4. Capture the answers in writing.
  5. Make it a ritual and assign someone to own follow-up.
- **Example**: In a six-person meeting, each person may leave with a different understanding of the decision—this ritual prevents that drift.

### Founder prenup
- **When to use**: Before starting a company with a co-founder, and periodically afterward, to prevent misalignment from becoming destructive conflict.
- **Steps**:
  1. Clarify each founder’s core values and discuss where they align or conflict.
  2. Discuss what success or the company’s full potential looks like.
  3. Explore how each person handles conflict, including getting outside perspective on your own style.
  4. Agree on how decisions will be made when you disagree.
  5. Discuss the kind of company culture each founder wants to build.
  6. Revisit these topics over time as the company evolves.
- **Example**: One co-founder wants weekend responsiveness while the other wants downtime; another pair disagrees on whether to build a small controllable company or a venture-scale one.

### Personal Operating Manual
- **When to use**: When teammates, managers, or collaborators need to work together more effectively by understanding each other’s preferences and working styles.
- **Steps**:
  1. Document preferred communication style.
  2. Clarify how you like to work, such as uninterrupted blocks versus frequent meetings.
  3. Explain the best way to reach you for urgent matters.
  4. Share pet peeves and what earns a “gold star” with you.
  5. Clarify delegation style and desired check-in cadence.
- **Example**: Specify whether you want weekly check-ins during a project or only want to hear about it when it’s complete.

### Patience vs. process diagnosis
- **When to use**: When an initiative, launch, or team effort is slipping and leaders are unsure whether to wait or intervene.
- **Steps**:
  1. Examine whether there is a credible plan in your mind for how the work will come together.
  2. Check whether you’ve actually talked to the people involved and looked at the data.
  3. Notice signs like uncomfortable silence, hidden issues, or lack of clarity.
  4. Determine whether the issue is simply time or whether a process breakdown needs intervention.
  5. Unstick the specific bottleneck or surface the larger process problem.
- **Example**: A CEO thinks a delayed launch just needs patience, but the real issue is an inexperienced PM, conflict with engineering, and a broken process.

## Core Advice
- **When you dread a difficult conversation**: Do the mindset work first and reframe it as helping the other person grow — because avoidance is often about your discomfort, not the conversation’s value.
- **When giving underperformance feedback**: Use observable facts and specific examples — because evidence is easier to hear than judgment.
- **When someone gets defensive**: Pause, name the shift, restate your intent, and decide whether to continue later — because regulation matters more than forcing the issue.
- **When delivering bad news about promotion**: Say it early, explain the criteria, and offer a real path forward — because clarity plus hope preserves trust.
- **When someone may need to be fired**: Have a crystal-clear warning conversation first — because termination should never be a surprise.
- **When meetings keep going nowhere**: End with the three questions — because alignment fails more often in follow-through than in discussion.
- **When leaders prioritize happiness over standards**: Focus on winning, accountability, and clarity — because people are more engaged in a well-run, successful organization.
- **When choosing or repairing a co-founder relationship**: Discuss values, vision, conflict style, and decision rules upfront — because unspoken assumptions become startup-ending conflict.
- **When team friction comes from different work styles**: Share a personal operating manual — because explicit preferences prevent avoidable misunderstandings.
- **When a project is behind**: Diagnose process before assuming patience is the answer — because delay can hide structural breakdowns.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: A good leader should keep employees happy and avoid upsetting them. → **Their view**: A leader’s job is to create the conditions for results, winning, and growth — because avoiding discomfort leads to weak standards and poor performance.
- **Conventional**: Hard feedback is harmful and should be softened or delayed. → **Their view**: Withholding hard feedback is often more harmful than giving it directly and compassionately — because people can’t improve without the truth.
- **Conventional**: Great culture is mostly perks, friendliness, and “feeling like family.” → **Their view**: Culture is how work gets done, what standards exist, and whether the organization is set up to win — because execution and accountability matter more than vibes.
- **Conventional**: Startups should avoid structure and traditional management. → **Their view**: Most teams eventually need clear process, roles, goals, and decision structures — because organizations spin their wheels without them.
- **Conventional**: If a project is behind, leaders should be patient and let the team work it out. → **Their view**: Delay can be a process failure that requires intervention — because silence and hidden conflict rarely resolve themselves.
