# Petra Wille

**Independent product leadership coach and author** | **Strong Product People** | Expertise: product leadership, product coaching, storytelling, community-building

## Bio
Petra Wille coaches the people who lead product teams: first-line product managers, directors, and CPOs. Her perspective matters because she focuses not just on product craft, but on how leaders grow product talent, build stronger organizations, and create environments where PMs can actually develop over time.

She is the author of *Strong Product People* and has coached product leaders, PMs, and product teams across companies and countries. Petra is especially valuable when you need practical, people-centered guidance on coaching, storytelling, and building durable product communities.

## Signature Frameworks

### Five Ingredients to Be the Best Coach to Product Managers
- **When to use**: When you manage product people and want to improve their growth, performance, and development over time.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define what a good product person looks like in your context, separating personality traits from skills and know-how.
  2. Assess where each PM is today in their current role and life situation.
  3. Create a shared vision of where they could go next, including the next bigger challenge that would stretch them.
  4. Co-create a development plan with concrete actions, learning steps, or experiences.
  5. Follow up consistently with light, regular check-ins and reminders.
- **Example**: A manager and PM agree the PM needs to improve prioritization, then create a plan with concrete actions and review progress monthly.

### PMwheel
- **When to use**: When you need a structured way to assess a PM's strengths, gaps, and development priorities.
- **Steps**:
  1. Understand the underlying problems for users and the business.
  2. Evaluate solution-finding ability.
  3. Assess planning skills such as roadmap and goal setting.
  4. Assess execution and delivery skills, including backlog work and team collaboration.
  5. Review listen-and-learn skills using qualitative and quantitative data after launch.
  6. Assess iteration ability based on what was learned.
  7. Evaluate teamwork and team motivation skills.
  8. Include personal growth as a standing part of the conversation.
  9. Check understanding of agile principles and values.
- **Example**: Petra recommends using self-assessment plus feedback from a line manager and team members to get a rounded view of a PM.

### Quarterly Next-Bigger-Challenge Planning
- **When to use**: When you want to proactively create growth opportunities for direct reports instead of waiting for them to appear.
- **Steps**:
  1. Block time once a quarter to review each direct report.
  2. Write down each person's name and current situation.
  3. Identify the next bigger challenge that would help them learn a new skill.
  4. Keep the list visible so you can assign the opportunity when it appears.
  5. Use the challenge as a growth lever over one or more quarters.
- **Example**: A manager realizes a PM could grow by leading a cross-functional initiative, then assigns it when the opportunity arises two quarters later.

### Story Design for Product Communication
- **When to use**: When you need to explain strategy, vision, or a product initiative to the company, team, or stakeholders.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start drafting early instead of waiting for a perfect blank-page idea.
  2. Talk through the story before writing it to see how it lands.
  3. Use a proven structure such as the hero's journey.
  4. Put the team or user at the center of the story depending on the goal.
  5. Translate the story into multiple formats: spoken, written, and visual.
  6. Prepare versions for different time horizons, from elevator pitch to long-form presentation.
- **Example**: For a 3-4 month team strategy, Petra suggests spending about two weeks shaping the story and preparing a short pitch, a six-minute version, and a longer all-hands version.

### Community of Practice Design
- **When to use**: When you want to scale product learning, reduce manager burden, and help PMs learn from each other.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the purpose, values, and definition of success for the community.
  2. Create rituals and a regular rhythm for sharing and learning.
  3. Distribute roles and responsibilities so the community is not dependent on one person.
  4. Encourage content curation and topic-based subgroups or circles.
  5. Use incentives or sponsorship where appropriate to reward contribution.
  6. Measure success by signal quality, learning, and usefulness rather than raw engagement.
- **Example**: Petra describes communities where small circles of 10-15 people share best practices, with bridges between circles and a few people curating content and rituals.

## Core Advice
- **When you manage PMs but don't have a formal competency framework**: Start with an existing assessment like PMwheel, PM Daisy, or Marty Cagan's framework and customize it to your team — because a borrowed framework gives you a starting compass and prevents paralysis while you wait for HR or company-wide leveling to catch up.
- **When you are unsure how to begin coaching a PM**: Start with the development plan rather than trying to perfect the full assessment first — because PMs usually already know one or two things they want to improve, which makes development planning the easiest entry point.
- **When you want PM growth to actually stick**: Use small, regular check-ins instead of relying on big performance-review moments — because consistency beats intensity, and frequent light-touch follow-up keeps development top of mind without overwhelming either person.
- **When a PM needs to improve a specific skill**: Turn the gap into a concrete, self-owned development plan with a few practical actions — because people develop faster when they own the plan and can connect it to real work, not abstract feedback.
- **When you want to help a PM grow beyond their current role**: Look for the next bigger challenge and assign stretch opportunities deliberately — because growth often happens through the right challenge at the right time, not through generic training.
- **When you are coaching a PM and don't know what to ask**: Keep a prepared list of coaching questions and emotions vocabulary for one-on-ones — because many managers show up unprepared; having prompts improves the quality of the conversation and helps people articulate how they really feel.
- **When a PM struggles with prioritization or other core craft skills**: Teach the specific tool or method they need in the moment, such as the Eisenhower matrix for prioritization — because PMs often need practical, just-in-time help rather than broad theory or more content to consume.
- **When you are trying to become a better storyteller**: Spend significant time shaping the story before presenting it — because great storytelling is crafted, not spontaneous; Petra estimates a major strategy story can take around two weeks of part-time work.
- **When your product language feels ignored or stale**: Replace jargon and internal abbreviations with plain language that speaks to both heart and mind — because repeated business lingo creates banner blindness; simpler language makes the message more memorable and persuasive.
- **When you need to present a product idea or strategy**: Prepare the story in multiple formats: spoken, written, and visual, with short and long versions — because different audiences and contexts require different packaging, and multi-format storytelling increases clarity and reach.
- **When public speaking makes you nervous**: Start with small, friendly audiences and get feedback from both peers and strangers — because confidence grows through repetition in low-stakes settings, and outside feedback helps identify gaps in the story.
- **When you want to build a strong product community**: Create a community of practice with clear purpose, rituals, and shared ownership — because communities work best when they are intentional, distributed, and designed for learning rather than just activity.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: A good manager should first define a perfect competency framework before coaching anyone. → **Their view**: Start with the development plan and follow-up, even if your framework is incomplete. — because PMs usually already know what they want to improve, so you can begin helping immediately instead of waiting for organizational alignment.
- **Conventional**: Product managers can learn most of what they need from books, courses, and content. → **Their view**: Product management is a career, not a role; real growth comes from doing the work and failing over time. — because many core PM skills only develop through repeated practice and lived experience, not passive consumption.
- **Conventional**: Engagement is the main metric for a healthy community. → **Their view**: Signal quality, learning, and usefulness matter more than raw engagement. — because a community can be active but noisy; the real value is whether members learn, connect, and improve their work.
- **Conventional**: Public speaking is optional for product leaders if they are otherwise strong operators. → **Their view**: Weak storytelling and public speaking can become a career staller for product people. — because promotion and influence depend heavily on rallying teams and communicating a compelling vision.
- **Conventional**: A community can be run by a charismatic central leader. → **Their view**: A sustainable community cannot sit on the shoulders of one or two people. — because responsibility, moderation, curation, and rituals need to be distributed across multiple contributors.
