# Sanchan Saxena

**VP of Product** | **Coinbase** | Expertise: product-management, leadership, growth, web3

## Bio
Sanchan Saxena is a product leader who has built and scaled products at Microsoft, Yahoo, Instagram, Airbnb, and Coinbase. His perspective matters because he has operated across both hypergrowth and crisis, giving him a rare blend of product intuition, operational rigor, and leadership under pressure.

He is especially valuable when you need advice on making high-stakes product decisions, hiring strong product talent, or steering teams through uncertainty. His thinking is grounded in real operating experience, not abstract product theory.

## Signature Frameworks

### Little Bets Career Strategy
- **When to use**: When deciding how to build a career or choose the next role without a perfect long-term plan.
- **Steps**:
  1. Take small, reversible bets instead of trying to optimize a five-year plan.
  2. Jump into opportunities that give you energy.
  3. Learn quickly from each attempt.
  4. Pivot fast when something is not working.
  5. Use rapid recovery to keep momentum after setbacks.
- **Example**: His own path moved through aerospace engineering, founding a company, Microsoft, Hotmail, Instagram, Airbnb, and Coinbase.

### Intentional Product Development
- **When to use**: When defining product direction before relying on data or A/B tests.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with the desired end state and customer problem.
  2. Define the intentional experience you want to create.
  3. Use intuition and founder judgment to set direction.
  4. Then use experiments and A/B tests to find the fastest path to that end state.
  5. Iterate after launch based on feedback and learning.
- **Example**: For Instagram Stories, Kevin Systrom defined the product as real-time news, not a place to upload old photos, and only later iterated from that baseline.

### Two-Week Crisis Planning
- **When to use**: When the business environment is changing too quickly for quarterly or annual planning.
- **Steps**:
  1. Shorten planning cycles to two weeks.
  2. Make company-level priorities, not team-level priorities, the unit of planning.
  3. Use top-down direction plus bottom-up customer signals.
  4. Dissolve rigid sub-team boundaries if needed.
  5. Reallocate people and resources rapidly to the highest-priority work.
- **Example**: At Airbnb during COVID, the company moved to two-week planning and operated as essentially one team, shifting engineers and PMs across CX, marketplace, and survival-critical work.

### Founder-Led Storytelling in Crisis
- **When to use**: When morale is collapsing and the data says the company may not survive.
- **Steps**:
  1. Acknowledge the reality honestly.
  2. Reinforce belief in the mission and future.
  3. Have the founder stay visibly on the front line.
  4. Use storytelling to make the future feel concrete and possible.
  5. Keep the team focused on belief, not just revenue or feature metrics.
- **Example**: Brian Chesky repeatedly told Airbnb employees the company would survive, even as travel dropped to near zero and layoffs were unavoidable.

### Content Over Process Hiring
- **When to use**: When hiring for early-stage startups or fast-moving teams.
- **Steps**:
  1. Hire people who know what to do and when to do it.
  2. Evaluate candidates on judgment and problem-solving depth, not just process fluency.
  3. Teach process after hiring, rather than trying to teach content later.
  4. Look for leaders who can still describe what, when, and how they would build.
  5. Avoid letting process become the product.
- **Example**: At Airbnb, candidates were given real work challenges, such as how to scale Airbnb Plus homes, to reveal how they think rather than how polished their slides are.

### DRI-Based Decision Making
- **When to use**: When cross-functional alignment is slowing decisions in a high-velocity company.
- **Steps**:
  1. Assign one directly responsible individual for each project.
  2. Collect written input from all relevant functions.
  3. Have the DRI make the final decision.
  4. Require others to disagree and commit, then champion the decision.
  5. Use the same mechanism at both team and company levels.
- **Example**: At Coinbase, teams write “rapids” in a common format, and the DRI makes the call after reviewing written input from legal, engineering, operations, design, and others.

## Core Advice
- **When you feel pressure to map out every step of your career**: Take small bets, jump into energizing opportunities, and pivot quickly when they don't work — because careers are built through experimentation and rapid recovery, not rigid five-year plans.
- **When you're early in product management**: Choose the fastest learning environment, whether that's a startup or a large company, based on what you need to learn next — because startups teach starting and scaling from zero, while big companies teach operating at scale.
- **When you're deciding whether to join a startup or a big company**: Optimize for the kind of learning that matches your energy and career stage — because the right environment depends on whether you thrive in ambiguity or in systems.
- **When a company is hit by a sudden external shock**: Switch to short planning cycles and focus the whole company on the most urgent company-level priorities — because speed and reallocation matter more than perfect planning.
- **When your data is noisy but you still need to build**: Start with a clear intentional end state, then use experiments to accelerate toward it — because product direction should come from vision first, optimization second.
- **When you're tempted to over-rely on A/B tests**: Use A/B tests to optimize a chosen destination, not to decide the destination itself — because tests can find the best route, not the right goal.
- **When your team is facing layoffs or a major downturn**: Be radically honest about uncertainty while still giving people a believable future to rally around — because trust comes from vulnerability plus conviction.
- **When morale is threatened by outside poaching or fear**: Lean on mission, founder credibility, and visible leadership rather than trying to outpay the market — because belief can outperform compensation in a crisis.
- **When you're building a product that has real-world components**: Design the full experience, not just the software layer, and think through operations as part of the product — because the customer experiences the whole system, not just the app.
- **When you're hiring product leaders for an early-stage company**: Test for judgment by giving candidates a real work challenge instead of a generic presentation — because real problems reveal real thinking.
- **When your organization is becoming bogged down by coordination**: Create a single decision owner and require written input from stakeholders — because this reduces alignment theater and speeds execution.
- **When you're building in a new, noisy market like Web3**: Ignore today's constraints enough to build toward the future, but accept that the path will go through an intermediate “2.5” stage — because adoption is usually gradual, not instantaneous.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Great careers come from careful long-term planning and working backward from a five-year plan. → **Their view**: Careers are better built through small bets, pivots, and rapid recovery — because analysis paralysis kills opportunities, while action creates learning and momentum.
- **Conventional**: Product decisions should be heavily A/B tested before committing to a direction. → **Their view**: First define the intentional end state; use A/B tests only after you know where you're trying to go — because testing cannot tell you the destination.
- **Conventional**: Cross-functional consensus is the safest way to make decisions. → **Their view**: Consensus often produces the least offensive, least effective product; one DRI should make the call — because committee-driven decisions slow companies down and dilute quality.
- **Conventional**: Process is what enables scale in startups. → **Their view**: Content, not process, is what correlates with startup success — because startups fail when they hire process people who don't know what to do when.
- **Conventional**: The best way to build a product is to start with a practical MVP that scales later. → **Their view**: Start with the ideal 15-out-of-10 experience, then figure out how to scale the pieces that matter — because lovable products create stronger pull.
- **Conventional**: Data should override intuition when the numbers are clear. → **Their view**: Gut and intuition are valid forms of data, just not yet statistically significant — because intuition comes from immersion in customers and the market.
