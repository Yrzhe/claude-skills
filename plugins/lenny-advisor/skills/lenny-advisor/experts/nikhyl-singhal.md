# Nikhyl Singhal

**Product leader, coach, and mentor** | **Meta** | Expertise: product leadership, career development, consumer products, management coaching

## Bio
Nikhyl Singhal leads product teams for the Facebook app at Meta and has held senior product leadership roles at Google and Credit Karma, with startup founder experience as well. His perspective matters because he combines deep product execution experience with unusually strong thinking about long-term career design, management, and how senior operators actually grow over time.

He is especially valuable for PMs and product leaders who want advice that goes beyond “get promoted” and instead focuses on building a durable career arc, choosing the right experiences, and becoming a better leader as responsibilities expand.

## Signature Frameworks

### Work backward from your career end state
- **When to use**: When deciding whether to take a new job, stay put, or plan your next move
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the long-term end state you want, not just the next title.
  2. Work backward to identify the job after next and the job after that.
  3. Treat current and next roles as stepping stones.
  4. Check whether a move is true progress or just an escape from frustration.
- **Example**: Instead of leaving because your boss is difficult, ask whether the next role gets you closer to becoming a GM, CPO, or founder.

### Career as a product roadmap
- **When to use**: When planning a multi-year career trajectory
- **Steps**:
  1. Imagine the ideal version of your career outcome.
  2. Break it into versions: version one, version two, version three.
  3. Choose experiences that build toward the next version.
  4. Update the roadmap as your goals evolve.
- **Example**: Map your career like a product plan: first build core craft, then leadership scope, then org-wide influence.

### Diagnose promotion blockers with four lenses
- **When to use**: When someone is stuck and not getting promoted
- **Steps**:
  1. Check whether there is real advocacy for the person.
  2. Check whether the next role actually exists.
  3. Assess whether the person is being impatient relative to the role’s timeline.
  4. Determine whether there is a genuine development gap.
- **Example**: A strong PM may be blocked not by performance, but because there is no open next-level role and no senior leader pushing for them.

### Build a story for your skip job
- **When to use**: When choosing projects, roles, or responsibilities in the present
- **Steps**:
  1. Write the paragraph you want to tell in 6, 12, and 24 months.
  2. Include the problem solved, the skill built, and the headwind overcome.
  3. Use “I” language so the story is about your growth and contribution.
  4. If the story is weak, change the work.
- **Example**: “I solved X, built Y skill, and overcame Z challenge” becomes the narrative that makes the next move compelling.

### Share the steering wheel in management
- **When to use**: When transitioning into management or managing a new team
- **Steps**:
  1. Treat management as a shared control system, not command-and-control.
  2. Earn the right to be involved by asking what you can help with.
  3. Wait for invitation rather than forcing your way into everything.
  4. Pick one or two areas where you can truly partner.
- **Example**: He describes the manager as a sidecar on a motorcycle: attached and useful, but not the driver.

### Reboot the meeting operating system
- **When to use**: When joining a scaled organization or trying to improve execution
- **Steps**:
  1. Treat meetings as a product with versions and feedback loops.
  2. Redesign cadence, attendees, and decision rights.
  3. Review and iterate every quarter.
  4. Use process improvements to unlock delegation and faster decisions.
- **Example**: A team’s meetings may be on “version seven,” reworked every 90 days based on feedback.

### Use community as a learning engine
- **When to use**: When formal training and manager coaching are insufficient
- **Steps**:
  1. Join or create a trusted peer community.
  2. Create psychological safety for honest discussion.
  3. Share real problems and compare notes.
  4. Use the group to normalize challenges and exchange solutions.
- **Example**: He built The Skip CPO community via WhatsApp and Zoom so senior product leaders could compare notes on CEO and org challenges.

### Identify the shadow of your superpower
- **When to use**: When feedback says your strength is also causing problems
- **Steps**:
  1. List your most celebrated strengths.
  2. Look for contradictory feedback or recurring complaints.
  3. Assume the strength may be overused at the next level.
  4. Rewire the behavior without denying the strength.
- **Example**: A collaborator may later struggle when leadership requires stronger opinions and more disagreement.

## Core Advice
- **When you feel tempted to leave a frustrating job immediately**: ask whether the issue is career-defining or just a short-term annoyance — because many problems follow you to the next role, making the move lateral rather than forward.
- **When deciding between promotion and growth**: treat promotion as a mechanism, not the goal — because a promotion without a longer-term purpose can become an empty milestone.
- **When early in your PM career**: pick one lane to become world-class in — because a clear skill identity makes your story stronger and your value easier to understand.
- **When choosing early-career experiences**: prioritize variety across stages, markets, and problem types — because exposure to pre-PMF, PMF, and scale makes you a better builder.
- **When evaluating an “ex-growth” company**: be skeptical if it is still searching for PMF while carrying a scaled structure — because the upside may no longer match the risk.
- **When unsure if a company has PMF**: look for customer pull and low-friction acquisition — because real demand shows up in behavior, not just metrics.
- **When stuck on promotion**: diagnose advocacy, role availability, impatience, and development gaps separately — because each requires a different fix.
- **When your manager seems unaware of your impact**: gather feedback from multiple perspectives — because manager views alone can miss the real pattern.
- **When receiving painful feedback**: repeat it back accurately and thank the person — because safety encourages honesty and better signal.
- **When becoming a new manager**: do not assume you are automatically qualified — because management is a distinct skill, not a reward for being a strong builder.
- **When you have a strong reputation**: look for the downside of your strengths — because what got you here can limit you at the next level.
- **When planning a long career**: design your “act three” now — because people can feel lost after achieving the goals that defined their first decades.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: A promotion is the main marker of career progress. → **Their view**: Promotion is just a company-specific system; judge progress by the long-term arc and the story you are building — because people often chase promotions only to leave right after.
- **Conventional**: If you can build products well, you are ready to manage people. → **Their view**: Product-building and management are different skills — because many strong builders become weak managers without training.
- **Conventional**: Senior leaders mainly need feedback on weaknesses. → **Their view**: They often need help seeing the shadow side of strengths — because success traits become liabilities at the next level.
- **Conventional**: The best career move is to collect prestigious logos. → **Their view**: Diverse, meaningful experiences matter more than brand accumulation — because breadth makes you better and more fulfilled.
- **Conventional**: Management is the natural next step for PMs. → **Their view**: The IC path is a real and often better long-term path for many PMs — because not everyone wants to manage, and strong IC tracks matter more in flatter orgs.
- **Conventional**: Learning happens mainly through managers and formal programs. → **Their view**: Community is often a stronger learning engine — because peers create honesty, safety, and practical problem-solving.
