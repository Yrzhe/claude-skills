## Signature Frameworks

### What Good Looks Like (Evals-first AI deployment)
- **When to use**: When building or deploying AI systems in enterprises or government where reliability and judgment matter.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the target outcome by specifying what “good” looks like for the workflow.
  2. Create evals that capture the desired recommendations, actions, or judgments.
  3. Test the model or agent against those evals in realistic environments.
  4. Identify failure cases and use expert feedback or labeling to improve performance.
  5. Keep humans in the loop for low-confidence or high-stakes decisions.
- **Example**: In healthcare, doctors define what the AI should surface from 200–300 pages of patient records, such as allergies or critical correlations, so the system can be evaluated against expert judgment.

### Environment-based agent training
- **When to use**: When training AI agents to perform actions inside real software systems like Salesforce, healthcare systems, or other enterprise tools.
- **Steps**:
  1. Place the agent in a sandboxed environment that mirrors the real system.
  2. Give it a concrete goal, such as finding a meeting or completing a business process.
  3. Train it to navigate data, configurations, and workflows inside that environment.
  4. Teach it when to escalate to a human if confidence is low or the task cannot be completed reliably.
  5. Prioritize collecting data from tasks and environments that generalize across many use cases.
- **Example**: Training agents in a Salesforce instance so they can understand configurations, navigate records, complete business processes, and know when to hand off to a human.

### Independent-insight startup filter
- **When to use**: When deciding whether to start a company or launch a new business line.
- **Steps**:
  1. Ask what unique insight you have that others likely do not.
  2. Validate why you are in a privileged position to see that insight.
  3. Check whether the market and business model can support a very large outcome.
  4. Assess whether you personally want to work on the problem for 5–10 years.
  5. Avoid falling in love with the first idea; keep questioning assumptions and discard weak ideas quickly.
- **Example**: At Uber, evaluating many ideas before choosing food delivery based on market structure, unit economics, and customer urgency.

### Customer incentive triangulation
- **When to use**: When customers say many things they want, but you need to identify the real urgent problem worth solving.
- **Steps**:
  1. Do not take customer statements literally.
  2. Analyze underlying incentives, including financial, career, and emotional drivers.
  3. Build an independent ground truth using data, observation, and outside sources.
  4. Compare what customers say with what the economics and behavior actually show.
  5. Design the product around the most urgent, high-value problem rather than the loudest complaint.
- **Example**: For Uber Eats, reverse-engineering restaurant economics by ordering food, estimating ingredient costs, and triangulating supplier data to see that incremental demand mattered most.

### Gross-margin stress test
- **When to use**: When evaluating whether a new business idea is structurally attractive and differentiated.
- **Steps**:
  1. Estimate the gross margin of the proposed business.
  2. Ask why the margin cannot be materially higher if the product is truly valuable.
  3. Identify the customer’s alternatives and their economics.
  4. Assess whether competitors can compress your margins over time.
  5. Proceed only if you have a credible path to durable differentiation, scale advantages, or another structural defense.
- **Example**: If a business starts at 40% gross margin while alternatives operate at 20%, ask why margins won’t collapse as the market matures.

### Not losing before winning
- **When to use**: When making strategic decisions in startups or high-volatility markets where survival matters.
- **Steps**:
  1. Evaluate not just upside but downside risk of each decision.
  2. Prefer asymmetrically positive bets over reckless high-risk moves.
  3. Preserve the company’s ability to stay in the game long enough for timing and product-market fit to improve.
  4. Take risks deliberately rather than because hype or FOMO demands it.
  5. Remember that survival is a prerequisite for eventual breakout success.
- **Example**: In AI hype cycles, avoid overextending just to match the narrative; customers will still need solutions years later.

### Team-as-organism hiring
- **When to use**: When building leadership teams or cross-functional groups that need to scale together over time.
- **Steps**:
  1. Hire for curiosity and problem-solving ability.
  2. Assess whether the person can work well across functions with humility.
  3. Evaluate leadership ability and adaptability.
  4. Compose teams around complementary strengths rather than only individual pedigree.
  5. For a small subset of roles, prioritize exact experience when speed to market is critical.
- **Example**: Keeping the Uber Eats management team largely intact from zero to $20B because the team understood each other’s strengths and weaknesses.

## Core Advice
- **When enterprise AI pilots look promising but stall before production**: do the hard work of defining evals, improving reliability, and planning for a 6–12 month implementation cycle — because the last 20–30% is where legal, policy, regulatory, change-management, and accuracy issues surface.
- **When deciding whether to start a company or business line**: rigorously check your unique insight, market quality, business model quality, and willingness to stay with the problem for years — because generic ideas rarely create durable alpha.
- **When customer feedback conflicts**: do independent research into customer incentives and economics instead of taking requests at face value — because customers often describe symptoms, not the real buying urgency.
- **When an AI use case already has very high human accuracy**: use AI selectively and keep humans involved for edge cases — because replacing a 98%-accurate workflow is much harder than improving a weak one.
- **When training AI agents for real work**: train in realistic environments and focus on tasks that generalize across many configurations — because the most valuable data transfers across many similar tasks, not one-off edge cases.
- **When pressure builds to move fast in a hype cycle**: take calculated risks and protect the company’s ability to survive — because only surviving companies can benefit when the market matures.
- **When hiring for a scaling organization**: optimize for curiosity, cross-functional collaboration, and leadership potential, while reserving exact-experience hiring for a few speed-critical roles — because adaptable people usually scale better.
- **When a new business idea seems exciting**: run a gross-margin stress test early — because margin structure quickly reveals differentiation and competitive vulnerability.
- **When learning a fast-moving technical domain as a leader**: use AI as a tutor and summarizer before pulling time from experts — because it accelerates learning and helps you ask better questions.
