## Bio
Janna Bastow is a product leader, founder, and community builder best known for co-founding ProdPad and Mind the Product. She is especially influential for challenging traditional roadmap thinking and helping product teams move from date-driven plans to strategy-driven decision-making.

Her perspective matters because she has seen product management from both the operator and platform sides: building products, leading teams, and helping thousands of PMs improve how they work. She brings a practical, contrarian view grounded in real-world product chaos, not theory.

## Signature Frameworks

### Now-Next-Later roadmap
- **When to use**: Use when you need to communicate priorities without pretending you can predict exact delivery dates far in advance.
- **Steps**:
  1. List the key problems or opportunities the team plans to tackle.
  2. Group them into three buckets: Now, Next, and Later.
  3. Keep the roadmap focused on problems and outcomes rather than a dated feature plan.
  4. Add dates only where there is a real external constraint, such as regulation or seasonality.
  5. Review and update the roadmap continuously as learning changes priorities.
- **Example**: A team replaces a quarterly feature roadmap with Now / Next / Later columns after realizing stakeholders kept asking to move dates by a month because the original dates were never truly reliable.

### Roadmap as a prototype for strategy
- **When to use**: Use when shaping product strategy and aligning stakeholders around assumptions before locking into a fixed plan.
- **Steps**:
  1. Draft your current assumptions about the problems to solve and their likely order.
  2. Share the roadmap early with teammates, customers, and stakeholders.
  3. Ask for feedback on whether the sequencing and problem framing are right.
  4. Revise the roadmap based on what you learn.
  5. Treat the value as the roadmapping process, not the artifact itself.
- **Example**: Like a product mockup, the roadmap is used to test assumptions and then updated or discarded as new information comes in.

### Soft launch / hard launch separation
- **When to use**: Use when engineering delivery timing and marketing launch timing are hard to synchronize.
- **Steps**:
  1. Let engineering ship when the product is actually ready.
  2. Treat that initial release as a soft launch.
  3. Once the product is live, start launch planning with marketing using the real product.
  4. Gather demos, customer feedback, and testimonials from early users.
  5. Run the hard launch later, on marketing’s timeline.
- **Example**: Marketing launches a product using real customer reactions and working demos instead of waiting on design mocks that may change before release.

### Product vision / elevator pitch template
- **When to use**: Use when defining or clarifying a product vision in a concise, structured way.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the target customer.
  2. State the customer need or opportunity.
  3. Name the product and its category.
  4. Explain the key reason to buy or use it.
  5. Identify the main alternative.
  6. State the product’s differentiation from that alternative.
- **Example**: A team uses Geoffrey Moore’s elevator pitch structure to clearly explain who the product is for, what problem it solves, and why it is better than the current alternative.

### Retrospectives as a psychological safety mechanism
- **When to use**: Use when a team needs to improve how it works and build a culture where people can question decisions and adapt.
- **Steps**:
  1. Create regular space for the team to discuss what is working and what is not.
  2. Encourage candid discussion across levels and functions.
  3. Identify changes the team wants to make.
  4. Give the team permission to actually change those practices.
  5. Repeat the cycle so the team keeps learning and improving.
- **Example**: A team uses retrospectives not just to vent, but to explicitly decide what process changes they are allowed to make next sprint.

## Core Advice
- **When you face pressure to create a date-driven roadmap for everything**: Do a Now-Next-Later roadmap instead, and only attach dates to items with genuine external deadlines — because it avoids false certainty and keeps the team focused on priorities.
- **When you face repeated roadmap slippage and constant requests to move delivery dates**: Do a root-cause analysis instead of just improving timeline mechanics — because the problem may be the roadmap format itself, not execution.
- **When you face tension between engineering release timing and marketing launch timing**: Do separate soft launches from hard launches — because engineering can ship when ready while marketing builds a stronger launch from the real product.
- **When you face a need to improve product team performance**: Do more discovery work and build stronger psychological safety through retrospectives — because great teams question assumptions and improve how they work.
- **When you face resistance to changing product culture in a large company**: Do start with a small pocket of the organization rather than trying to transform the whole company at once — because local wins spread more effectively than top-down mandates.
- **When you face anxiety about public speaking**: Do rehearse out loud, record yourself, use a confidence ritual, and familiarize yourself with the stage — because reducing uncertainty improves delivery.
- **When you face the challenge of building a high-quality community**: Do keep it grassroots, show up consistently, and recruit trusted contributors — because community quality comes from regularity and curation.
- **When you face uncertainty about becoming a founder from a PM background**: Do leverage your cross-functional PM experience and surround yourself with advisors — because PMs already have many founder skills and can learn the rest on the job.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: A roadmap should be a detailed, date-based delivery plan. → **Their view**: A roadmap should be a prototype for strategy — because its job is to test assumptions, not fake precision.
- **Conventional**: Good product teams should commit to exact delivery dates for most roadmap items. → **Their view**: Most roadmap items should not have dates unless there is a real deadline — because uncertainty grows over time.
- **Conventional**: Marketing and engineering should tightly coordinate around one launch date. → **Their view**: Release and launch should often be decoupled into soft launch and hard launch — because it reduces stress and improves launch quality.
- **Conventional**: Changing product culture in a large company requires a broad transformation effort. → **Their view**: Start with a small, high-potential pocket of the organization — because large companies are too calcified for all-at-once change.
- **Conventional**: If a speaker messes up on stage, the audience will judge them harshly. → **Their view**: Audiences are usually rooting for the speaker — because mistakes are often humanizing.
