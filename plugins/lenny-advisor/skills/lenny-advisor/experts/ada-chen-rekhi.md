## Signature Frameworks

### Curiosity Loops
- **When to use**: When you need better input for an important decision and want contextual advice rather than generic opinions.
- **Steps**:
  1. Form a good question that is specific, unbiased, and asks for rationale.
  2. Choose the right people: a mix of subject matter experts and people who know you well.
  3. Make the ask lightweight so it is easy to respond to quickly.
  4. Collect and process the responses, looking for surprises, disagreements, and missing angles.
  5. Close the loop by thanking people and sharing how their input affected your decision.
- **Example**: Before appearing on the podcast, Ada emailed about 10–11 people with nine possible topics and asked them to pick the top two or three that resonated and explain why. In about 20 minutes of work, she got rich input that helped shape the conversation.

### Values Exercise / Inner Scorecard
- **When to use**: When making career or life decisions that may be distorted by status, money, titles, or others’ expectations.
- **Steps**:
  1. Review a list of possible values and select the ones that resonate.
  2. Group and narrow them down into a smaller set.
  3. Stack-rank the most important values for your current season of life.
  4. Translate them into 3–5 sentences that define your personal scorecard.
  5. Use that scorecard to evaluate decisions and compare them against external pressures.
- **Example**: Ada used this exercise to evaluate a high-profile job opportunity that looked great on paper but would have required heavy travel and grueling hours. She realized it failed her top values and turned it down.

### Explore and Exploit
- **When to use**: When planning your early career or deciding whether to broaden your experiences versus go deeper in a proven path.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start in exploration mode early in your career to test different environments, functions, and industries.
  2. Treat each move as a hypothesis-driven experiment about what you like and what fits.
  3. Extract explicit learnings from each role about your strengths, preferences, and gaps.
  4. Switch into exploit mode once you find a rich area worth going deeper on.
  5. Use exploit mode intentionally to gain specific capabilities, not just titles.
- **Example**: Ada explored corporate life at Microsoft, startup marketing at Mochi Media, and founding a company. After her startup was acquired by LinkedIn, she intentionally used LinkedIn to exploit specific learning goals like growth, subscriptions, and pricing so she could become a better founder.

### Don’t Be the Frog
- **When to use**: When deciding whether to stay in a role that feels increasingly misaligned or stagnant.
- **Steps**:
  1. Pay attention to your environment and whether conditions are improving or deteriorating over time.
  2. Assess whether you are still learning, growing, and being challenged.
  3. Identify whether the discomfort is temporary and productive or a sign of inertia and drift.
  4. Try to change the situation proactively through conversations, projects, or role shaping.
  5. If the environment cannot provide growth, use the extra capacity to build skills or relationships elsewhere.
- **Example**: Ada described the risk of staying in a job because each issue seems small in isolation, only to realize later that you have stopped learning and become trapped by inertia.

### Eating Your Vegetables
- **When to use**: When you dislike a skill or activity and need to determine whether you truly dislike it or are just inexperienced and bad at it.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify a skill that matters for your goals but feels uncomfortable.
  2. Commit to repeated exposure rather than judging it after one attempt.
  3. Create a concrete rule or ritual that forces practice.
  4. Repeat enough times to separate beginner discomfort from genuine dislike.
  5. Reflect on whether the skill becomes easier, more useful, or more enjoyable over time.
- **Example**: To get better at networking in Silicon Valley, Ada forced herself to attend one event per week, hand out 10 business cards, and touch the back wall before leaving. Over time, she got much better and built relationships that still matter today.

## Core Advice
- **When you face a big decision and generic advice feels conflicting or unhelpful**: Do a curiosity loop: ask a specific, unbiased question to a small set of carefully chosen people and make the response easy — because this produces contextual input, reveals blind spots, and helps you spot surprises rather than blindly following one person’s opinion.
- **When you face a career choice that looks impressive on paper**: Do a values exercise and evaluate the option against your inner scorecard, not just status or title — because this helps you avoid optimizing for external validation at the expense of joy, meaning, autonomy, or relationships.
- **When you face uncertainty early in your career**: Do more exploration across roles, company sizes, and functions, but with a clear learning thesis for each move — because early career is the best time to discover what energizes you and what skills you want to deepen before committing to a path.
- **When you face a job that feels increasingly off but you are unsure whether to leave**: Do a trend check: ask whether you are still learning, whether the environment is improving, and whether you are staying out of inertia — because small dissatisfactions compound slowly, and without active reflection you can become trapped in a role that no longer serves your growth.
- **When you face a skill you dislike, such as networking, public posting, or outreach**: Do 10–12 repetitions before deciding you truly dislike it — because many activities feel bad at first because you are inexperienced, not because they are inherently wrong for you.
- **When you face the urge to hire a coach because you feel stuck**: Do a check for alternatives first: mentors, courses, community, or a curiosity loop may solve the problem better — because a coach is only one person’s perspective and may be less effective than broader learning or support systems depending on the goal.
- **When you face the decision to hire a coach**: Do conversations with 2–3 coaches before choosing one, and optimize for trust, safety, and fit rather than credentials alone — because coaching effectiveness depends heavily on personal connection and learning style fit, not just resume relevance.
- **When you face a hard but important task you keep avoiding**: Do just five minutes on the most important task as early as possible in the day — because starting is the biggest hurdle; once momentum begins, five minutes often turns into meaningful progress.
- **When you face the challenge of building a network in a new industry**: Do structured repetition with a simple rule, such as attending events regularly and forcing a minimum number of introductions — because networking gets easier with familiarity and practice, and repeated exposure compounds into durable relationships.
- **When you face the need to give someone difficult feedback**: Do it with care and clarity if it can materially help them, even if it feels uncomfortable — because avoiding hard feedback may protect you, but it can deprive the other person of information they need to succeed.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: If you need help growing in your career, you should get a coach. → **Their view**: Most people probably do not need a coach. — because many goals are better served by mentors, courses, communities, or structured advice gathering, and a coach is expensive and limited to one person’s perspective.
- **Conventional**: Career success means climbing the ladder, collecting bigger titles, and adding impressive logos to your resume. → **Their view**: You should optimize for learning, values alignment, and the life you actually want, not just resume prestige. — because external markers can pull you into roles that look successful but make you less happy and less aligned with your inner scorecard.
- **Conventional**: If something feels uncomfortable or unpleasant, it probably is not for you. → **Their view**: You often need repeated exposure before deciding whether you truly dislike something. — because beginner discomfort is often confused with genuine dislike; many important skills only become rewarding after enough practice.
- **Conventional**: When asking others for advice, you should follow what they tell you. → **Their view**: Advice should be treated as input, not instructions. — because other people’s advice is often well-intentioned but not contextual; the real value is in spotting surprises, disagreement, and blind spots.
- **Conventional**: The best way to use a big company is to maximize promotions and title growth. → **Their view**: Use big-company time intentionally to acquire specific capabilities you will need later. — because Ada used LinkedIn not to chase the biggest title, but to learn growth, subscriptions, and pricing so she could become a better founder.
