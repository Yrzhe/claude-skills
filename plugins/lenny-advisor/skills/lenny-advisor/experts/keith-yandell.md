## Signature Frameworks

### How to lead a team you don't know better than the team does
- **When to use**: When you are asked to lead a function outside your core expertise or manage specialists in a domain you are not an expert in.
- **Steps**:
  1. Accept that you are not the subject-matter expert and be transparent about it.
  2. Hire the best functional experts you can find.
  3. Explain clearly how you can still add value to the team.
  4. Get out of the way and let specialists do the work.
  5. Focus on enabling the team rather than trying to control the function.
- **Example**: Keith led marketing and legal without deep domain expertise, then hired leaders like Kofi Amoo-Gottfried and Tia Sherringham and let them excel.

### Empathy-driven decision making
- **When to use**: When a leadership group is debating a hard trade-off between competing priorities or business lines.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify what each person in the room is optimizing for.
  2. Ask each side to steelman the other side's argument.
  3. Make sure every perspective is heard and respected.
  4. Clarify who the decision maker or tiebreaker is.
  5. Set a time horizon for the decision.
  6. Once the decision is made, move on and execute.
- **Example**: In a debate between profitability for one business line and growth for another, Keith asked the profitability side to make the growth case, which often created empathy and sometimes changed minds.

### How to scale culture with written operating principles
- **When to use**: When a company is growing quickly and new employees need help understanding how to succeed.
- **Steps**:
  1. Write down how you work and what you expect from others.
  2. Include traits that predict success in the organization.
  3. Include your own weaknesses and what you are trying to improve.
  4. State your commitments to the team, including career support.
  5. Use the document in onboarding and ongoing relationships.
- **Example**: Keith created a “How to work with Keith” document that explains expectations, his growth areas, and his commitment to help people find their next job.

### Feedback loop as a management system
- **When to use**: When you want to create a culture where people can give and receive hard feedback without fear.
- **Steps**:
  1. Require constructive feedback in one-on-ones and reviews.
  2. Model receiving feedback without defensiveness.
  3. Thank people explicitly for hard feedback.
  4. Act on the feedback and communicate what changed.
  5. Follow up later to show whether things improved.
- **Example**: Keith used one-on-ones to demand constructive feedback for himself, and referenced Uber’s T3 B3 system where managers had to receive both positives and constructive criticism.

### Build BD like a product platform, not a one-off deal
- **When to use**: When business development opportunities require product and engineering support.
- **Steps**:
  1. Bring product in neither too early nor too late.
  2. Share pipeline visibility so product can flag high-value opportunities.
  3. Look for scalable platform solutions instead of bespoke builds.
  4. Test the thesis with low-cost operations before spending engineering resources.
  5. Use early experiments to define the parameters for negotiation.
- **Example**: Instead of building custom DashPass integrations for each partnership, Keith learned to build reusable platforms and test ideas with small pilots first.

## Core Advice
- **When you interview someone and feel a culture concern but otherwise like them**: Give direct feedback and see how they respond before deciding — because their reaction to tough feedback is a strong signal of how they will behave as a teammate.
- **When a candidate seems aloof or hard to read in an interview**: Have a follow-up dinner or informal conversation to test your impression — because a less structured setting can reveal warmth and humility.
- **When you are asked to lead a function outside your expertise**: Be honest that you are not the expert, then hire strong specialists and support them — because generalists add value by enabling experts, not by pretending to know more.
- **When you want to build trust with a new team**: Write down how you work, what you expect, and what you are trying to improve — because written clarity scales culture.
- **When you manage people and want them to stay engaged**: Help them think about and prepare for their next role, even if it is outside your company — because people trust managers who care about their careers.
- **When someone on your team is considering leaving**: Talk openly about it early and use the conversation to plan for backfill and growth — because transparency reduces surprise departures.
- **When a leadership debate gets stuck between two strong viewpoints**: Ask each side to argue the other side’s case — because steelmanning creates empathy and often changes minds.
- **When a group cannot reach consensus on a decision**: Name the tiebreaker and set a deadline — because debate without decision slows execution.
- **When you receive feedback from your team**: Thank them, act on it, and report back on what changed — because feedback only builds trust if people see it used.
- **When your company is growing very fast**: Use written documents to explain norms, expectations, and how to work with you — because informal culture transmission breaks at scale.
- **When evaluating a partnership that needs product work**: Test the idea with a small, scrappy experiment before asking for major engineering investment — because cheap tests expose weak theses early.
- **When fundraising is hard and investors keep saying no**: Keep going until you find the one yes — because persistence is often the difference between failure and survival.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Great leaders should be domain experts in the functions they run. → **Their view**: Generalists can be better leaders of specialized teams because they focus on enabling experts and avoid defaulting to conventional solutions.
- **Conventional**: You should keep top performers even if they don't fit the culture perfectly. → **Their view**: A strong culture bar matters enough to pass on highly qualified people who feel like jerks or don't fit.
- **Conventional**: Managers should keep career conversations focused on retaining talent inside the company. → **Their view**: Helping people find their next job, even elsewhere, builds trust and often leads to boomerang hires.
- **Conventional**: In tough times, companies should mainly preserve morale and avoid disruption. → **Their view**: Crises can be beneficial because they create focus, discipline, and clearer mission alignment.
- **Conventional**: Partnerships should be customized heavily to win deals. → **Their view**: Start with scalable platforms and cheap operational tests before building bespoke solutions.
- **Conventional**: After a big win, teams should celebrate and relax. → **Their view**: Stay hungry immediately after success, because complacency lets competitors catch up.
