## Bio
Jonny Miller is a former startup founder turned coach and educator who helps tech professionals, founders, and executives build calm, resilience, and emotional capacity through body-based nervous system practices. His perspective matters because he translates somatic work, breathwork, and nervous system science into practical tools for high-performers who are dealing with anxiety, stress, and burnout.

He is especially useful when someone is intellectually aware of their problems but still feels stuck in reactive patterns. Jonny’s lens is that performance, leadership, and emotional stability are deeply shaped by physiology, not just mindset.

## Signature Frameworks

### State Over Story
- **When to use**: When you're anxious, nervous, reactive, or trying to calm yourself before a stressful event like a meeting, presentation, or difficult conversation.
- **Steps**:
  1. Notice that your mind is creating a story about why you feel bad or anxious.
  2. Shift attention from thoughts to physiology first.
  3. Use a bottom-up intervention such as lengthening the exhale, humming, relaxing the eyes, or expanding awareness.
  4. Let the calmer body state change the thoughts and emotions that follow.
- **Example**: Jonny described using breathing for 15 minutes before a TEDx talk and going on stage “almost cool as a cucumber.”

### Top-Down vs. Bottom-Up Regulation
- **When to use**: When deciding whether to manage stress through thinking/reframing or through direct physiological regulation.
- **Steps**:
  1. Recognize that top-down methods include reframing, self-talk, and mindfulness.
  2. Recognize that bottom-up methods include breath, posture, gaze, grounding, and body awareness.
  3. Start with bottom-up regulation because the body sends more information to the brain than the reverse.
  4. Use top-down reflection after your state has stabilized.
- **Example**: Instead of telling yourself “this talk will go great,” Jonny recommends calming the body first so the mind naturally updates its interpretation.

### If This, Then Breathe
- **When to use**: When building a personalized toolkit for recurring emotional states such as overwhelm, anxiety, lethargy, or social stress.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify common states you experience repeatedly.
  2. Assign a specific practice to each state.
  3. Practice those tools regularly so they become accessible under stress.
  4. Use the matching tool in the moment when the trigger appears.
- **Example**: If I feel overwhelmed, do humming; if I feel anxious, do 4-4-8 breathing or alternate nostril breathing.

### APE Interoception Check
- **When to use**: When you feel off, reactive, uncertain, or want to build awareness of early stress signals before they escalate.
- **Steps**:
  1. **A — Awareness**: Notice whether your awareness is narrow and tense or expanded and relaxed.
  2. **P — Posture**: Check whether your body is collapsed, clenched, rigid, or grounded.
  3. **E — Emotion**: Sense the mood, body sensations, and emotional tone present in the moment.
  4. Optionally add breath and other sensations to deepen the scan.
  5. Use what you notice as data rather than ignoring it.
- **Example**: Use APE in a meeting to notice a clenched stomach, poor posture, or breath-holding before confidence drops further.

### Feather, Brick, Dump Truck
- **When to use**: When monitoring for burnout and trying to catch problems early instead of waiting for a crisis.
- **Steps**:
  1. Notice subtle early signals (“feathers”) such as waking tired or feeling slightly depleted.
  2. Treat those signals seriously and make adjustments early.
  3. Avoid ignoring them until they become bigger disruptions (“bricks”) like arguments, irritability, or losing your cool.
  4. Prevent full-scale crises (“dump trucks”) such as health breakdowns, getting fired, or severe burnout.
- **Example**: The feather is waking up tired, the brick is having a fight or losing your cool weeks later, and the dump truck is a health crisis or major life consequence.

### Emotional Debt
- **When to use**: When stress has accumulated over time and you notice fragility, reactivity, poor sleep, or inability to downshift without substances.
- **Steps**:
  1. Understand that incomplete stress cycles accumulate as allostatic load.
  2. Watch for signs like disproportionate reactions, poor sleep, relationship strain, and constant activation.
  3. Build self-regulation capacity through breathwork, NSDR, and downshifting practices.
  4. Process stored emotional material through somatic work rather than only intellectual analysis.
  5. Create regular recovery periods to pay down the debt.
- **Example**: Emotional debt is like technical debt in startups: useful in the short term, but dangerous if never repaid.

### NSDR for Downshifting
- **When to use**: When you need deep recovery, an afternoon reset, better downregulation, or support for burnout prevention and sleep.
- **Steps**:
  1. Lie down comfortably.
  2. Use an eye mask or close your eyes.
  3. Listen to a guided NSDR or yoga nidra body scan.
  4. Let the practice guide attention through the body and into a deeply restful state.
  5. Use it regularly, especially in the afternoon or after periods of intense work.
- **Example**: Jonny does NSDR most days for 15–20 minutes, often between 1 and 3 PM, and says it can feel like a two-hour nap.

## Core Advice
- **When you face anxiety before a meeting, presentation, or podcast**: do a breathing pattern where the exhale is twice as long as the inhale, such as 4-4-8, for 1–2 minutes — because a longer exhale activates the parasympathetic nervous system and changes your physiological state faster than trying to think your way into calm.
- **When you face low energy or afternoon lethargy**: do 2–3 rounds of 30 nasal “espresso breath” pumps from the lower belly, then pause and recover — because rapid nasal exhales create an activating effect and can provide a quick energy boost without relying immediately on caffeine.
- **When you face stress and forget to use your tools in the moment**: build a short daily practice in the morning for 7–10 days using 2–5 minutes of breathwork — because practicing when calm makes the technique more available when you're dysregulated.
- **When you face recurring overwhelm, anxiety, or social stress**: create an “if this, then breathe” playbook that maps specific states to specific practices — because pre-deciding your response reduces friction and helps you act quickly instead of improvising while stressed.
- **When you face subtle signs that something is off in your body**: use the APE check and treat what you notice as useful data — because interoception helps you catch stress and burnout early, before they escalate into reactivity or crisis.
- **When you face signs of burnout building over time**: respond at the “feather” stage instead of waiting for the “brick” or “dump truck” stage — because early intervention is far cheaper and easier than recovering from a health crisis, relationship blowup, or full burnout.
- **When you face the need to downshift after intense work**: use NSDR or yoga nidra for 15–20 minutes, especially in the afternoon or after demanding work blocks — because many high-performers are good at activation but poor at recovery.
- **When you face emotional patterns that keep repeating despite insight**: work with a somatic practitioner or therapist who includes body-based processing, not just talk — because unresolved stress is stored as body-linked patterns, so intellectual understanding alone often doesn't complete the stress response.
- **When you face a tendency to push through hard work for long periods**: pair a strong work ethic with a deliberate rest ethic, including recovery periods and regular downregulation practices — because sustained performance requires paying down emotional debt.
- **When you face a strong emotional reaction that feels disproportionate**: pause, downshift your nervous system first, and only then continue the conversation or make the decision — because reactive states distort judgment.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: If you're anxious, you should think positively, reframe the situation, or talk yourself down. → **Their view**: Change your physiological state first; the story in your mind usually follows the body — because bottom-up regulation is faster and more effective.
- **Conventional**: Talk therapy is the main path for resolving stress, trauma, and emotional struggles. → **Their view**: Talk therapy alone is often insufficient; body-based or somatic work is needed to address the root pattern — because unresolved stress is tied to somatic markers and incomplete mobilization cycles.
- **Conventional**: Emotions interfere with good decision-making; rational leaders should minimize feelings. → **Their view**: The best decision-making is emotional in the sense that access to bodily and emotional signals improves judgment — because ignoring feelings removes critical information.
- **Conventional**: Meditation is the primary or best tool for stress regulation and self-awareness. → **Their view**: Meditation is useful, but people have over-indexed on it and underused body-based regulation tools — because breath, posture, gaze, grounding, and NSDR are often more practical and immediate.
- **Conventional**: Burnout is mostly a personal wellness issue and not a major business concern. → **Their view**: Burnout is materially expensive and should be treated like a serious business risk or insurance problem — because it drives attrition, poor decisions, and major second-order costs.
