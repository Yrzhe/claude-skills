# Ebi Atawodi

**Director of Product Management** | **YouTube (Google)** | Expertise: product vision, product strategy, leadership, product-management

## Bio
Ebi Atawodi is a product leader who has built products and teams at Uber, Netflix, and YouTube. Her perspective matters because she combines strategic rigor with unusually practical communication tools for turning vague ideas into aligned execution.

She is especially strong on product vision, storytelling, and culture: how to define a future worth building, how to get teams to believe in it, and how to create the organizational habits that make it real.

## Signature Frameworks

### Good Product Vision Framework
- **When to use**: When defining a long-term product or company vision that should inspire and align a team.
- **Steps**:
  1. Make the vision lofty enough to be exciting and motivating.
  2. Ensure it still feels realistic and attainable.
  3. Describe the future state without being constrained by today’s technical limitations.
  4. Ground the vision in a clear, potent user problem.
- **Example**: At Uber, the mission was reliable transportation everywhere for everyone, while the vision was a world of continuous trips where you don’t need parking—freeing city space currently used for parking.

### Mission vs. Vision Distinction
- **When to use**: When teams confuse purpose with destination and need clearer strategic language.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the mission as why the company or team exists.
  2. Define the vision as what the world looks like when you succeed.
  3. Use the mission to guide purpose and principles.
  4. Use the vision to paint a vivid picture of the destination.
- **Example**: Uber’s mission was making transportation reliable everywhere for everyone; its vision was cities without parking because trips become continuous and connected.

### Once Upon a Time Vision Story
- **When to use**: When you need a simple storytelling format to communicate a product vision clearly.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with “Once upon a time” and describe the original state of the world.
  2. Explain what changed and what problem emerged.
  3. Introduce the turning point with “One day...”.
  4. Describe the resulting future state with “Because of that...” and end with the impact left on the world.
- **Example**: For YouTube Shorts, she described a world where YouTube became overly polished and intimidating, then Shorts re-enabled anyone to express themselves in 60 seconds and brought back the joy of creation.

### Future Headline / Article Framework
- **When to use**: When you want to force clarity on the impact of a product idea and socialize it internally.
- **Steps**:
  1. Imagine the future after the product succeeds.
  2. Write the headline that a publication would run about it.
  3. Write the subtitle to clarify the concrete product change.
  4. Optionally write the full article to flesh out the implications.
- **Example**: At Uber, she wrote a future article/headline about Uber replacing San Francisco’s Clipper card, helping shape the payments and commerce infrastructure needed for multimodal transit payments.

### Visualized Vision Mock
- **When to use**: When words alone are not enough and you need people to see the future product experience.
- **Steps**:
  1. Sketch the future experience by hand or with simple boxes.
  2. Show the key user interaction or outcome visually.
  3. Use a designer if available, but don’t wait for one.
  4. Refine the mock to help others emotionally connect to the future state.
- **Example**: At Uber, she sketched a future where users could top up Uber balance with cash at local stores or kiosks, which later became a real product.

### Empathize → Create → Evangelize
- **When to use**: When developing and rolling out a product vision from discovery through adoption.
- **Steps**:
  1. Empathize: deeply understand customers, the product, competitors, and the problem space.
  2. Create: turn those insights into a compelling future-state vision.
  3. Evangelize: repeatedly socialize the vision across team, stakeholders, and leadership.
- **Example**: At YouTube, she helped set a Vision 2026 and spent over a year socializing it before major investments were funded.

### Top 10 Things You Should Know
- **When to use**: When teams need a living, shared understanding of the most important product problems.
- **Steps**:
  1. Create a living document listing the top 10 problems in the product area.
  2. Include qualitative issues, quantitative issues, and tech debt/infrastructure issues.
  3. Update it regularly, such as every quarter.
  4. Use it across PM, engineering, design, research, and stakeholders to align on the same core problems.
- **Example**: At Uber, she created a “more money, more problems” document for the money team, pairing PMs with data scientists and researchers to maintain a shared problem list.

### Three-Day Strategy Session: Insights, Strategy, Big Rocks
- **When to use**: When aligning a leadership triad around a strategic plan that can later inform roadmap and vision work.
- **Steps**:
  1. Day 1: Insights—review product problems, teardowns, and stakeholder inputs.
  2. Day 2: Strategy—decide which problems matter most and in what order.
  3. Day 3: Big Rocks—identify the 3–5 major initiatives that will move the strategy forward.
- **Example**: At YouTube, she ran strategy sessions with product, engineering, design, research, and sometimes data science to consolidate stakeholder input into a final strategic direction.

### Three Concentric Circles of Evangelism
- **When to use**: When rolling out a vision and building buy-in across an organization.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with the core team who will build the product.
  2. Expand to stakeholders and adjacent teams who influence success.
  3. Then take it to leadership as high as possible.
- **Example**: At Uber, she presented a wallet vision internally and eventually got the opportunity to present it at an all-hands.

### Clarity and Conviction
- **When to use**: When defining the core craft of product management and evaluating PM effectiveness.
- **Steps**:
  1. Bring clarity by distilling the real problems and removing noise.
  2. Create a narrative that explains insights, strategy, and big rocks.
  3. Develop conviction about the future state and the path worth pursuing.
  4. Force prioritization so the team picks a lane instead of spreading resources thin.
- **Example**: She pushed PMs to choose one path rather than presenting leadership with multiple options and asking them to decide.

## Core Advice
- **When you face pressure to create a product vision but don’t know where to start**: do the problem work first and maintain a living list of the top product problems — because a credible vision must be grounded in real, shared user pain.
- **When you face vague or weak product storytelling**: use a simple “Once upon a time... one day... because of that...” narrative — because stories are more memorable and persuasive than abstract strategy language.
- **When you face difficulty getting others excited about a future product**: write a future headline, article, app store page, or rough sketch — because concrete artifacts force clarity and make the destination visible.
- **When you face uncertainty about what users really need**: do “understand work” by using the product, trying competitors, and synthesizing research — because PM judgment comes from direct exposure and pattern recognition.
- **When you face stakeholder chaos and too many requests**: ask each stakeholder group for “10 things you should know” and stack-rank them — because comparable inputs reduce noise and reveal the real priorities.
- **When you face resistance to a long-term vision because of current technical constraints**: vision in a vacuum first, then work backward — because anchoring on today’s limits causes under-imagination.
- **When you face low buy-in for a strategy or vision**: evangelize in layers from team to stakeholders to leadership — because repeated exposure and inclusion build support.
- **When you face roadmap sprawl and too many parallel bets**: choose 3–5 big rocks and force a lane — because conviction requires prioritization.
- **When you face a weak PM-engineering partnership**: learn the human side of your EM — because trust and candor improve when the relationship goes beyond coordination.
- **When you face pressure to become a PM without formal credentials**: do product management before you have the title — because repeated practice builds product sense and readiness.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Vision is mainly for senior leaders or executives. → **Their view**: Every PM, even junior PMs, needs some form of vision — because without a destination, teams lose confidence and alignment.
- **Conventional**: Vision should be tightly constrained by what is technically feasible today. → **Their view**: A good vision should be free from today’s technical limitations — because vision is about time-traveling to the future and shaping what must be built.
- **Conventional**: PMs should rely heavily on research and data to tell them what to build. → **Their view**: Research should inform judgment, but PMs must synthesize and decide — because the craft is in product sense, not just information gathering.
- **Conventional**: If you don’t have design resources, you can’t effectively communicate a product vision. → **Their view**: Sketch it yourself, even crudely — because making the future concrete matters more than polish.
- **Conventional**: Good leaders should aim to be liked. → **Their view**: Leaders should aim to be loved, not liked — because real care includes hard feedback and difficult conversations.
- **Conventional**: Tech debt is mainly an engineering concern. → **Their view**: Infrastructure is the product, and tech debt is product debt — because weak foundations limit what the team can build.
- **Conventional**: PMs should present options neutrally and let leadership choose. → **Their view**: PMs should recommend a path with conviction — because avoiding a point of view avoids responsibility.
