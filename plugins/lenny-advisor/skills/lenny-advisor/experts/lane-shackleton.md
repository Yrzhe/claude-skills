# Lane Shackleton

**Chief Product Officer** | **Coda** | Expertise: product-management, product-strategy, team-rituals, leadership

## Bio
Lane Shackleton is the Chief Product Officer at Coda, where he focuses on how product teams make decisions, build shared understanding, and create operating systems that improve velocity. With experience at Google and YouTube, he brings a practical, systems-oriented view of product leadership that blends strategy, rituals, and team design.

His perspective matters because he treats product management less as feature shipping and more as an organizational craft: reducing ambiguity, aligning stakeholders, and building repeatable habits that compound over time. He is especially useful when a team is stuck in debate, unclear on priorities, or operating with too much friction.

## Signature Frameworks

### Turn ambiguity into clarity
- **When to use**: When the team is unclear on the problem, customer, role, or solution path.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify where ambiguity is showing up.
  2. Clarify the problem, customer, role, or decision space.
  3. Use artifacts, rituals, and discussion to reduce uncertainty.
  4. Iterate until the team shares a common understanding of what matters.
- **Example**: A product team asks: What problem are we solving? Who is the target customer? What prototype would best test the idea?

### Systems, not goals
- **When to use**: When you want durable behavior change instead of a short-lived burst of effort.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define a repeatable system that produces the desired behavior.
  2. Make it default-on, not optional.
  3. Repeat it consistently.
  4. Measure compounding behavior, not just the target outcome.
- **Example**: Coda schedules customer conversations every Friday so the team builds customer intuition through habit, not a quarterly target.

### Cathedrals, not bricks
- **When to use**: When a team is too execution-focused and needs a shared sense of mission.
- **Steps**:
  1. Articulate the broader end-state.
  2. Show each function how it contributes.
  3. Use multiple artifacts to make the future concrete.
  4. Keep the team oriented toward the larger outcome.
- **Example**: Planning includes strategy writeups, metrics, mocks, and homepage concepts so people see the cathedral, not just the bricks.

### Learn by making, not talking
- **When to use**: When a team is debating an idea without enough evidence.
- **Steps**:
  1. Stop abstract debate.
  2. Build a prototype, mock, doc, or experiment.
  3. Test extremes when uncertainty is high.
  4. Use the artifact to create conviction quickly.
- **Example**: On YouTube, Lane’s team tested tiny and giant skip-button variants instead of debating the design for months.

### Two-way writeups
- **When to use**: When decisions are being made through docs or presentations and participation is weak.
- **Steps**:
  1. Write the proposal in prose.
  2. Add explicit read-status signals.
  3. Collect questions in a structured place.
  4. Capture sentiment or pulse.
  5. Treat discussion as part of the artifact.
- **Example**: Coda uses tables for questions and sentiment ratings so feedback is visible and prioritized, not buried in comments.

### Catalyst review system
- **When to use**: When product reviews are bottlenecked by the wrong attendees or sequential meetings.
- **Steps**:
  1. Reserve multiple shared review blocks each week.
  2. Define roles: driver, maker, braintrust, interested.
  3. Let people self-select involvement.
  4. Add topics only when ready.
  5. Use the system to increase throughput and ensure the right people are present.
- **Example**: Coda runs three one-hour review blocks per week and adds topics the day before so multiple reviews can happen in parallel.

### Tag-up
- **When to use**: When project discussions are trapped in one-on-ones and information is getting lost.
- **Steps**:
  1. Move discussion into a group setting.
  2. Review OKRs, metrics, and open topics together.
  3. Let anyone add topics and upvote priorities.
  4. Use the meeting to unblock decisions and align stakeholders.
- **Example**: A PM lead uses a shared table of upcoming decisions and asks stakeholders to mark whether they want to be heavily involved, notified later, or not involved.

### 100-dollar voting
- **When to use**: When planning or prioritization needs hidden disagreement surfaced quickly.
- **Steps**:
  1. List problems, solutions, or themes in a table.
  2. Give each participant a fixed budget, usually $100.
  3. Allocate dollars across items.
  4. Use vote spread to identify real disagreements.
- **Example**: In planning, one person may spend heavily on a problem another sees as irrelevant, forcing the team to discuss the elephant in the room.

### Separate strategy from OKRs
- **When to use**: When planning is muddled because strategy, goals, and metrics are being mixed together.
- **Steps**:
  1. Run a distinct strategy process.
  2. Keep OKR setting separate.
  3. Use OKRs to measure execution, not define strategy.
  4. Choose a strategy framework that fits the company, but keep the separation clear.
- **Example**: Coda intentionally separates strategy discussions from goal-setting discussions.

## Core Advice
- **When you want to know whether someone is growing**: ask how many “oh shit” moments they’ve had recently — because stretch moments are the clearest evidence of real growth.
- **When customer learning is weak**: build a default-on system for customer contact — because habits compound into intuition, while goals fade.
- **When the team is too task-focused**: frame the work as building a cathedral — because people do better when they understand the larger mission.
- **When a decision is stuck in debate**: make a prototype, mock, doc, or experiment — because artifacts reveal reality faster than discussion.
- **When feedback is noisy**: use a calibrated scale like FYI, suggestion, recommendation, plea — because not all feedback deserves equal weight.
- **When product reviews are slow**: use a flexible review system with explicit roles — because it improves throughput and gets the right people in the room.
- **When project work lives in one-on-ones**: move it into a group tag-up — because one-on-ones create a game of telephone.
- **When planning feels politically sensitive**: use $100 voting — because constrained allocation surfaces true priorities.
- **When writing a proposal**: design it as a two-way writeup — because participation, questions, and sentiment should be visible in the artifact.
- **When planning starts eating execution time**: keep planning to about 10% of the period — because overplanning often reflects fear of uncertainty.
- **When you want to grow as a product leader**: read broadly outside tech and notice your repeated advice — because principles emerge across domains.
- **When you’re early in your career**: seek uncomfortable situations — because stretch creates the fastest learning.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Career growth should be measured by whether you feel like you’re growing. → **Their view**: Measure growth by “oh shit” moments — because discomfort is more concrete evidence of stretch.
- **Conventional**: Set ambitious goals and use OKRs to drive execution. → **Their view**: Systems matter more than goals — because repeatable behavior compounds.
- **Conventional**: Product teams should optimize for efficient brick-laying. → **Their view**: Teams need a cathedral — because shared purpose creates coherence and motivation.
- **Conventional**: Debate big decisions extensively before building. → **Their view**: Learn by making immediately — because prototypes beat abstract discussion.
- **Conventional**: One-way docs and presentations are enough. → **Their view**: The future is two-way writeups — because discussion and disagreement should be part of the artifact.
- **Conventional**: Planning can take as long as needed. → **Their view**: Planning should be capped at about 10% of the execution window — because overplanning slows delivery.
- **Conventional**: More feedback is always better. → **Their view**: Feedback must be calibrated — because signal and noise need different weights.
