# Lane Shackleton

**Chief Product Officer** | **Coda** | Expertise: product-management, product-strategy, team-rituals, leadership

## Bio
Lane Shackleton is the Chief Product Officer at Coda, where he thinks deeply about how product teams actually work: how they make decisions, build shared understanding, and create rituals that improve speed and quality. With experience at Google and YouTube, he brings a systems-oriented view of product leadership that emphasizes clarity, learning, and team design over heroics.

His perspective matters because he focuses on the operating system of product teams, not just the output. If you want to improve how a team decides, aligns, learns from customers, and executes consistently, Lane’s approach is especially useful.

## Signature Frameworks

### Turn ambiguity into clarity
- **When to use**: When the team is unclear on the problem, customer, solution, or decision path.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify where ambiguity exists.
  2. Clarify the role, problem, customer, or solution space.
  3. Use artifacts, rituals, and discussion to reduce uncertainty.
  4. Iterate until the team shares the same understanding of what matters.
- **Example**: A PM asks: What problem are we solving? Who is the target customer? What prototype would best test this?

### Systems, not goals
- **When to use**: When you want durable behavior change, learning, or execution.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define a repeatable system that creates the desired behavior.
  2. Make it default-on, not optional.
  3. Repeat it consistently.
  4. Measure the compounding effect of the system, not just the target.
- **Example**: Coda schedules customer conversations every Friday so customer intuition compounds over time.

### Cathedrals, not bricks
- **When to use**: When teams are too execution-focused and need a stronger sense of mission.
- **Steps**:
  1. Articulate the broader mission or end-state.
  2. Show each function how it contributes.
  3. Use multiple artifacts to make the future concrete.
  4. Keep the team oriented toward the larger outcome.
- **Example**: Strategy writeups, metrics, and mocks are used together so the team sees the “cathedral,” not just isolated tasks.

### Learn by making, not talking
- **When to use**: When a team is debating without enough evidence.
- **Steps**:
  1. Stop abstract debate.
  2. Build a prototype, mock, doc, or experiment.
  3. Test extremes if the decision is uncertain.
  4. Use the artifact to build conviction quickly.
- **Example**: On YouTube, the team tested tiny vs. giant skip-button variants instead of debating for months.

### Two-way writeups
- **When to use**: When decisions are made through docs, comments, or presentations and you need better participation.
- **Steps**:
  1. Write the proposal in prose.
  2. Add explicit read signals.
  3. Collect questions in a structured place.
  4. Capture sentiment or pulse.
  5. Treat discussion as part of the artifact.
- **Example**: Coda uses tables for questions and sentiment ratings so feedback is visible and prioritized.

### Catalyst review system
- **When to use**: When product reviews are bottlenecked by the wrong attendees or sequential meetings.
- **Steps**:
  1. Reserve multiple shared review blocks each week.
  2. Define roles: driver, maker, braintrust, interested.
  3. Let people self-select involvement.
  4. Add topics only when ready.
  5. Use the system to increase throughput.
- **Example**: Coda runs three one-hour blocks per week and adds topics the day before.

### Tag-up
- **When to use**: When project work is trapped in one-on-ones and information is getting lost.
- **Steps**:
  1. Move discussion into a group setting.
  2. Review OKRs, metrics, and open topics together.
  3. Let anyone add topics and upvote them.
  4. Use the meeting to unblock decisions and align stakeholders.
- **Example**: A PM lead uses a shared table of upcoming decisions and asks people to mark their involvement level.

### 100-dollar voting
- **When to use**: When planning or prioritization needs hidden disagreement surfaced quickly.
- **Steps**:
  1. List problems or themes in a table.
  2. Give each participant $100.
  3. Allocate dollars across items.
  4. Discuss the spread in votes.
- **Example**: One person may see a problem as huge while another sees it as irrelevant, forcing the team to confront the real disagreement.

### Separate strategy from OKRs
- **When to use**: When planning is muddled because strategy, goals, and metrics are blended together.
- **Steps**:
  1. Run a distinct strategy process.
  2. Keep OKR setting separate.
  3. Use OKRs to measure execution, not define strategy.
  4. Choose a strategy framework that fits the company.
- **Example**: Coda intentionally separates strategy discussions from goal-setting discussions.

## Core Advice
- **When you want to know if someone is growing**: ask how many “oh shit” moments they’ve had recently — because stretch moments are the clearest evidence of real growth.
- **When your team keeps missing customer learning**: build a default-on system for customer contact — because habits compound, while quarterly goals fade.
- **When the team is too task-focused**: frame the work as building a cathedral — because people do better when they see the larger mission.
- **When a decision is stuck in debate**: make a prototype, mock, doc, or experiment — because artifacts reveal reality faster than discussion.
- **When feedback is noisy**: use a calibrated scale like FYI, suggestion, recommendation, plea — because not all feedback deserves equal weight.
- **When product reviews are slow**: use a flexible review system with explicit roles — because it increases throughput and gets the right people in the room.
- **When project work lives in one-on-ones**: move it to a group tag-up — because one-on-ones create a game of telephone.
- **When planning is politically sensitive**: use $100 voting — because constrained allocation reveals true priorities.
- **When writing a decision doc**: make it a two-way writeup — because participation and disagreement should be visible, not buried.
- **When planning starts eating execution time**: keep planning to about 10% of the period — because overplanning often reflects fear of uncertainty.
- **When you want to grow as a product leader**: read broadly outside tech — because strong principles often come from cross-domain thinking.
- **When you’re early in your career**: seek uncomfortable situations — because stretch creates durable capability.

- **Conventional**: Set ambitious goals and use OKRs to drive change. → **Their view**: Systems matter more than goals — because repeatable behavior compounds.
- **Conventional**: Product teams should optimize for efficient execution of tasks. → **Their view**: Teams need a cathedral, not just bricks — because purpose drives coherence and motivation.
- **Conventional**: Debate big decisions extensively before building. → **Their view**: Learn by making immediately — because prototypes beat abstract discussion.
- **Conventional**: One-way docs and presentations are enough for decisions. → **Their view**: Two-way writeups are better — because discussion and sentiment should be part of the artifact.
- **Conventional**: Planning should be as thorough as needed. → **Their view**: Keep planning to about 10% of the execution window — because too much planning slows delivery.
- **Conventional**: More feedback is always better. → **Their view**: Feedback must be calibrated — because signal and noise need different treatment.
