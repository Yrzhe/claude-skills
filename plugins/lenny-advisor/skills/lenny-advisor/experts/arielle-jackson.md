## Bio
Arielle Jackson is a startup marketing and brand strategist who has worked on growth and launches at companies like Google and Square, and now advises early-stage founders at First Round Capital. ## Signature Frameworks

### Startup Naming Framework
- **When to use**: When naming a new company, product, or rebranding an early-stage startup.
- **Steps**:
  1. Do product positioning first so the name is grounded in what the company is and how it should be understood.
  2. Write a naming brief: what you’re naming, what you want the name to communicate, what to avoid, competitor/related names, and constraints.
  3. Evaluate names against core criteria: trademark, domain availability, distinctiveness, timelessness, messaging fit, pronunciation, appearance, and length.
  4. Run a brainstorm with 5–7 people for about an hour, aiming for hundreds of bad ideas and a few promising ones.
  5. Use two modes: word-based exploration from the positioning statement, and thematic brainstorms from history/culture/related domains.
  6. Shortlist 10–25 concepts, then narrow to 3–5 contenders using red/yellow/green scoring.
  7. Check trademark and domain viability before committing; don’t get attached too early.
- **Example**: “Seesaw” works well for an elementary-school edtech product because it evokes childhood and the back-and-forth between teacher, parent, and student.

### Three-Part Brand Strategy Framework
- **When to use**: When building or refreshing a startup brand before visual design, website copy, or launch messaging.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define purpose: why the company exists and what change it wants to create.
  2. Define product positioning: how the target customer should understand the product.
  3. Define personality: how the brand shows up and what kind of “person” it would be.
  4. Use these inputs to guide visual identity, tone of voice, website copy, and style guide.
  5. Document the outputs in a creative brief or brand guide.
- **Example**: Volvo’s brand is not just its logo; it is the meaning built around safety, reinforced by decisions like inventing and freely sharing the three-point seatbelt.

### Purpose Development Exercise
- **When to use**: When clarifying a company’s long-term reason for existing.
- **Steps**:
  1. List the cultural tensions or zeitgeist shifts relevant to your business.
  2. List how your brand at its best improves customers’ lives or the world.
  3. Pick the strongest articulation from each side.
  4. Use prompts like “The world would be a better place if...” and “We exist to...”
  5. Refine to one sentence the founder can say naturally.
- **Example**: For LogicLoop, the purpose became: “to make operations data work harder than operations people.”

### Product Positioning Statement Framework
- **When to use**: When a startup needs a shared explanation of what it does, who it’s for, and why it’s different.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the target audience, narrowing from TAM to the segment you’ll actively acquire over the next 18 months.
  2. Identify the audience’s problem or unmet need.
  3. Describe how they solve it today, including workarounds and incumbents.
  4. Clarify what you make and how it works.
  5. Draft: “For [target audience] who [need], [product] is a [category] that [benefit], unlike [alternative], it [differentiator].”
  6. Pressure-test the language so it sounds like a real customer would say it.
- **Example**: Square Stand’s core benefit was “turn your iPad into a point of sale,” with the old ugly cash register as the foil.

### Concentric Circles Audience Framework
- **When to use**: When founders are being too broad and need to define who they will actively target first.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with the broadest possible market.
  2. Narrow through progressively smaller circles of relevance and fit.
  3. Define the target audience as the segment you’ll actively acquire for the next 18 months.
  4. Create a model persona with age, job, location, motivations, and context.
  5. Ensure each narrower circle fits inside the broader one.
- **Example**: For Eero, the target audience was “tech-savvy dads,” with a detailed persona of a suburban dad with teenage kids, a large brick house, and gaming-heavy Wi-Fi needs.

### Bar Test
- **When to use**: When refining positioning and messaging to make sure it sounds human.
- **Steps**:
  1. Imagine one target customer talking to another at a bar.
  2. Have them say: “I just started using [product]. It’s this [category] that [benefit].”
  3. Let the other person respond naturally.
  4. Explain the differentiator in plain language.
  5. Revise until it sounds like real speech.
- **Example**: A merchant should be able to say Square Stand “turns my iPad into a point of sale.”

### Brand Personality Framework
- **When to use**: When defining tone, creative direction, and brand behavior across copy, design, and social.
- **Steps**:
  1. Choose two strong dimensions from Jennifer Aaker’s five: sincerity, excitement, competence, sophistication, ruggedness.
  2. Don’t stop there; many tech brands cluster too closely.
  3. Define five personality attributes that create tension and specificity.
  4. Write each as “we are X, but not Y.”
  5. Use these attributes to guide writing, visuals, photography, and campaign tone.
- **Example**: Google can be described as “playful but not silly.”

## Core Advice
- **When naming a startup or product**: do positioning first — because a name works best when it’s anchored in what the product is and who it’s for.
- **When you’re chasing the “perfect” name**: don’t overestimate the damage of an imperfect one — because strong products and consistent marketing can make an ordinary name meaningful over time.
- **When you need a temporary company name fast**: choose a deliberately ridiculous code name — because it prevents emotional attachment to a weak placeholder.
- **When customer definitions are fuzzy**: narrow to the segment you’ll actively acquire for the next 18 months — because early startups need focus to sharpen messaging and acquisition.
- **When people describe your company differently**: test whether 10 employees or customers explain it the same way — because inconsistency signals a positioning problem.
- **When your messaging sounds like jargon**: rewrite it using the bar test — because spoken language is easier to understand and repeat.
- **When building a brand from scratch**: define purpose, positioning, and personality before logos or colors — because visuals are only an expression of strategy.
- **When brand work feels like a distraction**: spend a few weeks upfront on it — because it saves time later on copy, website, and decisions.
- **When preparing for a launch or PR**: get the story straight in one sentence first — because reporters can’t write clearly about what you can’t explain clearly.
- **When announcing fundraising**: use the round as a hook for a bigger story — because seed funding alone is less newsworthy than it used to be.
- **When seeking press coverage**: target the right outlet for your stage, including local press if relevant — because many founders overreach for outlets that won’t cover them.
- **When hiring your first marketer**: hire once marketing becomes ongoing and cross-functional — because that work needs an owner, not just freelancers.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: A bad company name can seriously hurt or kill a startup. → **Their view**: A bad name usually won’t kill a good company — because product quality and repeated brand-building matter more.
- **Conventional**: Branding is mostly logos, fonts, and colors. → **Their view**: Brand is what people think you are — because visual identity is only one expression of meaning.
- **Conventional**: Mission, vision, and values should all be separately defined. → **Their view**: Most startups should focus on one memorable purpose statement — because people remember one thing.
- **Conventional**: Startup launches should be broad embargoes across multiple outlets. → **Their view**: Early-stage launches are more realistically exclusives — because media attention is scarcer and seed news is less inherently interesting.
- **Conventional**: The best names are always short, one-syllable, and .com available. → **Their view**: Distinctiveness, memorability, and fit matter more — because many successful companies built meaning into non-ideal names.
- **Conventional**: If many users could benefit, messaging should stay broad. → **Their view**: Start narrow for acquisition — because startups need focus, not maximum inclusivity.
