# Tristan de Montebello

**CEO & co-founder** | **Ultraspeaking** | Expertise: public speaking, communication coaching, performance psychology, consumer education

## Bio
Tristan de Montebello is the co-creator of Ultraspeaking, a public speaking training system built around games, flow, and subconscious skill-building. He became the fastest competitor to reach the finals of the World Championship of Public Speaking, and his perspective matters because he teaches speaking as a performance skill you build through live reps, not theory.

He stands out for treating speaking anxiety, filler words, and rambling as symptoms of deeper state problems. If you want to speak more confidently, spontaneously, and with more presence, Tristan’s approach is especially useful.

## Signature Frameworks

### Root-cause speaking improvement
- **When to use**: When you want to improve speaking anxiety, filler words, monotony, or rambling instead of just masking symptoms.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the visible symptom, such as filler words, rushing, monotone delivery, or hedging.
  2. Ask what internal state or habit is causing it.
  3. Address the root cause through practice rather than trying to suppress the symptom directly.
  4. Repeat until the new behavior becomes automatic and subconscious.
- **Example**: Instead of counting filler words, Tristan suggests fixing the underlying inability to slow down, pause, and relax under pressure.

### Conductor
- **When to use**: When you want to build comfort with changing energy, accessing different emotional states, and speaking with more range and presence.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start speaking on a random prompt.
  2. Respond to changing intensity cues from 1 to 10 by matching your energy and voice.
  3. Pause when a “breathe” cue appears and relax instead of forcing speech.
  4. Notice which energy levels feel easiest or hardest.
  5. Use the exercise to expand your comfort zone across emotional states.
- **Example**: Tristan used the prompt “When I grow up” and shifted through different energy levels as the numbers changed.

### Triple Step
- **When to use**: When you get distracted easily, lose your train of thought, or want to build resilience under cognitive turbulence.
- **Steps**:
  1. Choose a clear direction for your answer.
  2. Speak for a short time while random words appear.
  3. Integrate each word naturally into the speech without abandoning the main direction.
  4. Stay composed even when interrupted by unexpected prompts.
  5. Use the exercise as a warm-up before high-stakes speaking.
- **Example**: In the demo, Tristan had to weave in words like “french fries,” “massage,” “daydreaming,” “cartwheels,” “microwave,” and “internet” while staying on topic.

### Conviction prompts
- **When to use**: When you want to sound more authoritative, increase executive presence, or advocate for an idea with more force.
- **Steps**:
  1. Pick a topic or speech title.
  2. Use sentence starters that imply conviction.
  3. Let your brain complete the sentence while staying in that stronger state.
  4. Advocate for an important idea related to the topic.
  5. Use the resulting energy to shape the content and tone.
- **Example**: For “Space Exploration,” Tristan and Lenny used prompts like “in fact,” “the entire world,” and “we cannot forget that” to strengthen the argument.

### Accordion Method
- **When to use**: When preparing a talk, keynote, all-hands, or interview answer and you want clarity without memorizing a script.
- **Steps**:
  1. Optionally dump your ideas onto paper to get them out of your head.
  2. Speak the talk for a set time, starting with a longer duration.
  3. Stay in character and end strong; do not use a script.
  4. Shorten the time and repeat, removing noise and nonessential material.
  5. Continue compressing until you reach the core essence.
  6. Then expand back up in time, adding back only the best supporting pieces intentionally.
  7. Internalize the talk so it can flex across different lengths.
- **Example**: Tristan described going from 3 minutes to 2 minutes to 1 minute to 30 seconds, then back up again, which left him with a clear, internalized talk.

### Bow and Arrow
- **When to use**: When designing a talk or slide deck and you need the audience to remember one thing clearly.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the one thing you want the audience to remember.
  2. Treat that sentence as the arrow.
  3. Add supporting weight with anecdotes, data, stories, or emotion.
  4. Use the support to pull the bow back and increase impact.
  5. Refine the arrow and bow iteratively as you prepare.
- **Example**: For the accordion method, Tristan said the arrow might be “the accordion method is more powerful than memorizing,” supported by the old-way explanation and the living-room analogy.

## Core Advice
- **When you feel yourself overthinking while speaking**: Treat enjoyment as a barometer and relax back into speaking conversationally, as if talking to a friend — because speaking is a natural, evolved skill; if it stops feeling enjoyable, you are probably forcing it or over-controlling it.
- **When you notice filler words, rushing, or rambling**: Look for the root cause, usually a lack of comfort with pausing, slowing down, or trusting yourself under pressure — because fixing the underlying state removes multiple symptoms at once instead of chasing each symptom separately.
- **When you need to gather your thoughts in a meeting or on Zoom**: Look up instead of down while thinking — because looking up makes you appear thoughtful and confident, while looking down reads as uncertainty or note-checking.
- **When you reach the end of an answer or presentation**: End decisively with a summary prompt like “So to wrap up...” or “In summary...” — because people remember endings strongly, and weak endings can overwrite a strong middle.
- **When you feel tempted to apologize, hedge, or mention your own insecurity mid-speech**: Stay in character and do not leak your doubts to the audience — because most audiences cannot see your internal discomfort; calling attention to it makes them notice problems they otherwise would have ignored.
- **When you want to get better at speaking**: Practice speaking by speaking, not by reading about speaking — because speaking is a performance skill; improvement comes from reps, feedback, and adaptation in the act itself.
- **When your practice feels boring or unsustainable**: Use short, game-like drills with immediate feedback — because enjoyable practice is more likely to continue, and short reps make it easier to learn quickly and stay engaged.
- **When you need to sound more confident or authoritative**: Raise your energy first and let the words follow — because energy drives emotion, and emotion shapes the language and conviction that come out naturally.
- **When you get distracted by interruptions or random prompts**: Choose one clear direction and make everything else serve that direction — because a strong direction creates resilience; you can absorb turbulence without losing the thread.
- **When preparing a talk or slide deck**: Write for what the audience should remember, not for everything you want to say — because audiences usually retain only one thing, so clarity beats completeness.
- **When you have too much content in a talk**: Compress it until only the essential pillars remain, then expand intentionally — because the compression phase reveals the core message and removes noise before you add supporting detail back in.
- **When you fear sounding like a bullshitter**: Use that fear as a compass, but do not let it shut down your ability to speak freely — because people who are sensitive to bullshit are usually not the ones who become bullshitters; they just need to calm the alarm and trust their judgment.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Public speaking is a specialized skill you learn by studying frameworks and techniques. → **Their view**: Speaking is a meta skill and a subconscious flow-oriented process — because when people speak best, they are not consciously managing the speaking itself; they are fully engaged with what they want to convey.
- **Conventional**: You should focus on fixing filler words, pacing, and other visible delivery problems directly. → **Their view**: You should focus on the root cause behind those symptoms — because symptoms are downstream; changing the internal state removes multiple problems at once.
- **Conventional**: Being transparent about your nerves or mistakes makes you more authentic and helps the audience understand. → **Their view**: Leaking insecurity usually hurts you — because audiences often did not notice the issue until you pointed it out.
- **Conventional**: The best way to prepare a talk is to write a script and memorize it. → **Their view**: Preparing by speaking, compressing, and expanding is better than writing and memorizing — because scripts stay in written mode and are hard to memorize; spoken iteration internalizes the talk and makes it flexible.
- **Conventional**: A good presentation should cover as much useful information as possible. → **Their view**: A good presentation should center on one memorable idea and support it with only the necessary evidence — because people usually remember one thing, so overstuffing a talk reduces retention and clarity.
- **Conventional**: Public speaking improvement is mostly about high-stakes performance opportunities. → **Their view**: Low-stakes, playful drills are often better for building the underlying skill and confidence — because games create turbulence safely, let you recover from mistakes, and build resilience before the real stakes arrive.
