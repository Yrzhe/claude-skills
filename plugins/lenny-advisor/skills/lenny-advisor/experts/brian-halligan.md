# Brian Halligan

**In-house CEO coach at Sequoia; co-founder and former CEO of HubSpot** | **Sequoia / HubSpot** | Expertise: leadership, hiring-culture, go-to-market, strategy

## Bio
Brian Halligan co-founded HubSpot and led it as CEO for roughly 15 years, helping scale it from startup to public company. He now coaches many of Sequoia’s fastest-growing founders, which gives him a rare view across both early-stage chaos and scale-stage leadership.

His perspective matters because he is unusually practical about what actually works in hiring, CEO development, organizational design, and crisis management. He is especially useful when a company is moving from founder-led improvisation to disciplined scale.

## Signature Frameworks

### LOCK (plus S) founder evaluation framework
- **When to use**: When evaluating founders/CEOs for investment, coaching, or senior leadership potential.
- **Steps**:
  1. **L**: Assess whether the founder is lovable or at least inspires strong followership.
  2. **O**: Check for deep obsession with the problem, not a recently chosen idea.
  3. **C**: Look for a chip on the shoulder that fuels ambition and resilience.
  4. **K**: Verify deep domain knowledge and founder-market fit.
  5. **S**: Evaluate whether they are a serious student of the craft and constantly learning.
- **Example**: Halligan uses this mental algorithm when meeting CEOs for Sequoia and points to founders like Winston Weinberg (Harvey), James from Profound, and Gabe from Rogo as examples of highly studious founders.

### 2004 Red Sox team-building model
- **When to use**: When building a leadership team during scale-up and deciding between internal talent and external executives.
- **Steps**:
  1. Develop strong homegrown talent through your own “farm system.”
  2. Add a few high-quality external hires selectively.
  3. Mix experienced outsiders with internal culture carriers.
  4. Avoid overloading the team with “been there, done that” hires from big companies.
- **Example**: Halligan compares great team-building to the 2004 Red Sox: a core of homegrown talent plus a few key free agents like David Ortiz and veteran leaders like Pedro Martinez and Curt Schilling.

### DRI ownership rule
- **When to use**: When cross-functional initiatives start failing due to ambiguity, especially after the company grows beyond everyone being in one room.
- **Steps**:
  1. Assign one directly responsible individual to every important initiative.
  2. Give that person enough authority to coordinate across functions.
  3. Avoid shared committee ownership for execution-critical work.
  4. Use the DRI to drive accountability across sales, product, engineering, and services.
- **Example**: Halligan uses the plant metaphor: if two people are asked to water a plant, it either gets overwatered or not watered at all. He says adult-stage CEOs become “religious” about DRI ownership.

### EV > TV > MEV (later CV > EV > TV > MEV)
- **When to use**: When aligning managers and executives to optimize for company and customer outcomes instead of local team incentives.
- **Steps**:
  1. Prioritize customer value first (CV).
  2. Then optimize for enterprise value (EV), the company as a whole.
  3. Then consider team value (TV), your function’s success.
  4. Put personal value (MEV) last.
- **Example**: At HubSpot, Halligan saw leaders optimize for their own department’s metrics, such as sales maximizing bookings while creating downstream service problems. HubSpot reinforced the framework in reviews, public feedback, and awards.

### Crisis over-correction playbook
- **When to use**: When the company experiences a major failure, outage, layoff need, or strategic shock.
- **Steps**:
  1. Deliver the bad news clearly and quickly; don’t drag it out.
  2. Use the crisis as a forcing function to make structural changes.
  3. Over-correct intentionally rather than making timid adjustments.
  4. Create visible organizational learning so the same failure does not recur.
- **Example**: After a major HubSpot outage on the last day of March 2019, Halligan says the company rethought software deployment and reliability standards, especially because CRM downtime is far more damaging than marketing software downtime.

### Startup-to-scale CEO shift: perspiration to inspiration
- **When to use**: When a founder is transitioning from early-stage operator to scale-stage CEO.
- **Steps**:
  1. In startup mode, spend most energy doing the work directly.
  2. As the company scales, shift from hands-on execution to inspiring and aligning the organization.
  3. Let go of more functions while staying close to customers and core decisions.
  4. Expand your trust surface so more leaders can own important work.
- **Example**: Halligan summarizes the shift as startup being 90% perspiration and 10% inspiration, while scale-up becomes 90% inspiration and 10% perspiration.

## Core Advice
- **When hiring C-level or VP leaders**: Use interactive interviews on real company materials and strong blind references — because resume walkthroughs and generic interviews miss how people actually think and operate.
- **When considering a polished big-company executive for a startup**: Be cautious and often prefer startup-context fit or internal talent — because there is often an “impedance mismatch” between mature-company habits and startup reality.
- **When an internal candidate is close to an external candidate**: Give the homegrown person a real shot — because internal leaders carry culture, context, and often get underrated.
- **When you want to know if a candidate will challenge you**: Send them an important internal document under NDA and discuss it critically — because strong leaders should identify issues, not just flatter.
- **When checking references for senior hires**: Ask forced-choice questions like “Would you enthusiastically rehire them?” — because comparative questions produce more honest signal.
- **When layoffs or restructures are necessary**: Act decisively and fully — because repeated partial cuts create repeated trauma and drag out uncertainty.
- **When a crisis hits**: Use it to make bigger structural fixes than feel comfortable — because urgency creates a rare window for lasting improvement.
- **When cross-functional work is slipping**: Assign one powerful DRI — because shared ownership diffuses responsibility.
- **When you are a first-time founder learning management**: Practice hard feedback early and often — because role changes and people decisions are unavoidable at scale.
- **When you feel isolated as a CEO**: Build a peer group of similar-stage CEOs — because many CEO problems are unnatural and easier to solve with peers.
- **When AI increases your company’s speed and options**: Shorten planning cycles and make one-way-door decisions faster — because optionality now has a real tax.
- **When your company is becoming employee-centric at the expense of customers**: Shift rituals and rewards toward customer outcomes — because culture follows what leaders measure and celebrate.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Experienced executives from big-name companies are the safest startup hires. → **Their view**: These hires often fail; homegrown talent and startup-fit matter more — because big-company leaders expect structure startups don’t have.
- **Conventional**: Consensus candidates with the fewest weaknesses are best. → **Their view**: Spikier candidates can be better — because standout strengths and independent thinking matter more than broad polish.
- **Conventional**: Shared ownership and committees improve execution. → **Their view**: Committees are bad for execution-critical work; one DRI should own the outcome — because ambiguity kills accountability.
- **Conventional**: Great CEOs should preserve optionality as long as possible. → **Their view**: Optionality has a massive tax now; decide faster — because AI and speed make distraction more dangerous.
- **Conventional**: The best companies are the best places to work. → **Their view**: Over-indexing on employee happiness can be a mistake — because customer outcomes should come first.
- **Conventional**: Anyone can become a founder/CEO with enough effort. → **Their view**: Not everyone can do it — because the role requires obsession, resilience, and a specific kind of followership.
