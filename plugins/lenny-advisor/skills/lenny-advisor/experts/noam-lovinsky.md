# Noam Lovinsky

**Chief Product Officer** | **Grammarly** | Expertise: product-management, growth, leadership, marketplace

## Bio
Noam Lovinsky is a product leader who has built and scaled products at YouTube, Thumbtack, Facebook, and Grammarly. His perspective matters because he has operated across both 0-to-1 incubation and large-scale turnaround work, especially in marketplaces and growth businesses.

He brings a rare combination of strategic rigor and practical operating experience: how to prioritize for the business, how to rebuild a marketplace when the core loop breaks, and how to create startup-like conditions inside a large company.

## Signature Frameworks

### Prioritize for the business, not just the team or feature
- **When to use**: When deciding what to build or where to focus, especially when customer, team, and business priorities conflict.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start from the company’s broader strategy and what the business is trying to achieve.
  2. Evaluate whether the work creates value for both the customer and the business.
  3. Use guiding principles to resolve tradeoffs when the answer is ambiguous.
  4. Be willing to deprioritize or cancel work if it is not the best use of company resources.
  5. Align the team toward the highest-leverage business outcome.
- **Example**: At Thumbtack, the principle was “customers first, pros second, Thumbtack last,” which pushed the team to improve customer experience because that ultimately helped pros and the business too.

### Rebuild a marketplace by fixing the core loop
- **When to use**: When a marketplace has grown but the experience is too high-friction or the monetization model no longer fits the business stage.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify where the marketplace loop creates friction for customers and supply.
  2. Question whether the current monetization model still matches user intent and liquidity.
  3. Redesign the experience to reduce waiting and increase immediacy.
  4. Change the supply-demand interaction so users see value faster.
  5. Test and iterate across segments until the new model works at scale.
- **Example**: Thumbtack moved from a request-and-wait model to instant quotes, similar to Airbnb’s shift from request-to-book to instant booking.

### Create a startup within a startup by changing the operating model
- **When to use**: When a large company wants to incubate new products or explore 0-to-1 ideas without core-business constraints.
- **Steps**:
  1. Set a different time horizon than the core business.
  2. Use a different incentive system than the standard performance process.
  3. Let the team choose the infrastructure and tools best suited to the problem.
  4. Reduce organizational constraints on customer access, experimentation, and decision-making.
  5. Firewall the team so it can move like a startup while still benefiting from the parent company.
- **Example**: Facebook’s New Product Experimentation team was designed to operate with startup-like freedom inside Meta, including different incentives and infrastructure choices.

### Turn around a struggling business by diversifying growth channels
- **When to use**: When a company is overly dependent on one acquisition channel and that channel becomes unstable.
- **Steps**:
  1. Audit the current growth mix and identify concentration risk.
  2. Go back to first principles on each potential channel.
  3. Experiment with multiple channels instead of relying on one dominant source.
  4. Use data from one channel to strengthen adjacent channels.
  5. Diversify before the primary channel fully breaks.
- **Example**: Thumbtack expanded beyond SEO into paid channels, referrals, SEM, and Facebook after Google changes hurt organic traffic.

### Choose career moves that stretch you while preserving a few strengths
- **When to use**: When evaluating a new role, team, or company and trying to maximize long-term growth without overwhelming yourself.
- **Steps**:
  1. Look for roles that force significant learning and growth.
  2. Make sure the role still lets you lean on one or two core strengths.
  3. Prefer teams with strong people and healthy operating culture.
  4. Accept that growth will be painful and require stamina.
  5. Avoid situations that are too net-new in every dimension.
- **Example**: Noam described choosing roles where he could rely on a couple of known strengths while stretching into new areas like managing larger teams or operating at a bigger strategic scale.

## Core Advice
- **When a project may not be the best use of company resources**: tell leadership early and honestly if the team should be redeployed or the project wound down — because advocating for the business and team can lead to better outcomes.
- **When your manager or operating level feels mismatched**: ask to be layered to a different manager if it will help you do better work — because a better reporting line can improve support, productivity, and learning.
- **When a project is dragging and the team is losing steam**: consider a reset or change of scene — because many projects fail from energy loss before they fail from lack of money.
- **When your business depends heavily on one acquisition channel**: diversify before the main channel dries up — because single-channel growth is fragile, especially with SEO.
- **When a marketplace has long waits or misaligned supply**: reduce friction and move toward instant or near-instant fulfillment — because high-friction loops hurt both customers and supply.
- **When growth is masking underlying issues**: use slower or negative growth as a forcing function to inspect the real problems — because growth can hide product, supply, and monetization flaws.
- **When building a startup-like team inside a large company**: create a different incentive system, time horizon, and operating model — because standard processes can optimize for the wrong behaviors.
- **When incubating new ideas inside a big organization**: let the team choose the infrastructure and tools that fit the problem — because speed and learning matter more than standardization early on.
- **When evaluating a new job or team**: prioritize the quality of the people and the team’s operating health over the brand name — because strong teams are the best predictor of a good experience.
- **When building your career presence**: do the work that feels authentic and energizing rather than forcing networking or content creation — because people get excellent at what genuinely motivates them.
- **When taking on an intimidating new role**: make sure you have at least one or two areas where you already feel competent — because a foundation of known strengths helps you absorb the pain of learning.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Product leaders should always optimize first for user experience or their own team’s goals. → **Their view**: Optimize for the business broadly, even if that means uncomfortable tradeoffs. — because the best outcomes usually align customer value with business value, but when they diverge, the business-level view should guide prioritization.
- **Conventional**: You should stick with a project and avoid talking yourself out of a job. → **Their view**: Sometimes the right move is to tell leadership the project should be canceled or that you should report to someone else. — because healthy teams reward honesty if it improves the organization.
- **Conventional**: A startup inside a big company should be judged by whether it creates the next breakout business. → **Their view**: Incubation teams also create features, recruiting value, organizational learning, and a model for doing things that don’t scale. — because small experiments can reveal what the parent organization cannot do well.
- **Conventional**: The best growth strategy is to double down on the channel that is already working. → **Their view**: Diversify before the channel fails. — because channels like SEO can change abruptly and expose fragility too late.
- **Conventional**: Fast growth means the business is healthy. → **Their view**: Growth can hide serious product and marketplace problems. — because slowing growth is often what reveals the underlying issues.
- **Conventional**: Career success requires heavy self-promotion and online visibility. → **Their view**: You can build a highly successful career by focusing on the work and relationships. — because deep work and authentic collaboration can outperform public posting.
