# Jenny Wen

**Leading design for Claude Cowork; formerly Head of Design for Claude** | **Anthropic** | Expertise: product design, ai-strategy, design leadership, product-management

## Bio
Jenny Wen is a design leader at Anthropic shaping Claude and Claude Cowork, with prior experience leading FigJam and Slides at Figma. Her perspective matters because she has lived through the shift from traditional product design to AI-native product development, where engineering velocity, live model behavior, and rapid iteration are changing what designers do.

She brings a rare blend of craft, leadership, and product judgment. Jenny is especially useful when teams need to rethink design’s role in a world where prototypes are no longer enough and the best work happens in close collaboration with engineers.

## Signature Frameworks

### Two Types of Design Work in the AI Era
- **When to use**: Use when design teams are struggling to adapt to faster AI-assisted engineering velocity and need to redefine where designers add value.
- **Steps**:
  1. Separate design work into execution support versus vision/direction setting.
  2. For execution work, pair closely with engineers and help maintain cohesion rather than blocking shipping.
  3. Use code and prototypes for last-mile polish instead of relying only on static mocks.
  4. For vision work, create shorter-horizon direction, roughly 3–6 months out.
  5. Express vision through prototypes or concrete artifacts that point teams in the right direction.
- **Example**: As engineers can rapidly build features with AI tools, designers spend more time jamming with engineers, polishing in code, and creating short-term prototypes rather than long-range vision decks.

### Build Trust Through Speed
- **When to use**: Use when launching imperfect AI products or research previews that are valuable but not yet polished.
- **Steps**:
  1. Be explicit that the product is early, experimental, or a research preview.
  2. Launch when the user value already outweighs the flaws.
  3. Collect feedback quickly from real users.
  4. Respond visibly to that feedback.
  5. Ship improvements continuously so users see momentum.
- **Example**: Claude Cowork was released as a research preview before it was fully polished, with the team committing to iterate quickly so early release would build trust rather than erode it.

### Legibility Framework
- **When to use**: Use when evaluating frontier ideas, prototypes, or product directions that are hard to understand but seem to generate unusual energy or excitement.
- **Steps**:
  1. Assess whether the idea is legible or illegible to most people.
  2. Assess whether the people behind it can clearly explain it.
  3. Pay special attention to illegible ideas that still create strong internal excitement.
  4. Investigate what users or builders find compelling even if the form factor is unclear.
  5. Translate the underlying value into a clearer product story, UX, or interaction model.
- **Example**: An internal prototype called Claude Studio initially felt dense and confusing, but the team’s excitement revealed useful concepts like plans, to-dos, context, and skills that later informed Claude Cowork.

### Designer Hiring Archetypes
- **When to use**: Use when hiring designers for fast-changing AI product environments where traditional role definitions are breaking down.
- **Steps**:
  1. Look for strong generalists who are excellent across multiple adjacent skills.
  2. Look for deep specialists who are top-tier in one area such as technical design or visual craft.
  3. Look for “craft new grads” who are humble, fast-learning, and not attached to legacy process.
  4. Screen for resilience, adaptability, and willingness to use new tools.
  5. Prioritize evidence of building real things over purely theoretical training.
- **Example**: Jenny’s three most interesting archetypes are strong generalists, deep specialists, and the “craft new grad” who builds a lot and learns quickly.

### Psychological Safety + High Standards
- **When to use**: Use when building high-performing creative teams that need both candor and trust.
- **Steps**:
  1. Create a baseline of psychological safety so teammates feel comfortable speaking openly.
  2. Encourage familiarity and comfort, including light humor when authentic.
  3. Make it clear that people are safe to challenge and be challenged.
  4. Pair that safety with explicit high standards for quality.
  5. Use trust to make direct feedback easier and more productive.
- **Example**: Jenny described teams where people felt comfortable lightly roasting each other and mimicking her recurring phrases in crits, while still knowing she expected excellent work.

## Core Advice
- **When engineers can ship much faster than designers can produce polished mocks**: do less gatekeeping and spend more time pairing with engineers, explaining design principles, and polishing directly in code — because design value shifts toward real-time shaping of execution.
- **When AI behavior is non-deterministic and workflows are unclear**: do more real-product testing with actual models and real user tasks instead of relying heavily on static mocks — because live systems reveal states and edge cases that mockups miss.
- **When you need to launch early without destroying trust**: do an explicit early or research-preview launch, then iterate rapidly and visibly — because users tolerate rough edges if the value is real and the team keeps improving.
- **When debating whether designers should learn to code**: do bring coding tools into your toolkit, even if you do not become a full engineer — because design increasingly includes implementation and last-mile polish.
- **When hiring designers for AI-heavy teams**: do prioritize adaptable builders: strong generalists, deep specialists, and high-agency early-career talent — because the role is changing too quickly for rigid profiles.
- **When managing a rapidly changing craft discipline**: do spend time back in IC work or close to the tools before leading teams through change — because firsthand understanding makes leadership more credible.
- **When a prototype or idea feels confusing but energizes smart people**: do investigate it — because some of the most important opportunities are initially illegible.
- **When developing as a young designer**: do build lots of real projects, share them publicly, and join communities that make and critique things — because practical building creates signal and adaptability.
- **When tempted to optimize only for high-leverage management work**: do take on some low-leverage tasks yourself, like testing the product deeply or fixing bugs — because hands-on work creates trust and product intimacy.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: The classic design process of research, discovery, divergence, convergence, and polished mockups should remain the core of design work. → **Their view**: That process is effectively dead as a default — because AI-assisted engineering and live model behavior make traditional handoff models too slow.
- **Conventional**: Designers primarily add value by producing polished mocks and prototypes before implementation. → **Their view**: A growing share of design value now comes from helping teams execute and polishing directly in code — because the bottleneck has shifted.
- **Conventional**: Long-term product visions should span years and live in strategy decks. → **Their view**: In frontier AI, useful vision is often only 3–6 months out and best expressed as a prototype — because the landscape changes too quickly.
- **Conventional**: Products should launch only when quality is high and polished. → **Their view**: It is often better to launch valuable but imperfect products early — because trust is built through responsiveness, not perfection.
- **Conventional**: Human designers will always retain a durable edge in taste and judgment. → **Their view**: AI will likely get much better at taste, judgment, and design than many expect — because progress in adjacent domains has been faster than people assume.
- **Conventional**: Managers should avoid low-leverage tasks and focus only on scalable work. → **Their view**: Some of the most impactful leadership comes from doing low-leverage tasks yourself — because they create credibility and cultural signal.
- **Conventional**: Managers should maintain professional distance. → **Their view**: Light roasting and playful teasing can be healthy signs of psychological safety — because trust lowers fear and improves candor.
