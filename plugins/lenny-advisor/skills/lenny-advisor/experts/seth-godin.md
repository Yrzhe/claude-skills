# Seth Godin

**Author, entrepreneur, and strategic thinker** | **Seths.blog / independent** | Expertise: strategy, branding, product-management, marketing, ai-strategy, go-to-market

## Bio
Seth Godin is one of the most influential modern thinkers on marketing, strategy, and product positioning. His perspective matters because he consistently reframes business decisions around trust, empathy, and the systems that shape behavior—not just tactics or hype.

He brings a rare combination of product management discipline and big-picture strategic thinking. If you want to understand how to build something people will remember, recommend, and pay for, Seth is especially useful.

## Signature Frameworks

### Promise-and-keep-it branding
- **When to use**: When building or repositioning a product or company brand, especially in crowded markets.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the brand as a promise, not a logo.
  2. Choose a difficult, remarkable promise that matters to the user.
  3. Make clear what you do and what you do not do.
  4. Deliver consistently so customers know what to expect.
  5. Use trust and consistency to create loyalty and willingness to pay extra.
- **Example**: Nike and Hyatt would create very different expectations if they swapped categories; the brand is the promise people expect to be kept.

### Choose-your-future strategy
- **When to use**: When making foundational product or business decisions that will shape the company’s trajectory.
- **Steps**:
  1. Choose your customers and define the smallest viable audience.
  2. Choose your competition by deciding what category and market you are really in.
  3. Choose your source of validation so you know whose taste you are serving.
  4. Choose your distribution channel because it changes the product and the business model.
- **Example**: The Humane Pin is a cautionary example of a product aimed at people expecting Apple-level polish before the product had earned that expectation.

### Remarkability / word-of-mouth design
- **When to use**: When you need growth without relying primarily on paid acquisition or platform luck.
- **Steps**:
  1. Build something that improves the user’s life if they talk about it.
  2. Know in advance what you want people to say.
  3. Make the product worth making a remark about.
  4. Design for customer traction: retention first, then referrals.
- **Example**: Google’s simple homepage was remarkable because it promised to get users where they wanted to go without clutter.

### System-aware strategy
- **When to use**: When trying to change behavior, enter a mature market, or understand why a “risky” move feels scary.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the invisible system you are operating inside.
  2. Name the rules the system is reinforcing.
  3. Notice when culture is protecting the system rather than serving users.
  4. Find leverage points that let you change the rules or work around them.
- **Example**: Hiring systems often reward people who resemble the existing team, which reinforces sameness and blocks real talent.

### Tension-centered innovation
- **When to use**: When launching a new product, writing, or creating any work that depends on belief before proof.
- **Steps**:
  1. Make a promise about what the product can do.
  2. Let the customer imagine their life if the promise is true.
  3. Create tension by making the outcome uncertain but plausible.
  4. Resolve the tension by telling the truth and delivering on the promise.
- **Example**: PF Flyers promised to make a kid fast enough to run away from a bully, but the promise did not hold up, turning tension into disappointment.

### Empathy-first product management
- **When to use**: When building software or leading a product team.
- **Steps**:
  1. Treat the customer’s experience as the spec.
  2. Assume the customer is not the problem when something is confusing.
  3. Meet the spec or improve the spec; do not hide behind perfectionism.
  4. Optimize for delighting the intended user rather than pleasing your boss.
- **Example**: At Spinnaker, he learned that if the intended buyer was delighted with the shipped software, it was good; excuses did not matter.

## Core Advice
- **When you face a crowded market full of similar products**: Make a difficult, specific promise to the user and then keep it consistently — because a brand is an expectation, not a logo.
- **When you are tempted to ship something that merely meets the minimum**: Improve the spec in service of the customer, but don’t confuse high standards with perfectionism — because the job is to delight the intended user.
- **When you are building software and hoping users will spread the word**: Design the product so users benefit when they tell others about it — because word of mouth is strongest when sharing helps the sharer.
- **When you are choosing a target market**: Pick the smallest viable audience instead of trying to be for everyone — because customer choice shapes the product, pricing, and language.
- **When you are deciding who will judge your work**: Choose validation from the people you actually serve, not from your boss’s taste — because optimizing for the wrong evaluator distorts the product.
- **When you are planning distribution**: Design the product around the channel you will actually use, because distribution changes the product.
- **When you are considering a risky move that feels scary**: Ask which system is being protected by that fear before deciding not to act — because systems often make change feel dangerous.
- **When you are hiring or evaluating candidates**: Look for real capability rather than people who merely resemble the current team — because similarity bias blocks talent.
- **When you are trying to build trust in a new category**: Use a few clear, memorable product choices that create a story people can repeat — because remarkable products are easier to recommend.
- **When you are leading a team or project**: Don’t run out of time or money; treat deadlines and budgets as part of the professional obligation — because professionals anticipate surprises.
- **When you are building a product for a specific category**: Choose the wave, not just the surfer — because market timing and team fit matter as much as effort.
- **When you are writing, editing, or thinking through an idea**: Use AI to identify what you missed and where your claims are unsupported — because a patient editor can surface gaps quickly.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: A brand is mainly a logo, visual identity, or marketing campaign. → **Their view**: A brand is a promise and an expectation of what it will be like to use the product. — because people pay for consistent expectations.
- **Conventional**: High standards mean polishing until something feels perfect. → **Their view**: High standards mean improving the spec for the customer and shipping once the spec is met. — because perfectionism can be a form of hiding.
- **Conventional**: AI companies should differentiate through more features, more hype, or more buzz. → **Their view**: AI will become a commodity-like feature; differentiation comes from the promise you make and keep. — because the user experience becomes the real brand.
- **Conventional**: Buzz and controversy are good substitutes for product-market fit. → **Their view**: Buzz does not sell; customer traction does. — because attention without transformation is temporary.
- **Conventional**: You should try to please your boss or internal stakeholders first. → **Their view**: Optimize for the specific customers you chose to serve. — because the wrong evaluator leads to misaligned decisions.
- **Conventional**: Fear means you should probably avoid the move. → **Their view**: Fear often signals that the system is being challenged, not that the move is wrong. — because systems protect themselves.
