# Gaurav Misra

**CEO and co-founder** | **Captions** | Expertise: ai-strategy, product-management, go-to-market, design

## Bio
Gaurav Misra is the CEO and co-founder of Captions, a consumer AI video creation company with over 10 million users. He previously led product and design engineering at Snap, and his perspective matters because he combines product taste, design intuition, and startup speed with a strong bias toward shipping AI-native consumer experiences.

He is especially useful when you need to decide what to build in a noisy AI market, how to ship faster without losing user trust, and how to turn novel product ideas into something people actually share and use.

## Signature Frameworks

### Every engineer ships a marketable feature every week
- **When to use**: When operating in a fast-moving startup environment with many possible AI opportunities and a need to stay top of mind with users.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define a “marketable feature” as something users would come to the app or pay for on its own.
  2. Set the expectation that each engineer ships one such feature every week.
  3. Launch features as true MVPs rather than polished full versions.
  4. Observe which features users adopt, complain about, or ignore.
  5. Double down on the features that show traction and expand them in subsequent weeks.
- **Example**: At Captions, the engineering goal is that every engineer ships a marketable product every week, using rapid MVP releases to discover what resonates.

### Ruthless scope cutting
- **When to use**: When a team is under time pressure and needs to ship quickly without compromising core quality.
- **Steps**:
  1. List every element of the proposed feature that takes time to build.
  2. Ask of each element: if we remove this, is the product still useful?
  3. Keep removing pieces until any further cut would make the product useless.
  4. Ship that smallest still-useful version.
  5. Use user complaints to determine what to add next.
- **Example**: For adding images to video, cut background removal, hue/saturation controls, cloud import, and custom UI until only native camera-roll picking into the video remains.

### Technical debt as startup leverage
- **When to use**: When a startup needs to move faster than larger incumbents and can defer non-critical infrastructure work.
- **Steps**:
  1. Treat technical debt as a strategic tool, not something to avoid entirely.
  2. Ask whether a problem must be solved today or can be solved by a future engineer.
  3. Take on debt only if it meaningfully accelerates shipping and learning now.
  4. Monitor the “interest payments” in bugs, crashes, maintenance, and lost engineering time.
  5. Pay down debt before interest consumes too much of the team’s capacity.
- **Example**: Captions uses Q4 as an infrastructure quarter to pay down debt accumulated during faster product shipping periods.

### Public roadmap vs. secret roadmap
- **When to use**: When balancing user-requested improvements with breakthrough ideas that competitors cannot easily anticipate.
- **Steps**:
  1. Maintain a public roadmap of features users explicitly request.
  2. Prioritize those requests based on impact and market size.
  3. Separately maintain a secret roadmap of ideas nobody asked for.
  4. Generate secret-roadmap ideas from unique insight into users, technology, and behavior change.
  5. Launch those ideas without pre-announcing them and observe whether they change user behavior.
- **Example**: Captions’ eye-contact feature was a secret-roadmap win: users had not asked for it, but it became highly viral and widely copied after launch.

### Virality as a prioritization signal
- **When to use**: When deciding which ideas in a noisy AI landscape are worth building.
- **Steps**:
  1. Look for ideas, demos, or concepts that people naturally share and talk about.
  2. Use social media and trend observation to identify what is resonating.
  3. Test interest before building by talking about the idea or showing the concept.
  4. Identify the core element driving the virality.
  5. Adapt that core into a repeatable product or business use case.
- **Example**: Captions spends significant time on social media to spot trends and uses virality as an early read on what may resonate before building.

### Design-first exploration
- **When to use**: When metrics and specs alone may not reveal novel product opportunities.
- **Steps**:
  1. Have designers explore concepts even before the team fully knows the product rationale.
  2. Review multiple designs with PMs and other stakeholders.
  3. Look for surprising ideas or patterns that emerge from the designs.
  4. Use those discoveries to shape product direction.
  5. Ship quickly enough that user feedback can validate the concept.
- **Example**: Captions sometimes starts by designing many things first, then reviews them with PMs until interesting ideas emerge that would not have surfaced from metrics alone.

### Storytelling vs. documentation safety framework for AI video
- **When to use**: When deciding which AI video use cases to enable versus restrict.
- **Steps**:
  1. Classify video use cases into documentation or storytelling.
  2. Treat documentation use cases as high-risk because realism can mislead people about reality.
  3. Avoid making it easy to use AI video for documentary or evidentiary scenarios.
  4. Treat storytelling use cases as positive-sum when audiences understand the content is fabricated or performative.
  5. Design product constraints and affordances to make storytelling easy and deceptive documentation hard.
- **Example**: Captions wants to avoid enabling fake documentary-style content while embracing ads, entertainment, social posts, and other storytelling formats.

## Core Advice
- **When you face too many possible AI product directions**: Do prioritization based on user demand signals and especially virality before building deeply — because virality often reveals that some core element genuinely resonates.
- **When you face pressure to ship faster**: Do scope cuts, not quality cuts — because a smaller feature that works teaches you more and preserves trust.
- **When you face uncertainty about whether a feature is worth investing in**: Do the smallest version that is still useful, ship it, and let user complaints guide the next iteration — because users quickly reveal what matters.
- **When you face the need to move faster than larger competitors**: Do take on strategic technical debt, but track the maintenance burden as interest — because debt creates leverage until it starts consuming capacity.
- **When you face a roadmap dominated by user requests**: Do split your roadmap into a public roadmap and a secret roadmap — because breakthrough wins often come from ideas users could not have asked for.
- **When you face weak differentiation from competitors**: Do build features that are marketable on their own rather than only table-stakes improvements — because users will overlook some missing basics for something uniquely compelling.
- **When you face ambiguity in product discovery**: Do use design-first exploration in addition to PM-led specs and metrics — because novel ideas often emerge from visualizing possibilities.
- **When you face hiring for product roles in an AI startup**: Do look for PMs who can think through marketing and distribution, not just feature definition — because acquisition is part of the product journey.
- **When you face internal alignment problems in a larger organization**: Do build prototypes people can actually try and let them spread internally — because hands-on prototypes create internal virality faster than docs.
- **When you face a potentially deceptive AI media use case**: Do distinguish between storytelling use cases and documentation use cases before shipping — because this captures upside while reducing harm.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Startups should minimize technical debt and keep systems clean from the beginning. → **Their view**: A startup’s job is to take on technical debt strategically because that is how it moves faster than bigger companies — because debt creates leverage, and future engineers can pay it down if the company wins.
- **Conventional**: When deadlines tighten, teams should trim quality to hit the date. → **Their view**: Teams should cut scope aggressively instead of cutting quality — because a narrow feature that works preserves trust and gives cleaner learning.
- **Conventional**: The roadmap should primarily reflect what users ask for. → **Their view**: The biggest wins often come from a secret roadmap of ideas nobody asked for — because if users are asking for it, competitors already know about it too.
- **Conventional**: PMs own product, while marketing owns acquisition and messaging. → **Their view**: PMs should think all the way through marketing because acquisition surfaces are part of the product journey — because first impression and activation are product problems too.
- **Conventional**: User research should directly tell teams what to build. → **Their view**: What matters more is building a deep internal mental model of the user, then using that model to make product decisions — because the best bets often come from insight, not explicit requests.
- **Conventional**: More process and specialization improve product development as companies scale. → **Their view**: Innovation often comes from hybrid operators who combine functions like design+PM or design+engineering — because fewer handoffs create more discovery.
