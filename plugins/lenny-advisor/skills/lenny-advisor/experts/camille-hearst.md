# Camille Hearst

**Head of Fan Monetization** | **Spotify** | Expertise: creator economy, marketplace strategy, pricing, product-management

## Bio
Camille Hearst is a product leader who has spent her career at the intersection of creators, marketplaces, and monetization. She has led creator products at Patreon, worked on creator experiences at YouTube, helped shape iTunes at Apple, and founded Kit, a creator-curation startup acquired by Patreon.

Her perspective matters because she has seen the creator economy from multiple angles: as a platform builder, marketplace operator, and founder. She is especially useful when you need practical advice on how creators grow, how marketplaces actually work, and how to build monetization without losing sight of product craft.

## Signature Frameworks

### Dual-track agile with risk-first discovery
- **When to use**: When building new products or features with meaningful uncertainty and you need to balance innovation with execution.
- **Steps**:
  1. Run discovery and delivery simultaneously rather than sequentially.
  2. Continuously identify the riskiest assumptions behind your ideas.
  3. Prioritize de-risking the biggest swings first instead of defaulting to safer, smaller bets.
  4. Use fast learning loops to turn unknowns into predictable execution work.
  5. Once risk is reduced, move validated ideas into delivery and ship.
- **Example**: Instead of letting design work for months before engineering sees anything, teams test assumptions continuously and focus first on the highest-risk opportunities.

### Marketplace supply-first strategy
- **When to use**: When building or scaling a two-sided marketplace where demand quality depends on reliable supply.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the critical supply side of the marketplace.
  2. Solve real pain points for suppliers so they can reliably participate.
  3. Ensure enough supply exists before over-optimizing demand acquisition.
  4. Use demand-side optimization only after the marketplace can fulfill user intent.
  5. Keep supplier satisfaction central because marketplace health depends on it.
- **Example**: At Hailo, no amount of app polish or marketing mattered if riders opened the app and there were no cars available.

### Creator success playbook
- **When to use**: When trying to grow as an independent creator or helping creators succeed on a platform.
- **Steps**:
  1. Publish consistently and predictably over time.
  2. Collaborate with other creators to share audiences.
  3. Build relationships through events or direct connection to encourage cross-pollination.
  4. Associate with trusted curators whose audience and taste align with your work.
- **Example**: At YouTube, consistency was one of the strongest predictors of creator success; at Kit, creator events were used to spark collaborations and audience sharing.

### Acquisition process management
- **When to use**: When a startup is considering a sale or wants to preserve acquisition as a strategic option.
- **Steps**:
  1. Treat selling the company like a managed process, similar to fundraising.
  2. Build a list of potential acquirers and cultivate relationships early.
  3. Share your vision over time so potential buyers understand your company before a formal process starts.
  4. Frame early conversations around alignment or partnership, not immediately around acquisition.
  5. Run a structured process rather than waiting passively for inbound interest.
- **Example**: Hearst believes Kit should have been talking to potential acquirers from the beginning so those relationships were already warm.

## Core Advice
- **When you face uncertainty about a new product idea**: Do discovery and delivery in parallel, and test the riskiest assumptions first — because it speeds learning and avoids waterfall handoffs.
- **When you are building a marketplace from scratch**: Start with supply and solve supplier pain points before over-investing in demand — because demand is wasted if the marketplace cannot fulfill it.
- **When you are growing as a creator**: Commit to consistent publishing and collaborate with other creators — because consistency compounds trust and collaboration expands reach.
- **When you feel awkward charging for creative work**: Let the market help determine value and use platforms that handle pricing and payments — because creators often need infrastructure to reduce emotional friction around monetization.
- **When your startup may eventually be acquired**: Build relationships with potential acquirers early — because familiarity and strategic context improve outcomes later.
- **When you are starting a company for creators**: Pick a specific, real creator problem rather than generic tooling — because the space is broad, but winning products solve acute pain.
- **When pressure pushes teams toward only safe bets**: Give teams permission to explore bigger swings and take accountability for failed experiments — because breakthrough products rarely come from low-risk work alone.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Marketplace businesses are mainly a chicken-and-egg problem where both sides should be treated equally from the start. → **Their view**: Start with supply — because demand generation is useless if users cannot actually get what they came for.
- **Conventional**: Creators mainly need better monetization tools first. → **Their view**: Audience growth is often more fundamental — because audience creates the optionality that makes monetization possible.
- **Conventional**: Artists naturally want to monetize fandom as much as possible. → **Their view**: Many musicians resist monetization and want to give everything away — because the “starving artist” ethos is real and emotionally powerful.
- **Conventional**: Thinking about acquisition early is a distraction or sign of weak ambition. → **Their view**: Prepare to sell from day one — because early relationship-building preserves strategic options.
- **Conventional**: Product management should be primarily metrics- and strategy-led. → **Their view**: Design- and craft-led product thinking can be highly effective — because consumer products often win through feel, messaging, and taste.
