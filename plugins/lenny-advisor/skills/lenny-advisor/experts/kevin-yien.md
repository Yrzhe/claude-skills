# Kevin Yien

**Head of Product for Merchant Experiences** | **Stripe** | Expertise: product-management, leadership, hiring-culture, user-research, strategy, design

## Bio
Kevin Yien leads product for merchant experiences at Stripe and has built products and teams at Square and Mutiny. His perspective matters because he combines deep product craft with unusually tactical opinions on writing, hiring, customer research, and team dynamics.

He is especially valuable when you need a sharp, opinionated view on how product teams should make decisions, how PMs should operate, and when a company should or should not hire a PM at all.

## Signature Frameworks

### PM as conversion of potential energy into realized customer value
- **When to use**: When defining what product management actually does, or deciding whether a team needs a PM.
- **Steps**:
  1. View the team as having potential energy across engineering, design, sales, and support.
  2. Use product management to convert that potential into realized value with minimal loss.
  3. Recognize that the people closest to the work should initially do the PM-like activities.
  4. Add a PM when the team needs someone to delegate decision-making and coordination to because the space is complex or the builders are not the customer.
- **Example**: Kevin pointed to Stripe, Twilio, and Figma as companies that could delay PM hiring because they were building for themselves.

### Draw the perimeter
- **When to use**: When leading a product team and you want engineers and designers to generate strong solutions without over-constraining them.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the customer segment clearly.
  2. Clarify the job to be done and the pathways you are willing to entertain.
  3. Set key platform or channel constraints, such as desktop web vs mobile vs native.
  4. State the product principles or tradeoffs that matter most, such as speed vs consistency.
  5. Remove unnecessary decisions so the team can focus on creative execution inside the box.
- **Example**: For a restaurant POS, Kevin would frame whether the product is for a sit-down restaurant with a huge wine list or a taco truck, because that constraint radically changes the solution space.

### Tuning fork feedback
- **When to use**: When you need high-quality feedback on a PRD, strategy doc, or product concept.
- **Steps**:
  1. Create a rough first pass instead of asking for abstract opinions.
  2. Put the concrete artifact in front of people and ask them to react to it.
  3. Use a silent read in a live meeting so reviewers can focus.
  4. Comment and respond in real time to compress feedback latency.
  5. Rev the document immediately based on the discussion.
- **Example**: At Square, Kevin would force a 20-minute doc review where everyone read silently and commented live instead of waiting for async feedback that might never come.

### Decision log practice
- **When to use**: When you want to improve product sense, decision quality, or interview judgment over time.
- **Steps**:
  1. Write down the decision you made and the rationale behind it.
  2. Include decisions from your own work and from other teams or companies.
  3. Set a future reminder to review the outcome.
  4. Compare what you predicted with what actually happened.
  5. Repeat consistently so decision-making becomes a trained skill.
- **Example**: Kevin logged his prediction that Shopify’s Shop app was a strategic move to own the consumer buying loop, then revisited it later as the product evolved.

### Unsell email hiring filter
- **When to use**: When making an offer and you want to avoid hiring people who will be unhappy or misaligned after joining.
- **Steps**:
  1. Collect the candidate’s hidden fears and concerns during the interview process.
  2. Wait until offer stage so you know what actually matters to them.
  3. Send a candid email listing the hard truths, tradeoffs, and likely pain points.
  4. Let the candidate react honestly to the reality of the role.
  5. Proceed only if they remain equally excited after seeing the downsides.
- **Example**: Kevin would tell startup candidates they might need to be online at 10 p.m. or take weekend calls; if they still wanted the job, he considered them an A+ hire.

### Automated customer research pipeline
- **When to use**: When you need a steady stream of customer conversations without manually sourcing every interview.
- **Steps**:
  1. Use a source of customer signal such as Gong, support forums, Reddit, or Twitter.
  2. Set alerts for relevant terms, competitors, or pain points.
  3. Push those alerts into Slack or another workflow hub.
  4. Use automation to extract the customer identity and email.
  5. Trigger outreach with a research-specific Calendly link so interviews get scheduled automatically.
- **Example**: At Mutiny, a Gong alert could post to Slack, then Zapier could email the customer and drop in a Calendly link for user research.

## Core Advice
- **When you want to become a great PM but are early in your career**: Start as an engineer, designer, or salesperson before moving into PM — because those roles give you the front-line reps in building, listening, and translating customer needs.
- **When your team is building for itself or the space is simple**: Delay or skip hiring a PM — because the builders can often make decisions directly without an intermediary.
- **When you are writing product docs or PRDs**: Write with clarity, crispness, and the voice of the customer you serve — because writing is clarity at scale.
- **When your writing feels flat or hard to follow**: Vary sentence length and cadence intentionally — because monotony makes readers tune out.
- **When you need better feedback on a product idea or doc**: Bring a rough draft to a live silent-read session — because concrete artifacts produce better feedback than abstract discussion.
- **When you are tempted to keep a decision vague**: Add constraints to remove unnecessary choices for the team — because the best decision is often no decision.
- **When you are prioritizing product work**: Protect time for the most important customer-facing detail, even if it means slipping an arbitrary launch date — because real value comes from the final experience, not internal busyness.
- **When you want to get better at product sense**: Keep a decision log and review outcomes later — because product sense improves through repeated reps plus reality checks.
- **When you are trying to build a decision-making habit**: Start with a tiny weekly practice, like logging one market observation and one prediction — because small habits are easier to sustain.
- **When you are hiring and want to avoid bad fits**: Be brutally honest about the downsides of the role before the candidate accepts — because front-loading hard truths filters out misaligned people.
- **When a candidate raises a concern during hiring**: Treat it as a signal to go deeper and adapt your process — because strong hiring requires real investment in the individual.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Great PMs should be generalists who can jump straight into product management. → **Their view**: The best path into PM is often to start as an engineer, designer, or salesperson first — because those roles build stronger judgment.
- **Conventional**: Every team needs a product manager. → **Their view**: Not every team needs a PM, especially if the team is building for itself — because the people closest to the work can often own the decisions.
- **Conventional**: PMs can be effective with average writing skills. → **Their view**: Great PMs need to be great writers — because writing is clarity at scale.
- **Conventional**: Feedback should be gathered asynchronously to avoid meetings. → **Their view**: A live silent-read meeting is often better — because it reduces latency and improves iteration speed.
- **Conventional**: Hiring should optimize for closing candidates efficiently. → **Their view**: You should actively unsell the role — because honesty prevents churn and disappointment later.
- **Conventional**: User research is mainly the job of UX researchers or can be consumed through reports. → **Their view**: PMs need direct exposure to raw customer material and should automate access to it — because secondhand research is filtered.
- **Conventional**: A layoff or rejection means you are not good enough. → **Their view**: It often means the company’s needs and your strengths are misaligned — because fit is environment-dependent.
