# Wes Kao

**Coach, entrepreneur, advisor, co-founder of Maven** | **Maven** | Expertise: communication, teaching, cohort-based courses, managing up

## Bio
Wes Kao is a coach and entrepreneur best known as the co-founder of Maven, a platform for cohort-based courses. She previously co-founded the altMBA with Seth Godin and has advised many creators and operators on communication, writing, and course design.

Her perspective matters because she thinks deeply about how ideas land in real life: in meetings, docs, pitches, and live teaching. Wes is especially useful when you need communication that is clear, high-trust, and actually changes behavior.

## Signature Frameworks

### Super Specific How
- **When to use**: When writing, teaching, or explaining a concept that your audience already broadly accepts.
- **Steps**:
  1. Assume the audience already agrees with the general premise.
  2. Spend less time on the what and why.
  3. Focus on the how: implementation, nuance, and application.
  4. Use concrete examples that show how the idea works in practice.
  5. Cut preamble and backstory that do not help the reader act.
- **Example**: Instead of explaining why communication matters for product managers, explain how to get buy-in without positional authority or how to communicate across multiple stakeholders.

### Start Right Before You Get Eaten by the Bear
- **When to use**: When telling stories, writing posts, or presenting and you want to avoid unnecessary setup.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the most interesting or high-stakes moment in the story.
  2. Remove early setup that does not change understanding of the key moment.
  3. Start just before the juicy part.
  4. Add only the minimum context needed for the audience to follow along.
- **Example**: In a camping story, skip the REI trip, campsite booking, and drive details; start when the friend left a Clif Bar in the tent and the bear showed up.

### Content Hierarchy of BS
- **When to use**: When choosing a format for sharing ideas and when pressure-testing your own claims.
- **Steps**:
  1. Recognize that some formats allow more unsupported claims than others.
  2. Use one-directional, low-accountability formats carefully because they permit more BS.
  3. Move toward formats with more interaction and challenge when rigor matters.
  4. In high-accountability formats, be prepared to defend claims and answer questions.
  5. Use the format itself as a quality filter for your thinking.
- **Example**: Twitter and keynote speeches allow more BS than long-form articles, books, and especially cohort-based courses where students can challenge you live.

### State Change Method
- **When to use**: When running Zoom meetings, presentations, webinars, or any monologue-heavy session.
- **Steps**:
  1. Avoid long stretches of uninterrupted talking.
  2. Insert a state change every few minutes or every few slides.
  3. Change the audience's mode of participation or attention.
  4. Use tools like chat, polls, screen share, breakouts, unmuting, or having someone else speak.
  5. Return to the main thread after the interaction.
- **Example**: In a presentation, ask the audience to guess an answer, then reveal it, then switch to screen share, then back to discussion.

### Eyes Light Up Detection
- **When to use**: When pitching, teaching, interviewing, or explaining something and you want to find what truly resonates.
- **Steps**:
  1. Watch for moments when the listener's face changes from polite attention to genuine interest.
  2. Treat facial and vocal cues as data, not just verbal responses.
  3. Note the exact phrase, angle, or example that triggered the reaction.
  4. Trim away the parts that cause people to go dead in the eyes.
  5. Double down on the language and framing that create energy.
- **Example**: In a sales pitch or internal meeting, notice when someone leans forward or their eyes light up after hearing a specific benefit, then use that framing more often.

### Manage Up Proactively
- **When to use**: When working with a manager or senior stakeholder and you want more trust, autonomy, and opportunities.
- **Steps**:
  1. Keep your boss informed about what you're working on and the decisions you're making.
  2. Share enough context for them to weigh in without overwhelming them.
  3. Distinguish reversible from hard-to-reverse decisions.
  4. Surface trade-offs and priorities proactively.
  5. Avoid surprises by communicating early and often.
- **Example**: A weekly “state of me” email with priorities, blockers, and things on your mind keeps a manager aligned and reduces surprise.

### Trade-Off-Based No
- **When to use**: When you need to decline a request without damaging the relationship.
- **Steps**:
  1. Acknowledge the request warmly.
  2. Explain what would need to move if you take it on.
  3. Make the trade-off explicit in terms of priorities and bandwidth.
  4. Invite the requester to help decide what should be prioritized.
  5. Use this especially in ongoing relationships like manager, teammate, or collaborator.
- **Example**: “Yes, I can design this PDF, but that means the page redesign I was working on will move later this week. Does that trade-off make sense?”

### Intentional Recommendation Writing
- **When to use**: When writing Slack messages, emails, docs, or decision memos where you need clarity and trust.
- **Steps**:
  1. State your recommendation explicitly if you have one.
  2. Separate recommendation from supporting context.
  3. Use pros and cons honestly, without skewing the structure.
  4. Put the conclusion or TLDR first.
  5. Add context below for readers who want the reasoning.
- **Example**: Start with “My recommendation is X,” then list pros, cons, risks, and supporting context, rather than burying the conclusion at the end.

## Core Advice
- **When your audience already agrees with the general idea**: Spend your energy on the how: implementation, nuance, and concrete examples — because people usually do not need more persuasion on the premise; they need help applying it in real life.
- **When you're writing or telling a story and the setup is getting long**: Cut the preamble and start closer to the most interesting moment — because extra backstory often adds little value and dilutes attention.
- **When you're creating content in a format that is one-directional and low-accountability**: Pressure-test your claims more aggressively before publishing — because formats like Twitter or keynote talks allow unsupported assertions to sound smarter than they are.
- **When you're running a Zoom meeting or presentation**: Insert a state change every few minutes to reset attention — because monologues are draining; variety keeps people awake and engaged.
- **When you want to know what actually resonates in a pitch or explanation**: Watch for eyes-light-up moments and use them as feedback — because facial reactions often reveal genuine interest more reliably than polite verbal responses.
- **When you're working with your manager**: Keep them proactively in the loop on decisions, priorities, and blockers — because managers trust people who reduce uncertainty and prevent surprises.
- **When a decision is hard to reverse or expensive to undo**: Escalate with more context and invite feedback before acting — because the more irreversible the decision, the more important it is to align early.
- **When someone asks you to take on more work than you can handle**: Frame your response as a trade-off rather than a flat no — because this protects the relationship while making bandwidth constraints explicit.
- **When you need to communicate a decision in writing**: Put the recommendation or TLDR first, then add context below — because readers can stop early if aligned, or continue if they need the rationale.
- **When you write pros and cons**: Be honest about your recommendation instead of hiding it inside a supposedly neutral list — because skewed “objective” lists reduce trust and confuse the reader.
- **When you're tempted to skip applying because your submission won't be perfect**: Put your foot forward before you reject yourself — because you often lose opportunities by self-selecting out too early.
- **When you're trying to say no to protect your bandwidth**: Make the prioritization conversation explicit and ask what should move — because this shifts the discussion from your helpfulness to the team's actual priorities.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Managers should manage their direct reports; employees shouldn't have to manage up. → **Their view**: Managing up is part of being effective at any level, and the best employees and leaders do it proactively — because it builds trust, reduces surprises, and increases opportunities.
- **Conventional**: Senior people no longer need to manage up. → **Their view**: The most senior people are often the best at managing up — because they got promoted partly by understanding what their bosses cared about and removing problems from their plate.
- **Conventional**: Good communication means giving lots of context and background. → **Their view**: Most people need far less preamble than you think; get to the juicy part quickly — because extra setup often wastes attention and obscures the real point.
- **Conventional**: Twitter threads and short posts are a good proxy for insight. → **Their view**: Short, one-directional formats can allow a lot of BS because they don't require defense or challenge — because the format can make shallow ideas sound profound without scrutiny.
- **Conventional**: Saying no should be direct and final. → **Their view**: Often it's better to talk about trade-offs and priorities instead of saying no outright — because that preserves relationships while still protecting bandwidth.
- **Conventional**: Writing well is mostly about style and what performs on social media. → **Their view**: More people should study the mechanics and craft of writing, not just mimic high-performing posts — because technical writing skill helps you say exactly what you mean and avoid misleading readers accidentally.
