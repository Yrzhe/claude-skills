# Nickey Skarstad

**Director of Product Management** | **Duolingo** | Expertise: product strategy, marketplaces, product quality, cross-functional leadership

## Bio
Nickey Skarstad is a product leader with experience spanning Etsy, Airbnb, Shopify, The Wing, and Duolingo. Her perspective matters because she has repeatedly worked on products where growth, quality, and marketplace health can conflict—and she’s developed practical ways to align teams around long-term customer value.

She is especially strong at turning vision into execution, operationalizing quality, and leading cross-functional teams through ambiguous, high-stakes product decisions. If you need someone who thinks deeply about strategy but stays grounded in how teams actually ship, her approach is highly relevant.

## Signature Frameworks

### Vision-to-Execution Pyramid
- **When to use**: When you need to turn a long-term product vision into concrete team priorities and quarterly execution.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the long-term vision: what the ideal future looks like in 5-10 years.
  2. Clarify the mission: how the company will make the vision real.
  3. Set the strategy: the key bets and choices required to achieve the vision.
  4. Translate strategy into objectives/OKRs for the next 3-6 months.
  5. Use the objectives to drive feature-level work and day-to-day execution.
- **Example**: She described using a vision-mission-strategy-objectives pyramid, then turning that into quarterly OKRs that teams can actually execute against.

### Quality Metric Balancing
- **When to use**: When growth alone can create bad user outcomes, especially in marketplaces or experience-driven products.
- **Steps**:
  1. Choose a quality metric that reflects the real user experience.
  2. Make that metric a top-line goal or balancing metric alongside growth.
  3. Operationalize it through coaching, education, and product/ops processes.
  4. Dogfood the product to detect quality issues firsthand.
  5. Use the metric to balance short-term growth with long-term customer success.
- **Example**: At Airbnb Experiences, review rate was used as the top-line quality metric to ensure people who booked actually had a great experience.

### Second-Order Thinking for Product Decisions
- **When to use**: When a product change could have cascading effects across a complex system or marketplace.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the immediate effect of the decision.
  2. Map the downstream effects on users, operations, design, engineering, and future product changes.
  3. Ask what becomes harder or more expensive to change later.
  4. Use first principles to align on what matters before implementation.
  5. Document second-order impacts in specs, strategy docs, or brainstorms.
- **Example**: She used Airbnb listing title length as an example: a small change can trigger host complaints, design changes, and system-wide complexity.

### Cross-Functional Brainstorming for Strategy
- **When to use**: When setting vision, exploring new opportunities, or aligning a team around a new direction.
- **Steps**:
  1. Pre-fill the whiteboard with prompts and agenda sections.
  2. Bring in cross-functional participants beyond the core product team.
  3. Use a long-horizon prompt (5-10 years) to encourage existential thinking.
  4. Timebox idea generation with a shared whiteboard tool.
  5. Synthesize similar ideas together in the meeting, then refine later.
- **Example**: For visioning, she would ask what the team thinks the competitive landscape will look like in 10 years and what experiences users will remember most.

### Three-Checkpoint Product Review Process
- **When to use**: When you want to keep teams aligned and avoid late-stage surprises before launch.
- **Steps**:
  1. Checkpoint 1: align on first principles and what problem you are solving.
  2. Checkpoint 2: review the proposed approach, including technical feasibility and architecture.
  3. Checkpoint 3: review the final product for readiness to ship and quality bar.
  4. Use cross-functional meetings and pre-reads so feedback is shared, not siloed.
  5. Keep the process lightweight for two-way-door decisions and stricter for one-way-door decisions.
- **Example**: She described a process where teams first align on the problem, then the approach, then the final ship decision.

### Energy Audit for Role Fit
- **When to use**: When deciding whether to stay in a role or move to a new company/team.
- **Steps**:
  1. Reflect on which meetings and tasks give you energy versus drain you.
  2. Color-code meetings or work blocks as red, yellow, or green based on energy.
  3. Look for patterns over several weeks.
  4. Use the pattern to infer whether the role matches your strengths and interests.
  5. Optimize future roles for the work that consistently feels energizing.
- **Example**: At Shopify, she color-coded her calendar after each meeting and realized most of her work was red/yellow, which told her the platform role was not a fit.

## Core Advice
- **When you are setting strategy in a vacuum**: Bring your team and key cross-functional partners into the strategy process early, but keep one clear decision-maker — because people are far more likely to buy into a strategy they helped shape.
- **When your product can grow in ways that hurt user experience**: Choose a quality metric that balances growth, and make it visible to the whole team — because optimizing only for growth can damage long-term retention and marketplace health.
- **When you want to maintain product quality**: Dogfood the product regularly and use firsthand experience to surface issues — because using the product yourself makes quality problems obvious and urgent.
- **When onboarding or acquisition metrics look good but downstream success is weak**: Add friction if needed to improve downstream outcomes like first success or first sale — because slowing users down can improve long-term value.
- **When you are deciding whether a role is right for you**: Track your energy after meetings and tasks for a few weeks — because your calendar patterns reveal whether the role fits your strengths.
- **When you are building a new product or business**: Use a functional org if the company is early and the work is similar across teams; use a GM-style org if the business is distinct and needs autonomy — because org structure should match the product and stage.
- **When a decision will be hard to reverse later**: Treat it as a one-way door and slow down to get broader input and alignment — because irreversible decisions can shape the entire system.
- **When a decision can be changed later without major cost**: Treat it as a two-way door and let the team move quickly with autonomy — because not every decision needs heavy process.
- **When you need team alignment on a new direction**: Use a cross-functional brainstorm with a pre-filled agenda and pre-read, then synthesize ideas live — because structured brainstorming creates ownership before strategy is finalized.
- **When you are rolling out goals or strategy to the team**: Use a tight feedback loop, such as a Loom recap in Slack, instead of waiting for the next meeting — because fast, visible communication reduces confusion.
- **When your team is getting distracted by shiny new ideas**: Keep revisiting the strategy in weekly meetings and explicitly say no to work that does not ladder up — because repeated reinforcement helps maintain focus.
- **When you are working remotely**: Shift more communication to async tools like Slack, Loom, Miro, and FigJam instead of defaulting to meetings — because remote work requires intentional communication and visible follow-through.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Strategy should be created by leadership and then communicated to the team. → **Their view**: Strategy should be built with the team, not in a vacuum, though one person should still own the final decision. — because involvement creates buy-in and prevents strategy from feeling imposed.
- **Conventional**: Growth is the primary metric that matters early on. → **Their view**: Growth should often be balanced or even constrained by quality metrics that reflect real user success. — because shallow growth can create a product that scales badly.
- **Conventional**: Friction is bad and should always be removed from onboarding or activation flows. → **Their view**: Adding friction can improve outcomes if it helps users make better choices and reach success faster. — because better downstream success matters more than raw top-of-funnel speed.
- **Conventional**: Product reviews should happen within each function separately. → **Their view**: Reviews work better when they are shared cross-functionally in one central moment. — because siloed feedback causes surprises and misalignment.
- **Conventional**: Every product decision needs heavy process and leadership approval. → **Their view**: Only one-way-door decisions deserve deep debate; two-way-door decisions should move fast with team autonomy. — because over-processing reversible decisions slows teams down.
- **Conventional**: A PM should always know the answer and drive decisions forcefully. → **Their view**: Good PMs should be empathetic, listen carefully, and be willing to realize they are wrong. — because alignment comes from understanding disagreement, not overpowering it.
