# Kristen Berman

**CEO and co-founder** | **Irrational Labs** | Expertise: behavioral science, behavioral design, product growth, consumer psychology

## Bio
Kristen Berman is the CEO and co-founder of Irrational Labs, a behavioral science consulting and design firm that helps companies change user behavior through psychology-informed product design. She previously helped build behavioral economics capabilities at Google and has worked with companies including TikTok, One Medical, Credit Karma, Microsoft, and LinkedIn.

Her perspective matters because she focuses on what people actually do, not what they say they want to do. She is especially valuable when teams need to diagnose why a product flow is failing, design behavior change ethically, or turn vague growth goals into concrete actions.

## Signature Frameworks

### The Three Bs
- **When to use**: Use when diagnosing why a product flow is underperforming or when designing an intervention to change user behavior.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the behavior uncomfortably specifically; focus on the exact action you want users to take, not a vague outcome.
  2. Identify barriers to that behavior, separating logistical barriers from cognitive barriers.
  3. Increase immediate benefits so the user has a reason to act now, not just in the future.
- **Example**: For Peloton, the behavior might be: “within seven days of starting the app, do two 10-minute workouts with two different instructors.”

### Behavioral diagnosis
- **When to use**: Use before proposing solutions, especially when a team thinks it knows what users want but needs to understand why behavior is not happening.
- **Steps**:
  1. Map the full user journey step by step, from before product entry through the target behavior.
  2. Observe what people actually do, not what they say they will do.
  3. Attach psychologies or biases to each step to identify where the real friction is.
  4. Use the diagnosis to generate intervention ideas that target the true barrier.
- **Example**: In a budgeting-feature case, mapping the steps showed that budgeting required too many actions and decisions, making it unlikely to change spending behavior.

### Show the benefit before adding friction
- **When to use**: Use in signup or onboarding flows when you need users to tolerate extra steps or questions.
- **Steps**:
  1. Ask a question or show content that makes users think about the product’s benefit.
  2. Get users to actively engage with the value proposition before asking them to continue.
  3. Add the necessary friction after motivation has been increased.
- **Example**: Apartment List asks what kind of apartment the user wants during signup, which makes users think about the benefit and increases motivation to continue.

### Right for Wrong
- **When to use**: Use when the desired behavior is good, but the natural motivation for doing it is weak or delayed.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the behavior you want users to do.
  2. Find an immediate, often unrelated motivator that can get them to do it now.
  3. Use that motivator ethically to nudge the desired action.
- **Example**: People may get vaccinated not because they are thinking about public health, but because of immediate incentives like donuts, lottery tickets, or free marijuana.

### Reduce choice and make the next step concrete
- **When to use**: Use when users face too many options or abstract next steps during onboarding or activation.
- **Steps**:
  1. Limit the number of choices presented.
  2. Recommend a default or best option.
  3. Make the next step concrete and time-bound.
  4. Explain why the recommendation is good for the user.
- **Example**: One Medical asked onboarding questions about health, recommended one provider, and suggested a virtual appointment for tomorrow, increasing bookings by 20%.

### Use deadlines as a gift
- **When to use**: Use when users procrastinate on important actions and need help prioritizing.
- **Steps**:
  1. Add a deadline or time constraint to the flow.
  2. Frame it as helping the user prioritize and complete the action.
  3. Use it especially when the behavior is valuable but easy to delay.
- **Example**: Kiva added a deadline to borrower signup and increased conversion.

## Core Advice
- **When a feature request seems obvious because users keep asking for it**: run an experiment before building it, and compare the requested feature against a control and alternative approaches — because user requests can be misleading.
- **When a flow requires too much effort**: first make the behavior easier logistically; if that is hard, make it cognitively easier with simple rules of thumb — because people do what is easy and familiar.
- **When designing signup or onboarding**: ask a question that gets users thinking about the product’s benefit before you add friction — because motivation should come before effort.
- **When onboarding has too many choices**: reduce the number of choices and recommend a specific next step — because choice overload slows people down.
- **When users drop out of a multi-step flow**: send reminders and return them to the exact step they left off on — because restarting from the beginning adds unnecessary friction.
- **When you want to reduce harmful behavior like misinformation sharing**: add friction and slow people down at the moment of action — because interruption reduces impulsive behavior.
- **When a behavior is valuable but motivation is weak**: create a short-term reward or social cue — because people are present-biased.
- **When your team debates “engagement”**: define it as a specific behavior, not a vague metric — because shared behavioral targets improve design and measurement.
- **When a required step has high drop-off**: reframe the step around the immediate value it unlocks and use completion cues — because users need to see the payoff and feel progress.
- **When incentives push teams toward short-term conversion at any cost**: set KPIs around customer behavior and extend the incentive horizon — because short-term metrics can encourage manipulation.
- **When considering open-ended questions in a flow**: avoid hard open-text questions unless they are truly easy — because they create more cognitive effort than teams expect.
- **When you do not know where to start improving a flow**: do a behavioral diagnosis — because it reveals the actual point of failure.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Reduce friction in signup flows and conversion will always improve. → **Their view**: Sometimes adding friction increases conversion if the user is first made more motivated by thinking about the benefit — because questions can prime value before effort.
- **Conventional**: The best way to change behavior is to appeal to goals, attitudes, or intentions. → **Their view**: Behavior change usually requires changing the environment and the actual behavior, not just mindset — because context and barriers drive action.
- **Conventional**: Users ask for features, so building the most requested feature is the safest product decision. → **Their view**: The most requested feature may do nothing if it does not address the real behavioral bottleneck — because demand is not the same as impact.
- **Conventional**: More choice is better because it gives users control. → **Their view**: Reducing choice can improve outcomes because too many options create cognitive load and paralysis — because guidance lowers effort.
- **Conventional**: The right reason for a behavior is always the best reason to motivate it. → **Their view**: Sometimes the best way to get people to do the right thing is to use a different immediate motivator — because present bias is powerful.
- **Conventional**: Interviews are a good way to predict job performance. → **Their view**: Interviews do not reliably predict performance; skill assessments and trials are better — because interviews mostly test affinity and culture fit.
