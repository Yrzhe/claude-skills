# Matt Mullenweg

**Co-creator of WordPress; Founder and CEO of Automattic** | **Automattic** | Expertise: open source, publishing platforms, distributed work, AI strategy

## Bio
Matt Mullenweg co-created WordPress at 19 and has spent more than two decades building the infrastructure behind a huge share of the web. His perspective matters because he has seen open source succeed not just as a licensing model, but as a durable governance, distribution, and freedom model at internet scale.

As founder and CEO of Automattic, he also brings a rare operator’s view of how to run a fully distributed company, steward a massive ecosystem, and adapt a platform through major technology shifts like mobile, block editing, and AI.

## Signature Frameworks

### Open-source stewardship model
- **When to use**: When building software meant to outlast one company and remain broadly usable, forkable, and community-owned.
- **Steps**:
  1. Choose a license that preserves the four freedoms: use, inspect, modify, redistribute.
  2. Build a community around the software, not just a product.
  3. Keep the core open so others can fork and continue it if needed.
  4. Use commercial services to complement the open core without enclosing it.
  5. Treat the project as a public good and optimize for long-term freedom, not short-term control.
- **Example**: WordPress is GPL-licensed, and even if Automattic disappeared, the community could fork the code and continue building on it.

### Movement-building around software
- **When to use**: When you want a product to become a durable ecosystem rather than a feature that can be copied.
- **Steps**:
  1. Give people a philosophy or worldview to believe in.
  2. Create rituals and symbols that reinforce identity.
  3. Build contribution pathways for many roles, not just engineers.
  4. Invest in meetups, forums, office hours, and town halls.
  5. Let the ecosystem create the moat through plugins, themes, and community participation.
- **Example**: WordPress uses “Code is poetry,” jazz musician release names, and a broad contribution model spanning documentation, translations, meetups, and core code.

### Long-horizon platform evolution
- **When to use**: When a product must survive multiple technology shifts without losing relevance.
- **Steps**:
  1. Make unpopular but necessary bets early.
  2. Ship iteratively over years rather than waiting for perfection.
  3. Preserve a stable core while modernizing the interface and capabilities.
  4. Use the platform as a base for new paradigms like collaboration and AI-assisted creation.
  5. Continuously prune technical debt and outdated features.
- **Example**: Gutenberg took years to mature, but it kept WordPress relevant through the shift to block-based editing and set up future collaboration features.

### Acquisition as stewardship
- **When to use**: When evaluating whether to buy a company or product into a holding company or platform ecosystem.
- **Steps**:
  1. Prefer assets with strong products or teams that can be accelerated.
  2. Look for alignment with your mission and user values.
  3. Assess whether the founder or team wants a good steward, not just a buyer.
  4. Avoid turnarounds unless the strategic value is exceptional.
  5. Measure success by long-term product health and community trust, not just cost cutting.
- **Example**: Automattic bought Day One to accelerate an already strong product, while Tumblr required a much harder turnaround.

### Open-source + commercial + nonprofit tripartite governance
- **When to use**: When a project needs both community legitimacy and commercial sustainability.
- **Steps**:
  1. Separate the open-source project from the commercial business.
  2. Use a nonprofit or foundation for educational/community functions.
  3. Retain a commercial entity to fund services and distribution.
  4. Create explicit trademark and licensing rules between the entities.
  5. Use checks and balances so no single layer fully controls the ecosystem.
- **Example**: WordPress combines a foundation, commercial operations, and community governance in a structure Matt compares to the three branches of government.

## Core Advice
- **When you want software to become a durable foundation rather than a disposable app**: build it open source with a license that preserves the four freedoms and allows forks — because freedom and forkability create resilience over decades.
- **When you are trying to create a community around a product**: build a movement with beliefs, rituals, and contribution paths, not just a feature set — because identity and mission keep people engaged.
- **When your platform has many contributors and extension points**: invest heavily in ecosystem tooling like plugins, themes, documentation, meetups, and support channels — because the ecosystem becomes the moat.
- **When a product needs to survive major technology shifts**: make long-term bets early and iterate for years, even if the first few years are painful — because relevance across generations requires persistence.
- **When you are evaluating an acquisition**: prefer buying strong products or teams you can accelerate rather than distressed assets you need to rescue — because turnarounds are far harder and riskier.
- **When a company is using your trademark or brand in a confusing way**: act quickly to protect the trademark and clarify what is and is not official — because brand confusion damages the whole ecosystem.
- **When you inherit a large software surface area**: ruthlessly cut outdated features and go back to basics on user experience — because feature sprawl and technical debt compound.
- **When you are building open source security at scale**: use automated scanning and AI-assisted review to complement human review — because humans cannot keep up with massive code and plugin ecosystems.
- **When you are deciding whether to engage in public controversy**: use long-form, contextual channels instead of short-form social media — because nuance gets flattened by outrage-driven feeds.
- **When you want to build a business on top of another platform**: prefer open source foundations over proprietary platforms — because open source reduces platform risk.
- **When you are scaling a distributed company**: make remote, asynchronous work a first-class operating model rather than a compromise — because it can be a structural advantage.
- **When you are choosing where to contribute as a developer, designer, or PM**: pick an open-source project you already use and contribute in the role that matches your strengths — because visible impact compounds quickly.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Open source is just a licensing choice or a developer preference. → **Their view**: Open source is a freedom model and a civilizational infrastructure choice — because software increasingly shapes human life and should preserve user liberty.
- **Conventional**: “Open source” means downloadable and usable locally. → **Their view**: Llama is not truly open source if it adds usage thresholds and permission requirements — because freedom must include use for any purpose.
- **Conventional**: Major software projects should be governed by committees. → **Their view**: Great software is usually driven by a singular steward with community checks — because committees would reject bold bets like Gutenberg.
- **Conventional**: Private equity is just efficient capital allocation. → **Their view**: Control-oriented PE can hollow out products and ecosystems — because short-term extraction often harms users and contributors.
- **Conventional**: Social media is a neutral place for complex disputes. → **Their view**: Short-form networks are structurally bad at nuance — because they amplify outrage and falsehoods faster than corrections.
- **Conventional**: Platform companies should maximize control over their ecosystem. → **Their view**: A true platform lets the ecosystem thrive independently — because builders need protection from the rug being pulled out.
