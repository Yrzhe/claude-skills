# Austin Hay

**Head of Marketing Technology at Ramp** | **Ramp** | Expertise: martech, growth systems, attribution, go-to-market, operations

## Bio
Austin Hay is a marketing technology leader who has helped scale growth systems at companies like Notion, Airbnb, Walmart, Postmates, and Robinhood. He has also held leadership roles at Runway, mParticle, and Branch Metrics, and teaches martech at Reforge.

His perspective matters because he treats martech as a systems discipline, not a tool-shopping exercise. He is especially useful when teams need to design scalable attribution, ownership, permissions, and stack architecture that will still work two years from now.

## Signature Frameworks

### Tools are meant to solve problems
- **When to use**: When evaluating software, designing internal systems, or deciding whether to add new martech tools.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the business problem clearly.
  2. Avoid anchoring on a familiar vendor or tool.
  3. Decide whether the solution should be third-party, first-party, or process-based.
  4. Choose the simplest solution that solves the problem without adding unnecessary complexity.
- **Example**: Instead of defaulting to a known marketing automation platform, start with the actual growth problem and work backward to the right system.

### PPS: Problem, People, System
- **When to use**: When someone requests a tool change, access, automation, or a new workflow.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the problem: what exactly needs to be solved?
  2. Identify the people: who is affected, who approves, who must change behavior?
  3. Design the system only after understanding the problem and stakeholders.
- **Example**: If a sales manager wants easier onboarding for new hires, first clarify the onboarding pain, then identify permissions and training needs, and only then automate.

### Build and Buy, not Build versus Buy
- **When to use**: When deciding whether to purchase a vendor product or build an internal solution.
- **Steps**:
  1. Break the problem into components.
  2. Buy the commodity layer.
  3. Build the differentiated layer unique to your business.
  4. Compare speed, cost, and long-term leverage.
- **Example**: Buy an experimentation platform like Split, then build custom workflows and capabilities around it instead of building the whole thing from scratch.

### Future-proof attribution data collection
- **When to use**: When setting up analytics and attribution early so you can support first-touch, last-touch, or multi-touch later.
- **Steps**:
  1. Capture landing URL, referrer, UTMs, and ad-network parameters like Google Click ID and Facebook parameters.
  2. Store first-touch and last-touch values locally on the device/browser.
  3. Update last-touch values whenever the user returns through a new source.
  4. Write attribution data to both the user profile and events.
  5. Send everything to the warehouse for later reconstruction.
- **Example**: A company that only collects UTMs today may regret it later when it wants MTA; Hay recommends collecting first and last touches from day one.

### Think gray
- **When to use**: When facing high-stakes decisions about systems, people, or strategy where premature certainty can cause mistakes.
- **Steps**:
  1. Resist forcing a black-or-white decision too early.
  2. Let the situation develop until a decision is truly necessary.
  3. Use the time to gather context and observe behavior.
  4. Decide later with better information.
- **Example**: In hiring or cross-functional work, he advises waiting before judging someone too quickly.

### Centralized vs. decentralized martech org design
- **When to use**: When deciding where martech should sit in the organization and who should own it.
- **Steps**:
  1. Determine whether the company is B2C, B2B, or B2B2C.
  2. Assess whether ownership should be centralized or distributed.
  3. Identify the primary customer of the martech function.
  4. Choose the structure that best matches resources, priorities, and technical depth.
- **Example**: Ramp moved martech between centralized rev ops and the CMO org as it learned whose problems the function was truly solving.

## Core Advice
- **When too many tools have no clear owner**: hire a dedicated martech or systems owner around 100–150 people — because the “village approach” stops scaling and creates operational risk.
- **When adding a new marketing tool**: start with the problem, not the vendor — because tool bias leads teams to recreate old setups that may not fit the current business.
- **When deciding build vs. buy**: buy the commodity layer and build the differentiated layer — because it gets value faster and preserves custom advantage.
- **When setting up attribution early**: collect first-touch and last-touch UTMs, referrers, and ad IDs from day one — because missing data is painful to recover later.
- **When choosing a B2C stack with limited engineering**: prefer a simpler CDP-centered stack — because it is faster and easier to manage than a warehouse-first architecture too early.
- **When you have strong engineering and data modeling**: consider reverse ETL with warehouse-centric modeling — because it gives more flexibility and activation power.
- **When hiring for martech**: prioritize curiosity and scrappiness over tool expertise — because the landscape changes too fast for static expertise to matter most.
- **When evaluating martech candidates**: ask how they prepared and what they would audit by Friday — because it reveals systems thinking and problem orientation.
- **When SaaS spend is rising**: model cost over time and optimize for declining cost per user/seat — because the stack should become more efficient as the company scales.
- **When attribution is imperfect after privacy changes**: make decisions with probabilistic data — because deterministic certainty is often unavailable now.
- **When permissions and admin requests pile up**: automate access control and approvals — because manual permissioning is risky and inefficient.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Martech is mostly a collection of third-party marketing tools. → **Their view**: Martech is a product/system function spanning people, process, first-party systems, and tools — because the real leverage comes from architecture and governance.
- **Conventional**: The right decision is usually build versus buy. → **Their view**: The better framing is build and buy — because most companies should buy commodity infrastructure and build differentiation on top.
- **Conventional**: Decide quickly and clearly on systems or people issues. → **Their view**: Often you should think gray and delay the decision — because waiting can improve judgment and reduce premature mistakes.
- **Conventional**: The best martech hire knows the most tools. → **Their view**: The best hire is curious, technically scrappy, and tool-agnostic — because adaptability matters more than vendor familiarity.
- **Conventional**: Privacy changes mean you should jump straight to MMM. → **Their view**: Many teams should first improve MTA foundations and probabilistic modeling — because MMM requires maturity many companies don’t yet have.
- **Conventional**: Decentralized ops ownership is more scalable. → **Their view**: Most startups should keep martech centralized unless they are truly massive — because decentralization often creates duplication and thin resources.
