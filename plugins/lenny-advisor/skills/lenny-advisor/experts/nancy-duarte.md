# Nancy Duarte

**Founder and CEO** | **Duarte** | Expertise: storytelling, presentation design, visual communication, leadership communication

## Bio
Nancy Duarte is the founder and CEO of Duarte, one of the most influential presentation and communication firms in the world. Her work has shaped thousands of decks and talks for major companies like Apple and Cisco, and she is known for turning presentation craft into repeatable frameworks that leaders can actually use.

Her perspective matters because she treats communication as a strategic tool for persuasion, alignment, and change—not just polish. If you need to move people emotionally and intellectually, especially around complex ideas or organizational transformation, her methods are unusually practical.

## Signature Frameworks

### What Is / What Could Be / New Bliss
- **When to use**: Use when you need to persuade, motivate, or create desire for change in a presentation, meeting, or even a short conversation.
- **Steps**:
  1. State the current reality clearly: what is.
  2. Contrast it with the desired future: what could be.
  3. Repeat the contrast to build tension and longing.
  4. End with the envisioned future state: the new bliss.
- **Example**: Nancy described using this framework even with her husband to get chores done, and cited Steve Jobs and Al Gore as examples of speakers who repeatedly contrasted the current state with a better future.

### Audience Journey / Audience as Hero
- **When to use**: Use when building any deck or talk where the audience must accept an idea, change behavior, or take action.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify who the audience is and what they care about.
  2. Understand their current pain, skepticism, and constraints.
  3. Position the presenter as the mentor, not the hero.
  4. Show how your idea helps the audience get unstuck.
  5. Make the call to action feel achievable for them.
- **Example**: She explained that in presentations, the audience has the power to accept or reject the idea, so the presenter should act like Obi-Wan Kenobi helping Luke Skywalker.

### Story Structure with Rise and Fall
- **When to use**: Use when you need to make a presentation memorable, emotionally engaging, or capable of creating longing for a future state.
- **Steps**:
  1. Open with the current state.
  2. Introduce conflict, tension, or a messy middle.
  3. Show the contrast between current reality and future possibility.
  4. Resolve into a clear ending or future vision.
  5. Use the cadence of rise and fall to keep attention and build emotional momentum.
- **Example**: Nancy mapped Steve Jobs’s iPhone launch and classic speeches by hand on graph paper to show the repeated rise-and-fall pattern across great talks.

### Make It Visible
- **When to use**: Use when the audience needs alignment, understanding, or a shared mental model of a complex idea.
- **Steps**:
  1. Decide what the audience must be able to see or remember.
  2. Choose the right visual aid: slide, diagram, sketch, data point, or live drawing.
  3. Use one memorable “star moment” such as a dramatic number, image, or story.
  4. Co-create the visual if needed so everyone sees the same thing.
  5. Remove ambiguity so the room leaves with the same interpretation.
- **Example**: She described drawing a graphic live for a CEO whose vision was hard to articulate, which helped roll out a program to 100,000 retail workers.

### Torch Bearer Leadership
- **When to use**: Use when leading a movement, transformation, or multi-stage organizational change over time.
- **Steps**:
  1. Articulate the dream and the destination.
  2. Invite people to commit to the journey.
  3. Expect a fight-and-climb middle where momentum drops.
  4. Use speeches, stories, ceremonies, and symbols to sustain belief.
  5. Keep relighting the path with enough clarity to reduce fear, even if the full future is not visible.
- **Example**: In *Illuminate*, she described leaders as torch bearers in a cave: they can only illuminate a few feet ahead, but that is enough to help people move forward.

### Slide Doc
- **When to use**: Use when you need a presentation that can be circulated and understood without a live presenter.
- **Steps**:
  1. Write full sentences and full prose where needed.
  2. Use stronger visuals than a memo would allow.
  3. Include enough detail for the audience to follow your thinking.
  4. Optionally add a longer appendix behind a short front section.
  5. Treat it as a hybrid between a deck and a memo.
- **Example**: She compared slide docs to Amazon-style memos, but argued that words plus pictures are often better for persuasion and comprehension.

## Core Advice
- **When you start a deck and feel stuck**: Start by defining the audience’s current reality, desired future, and the gap between them — because a presentation becomes persuasive when it is built around contrast rather than a random sequence of slides.
- **When you want your audience to care about your idea**: Make the audience the hero and position yourself as the mentor who helps them get unstuck — because people are more likely to accept an idea when it is framed around their needs, not the speaker’s ego.
- **When your talk feels flat or forgettable**: Infuse it with story structure: beginning, messy middle, and resolution — because story creates tension, longing, and emotional momentum that pure information cannot.
- **When you need to influence someone in a meeting or one-on-one**: Use the same contrast framework in short form: what is, what could be, and the new bliss — because Nancy said the framework works in any format, not just stage talks.
- **When your audience is confused by a complex idea**: Draw it, diagram it, or otherwise make the idea visible instead of relying only on words — because visuals create alignment and reduce the chance that different people leave with different interpretations.
- **When you are making slides for an internal meeting**: Make each slide support exactly one point and ensure every slide serves the deck’s one big idea — because people cannot process too many ideas at once, and slide clutter weakens the message.
- **When you are preparing a high-stakes presentation**: Do the thinking first, sketch the narrative, and only then open the presentation software — because the narrative and structure matter more than slide polish in the early stages.
- **When your audience is a technical or operational team**: Use the slide format and density that matches their communication culture, even if it is less cinematic — because a dense table or spreadsheet can be the right tool when the team needs shared operational detail.
- **When you are presenting remotely**: Practice camera eye contact, manage your presence deliberately, and compensate for the loss of body language — because remote communication removes many cues people normally use to build trust and authority.
- **When you feel nervous before speaking**: Calm your body with breathing, quiet focus, and a pre-talk ritual that shifts your chemistry — because she described stage fright as a fight-or-flight response and said the goal is to regulate your nervous system.
- **When you need to rally people around change over time**: Use repeated speeches, stories, ceremonies, and symbols to sustain belief through the hard middle — because large transformations require ongoing emotional fuel, not a single announcement.
- **When you are building product strategy or a roadmap**: Walk through the customer’s day in detail and use that story to test whether your strategy is actually right — because Nancy cited Airbnb’s customer journey mapping as a way to discover the need for a mobile-first strategy.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Great presentations are mainly for big stage talks and keynote moments. → **Their view**: The same contrast framework works in everyday meetings, phone calls, and even household negotiations. — because Nancy said the what-is/what-could-be structure is a mental model that works in any moment of influence.
- **Conventional**: The presenter is the hero of the presentation. → **Their view**: The audience is the hero; the presenter is the mentor. — because the audience has the power to accept or reject the idea, so the presenter’s job is to help them move forward.
- **Conventional**: More polished production always makes content better. → **Their view**: Raw, informative content can outperform highly produced video or slides. — because she said her informal, less polished video got more views than a highly produced one because people wanted the substance.
- **Conventional**: Every presentation should be cinematic and visually rich. → **Their view**: Sometimes the right choice is a dense table, a spreadsheet, or even no slides at all. — because the format should match the audience and the communication task, especially for technical or internal teams.
- **Conventional**: You should always start with the details and build up to the conclusion. → **Their view**: For execs and fundraising, starting with the conclusion or the new bliss can be the right move. — because different audiences need different entry points; some want the destination first.
- **Conventional**: A presentation deck should be assembled quickly from old slides in a repository. → **Their view**: That is usually a cop-out; high-stakes decks should be remapped to the specific audience and context. — because reusing old slides may save time, but it often weakens empathy and relevance.
