# Sam Schillace

**Corporate Vice President and Deputy Chief Technology Officer** | **Microsoft** | Expertise: product innovation, browser-based software, AI applications, distributed systems

## Bio
Sam Schillace is a veteran software builder and product thinker best known for creating Writely, the precursor to Google Docs. His perspective matters because he has repeatedly worked at the edge of what users think is possible, from browser-based productivity to AI-native workflows, and he has a strong instinct for separating real product value from hype.

At Microsoft, he works across consumer products, infrastructure, and AI. He brings a rare combination of technical depth and product judgment, especially around disruptive innovation, low-friction user experiences, and how new software paradigms actually get adopted.

## Signature Frameworks

### Why-not vs. what-if thinking
- **When to use**: When a new idea feels impossible, risky, or obviously wrong.
- **Steps**:
  1. Notice the immediate “why not” objections.
  2. Separate real constraints from reflexive dismissal.
  3. Ask “what if it works?”
  4. Explore the upside and new possibilities.
  5. Only then break down the hard parts into solvable problems.
- **Example**: With Google Docs, instead of stopping at “browsers aren’t ready,” Sam asked what browser-based collaboration could enable if it actually worked.

### Cheap experiments and sharp tools
- **When to use**: When testing a new product or technology without overcommitting.
- **Steps**:
  1. Make the environment low-friction.
  2. Build the smallest experiment that can reveal signal.
  3. Ship quickly, even if crude.
  4. Watch for surprising behavior.
  5. Invest more only after the idea shows real promise.
- **Example**: Writely was initially built in a couple of days to test whether a browser could feel like a word processor.

### Love/hate signal for disruptive products
- **When to use**: When judging whether something is truly disruptive.
- **Steps**:
  1. Look for polarization, not mild approval.
  2. Treat passionate fans as evidence of real value.
  3. Treat intense detractors as evidence the product challenges assumptions.
  4. If people are mostly indifferent, assume it is incremental.
  5. Use the reaction as a signal, not a verdict.
- **Example**: Google Docs had early fans and people who wanted it to “die in a fire,” which Sam saw as proof it was changing behavior.

### User-value-first product evaluation
- **When to use**: When deciding whether to build or scale a feature.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the user’s real problem.
  2. Ask whether the product makes life meaningfully easier.
  3. Ignore novelty if it doesn’t change outcomes.
  4. Compare value against adoption friction.
  5. If a lazy, cynical user wouldn’t care, assume it won’t work.
- **Example**: Sam rejected crypto as a consumer product because he couldn’t see a clear user benefit.

### North-star experimentation
- **When to use**: When exploring a new technology without drifting into random tinkering.
- **Steps**:
  1. Choose a concrete target that would matter if achieved.
  2. Use it to focus learning.
  3. Keep the target ambitious but meaningful.
  4. Build enough to expose what is hard.
  5. Decide whether to continue or pivot based on what you learn.
- **Example**: At Microsoft, Sam used programming agents as a north star, asking whether they could independently complete a task and self-monitor their work.

### Mission rigidity, feedback flexibility
- **When to use**: When building in a contested or uncertain space.
- **Steps**:
  1. Be clear about the mission.
  2. Expect pushback.
  3. Stay flexible on implementation details.
  4. Distinguish useful criticism from fear-based rejection.
  5. Adapt the product without losing the core goal.
- **Example**: Google Docs stayed committed to browser-based collaboration while changing features based on user feedback.

## Core Advice
- **When people dismiss a new idea as impossible**: ask “what if it works?” — because upside is often invisible at first.
- **When a product feels exciting but hard**: prototype the smallest version quickly and cheaply — because expensive experiments can prevent learning.
- **When evaluating innovation**: look for polarization — because disruptive products create strong love and strong hate.
- **When deciding what to build**: start with user value, not technical novelty — because users adopt outcomes, not demos.
- **When a feature adds only a little value**: assume adoption will be slow — because hidden friction is high.
- **When building onboarding**: remove friction aggressively — because you may have only seconds to prove value.
- **When a technology has flaws**: separate engineering problems from user value — because hard problems can still be worth solving.
- **When exploring AI**: pick a real task in your domain — because concrete goals accelerate learning.
- **When you feel pessimistic**: choose optimism as a working habit — because it keeps you open to discovery.
- **When you want long-term impact**: lean into what feels easy and energizing — because that may be your highest-leverage strength.
- **When you fear failure**: take more shots — because innovation requires experimentation.
- **When a hard idea still feels compelling**: stay with it — because transformative upside can justify years of effort.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Work should be hard, unpleasant, and grinding to be worthwhile. → **Their view**: Do the thing you feel guilty getting paid for, and do it intensely if it creates value — because joy plus intensity can be highly productive.
- **Conventional**: Pessimism is more realistic than optimism. → **Their view**: It’s better to be optimistic and wrong than pessimistic and right — because pessimism can blind you to transformative opportunities.
- **Conventional**: More features means more value. → **Their view**: Convenience and simplicity often win — because users adopt what reduces effort.
- **Conventional**: Flawed technologies should be dismissed until they’re polished. → **Their view**: The bigger question is whether they unlock a new platform — because category shifts matter more than current imperfections.
- **Conventional**: Broad approval is the best sign of product success. → **Their view**: Strong love and strong hate are better signals — because polarization means the product is changing behavior.
- **Conventional**: AI is just another feature layer on existing software. → **Their view**: AI will become the platform, and products will be features of AI — because AI-native workflows may define the next software era.
