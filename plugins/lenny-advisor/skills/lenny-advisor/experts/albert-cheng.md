# Albert Cheng

**Growth and monetization leader at Chess.com** | **Chess.com** | Expertise: growth, monetization, experimentation, consumer subscriptions

## Bio
Albert Cheng is a consumer growth leader who has driven growth and monetization at Chess.com, Duolingo, and Grammarly. His perspective matters because he combines product thinking, analytics, experimentation, and user psychology to find growth opportunities that are both measurable and durable.

He is especially strong on subscription businesses where retention, habit formation, and value communication matter more than short-term conversion tricks. If you want a view on how to scale consumer products without losing sight of user value, Albert is a useful expert.

## Signature Frameworks

### Explore and Exploit
- **When to use**: Use when deciding whether to search for new growth opportunities or double down on a proven insight.
- **Steps**:
  1. Explore broadly with tests and behavioral analysis to find surprising user insights.
  2. Identify a breakthrough signal from experiments or data.
  3. Exploit by concentrating resources on that insight.
  4. Share the insight across teams so adjacent surfaces can benefit.
  5. Watch for saturation; if tests stop producing meaningful results, return to exploration.
- **Example**: At Chess.com, the team found that most users reviewed games after wins, not losses. They redesigned the post-loss experience to emphasize brilliant moves and coaching, which increased game reviews by 25%, subscriptions by 20%, and improved retention.

### Growth as Connecting Users to Product Value
- **When to use**: Use when defining the role of growth teams and prioritizing work across the user journey.
- **Steps**:
  1. Map the full journey from acquisition to activation to retention.
  2. Define what “value” means at each stage.
  3. Organize teams and metrics around helping users reach value faster.
  4. Remove friction between users and value.
  5. Reassess as user needs evolve.
- **Example**: At Grammarly, Albert reframed monetization around helping free users understand the full value of the product by sampling premium suggestions in the free experience.

### Gamification Pillars: Core Loop, Metagame, Profile
- **When to use**: Use when building habit-forming products that need repeated engagement.
- **Steps**:
  1. Tighten the core loop so users complete a meaningful action and get a reward.
  2. Add a metagame with longer-term goals like leaderboards or progression.
  3. Create a profile that reflects user investment over time.
  4. Make sure all three reinforce each other.
- **Example**: At Duolingo, the core loop was doing a lesson and extending a streak, the metagame included the path and leaderboards, and the profile showed accumulated progress.

### Freemium Sampling / Reverse Free Trial in Real Time
- **When to use**: Use when free users undervalue the paid product because they only see a narrow slice of functionality.
- **Steps**:
  1. Instrument the product to see what free users encounter and ignore.
  2. Find where the free experience underrepresents the product’s true value.
  3. Sample premium features inside the free workflow in limited amounts.
  4. Intermix free and paid value so users feel the broader promise.
  5. Place paywalls after users have tasted premium value.
- **Example**: At Grammarly, free users mostly saw spelling and grammar fixes, so the team interspersed premium suggestions like tone and clarity improvements. Upgrade rates nearly doubled.

### Experimentation System First
- **When to use**: Use when trying to scale experimentation beyond isolated tests.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with a growth model.
  2. Instrument the product thoroughly.
  3. Use experimentation tooling that fits your scale.
  4. Build observability and repositories to spot patterns.
  5. Celebrate wins and share learnings to build momentum.
- **Example**: At Chess.com, Albert set a goal of 1,000 experiments a year to force the company to invest in no-code tooling, lifecycle experimentation, observability, and broader participation.

## Core Advice
- **When recent experiments stop producing clear wins**: shift back into exploration mode — because a rising share of insignificant tests usually means you’ve saturated a local maximum.
- **When free-to-paid conversion is weak**: sample premium features inside the free experience — because users rarely pay for value they haven’t felt.
- **When subscription economics are fragile**: prioritize retention before paywalls or paid acquisition — because durable usage is a stronger business foundation than immediate conversion.
- **When many users are dormant**: invest in resurrection experiences — because reactivated users can become a meaningful share of active usage.
- **When you don’t know what users naturally share**: instrument sharing hotspots and amplify them — because it’s easier to scale organic behavior than invent forced virality.
- **When beginners feel discouraged early**: protect confidence with guided onboarding and gentler first steps — because early self-doubt kills habit formation.
- **When analysis and prototyping are slow**: use AI for summarization, text-to-SQL, and rapid prototyping — because faster insight loops create more opportunities to learn.
- **When a team has never experimented**: start with one simple A/B test — because experimentation compounds only after the team gets an early win.
- **When hiring in fast-changing environments**: screen for agency, energy, and learning speed — because adaptability matters more than static expertise.
- **When pressure builds to announce early**: validate before PR — because publicity can create sunk-cost pressure around weak ideas.

- **Conventional**: Users review mistakes after losing because that’s when they need feedback. → **Their view**: Many users are more willing to review after wins — because people are more receptive when they feel good or supported.
- **Conventional**: Giving away premium features hurts upgrades. → **Their view**: Strategic sampling can increase conversion — because users often underestimate value they haven’t experienced.
- **Conventional**: Deep prior domain experience is the best hiring signal. → **Their view**: High-agency, fast-learning people can outperform — because old experience can become a crutch in fast-changing environments.
- **Conventional**: Brand, community, and viral marketing are separate from product-led growth. → **Their view**: They can be rocket fuel when integrated with product and measurement — because cultural moments can materially drive acquisition.
- **Conventional**: New-user onboarding is the main lever in mature products. → **Their view**: Retention and resurrection can matter more — because existing and returning users often drive most active usage.
