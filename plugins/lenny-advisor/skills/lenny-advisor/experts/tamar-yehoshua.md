# Tamar Yehoshua

**President of Product and Technology** | **Glean** | Expertise: product leadership, enterprise AI, cross-functional management, search and information retrieval

## Bio
Tamar Yehoshua leads product and technology at Glean, where she helps build enterprise AI products for knowledge work. She brings a rare mix of product, engineering, and search expertise from leadership roles at Slack, Google, and Amazon, which gives her a practical view of how great products are built, scaled, and adopted.

Her perspective matters because she has operated at the intersection of product strategy, technical execution, and organizational design inside some of the most influential companies in tech. She is especially strong on how to grow careers, make product decisions under uncertainty, and build AI products that stay useful as models evolve.

## Signature Frameworks

### Impact-first career progression
- **When to use**: When deciding how to grow in your current role or earn promotion
- **Steps**:
  1. Do an excellent job at the job you already have.
  2. Focus on whether your work moves the business forward, not just whether you completed assigned tasks.
  3. Measure success by whether people actually use what you built and whether the organization becomes more productive.
  4. Build deep technical, product, and metrics understanding as table stakes.
- **Example**: She turned down a management role because the team structure was not right for the company, then proposed a better organization instead.

### Follow people, not plans
- **When to use**: When choosing your next job or trying to accelerate your career without a rigid five-year plan
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the best people you can learn from.
  2. Choose roles where you will learn the most, not just where you like the brand or domain.
  3. Look at where great people are clustering and go to those companies.
  4. Build relationships that compound across future jobs.
- **Example**: She joined Amazon because Udi Manber spent two hours convincing her how exceptional Jeff Bezos was, and she later followed strong people into Google and Slack.

### Principled leadership at scale
- **When to use**: When leading a large organization or trying to make decision-making more consistent
- **Steps**:
  1. Define a small set of clear principles.
  2. Make those principles visible through repeated decisions and questions.
  3. Ensure the team knows what you care about so they can operate without constant escalation.
  4. Use consistency to reduce ambiguity across a large org.
- **Example**: She said Bezos always made decisions that were customer-driven and consistent, which made it easier to operate inside Amazon.

### Prototype to feel the product
- **When to use**: When evaluating a new product direction, UX change, or interface redesign
- **Steps**:
  1. Do not rely on mockups alone.
  2. Build a prototype quickly, even if it will be thrown away.
  3. Use real interactions to understand what feels right or wrong.
  4. Iterate until the team can see what actually belongs in the product.
- **Example**: At Slack, Stewart Butterfield asked the team to put everything behind one button to understand what users truly needed in the interface.

### Async operating model for cross-functional alignment
- **When to use**: When OKR reviews, planning, or team updates are consuming too much meeting time
- **Steps**:
  1. Have teams post written docs and short videos asynchronously.
  2. Review updates together with key leaders to stay aligned.
  3. Use a small set of live meetings only for high-priority issues or follow-ups.
  4. Periodically measure the time cost and redesign the process if it becomes too heavy.
- **Example**: At Slack, Tamar and the CTO moved OKR reviews from live meetings to Slack channels plus videos after calculating the process was consuming hundreds of hours.

### Build with AI guardrails, not assumptions
- **When to use**: When shipping AI/LLM features that users may misuse or misunderstand
- **Steps**:
  1. Define what the system can and cannot know.
  2. Add prompts, suggestions, and guardrails to steer users toward good use cases.
  3. Educate users that outputs are non-deterministic.
  4. Design the product so it improves as the underlying models improve.
- **Example**: At Glean, users sometimes ask for things the system cannot know, like their top priority next week, so the team builds guidance around valid queries and use cases.

## Core Advice
- **When you want to get promoted or move up in your career**: Do exceptional work in your current role before chasing the next one — because promotion follows demonstrated impact, not ambition alone.
- **When you are deciding what job to take next**: Choose the people you will learn from, not just the company brand or a domain you think sounds exciting — because skills and relationships compound, and they outlast any single company.
- **When you are evaluating a leadership role**: Make sure you have a strong engineering partner and clear division of responsibilities before joining — because great product ideas go nowhere if they cannot be built, and misalignment creates organizational confusion.
- **When your team is debating whether a feature should be prominent**: Use user goals and intuition, not just metrics, to decide what deserves attention in the product — because metrics alone can miss what users actually need in the moment.
- **When your company feels chaotic during hypergrowth**: Do not assume chaos means failure; focus on whether the company has product-market fit, distribution, sales, and enough capital — because fast-growing companies often break operationally while still winning in the market.
- **When you are tempted to optimize for a big payday**: Prioritize learning and skill-building over short-term financial upside — because financial outcomes are hard to predict, but skills cannot be taken away.
- **When you are leading a team and need to recruit strong people**: Take roles where hiring great people will make their careers better — because top talent wants to join places where they can grow, and that bar improves your own leadership choices.
- **When a product change upsets a vocal minority**: Design for the larger future user base, but explain the change transparently and respectfully — because the loudest unhappy users are often not the majority, but they still deserve to feel heard.
- **When you need to align product and engineering leaders**: Spend substantial time together, document decisions, and speak up directly when priorities differ — because alignment requires explicit discussion; passive disagreement creates hidden conflict.
- **When you are trying to keep up with AI**: Use the tools constantly in your own workflow and experiment with real use cases — because hands-on use reveals what AI can actually do and helps you avoid falling behind.
- **When you are building AI products**: Do not make your differentiation depend on compensating for current model weaknesses — because models will improve quickly, so your product should get better with them rather than be built around their limitations.
- **When you are unsure what to do in your career**: Make a decision and then make it right instead of waiting for the perfect choice — because you cannot predict the future, so progress comes from commitment and execution.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Well-run companies are the ones that win. → **Their view**: Companies can be messy, poorly run, and still grow extremely fast and succeed — because success is more tightly tied to product-market fit, distribution, sales, and capital than to internal neatness.
- **Conventional**: You need a detailed five-year career plan. → **Their view**: You do not need a career plan; following great people and learning step by step is often better — because career paths are hard to predict, and the best learning comes from working with exceptional people.
- **Conventional**: Metrics should drive most product decisions. → **Their view**: Strong product intuition matters, and sometimes the right decision is the one that feels right after deep understanding — because overreliance on metrics can miss user motivation, context, and product judgment.
- **Conventional**: You should avoid making users unhappy at all costs. → **Their view**: It is often better to make a larger future audience happy, even if a vocal minority is upset by a change — because the people complaining are often a small subset, while the new experience may be much better for the majority.
- **Conventional**: AI will replace product managers. → **Their view**: AI will mostly eliminate low-value execution work and make great PMs more powerful — because AI is weak at creativity and strategic synthesis, which are the core strengths of strong PMs.
- **Conventional**: A product should be judged by its current model quality. → **Their view**: The product should be judged by how well it improves as models improve — because durable value comes from product design and workflow integration, not from patching model weaknesses.
