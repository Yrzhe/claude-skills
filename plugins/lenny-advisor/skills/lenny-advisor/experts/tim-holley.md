# Tim Holley

**VP of Product** | **Etsy** | Expertise: marketplaces, product strategy, growth, experimentation

## Bio
Tim Holley leads product at Etsy and has spent more than a decade shaping one of the world’s most distinctive two-sided marketplaces. His perspective matters because he has operated through both growth and constraint: aligning a large organization around a single business metric, improving buyer confidence in unique inventory, and balancing seller success with marketplace trust.

He also brings a pragmatic view of product leadership: use experimentation where it fits, but don’t let it become dogma. Tim is especially valuable when you need to understand how to scale a marketplace without losing its identity.

## Signature Frameworks

### North-star KPI Alignment for Organizational Change
- **When to use**: When a company needs to shift from consensus-heavy or mission-only thinking to faster, more business-driven execution.
- **Steps**:
  1. Choose a single north-star metric that reflects the business outcome you care about.
  2. Make that metric the drumbeat in every meeting and launch review.
  3. Require teams to explain how their work will move the metric within a relevant time horizon.
  4. Use the metric to prioritize work and cut projects that cannot clearly contribute.
  5. Pair the KPI with a consistent narrative so people understand both the goal and the why.
- **Example**: At Etsy, GMS (gross merchandise sales) became the front-and-center KPI used to evaluate launches and prioritize work.

### Marketplace Balancing Loop
- **When to use**: When managing a two-sided marketplace where supply and demand must stay in balance.
- **Steps**:
  1. Continuously assess whether the marketplace is constrained by supply, demand, or both.
  2. Invest in the side that is currently limiting transactions.
  3. Use marketplace data to guide sellers toward actions that improve conversion and sales.
  4. Adjust focus by category or subcategory rather than treating the marketplace as uniform.
  5. Keep the core job-to-be-done in view: enabling a transaction between buyer and seller.
- **Example**: Etsy has over 100 million items overall, but may still see supply gaps in specific categories like wall decor or other subcategories.

### Buyer Confidence Design for Unique Inventory
- **When to use**: When buyers are evaluating one-of-a-kind or hard-to-compare items from independent sellers.
- **Steps**:
  1. Surface structured data that helps buyers compare options.
  2. Highlight trust signals such as reviews, response speed, and seller service quality.
  3. Use buyer photos and videos to show items in real-world context.
  4. Add concise nudges and summaries that reduce decision friction.
  5. Make key details easy to scan, such as availability, size, shipping time, and price.
- **Example**: Etsy uses buyer review photos and snippets like “only one left” to help shoppers feel confident buying from a seller they may not know.

### Habit-Loop Retention Design
- **When to use**: When you want to bring users back after an initial action rather than optimizing only for the first conversion.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify a meaningful user action that signals intent, such as favoriting an item.
  2. Trigger follow-up events when the item changes, such as a sale or low inventory.
  3. Use feeds and push notifications to surface those changes.
  4. Close the loop by making the return visit feel timely and personally relevant.
  5. Measure success over longer horizons like 30, 60, or 90 days.
- **Example**: If a user favorites an Etsy item, Etsy can notify them when it goes on sale or is nearly sold out.

### Weekly Focus Accountability Ritual
- **When to use**: When a team needs a lightweight way to stay aligned on priorities and learn from execution.
- **Steps**:
  1. Have each person write down what they are focused on this week.
  2. Review what they said they would do last week and whether it got done.
  3. Share the update in a team channel or standup-like format.
  4. Track patterns over time to improve planning and time estimates.
  5. Use the ritual to focus on priorities rather than task lists.
- **Example**: Tim’s team posts weekly focus updates in a product channel on Mondays and reviews what was completed versus still in progress.

## Core Advice
- **When your company is trying to change how teams work and make decisions**: Pick one clear KPI and make it the shared measuring stick for every team — because a single metric creates alignment, speeds prioritization, and makes it easier to evaluate whether work is actually moving the business.
- **When people are attached to old ways of working and change feels existential**: Repeat the narrative consistently and often until it is internalized, not just understood — because clarity on the goal is not enough; people also need to hear the why repeatedly before it sticks.
- **When you are building a marketplace with unique inventory**: Spend real time with sellers in their homes, workshops, and workflows — because deep seller immersion helps you understand their constraints and design tools that actually improve their ability to make sales.
- **When your marketplace has lots of inventory but buyers struggle to choose**: Invest in trust and decision-support signals like reviews, photos, availability, and response-time cues — because unique inventory increases uncertainty, so buyers need more help feeling confident about a purchase.
- **When sellers are overwhelmed by too many requests or recommendations**: Tell them the single most important action to take right now, and tailor that guidance by season or category — because sellers have limited time and want to spend it making; focused guidance is more actionable than broad advice.
- **When you want to improve conversion with minimal engineering effort**: Test small copy changes and value statements in high-intent surfaces like cart and search — because one-line text changes can produce outsized impact, especially when they reinforce buyer values.
- **When you are deciding whether to run an A/B test**: Use experimentation as the highest bar for causal proof, but don’t force it when the change is about a broader system or long-horizon outcome — because some initiatives, like retention or SEO, need different measurement approaches than standard short-term conversion tests.
- **When a seller-side policy or discounting action affects the marketplace**: Honor or show the seller action even if you cannot cleanly A/B test it — because Etsy treats some seller inputs as non-optional to surface, and uses pre/post or other analyses when experimentation is inappropriate.
- **When a marketplace is growing but some sellers are reaching production limits**: Create a constrained form of scaling support, like production assistance, that preserves provenance and trust — because you can help sellers grow without allowing anonymous or untraceable manufacturing that breaks marketplace standards.
- **When you need to retain users after a first purchase or intent signal**: Build loops that notify users when something they cared about changes — because favorites, updates feeds, and push notifications turn passive intent into a reason to return.
- **When you are hiring product managers for a small, fast-changing organization**: Prioritize collaboration, decisiveness, and curiosity over a narrow domain-only mindset — because Etsy needs PMs who can work cross-functionally, make calls with imperfect data, and adapt to changing priorities.
- **When a team is tempted to launch a new adjacent product or initiative**: Kill or pause it if it no longer supports the current business constraints and goals — because Etsy shut down Etsy Studio because it did not help near-term sales or marketing ROI once the company refocused on the core marketplace.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Marketplaces should always maximize breadth of supply to capture every possible transaction. → **Their view**: A marketplace should limit supply to items that fit its brand, policies, and quality bar, even if that means turning away volume — because too much irrelevant or off-brand supply weakens the marketplace’s identity and can reduce trust and relevance for buyers.
- **Conventional**: Consensus-based product culture leads to better decisions because more voices are included. → **Their view**: Heavy consensus can slow teams down and become a liability when the business needs speed — because Etsy found that its old consensus culture produced thoughtful products but made it hard to launch quickly and drive predictable GMS.
- **Conventional**: The best product orgs should treat experimentation as the answer for most decisions. → **Their view**: A/B testing is powerful, but it is not the right tool for every change or every horizon — because retention, SEO, and broader strategic bets often require cohort analysis, pre/post methods, or other validation approaches.
- **Conventional**: A marketplace should optimize equally for both sides at all times. → **Their view**: At Etsy’s current stage, the buyer’s ability to make a purchase is paramount because that is what enables seller success — because the marketplace’s job is to facilitate transactions; qualified buyers drive sales, which is the seller outcome that matters.
- **Conventional**: Product teams should centralize decision-making in a strong PM who acts like a mini-CEO. → **Their view**: The best decisions come from a tightly collaborative, cross-functional leadership model with PM accountable but not unilateral — because Etsy believes product, engineering, design, research/analytics, and marketing should jointly shape decisions.
- **Conventional**: Referral programs are always a straightforward growth lever. → **Their view**: Referral programs can fail if you do not understand the long-term value of the referred user or the right incentive structure — because Etsy’s buyer referral program was not a major success, while seller referral credits worked better because they aligned with listing behavior and reduced friction.
