# Ryan J. Salva

**VP of Product** | **GitHub** | Expertise: ai-strategy, product-management, developer-tools, platform-product

## Bio
Ryan J. Salva leads product at GitHub and helped incubate and launch GitHub Copilot, one of the most influential AI products for developers. His perspective matters because he sits at the intersection of AI strategy, developer experience, and large-scale product execution, with a strong focus on how AI can amplify human creativity rather than replace it.

He brings an unusually practical view of innovation inside a large company: how to fund moonshots, when to graduate research into product, and how to design AI that fits naturally into a developer’s workflow.

## Signature Frameworks

### Horizon-based innovation portfolio
- **When to use**: When managing a product portfolio that needs both near-term execution and long-term bets.
- **Steps**:
  1. Reserve a small slice of capacity for bold, uncertain research bets.
  2. Keep a larger portion focused on operational excellence for in-market products.
  3. Allocate the majority to incremental improvements on existing products and prior bets.
  4. Adjust the mix based on company stage and circumstances rather than a fixed rule.
- **Example**: Ryan described a rough allocation of 5-10% for audacious experimental research, 25-30% for operations, and about 60% for incremental progress.

### R&D-to-product graduation model
- **When to use**: When turning a moonshot prototype into a scalable product inside a larger company.
- **Steps**:
  1. Let a ring-fenced R&D team explore without immediate pressure for monetization or operational constraints.
  2. Look for a real customer problem and medium-confidence signal that the idea solves it in a novel way.
  3. Bring prototypes in front of more customers to validate demand and usefulness.
  4. Create a transition team that includes some original researchers plus productized engineering talent.
  5. Transfer ownership only after a replacement is fully ready and continuity of expertise is preserved.
  6. Move the original researchers back to the R&D team once the product team can operate independently.
- **Example**: GitHub Next incubated Copilot, then researchers temporarily moved into a new EPD squad for technical preview and knowledge transfer before returning to Next.

### AI product experience design loop
- **When to use**: When designing an AI feature that must feel useful, fast, and non-disruptive in a workflow.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with the user workflow and identify where AI can remove drudgery without breaking flow.
  2. Experiment with different interaction patterns, including side panels and inline suggestions.
  3. Optimize for latency so the AI response arrives fast enough to feel immediate.
  4. Craft prompts and model inputs to improve the quality of suggestions.
  5. Use visual cues that signal suggestions are ephemeral and optional.
  6. Continuously tune the experience based on developer feedback and usage.
- **Example**: Copilot moved from experimental side-panel ideas to inline gray italicized autocomplete, with a target latency around 200 milliseconds.

### Responsible AI guardrail process
- **When to use**: When shipping AI that can generate language or content with ethical, legal, or safety implications.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the AI as an assistant with a bounded role, not a replacement for the human user.
  2. Set principles for what behavior is appropriate in the product context.
  3. Work closely with legal, privacy, security, and domain experts.
  4. Start with simple filters or block lists if needed, but recognize their limitations.
  5. Replace crude rules with more nuanced detection models when available.
  6. Keep humans accountable for reviewing and deciding on outputs.
- **Example**: GitHub initially used block lists to filter offensive output, then partnered with Azure Responsible AI models to detect patently offensive sentiment more accurately.

### Community-led product validation
- **When to use**: When launching a novel product in a skeptical or fast-evolving market.
- **Steps**:
  1. Expose the product to a technical preview with a limited but growing audience.
  2. Watch for strong emotional and behavioral signals, not just survey responses.
  3. Use community chatter, tweets, and forum reactions as evidence of resonance.
  4. Scale hiring and product investment only after repeated customer validation.
  5. Keep a tight feedback loop with users as the product matures.
- **Example**: Copilot moved from tens of thousands to hundreds of thousands in technical preview, with mind-blown reactions on Twitter and Hacker News signaling readiness to scale.

## Core Advice
- **When you want to create a breakthrough product inside a large company**: Give a small team real space to experiment without immediate pressure to monetize or meet operational standards — because moonshots need room to explore before the organization can know whether they solve a real problem.
- **When a prototype starts showing real customer pull**: Test it with more customers before fully productizing it — because medium-confidence signal from representative users is the right trigger to move from research to market testing.
- **When transitioning an R&D project into a product team**: Keep the original researchers involved long enough to transfer knowledge, then move them back only after a replacement is fully ready — because continuity of expertise prevents the product from losing the original insight while operationalizing.
- **When building an AI feature for a workflow like coding**: Design it to remove drudgery and preserve flow rather than forcing users to context switch — because users value staying in the creative loop more than interacting with a separate AI interface.
- **When an AI suggestion feels slow in an editor or interactive tool**: Optimize aggressively for latency, aiming for response times that feel instantaneous — because even small delays interrupt flow and reduce the perceived usefulness of the AI.
- **When your AI model can produce offensive or inappropriate output**: Use a bounded product persona and explicit guardrails to define acceptable behavior — because a clear role for the AI helps teams decide what kinds of outputs are appropriate in context.
- **When simple block lists start causing false positives or awkward edge cases**: Move to more nuanced detection models instead of relying only on crude keyword filters — because context matters, and simple filters can block legitimate usage while still missing harmful content.
- **When launching an AI product that people may distrust**: Be explicit that the AI augments humans and does not replace them — because this reduces anxiety and keeps humans accountable for quality, security, and judgment.
- **When you are scaling a novel product with uncertain market norms**: Spend more time in dialogue with customers and the community than you might for a conventional product — because novel AI products require social and technical legitimacy, not just feature shipping.
- **When deciding how much of a team’s capacity to allocate to innovation**: Reserve a small but explicit percentage for audacious bets and keep the majority on operations and incremental progress — because a portfolio approach balances long-term upside with near-term reliability.
- **When building products for developers**: Focus on helping them think about design patterns and outcomes, not just syntax and parameter order — because AI can handle rote work, freeing developers to spend more time on creative and architectural decisions.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: AI tools should be judged mainly by how much code they can generate. → **Their view**: The real value is in keeping developers in flow and removing drudgery, not maximizing raw code output — because Copilot is a creativity amplifier, not a code factory.
- **Conventional**: A successful R&D project should be handed off cleanly to the product team as soon as it works. → **Their view**: The original researchers should stay involved through knowledge transfer, and only move back once a replacement is fully ready — because continuity of expertise is essential.
- **Conventional**: AI products can be governed with simple rules or keyword filters. → **Their view**: Crude block lists are inadequate and can be harmful; nuanced models and human judgment are needed — because context determines whether language is offensive or legitimate.
- **Conventional**: The goal of AI coding tools is eventually to replace developers or write most of the code. → **Their view**: Copilot should never replace the developer; it should always augment a human who is making the final decisions — because humans must remain accountable.
- **Conventional**: Innovation can be outsourced to a separate moonshot team. → **Their view**: The product team that owns the customer relationship must own the roadmap; R&D can seed ideas but not permanently own them — because the team closest to the customer needs control over the product’s future.
