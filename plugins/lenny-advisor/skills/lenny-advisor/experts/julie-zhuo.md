# Julie Zhuo

**Co-founder** | **Sundial** | Expertise: design, leadership, product strategy, management

## Bio
Julie Zhuo is a former Facebook design leader who started as an individual contributor and rose to VP of Design, eventually leading design for the Facebook app. She now co-founds Sundial and is also the author of *The Making of a Manager*, making her perspective especially valuable for people navigating design leadership, management growth, and product judgment.

What makes Julie unique is her ability to translate hard-won experience into practical frameworks for thinking, writing, giving feedback, and growing into leadership. She is especially useful when you need a grounded view on how great product and design decisions are actually made inside fast-moving companies.

## Signature Frameworks

### Impostor Syndrome as a Growth Signal
- **When to use**: When you feel unprepared, out of your depth, or unsure whether you deserve a new role or responsibility.
- **Steps**:
  1. Recognize that discomfort often accompanies the fastest periods of growth.
  2. Reframe feeling like an impostor as evidence that you are stretching into something new.
  3. Normalize the feeling by remembering that even senior leaders experience it.
  4. Ask for help instead of hiding uncertainty.
  5. Be vulnerable with peers, managers, or reports so others can support and problem-solve with you.
- **Example**: Julie said that for her first seven or eight years at Facebook, she felt like an impostor nearly every week, even while growing from designer to senior leadership.

### Writing as Skill-Building Practice
- **When to use**: When you want to improve clarity of thinking, confidence in expressing opinions, or communication in larger groups.
- **Steps**:
  1. Pick a specific skill you want writing to improve, such as speaking up, clarity, or concise communication.
  2. Create a simple recurring structure, like publishing once a week or writing daily for 30 days.
  3. Set an action goal you control, such as word count or hitting publish, rather than audience outcomes.
  4. Write primarily for yourself, as a letter to yourself or a way to clarify your own thinking.
  5. Use repetition to reduce fear and build fluency.
- **Example**: Julie began publishing one post every week for a year after feedback that she stayed quiet in large meetings, and found that the practice improved both her thinking and her ability to speak up.

### Word-Count-First Writing Method
- **When to use**: When you struggle to start writing, over-edit too early, or want to build a sustainable writing habit.
- **Steps**:
  1. Focus first on getting words out rather than quality.
  2. Set a concrete word-count target instead of a vague time-based goal.
  3. Draft without over-editing.
  4. Revise only after you have material on the page.
  5. Break large projects into daily or weekly word-count goals.
- **Example**: For her book, Julie divided the total manuscript into a daily target of about 500 words, written five nights a week.

### Product Sense Development Loop
- **When to use**: When you want to improve product judgment, intuition, and understanding of why products succeed or fail.
- **Steps**:
  1. Observe your own reactions while using products, including confusion, delight, and motivation.
  2. Reflect on each step of the user experience, from discovery to onboarding to repeated use.
  3. Discuss products with others to compare interpretations and uncover blind spots.
  4. Study deep product analyses from strong thinkers and practitioners.
  5. Pair qualitative observation with quantitative validation through experiments and data.
- **Example**: Julie described paying attention to why you downloaded an app, where you got confused, and how many times you tapped into the wrong path as a way to build product sense.

### Founder Intuition vs. Research Decision Model
- **When to use**: When deciding whether to trust founder instinct or invest more in customer research and experimentation.
- **Steps**:
  1. Assess whether you are truly part of the target audience.
  2. Evaluate how deeply you understand the customer's day-to-day reality.
  3. If you are the user, lean more on instinct while still validating key assumptions.
  4. If you are not the user, spend significant time interviewing customers and learning their workflow.
  5. As the user base broadens, rely less on intuition and more on research and data.
- **Example**: Julie contrasted early Facebook, where the team was building for people just like themselves, with later Facebook, where intuition became less reliable as the audience expanded globally.

### Product Review Prioritization Framework
- **When to use**: When running product reviews, design critiques, or synthesizing conflicting feedback from multiple stakeholders.
- **Steps**:
  1. Gather feedback from multiple audiences, including designers, direct teammates, outsiders, and target customers.
  2. Anchor everyone on the target audience, the core problem, and the job to be done.
  3. First evaluate whether the product is valuable and solves the core problem.
  4. Next evaluate ease of use and whether users can access the value without friction.
  5. Finally evaluate delight, aesthetics, and whether the experience exceeds expectations.
  6. State upfront in each review what stage you are in and what type of feedback matters most.
- **Example**: Julie advised teams not to debate colors or animation before validating whether the product actually solves an important problem and is easy to use.

### Problem-First Feedback for Designers
- **When to use**: When giving feedback to designers or product teams in critiques and review sessions.
- **Steps**:
  1. Describe the problem you see rather than jumping immediately to a solution.
  2. Explain where you got confused, what felt unclear, or what seemed misaligned.
  3. Provide examples or evidence for why you think it is a problem.
  4. Offer solution ideas only as suggestions, not directives.
  5. Let the designer or owner synthesize the problem and develop the best solution.
- **Example**: Julie warned against feedback like “make the logo purple” without first explaining the underlying issue, such as lack of clarity or poor visual fit.

### Path to Management Through Pre-Management Practice
- **When to use**: When you want to become a manager but do not yet have the title or formal opportunity.
- **Steps**:
  1. Tell your manager explicitly that you want to become a manager.
  2. Ask what skills or evidence are required for them to believe you can succeed.
  3. Create a development plan together around those skills.
  4. Practice management-adjacent work as an IC, such as recruiting, mentoring, onboarding, and improving process.
  5. Use those experiences to test your interest and demonstrate readiness.
  6. If the company is not growing and no role exists, consider moving to an environment with more opportunity.
- **Example**: Julie suggested mentoring interns, acting as an onboarding buddy, or leading process improvements as ways to build management skills before formally becoming a manager.

### Hiring Designers by Signaling Design Commitment
- **When to use**: When founders or PMs are struggling to attract strong design talent.
- **Steps**:
  1. Demonstrate that design matters at your company, not just as a checkbox.
  2. Invest in visible design quality, even through agencies or contractors if needed.
  3. Learn the language, tools, and values of design so you can speak credibly with candidates.
  4. Immerse yourself in the design community by following designers and talking to practitioners.
  5. Build authentic relationships with designers over time.
- **Example**: Julie noted that candidates will inspect your website and product to judge whether you truly care about design and whether they can build a strong design culture with you.

## Core Advice
- **When you face impostor syndrome in a new role**: Reframe the discomfort as a sign that you are in a high-growth stretch, and ask for help instead of hiding uncertainty — because new challenges naturally create discomfort; treating that feeling as normal reduces shame and accelerates learning through support and feedback.
- **When you face difficulty speaking up in large meetings**: Build a regular writing habit with a simple publishing cadence and focus on hitting publish rather than being brilliant — because writing clarifies thinking and lowers the emotional barrier to expressing opinions publicly, which transfers into verbal communication.
- **When you face trouble starting or sustaining writing**: Use a word-count goal instead of a quality goal, and separate drafting from editing — because a controllable output target reduces perfectionism and helps you get past the blank page.
- **When you face the need to improve product sense**: Observe your own product experiences in detail, discuss products with others, and pair those observations with experiment results and data — because strong product judgment comes from repeated observation, comparison, and validation rather than abstract theory alone.
- **When you face disagreement over whether to trust founder intuition or do more research**: Calibrate based on how deeply the founder knows the customer and how broad the user base has become — because intuition is strongest when the builder is the user; as distance from the customer or audience diversity increases, research becomes more important.
- **When you face messy or unproductive product review meetings**: Gather feedback from multiple groups, but synthesize it using a clear order: value first, ease of use second, delight third — because this prevents teams from over-indexing on aesthetics before confirming the product solves a real problem and is usable.
- **When you face the need to give feedback to a designer**: Explain the problem you see before proposing a solution — because problem-first feedback preserves the designer's ability to explore better solutions and ensures the team aligns on what actually needs fixing.
- **When you face a stalled path to management**: Tell your manager you want to become a manager, ask what skills are missing, and start practicing management-adjacent work as an IC — because many management skills can be demonstrated before promotion, which both builds readiness and creates evidence for advancement.
- **When you face difficulty hiring strong designers**: Visibly demonstrate that your company values design and learn enough about design culture to speak credibly with candidates — because great designers want to work where design is respected and where leaders understand its role in building products.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Feeling like an impostor means you are not ready or not qualified. → **Their view**: Impostor feelings often coincide with the fastest periods of growth and are not inherently a bad sign. — because if you are doing something new for the first time, discomfort is expected; repeated exposure and support build competence over time.
- **Conventional**: You should project confidence and avoid showing uncertainty, especially as a leader. → **Their view**: Being vulnerable, asking for help, and openly sharing what is hard can make you more effective. — because openness creates support, better collaboration, and deeper trust, while pretending to know everything blocks learning.
- **Conventional**: Writing should be driven by audience growth, quality, and polished output. → **Their view**: The best writing habit starts by writing for yourself with action-based goals like word count or hitting publish. — because external validation creates barriers you cannot control, while self-oriented practice builds consistency and clarity.
- **Conventional**: Design intuition and quantitative analysis are opposing approaches. → **Their view**: Qualitative observation and quantitative data should reinforce each other in building product sense. — because data can validate or challenge assumptions from intuition, while observation helps explain why the numbers move.
- **Conventional**: A product review should happen in one main meeting where everyone aligns on the best design. → **Their view**: More feedback from more groups is better, and the real work is in synthesizing conflicting input rather than forcing consensus in one room. — because different audiences see different issues; consensus is unrealistic, but structured synthesis around user, problem, and priority leads to better decisions.
- **Conventional**: Good feedback means telling the team what solution to implement. → **Their view**: The most useful feedback is to clearly identify the problem, not prescribe the fix. — because jumping to solutions hides assumptions and can constrain better ideas from the people closest to the work.
