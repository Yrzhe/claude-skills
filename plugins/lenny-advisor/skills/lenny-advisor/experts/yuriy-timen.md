## Signature Frameworks

### Choose the right primary growth engine
- **When to use**: When evaluating how a subscription business should scale acquisition efficiently.
- **Steps**:
  1. Check whether the product has inherent network effects or collaboration-driven utility.
  2. If not, assess whether the business has strong SEO potential via long-tail programmatic or data-driven content.
  3. If neither network effects nor SEO are strong, evaluate whether paid acquisition can work given unit economics and payback windows.
  4. Use the strongest engine as the primary growth driver, then layer on secondary channels later.
- **Example**: Canva used long-tail programmatic SEO; Grammarly leaned heavily on performance marketing; Airtable and Whimsical benefit from collaboration/network effects.

### SEO opportunity audit
- **When to use**: When deciding whether SEO is worth investing in for a company with limited resources.
- **Steps**:
  1. Determine whether you have a unique editorial angle that can contribute something new to search results.
  2. Check for a programmatic angle, such as templates or scalable page generation.
  3. Check for a unique data angle that can power useful organic experiences.
  4. If you can satisfy at least two of the three, SEO may be promising.
  5. Time-box the experiment to about three months and evaluate whether the channel is likely to work.
- **Example**: Monarch Money could potentially leverage unique user-linked financial data to create organic search experiences.

### Paid growth viability test
- **When to use**: When considering whether paid acquisition should be a core growth strategy.
- **Steps**:
  1. Verify that the funnel converts well enough to support paid traffic.
  2. Check retention and lifetime value to ensure payback is viable.
  3. Assess runway and monthly burn to see whether the company can tolerate delayed payback.
  4. Look at channel saturation and competitor efficiency.
  5. Use paid not only for acquisition, but also as a fast learning loop for messaging, positioning, design, and feature testing.
- **Example**: A company with strong conversion, strong LTV, and enough runway may use paid to test headlines and positioning while also scaling acquisition.

### Channel experimentation with guard rails
- **When to use**: When testing a growth channel without wasting too much time or money.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define objective benchmarks for the channel where possible.
  2. Set minimum volume thresholds, such as impressions or traffic.
  3. Test multiple creative or tactical angles.
  4. Compare results against expected KPI ranges.
  5. Continue only if the channel shows promise; otherwise abandon it.
  6. Revisit abandoned channels later if conditions change.
- **Example**: For YouTube, define minimum impressions, test 2-3 creative angles, and use click-through-rate benchmarks to decide whether to continue.

### Diversify only after one engine is truly working
- **When to use**: When a company is tempted to spread growth efforts across many channels too early.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the one channel or motion that is driving the majority of growth.
  2. Lean into it until it becomes a real strategic advantage.
  3. Avoid premature diversification when total growth is still small.
  4. At later stages, carve out resources to reduce dependence on a single channel.
- **Example**: Early-stage companies should not stop optimizing a channel that drives 80% of acquisition just because they are worried about future concentration risk.

### Onboarding-first activation improvement
- **When to use**: When a product has a complex workflow and users struggle to reach value quickly.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the user's intended job-to-be-done as early as possible.
  2. Collect relevant context up front.
  3. Customize the first-run experience based on that context.
  4. Avoid exposing users to the full complexity of the product immediately.
  5. Guide users to the aha moment faster and more reliably.
- **Example**: Airtable or Canva can tailor the initial experience so users are not dropped into a blank, complex editor without guidance.

## Core Advice
- **When a channel “doesn’t work”**: Audit whether you gave it a fair shot before concluding it failed — because many teams abandon channels after weak, poorly designed tests that were never capable of producing a valid signal.
- **When your product becomes more useful as more people use it**: Invest in referral and viral loops — because inherent network effects make virality much more likely to work.
- **When your product has no inherent network effects**: Do not assume you can manufacture virality from scratch — because network effects are usually either present from the start or not.
- **When your company has strong LTV and healthy conversion from free to paid**: Consider paid acquisition as a serious growth engine — because those economics can support efficient payback.
- **When deciding whether to invest in SEO**: Look for a unique editorial, programmatic, or data angle before committing — because SEO is most promising when you can contribute something differentiated.
- **When testing SEO with limited resources**: Time-box the experiment to about three months — because SEO has a long feedback loop and you need a bounded test.
- **When you need SEO expertise**: Get an outside audit from a specialist or agency — because SEO is nuanced and changes quickly.
- **When paid acquisition is under pressure**: Tighten payback expectations and attribution discipline — because the market has shifted toward shorter payback windows and less tolerance for ambiguity.
- **When your company is early and one channel is already working**: Double down on the winning channel before diversifying — because premature diversification can distract from building a durable advantage.
- **When your company is later stage and overly dependent on one acquisition channel**: Allocate resources to diversify growth — because concentration risk becomes a real vulnerability at scale.
- **When your onboarding is complex or users get lost in the product**: Invest heavily in onboarding and contextual guidance — because it can materially improve activation and retention.
- **When you are unsure where to spend time for highest ROI**: Do customer research first — because interviews and surveys create clarity, alignment, and momentum.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: If a channel doesn't work, move on quickly. → **Their view**: Often the real problem is that the channel was never tested properly — because weak experiments can produce false negatives.
- **Conventional**: Every company should try to build virality. → **Their view**: Virality is usually not manufacturable unless the product already has inherent network effects — because referral loops work best when the product naturally gets better with more users.
- **Conventional**: SEO is a generic growth lever that any company can eventually use. → **Their view**: SEO only makes sense when you have a differentiated angle: editorial, programmatic, or data-driven — because generic content rarely becomes a durable advantage.
- **Conventional**: Paid acquisition is less attractive now, so startups should avoid it. → **Their view**: The current contraction creates an advantage for disciplined companies with strong economics and attribution — because weaker spenders pull back and competition drops.
- **Conventional**: Attribution should be as precise as possible before spending heavily. → **Their view**: Over-investing in attribution can be as harmful as under-investing — because the right level depends on stage and spend complexity.
- **Conventional**: Diversify growth as soon as one channel starts working. → **Their view**: Early-stage companies often should not diversify yet; they should intensify the winning channel first — because focus matters more than resilience before scale.
