# Dan Shipper

**Co-founder and CEO** | **Every** | Expertise: ai-strategy, go-to-market, product-management, operations

## Bio
Dan Shipper is the co-founder and CEO of Every, a media-plus-software company building at the frontier of AI. He’s especially valuable as a perspective because he runs a small, AI-native organization that both uses and ships AI products, giving him unusually practical insight into what actually changes when AI becomes part of the operating system of a company.

He thinks about AI less as a novelty and more as a management and workflow revolution. That makes his viewpoint especially useful for leaders who want to turn AI from isolated experiments into durable leverage across product, operations, and company culture.

## Signature Frameworks

### Head of AI Operations
- **When to use**: When a company wants to systematically increase employee leverage with AI instead of relying on ad hoc individual experimentation.
- **Steps**:
  1. Hire or assign a person focused on AI operations rather than day-to-day functional output.
  2. Keep a running list of repetitive tasks across the organization.
  3. Meet regularly with team members to identify workflows worth automating.
  4. Turn repeated work into prompts, workflows, or lightweight internal tools.
  5. Drive adoption behaviorally by ensuring teams actually use the new workflows.
- **Example**: At Every, a head of AI operations turns repetitive editorial work into reusable prompts and commands. One example is a style-guide prompt converted into a Claude Code command that scans the codebase for copy, creates a pull request, and lets the editor review changes instead of manually editing everything.

### Compounding Engineering
- **When to use**: When engineering teams are building quickly with AI agents and want each unit of work to make future work faster.
- **Steps**:
  1. Notice recurring work patterns in the build process.
  2. Identify the “platonic ideal” of the artifact you keep producing, such as a PRD.
  3. Create prompts, slash commands, or automations that generate that artifact from rough input.
  4. Store and share these reusable building blocks in a shared repository.
  5. Continuously refine the system so every project reduces effort on the next one.
- **Example**: The Cora team created prompts that turn rambling thoughts into high-quality PRDs, so instead of rewriting specs from scratch each time, they use reusable prompt infrastructure that speeds up every subsequent feature.

### Internal-First AI Product Incubation
- **When to use**: When exploring new AI product opportunities in a fast-changing market where workflows are still emerging.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start from an expensive service or capability you wish you had more of.
  2. Use general-purpose AI tools like ChatGPT or Claude to test whether the workflow is genuinely useful.
  3. If the use case proves valuable internally, unbundle it into a dedicated product.
  4. Use your own team as the first power users and quality filter.
  5. Expand to an adjacent audience that shares similar needs and behavior.
- **Example**: Every noticed repeated internal demand for AI help with email, writing, and file organization. They first used general-purpose models for those jobs, then turned validated workflows into products like Cora, Sparkle, and Spiral.

### AI Adoption Rollout for Companies
- **When to use**: When a larger organization wants to move from AI curiosity to broad, practical adoption.
- **Steps**:
  1. Research the organization through interviews to understand team workflows and repetitive tasks.
  2. Produce a report and interactive analysis showing where leverage is likely and which teams are engaged or lagging.
  3. Customize training by function so each team gets prompts and examples tied to its actual work.
  4. Run repeated training sessions over several weeks rather than a one-off workshop.
  5. Create organizational rituals such as weekly prompt-sharing and usage updates to sustain momentum.
- **Example**: Every interviews teams, builds a dashboard showing likely leverage and adoption patterns, then trains each team with role-specific prompts over four weeks. Dan cited companies that send weekly AI usage emails and hold meetings where employees share prompts and use cases.

### Allocation Economy / Model Management
- **When to use**: When thinking about which human skills become more valuable as AI handles more execution work.
- **Steps**:
  1. Frame AI usage as a management problem rather than a pure execution problem.
  2. Define the task clearly and gather the right context.
  3. Choose the right model or agent for the job.
  4. Break work into sub-tasks and allocate them across tools or agents.
  5. Evaluate outputs using taste, vision, and quality criteria.
  6. Give feedback and decide when to intervene versus delegate.
- **Example**: Dan argues that using AI well looks like management: deciding how to communicate the problem, what context to provide, which model to use, and how to judge whether the result is good.

### Leash-Length Definition of AGI
- **When to use**: When evaluating AI progress in practical terms rather than abstract benchmark definitions.
- **Steps**:
  1. Measure how long an AI system can work autonomously before needing human intervention.
  2. Compare systems by the length and usefulness of that autonomous work period.
  3. Assess whether the agent can stay productive without constant prompting.
  4. Use economic profitability as the threshold for meaningful autonomy.
  5. Treat AGI as the point where it is profitable to keep agents running indefinitely.
- **Example**: Dan contrasts tab-complete, chat responses, and 20–30 minute autonomous agent runs, then proposes that AGI is when it becomes economically worthwhile to keep agents running all the time.

## Core Advice
- **When repeated tasks pile up across the company**: hire a dedicated head of AI operations or assign someone explicitly responsible for turning repetitive work into prompts, workflows, and internal tools — because most employees default to the manual path unless someone owns the leverage work.
- **When useful AI workflows aren’t being adopted**: pair the workflow with behavioral reinforcement, such as requiring people to run work through the prompt before handing it off — because the bottleneck is usually habit change, not capability.
- **When you’re a non-technical person with lots of text-heavy work**: use agentic command-line tools like Claude Code or Gemini CLI to process local files and long-running tasks — because they can handle large, messy workflows better than one-shot chat.
- **When product specs or setup work keep repeating**: build prompts and slash commands that transform rough thoughts into standardized PRDs or other reusable artifacts — because each automation compounds into future savings.
- **When you’re unsure what AI product to build**: start with an expensive service you wish you had more access to, test it with general-purpose models, and only then turn it into a dedicated product — because real internal demand is a better signal than novelty.
- **When you need broad company adoption of AI**: make sure the CEO uses AI personally and visibly, then create rituals like weekly prompt-sharing sessions and company-wide usage updates — because leadership behavior legitimizes the change.
- **When juniors may lose entry-level work**: mentor them closely and encourage them to capture feedback in prompts — because AI can compress learning loops and accelerate growth if coaching is intentional.
- **When evaluating AI tools**: use “vibe checks” and real workflow usefulness in your own context — because benchmarks often miss the actual constraints of day-to-day work.
- **When deciding what skills matter most in the AI era**: invest in management-like skills: defining problems, allocating work, evaluating outputs, exercising taste, and knowing when to intervene — because AI shifts value from execution to orchestration.
- **When your startup doesn’t fit a standard playbook**: design the company around the work you’re uniquely energized to do — because suppressing founder strengths creates friction and weakens the business.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: AI will mainly eliminate jobs, especially entry-level roles. → **Their view**: AI may reshore jobs and accelerate junior talent rather than simply destroy employment — because cheap intelligence expands what becomes economically feasible.
- **Conventional**: Command-line AI tools are mainly for engineers. → **Their view**: Tools like Claude Code are massively underrated for non-coders — because they can process large local datasets and long-running text tasks.
- **Conventional**: GPT wrappers are weak businesses with little defensibility. → **Their view**: GPT wrappers are often the right way to productize useful workflows — because the value is in the workflow, context, interface, and judgment loop, not just the model call.
- **Conventional**: AGI should be defined by benchmarks or abstract intelligence tests. → **Their view**: AGI is when it becomes economically profitable to run agents indefinitely — because practical autonomy matters more than philosophical labels.
- **Conventional**: AI makes people think less in a net-negative way. → **Their view**: AI shifts which cognitive skills matter, like writing once did — because technologies often trade one skill for another while increasing leverage.
- **Conventional**: One best AI agent will dominate most workflows. → **Their view**: There will be a market for multiple agents with different strengths and tastes — because different tasks require different styles and capabilities.
- **Conventional**: Serious software still requires hand-written code as the core activity. → **Their view**: Frontier teams are already moving from writing code to managing agents that write code — because the bottleneck is moving up the abstraction stack.
- **Conventional**: Startups should either raise VC or bootstrap. → **Their view**: There’s a middle ground of small, flexible capital raises that preserve optionality — because AI-native companies can do more with less money.
