# Fareed Mosavat

**Chief Development Officer** | **Reforge** | Expertise: product-management, leadership, growth, go-to-market

## Bio
Fareed Mosavat is a veteran product leader who has led growth and product teams at Reforge, Slack, Instacart, Runkeeper, and Zynga, and started his career as an engineer at Pixar. His perspective matters because he combines deep operator experience with unusually practical frameworks for PM growth, trust-building, and the transition from individual contributor to leader.

He is especially strong on the “how” of product leadership: how to learn faster, earn sponsorship, scale your scope, and avoid the common traps that stall PM careers.

## Signature Frameworks

### PM learning acceleration loop
- **When to use**: When trying to improve as a product manager, especially early-to-mid career, or when designing how to accelerate PM growth.
- **Steps**:
  1. Execute on real product problems with real customers and real data.
  2. Generalize what you learned into reusable principles and mental models.
  3. Communicate the work, learnings, and implications so others understand your impact.
  4. Scale your opportunities by taking on broader, more ambiguous problems.
  5. Repeat the loop with increasingly unknown problems and larger scope.
- **Example**: Learning from Instacart onboarding experiments that higher-friction onboarding can outperform low-friction flows when activation requires meaningful setup, then applying that lesson beyond one product.

### Two stack levels up, two stack levels down, left and right
- **When to use**: When building trust, earning sponsorship, and becoming a more strategic PM or product leader.
- **Steps**:
  1. Understand the technical and operational details below your layer of ownership.
  2. Understand your manager's priorities and your manager's manager's priorities.
  3. Map adjacent teams and functions to the left and right of your work.
  4. Build a mental model of how your area connects to company strategy and execution.
  5. Use that understanding to communicate in ways that matter to leadership and peers.
- **Example**: For activation or monetization work, understand billing mechanics and Stripe details below you, while also understanding free vs. paid trade-offs and how enterprise, sales, and core product teams connect to your area.

### Shift from doer to editor
- **When to use**: When moving from senior IC to manager or leader and trying to avoid becoming a bottleneck.
- **Steps**:
  1. Stop assuming your job is to personally do the highest-leverage work.
  2. Assess each team member's capability and the level of support they need.
  3. Choose the least amount of intervention needed to make the work great.
  4. Coach, review, and improve others' work instead of owning every detail yourself.
  5. Deliberately give meaningful opportunities away so others grow and you scale.
- **Example**: A new manager keeps the most important projects for themselves, gives the team less interesting work, becomes overworked, and blocks team growth.

### Four types of product work
- **When to use**: When evaluating a product portfolio, broadening as a leader, or diagnosing whether you're over-indexed on one style of PM work.
- **Steps**:
  1. Feature work: add features and experiences for existing customers.
  2. Growth work: connect more customers to existing value and improve key business metrics.
  3. Product-market-fit expansion: either sell the same product to a new audience or a new product to the same audience.
  4. Scaling work: solve technical, trust, safety, and user-scale problems that emerge as the product grows.
- **Example**: A growth leader over-applies experimentation to everything, even though scaling work or PMF expansion may require different approaches.

### Own the outcome, not just the resources
- **When to use**: When stepping into leadership roles where you are accountable for business results rather than just roadmap execution.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start from the outcome the business needs.
  2. Determine what team, cross-functional support, and resources are actually required.
  3. Make the case for those resources explicitly.
  4. Clarify trade-offs if the resources are not available.
  5. Avoid acting like a victim of current staffing; propose what it would take to succeed.
- **Example**: At Instacart and Slack, defaulting to working within the team you have instead of proactively arguing for more headcount or deeper partnerships with marketing, sales, data, and finance.

## Core Advice
- **When you want to become a better PM**: Do real product work with real customers and real data, then deliberately extract general lessons from each project — because PM skill is built through execution, not theory alone.
- **When you want bigger opportunities**: Do strong work and communicate not just results, but what you learned and how it applies more broadly — because leaders sponsor judgment, not just output.
- **When you want sponsorship**: Work on one of the company’s top priorities and make your thinking useful to the broader organization — because visible, important problems attract advocates.
- **When you want to build trust as a PM**: Understand two levels up, two levels down, and adjacent teams around your area — because trust comes from seeing the system, not just your slice.
- **When you transition from IC to manager**: Shift from being the best doer to being an editor, coach, and multiplier — because keeping the best work for yourself makes you the bottleneck.
- **When you’re tempted to hoard high-impact projects as a new manager**: Give meaningful work to others and calibrate your involvement based on their skill and the stakes — because that creates leverage and develops the team.
- **When you step into leadership with business accountability**: Define the resources and cross-functional support needed instead of only planning around the team you already have — because leaders own outcomes and trade-offs.
- **When you want to become an advisor or portfolio operator**: Build both depth and breadth, then articulate a narrow area where you are exceptional — because advisory value comes from specific expertise that generalizes.
- **When choosing early or mid-career roles**: Do your best work at strong companies with meaningful momentum — because impact is easier to create and easier to sell later.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: You can become a strong PM mainly through courses, books, and training before doing the job. → **Their view**: Real PM growth only happens through execution on real products with real customers and data; training is just a layer on top. — because product management depends on specific knowledge that can’t be simulated well.
- **Conventional**: Mentorship is the main driver of PM career growth. → **Their view**: Sponsorship is often more important than mentorship for career inflection points. — because career jumps come from being trusted with bigger problems.
- **Conventional**: The best new manager should keep the most important work because they can do it fastest and best. → **Their view**: That instinct creates a manager death spiral; leaders must give away meaningful work and become editors instead of doers. — because leverage and team growth matter more than personal speed.
- **Conventional**: A leader’s job is to do the best possible with the resources they are given. → **Their view**: A leader’s job is to define what resources are needed to achieve the outcome and make that case clearly. — because leadership means owning trade-offs, not just constraints.
- **Conventional**: In product, realism and internal correctness matter most. → **Their view**: What matters most is the end experience and customer outcome, not whether the internal model is perfectly “real.” — because fidelity only matters insofar as it improves the user result.
