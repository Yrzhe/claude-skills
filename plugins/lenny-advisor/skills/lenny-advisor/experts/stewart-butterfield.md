# Stewart Butterfield

**Founder, product leader, and former CEO** | **Slack (also co-founder of Flickr; former CEO of Slack/Salesforce acquisition)** | Expertise: product design, B2B SaaS, consumerized enterprise software, product strategy

## Bio
Stewart Butterfield is the founder of Flickr and Slack, and one of the clearest voices on building products people genuinely love. His perspective matters because he combines product taste with operational discipline: he cares about craft, but he also knows when to pivot, when to say no, and when generosity is a strategic advantage.

He is especially influential for his thinking on comprehension, utility, and the difference between real value and busywork. If you are building a product that needs to feel simple, humane, and indispensable, his lens is unusually useful.

## Signature Frameworks

### Utility Curve
- **When to use**: When deciding whether a feature, improvement, or product investment is worth the effort.
- **Steps**:
  1. Plot effort or cost on the horizontal axis and value, convenience, or quality on the vertical axis.
  2. Identify whether the idea is on the shallow part of the curve, the steep value-creating middle, or the flattening diminishing-returns zone.
  3. Invest until you reach the threshold where users get a meaningful jump in utility or aha moment.
  4. Stop when additional work adds complexity but little incremental value.
  5. Re-evaluate continuously because the curve shifts upward as user expectations rise.
- **Example**: Slack’s login and notification decisions were treated as utility-curve problems: the team invested in magic links and notification defaults because they created a big jump in usefulness, not just a small convenience gain.

### Comprehension Over Friction
- **When to use**: When users are not failing because a flow is too long, but because they do not understand what the product is, what a choice means, or what happens next.
- **Steps**:
  1. Determine whether the real problem is friction or comprehension.
  2. Ask whether the user already has clear intent and specificity.
  3. Use UI hierarchy, defaults, labels, and progressive disclosure to make the next action obvious.
  4. Reduce the amount of thinking required, not just the number of clicks.
  5. Only optimize for fewer steps when the user already knows exactly what they want.
- **Example**: Slack’s onboarding and feature discovery were designed to explain what the product does and what to do next, because new users often arrived with only vague intent.

### Taste as a Competitive Advantage
- **When to use**: When building products in crowded markets where small details can create emotional attachment and word-of-mouth growth.
- **Steps**:
  1. Develop discernment through practice and exposure.
  2. Notice small inconveniences that others ignore.
  3. Fix the details that create empathy, courtesy, and delight.
  4. Use craft to create emotional connection, not just functional completion.
  5. Treat taste as something that can be trained and operationalized.
- **Example**: The “tilt your umbrella” story became a Slack product principle: small acts of courtesy and empathy were turned into product behavior and company culture.

### Known Valuable Work
- **When to use**: When an organization starts drifting into busywork, meetings, and low-signal activity.
- **Steps**:
  1. Clarify what work is both known and valuable.
  2. Ensure the team has enough concrete, high-value tasks to do.
  3. Explicitly say no to lower-value work before it starts.
  4. Keep alignment tight so people do not invent work to fill the gap.
  5. Continuously refresh priorities as the organization scales.
- **Example**: At Slack, Butterfield argued that leaders must ensure there is sufficient supply of known valuable work, rather than letting teams create fake work to stay busy.

### Coldly Rational Pivot Evaluation
- **When to use**: When deciding whether to persevere, pivot, or shut down a project.
- **Steps**:
  1. Ask whether you have exhausted the realistic possibilities.
  2. Separate the decision from emotion and humiliation.
  3. Estimate expected value of continuing versus switching.
  4. Account for the cost to employees, users, and investors.
  5. Pivot only when the current path is no longer rationally defensible.
- **Example**: Butterfield described Slack as emerging after Glitch had exhausted its commercially realistic options, making the pivot a rational fold rather than an impulsive one.

### Generosity as a Long-Run Business Strategy
- **When to use**: When setting policies for customers, employees, or partners in ways that affect trust and retention.
- **Steps**:
  1. Default to cooperation and fairness.
  2. Make policies generous when the downside is manageable.
  3. Use generosity to signal trustworthiness and reduce friction for others.
  4. Measure success by value created for customers over time.
  5. Avoid short-term extraction that damages long-term trust.
- **Example**: Slack’s fair billing, employee-friendly IPO structure, and automatic outage credits reflected a deliberate bias toward generosity.

## Core Advice
- **When a feature seems useful but not obviously transformative**: use a utility-curve lens to ask whether you are still on the steep part of the curve or already in diminishing returns — because many features add complexity without crossing the threshold where users feel a meaningful jump in value.
- **When users are dropping off a flow**: check whether the real issue is comprehension rather than friction — because if users do not understand what the product is or what happens next, removing clicks will not help.
- **When designing onboarding or first-use experiences**: make the next action obvious and reduce the amount of thinking required — because new users often arrive with weak intent and low context, so clarity matters more than speed.
- **When a product has many possible actions**: show the 2-5 most common actions and hide the rest behind an “other” path — because presenting every option equally increases cognitive load and makes decisions harder.
- **When users are already highly intent-driven**: optimize aggressively for fewer steps and less friction — because if someone knows exactly what they want, like buying concert tickets, speed and convenience matter a lot.
- **When a product choice could annoy or confuse users**: use defaults and progressive disclosure to get them over the hump, then let them customize later — because people often will not choose the best setting on their own unless the product first teaches them why it matters.
- **When a feature could be abused in a social or collaborative product**: shape behavior with guardrails, warnings, or playful friction — because Slack’s rooster warning for @everyone reduced abuse and taught users the cost of overusing the feature.
- **When evaluating whether to pivot**: wait until you have exhausted the realistic good ideas, then make the decision coldly and rationally — because pivoting is emotionally humiliating, but continuing past the point of rationality wastes time and capital.
- **When your team starts creating meetings and analysis that do not clearly move the business**: treat it as a leadership problem and restore clarity around priorities and valuable work — because people often create work to fill time and signal productivity, even when it is not actually useful.
- **When setting customer policies or SLAs**: be generous when the downside is bounded and the upside is trust — because generosity can create loyalty, reduce internal conflict, and strengthen long-term customer relationships.
- **When building a new product category**: do not just build the product; also explain the problem it solves and the outcome it enables — because you are often creating the market as much as the product, especially when the category is unfamiliar.
- **When you are tempted to optimize for fewer clicks**: ask whether the user is comparing options or simply trying to accomplish a known task — because reducing clicks can backfire if it forces users to make harder decisions or understand too much at once.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Always reduce friction and minimize clicks. → **Their view**: Often the real problem is comprehension, and a few extra steps can be better if they make the product simpler to understand — because users abandon products when they do not understand what they are signing up for or what happens next.
- **Conventional**: The best product is the one with the fewest steps. → **Their view**: A product with more steps can be superior if each step is trivially easy and the overall experience is clearer — because trying to force everything onto one screen often creates an overwhelming decision problem.
- **Conventional**: More features and more options make products more valuable. → **Their view**: Extra features often add complexity without crossing the utility threshold, so they can reduce value — because diminishing returns are real.
- **Conventional**: Pivoting is a sign of failure or lack of perseverance. → **Their view**: A pivot can be the rational choice once you have exhausted the realistic possibilities — because continuing out of pride or humiliation is strategically wrong.
- **Conventional**: Work is work if people are in meetings discussing product decisions. → **Their view**: A lot of that is hyper-realistic work-like activity that looks like work but produces little value — because organizations naturally generate busywork.
- **Conventional**: Customer generosity is a cost center that should be tightly minimized. → **Their view**: Generosity can be a strategic advantage and a long-term growth driver — because trust, loyalty, and goodwill compound over time.
