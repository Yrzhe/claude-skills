# Graham Weaver

**Founder and CEO of Alpine Investors; Lecturer at Stanford Graduate School of Business** | **Alpine Investors / Stanford Graduate School of Business** | Expertise: leadership, career development, entrepreneurship, private equity

## Bio
Graham Weaver is the founder and CEO of Alpine Investors, a top-performing private equity firm, and a Stanford GSB lecturer known for helping people make more intentional decisions about work and life. His perspective matters because he combines hard-earned operator/investor experience with unusually practical frameworks for overcoming fear, clarifying direction, and building momentum.

He stands out for treating career choice as an identity and energy problem, not just a rational optimization problem. His advice is especially useful for people who feel stuck between a safe path and a more meaningful one.

## Signature Frameworks

### The Genie Framework
- **When to use**: When you're unsure what career or life path to pursue, especially when fear of failure is distorting your decision-making.
- **Steps**:
  1. Imagine a genie guarantees that whatever you devote your life and career to will turn out great, though it may take longer and be harder than expected.
  2. Ask yourself: what would you choose if you knew you would not fail?
  3. Identify the answer that feels closest to your heart rather than the socially approved option.
  4. Treat that answer as your long-term direction, even if you cannot pursue it immediately.
  5. Work backward from that vision to determine what experience, money, or preparation you need to get onto that path.
- **Example**: A Stanford student from Brazil realized his real dream was to start a nonprofit expanding access to education in Brazil, rather than returning to a conventional consulting path.

### Limiting Beliefs Deconstruction
- **When to use**: When you know what you want but feel blocked by fear, uncertainty, or practical objections.
- **Steps**:
  1. Write down every fear, objection, and limiting belief related to the path you want.
  2. Move those beliefs out of your subconscious and into explicit language on paper.
  3. Notice how naming them reduces their emotional power.
  4. Translate each belief into a concrete obstacle or to-do item.
  5. Solve those obstacles one by one like any other problem.
- **Example**: Fears like “I don't know how to fund this” or “I don't know how to start” become planning tasks instead of vague blockers.

### Nine Lives Exercise
- **When to use**: When choosing a single life path feels too intimidating or constraining, and you want to explore multiple meaningful futures.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define life one as your current life starting from today.
  2. Create eight additional possible lives, all starting from today.
  3. Make sure each life is genuinely exciting to you.
  4. Look for the life that gives you the most energy.
  5. Pull a piece of that life into your current life now, even if only as a side project or experiment.
  6. Recognize that over a long enough life, you may be able to live many of these lives sequentially.
- **Example**: Weaver described lives such as founder, author, professor, video creator, and actor, and noted that over time he has incorporated many of those identities into his actual life.

### Weekly Goal + Three Actions Practice
- **When to use**: When you need a simple system to create focus, reinforce identity, and make progress toward a long-term goal.
- **Steps**:
  1. Write down a bold identity-based goal at the top of the page.
  2. List three things you will do today to move toward that goal.
  3. Repeat this consistently, ideally multiple times per week.
  4. Use the repetition to align your subconscious attention with your desired future.
  5. Track progress over time rather than waiting for motivation.
- **Example**: As a college freshman trying to row crew, Weaver wrote every morning, “I am the number one rower in the country,” and then listed three actions for that day.

### Accountability Through Coaching
- **When to use**: When self-reflection alone is not enough and you need structure to make intentional life changes stick.
- **Steps**:
  1. Create recurring space to reflect on major life domains such as career, relationships, health, spirituality, and family.
  2. Use an executive coach, or if that is not affordable, a like-minded friend.
  3. Before each session, document your annual goals, what you did last week, what you will do next week, and what you want from the conversation.
  4. Review progress regularly and let the other person hold you accountable.
  5. Talk through your goals out loud to activate deeper thinking and commitment.
- **Example**: One of Weaver's coaches requires him to complete a weekly form covering yearly goals, prior-week actions, next-week actions, and desired outcomes for the call.

### Scale Your Bright Spots
- **When to use**: When deciding whether to persist through difficulty and how to improve something that is not yet working.
- **Steps**:
  1. Look for small signs of what is working, even in an otherwise struggling effort.
  2. Identify those bright spots explicitly.
  3. Do more of what is working and reduce what is not.
  4. Let repeated bright spots gradually define your strategy.
  5. Quit only when you can no longer see or believe in the vision and no bright spots remain.
- **Example**: At Alpine, even during difficult early years, Weaver kept noticing small wins such as a good deal or a strong hire, and over time those bright spots became the firm's strategy and operating model.

## Core Advice
- **When you face uncertainty about what to do with your life or career**: Do the genie exercise and ask what you would pursue if you knew you would not fail — because this strips away fear-based decision-making and reveals the path that is most aligned with your real energy.
- **When you face a choice between a safe path and one that clearly excites you more**: Identify the fears and social pressures pushing you toward the safe option, then challenge them directly — because people often already know what they want but talk themselves out of it.
- **When you face vague fear about pursuing a dream**: Write down every limiting belief and convert each one into a concrete obstacle or to-do item — because what feels overwhelming in the subconscious becomes manageable once named.
- **When you face the feeling that now is not the right time to start**: Accept that it will never feel fully safe, and define the minimum conditions needed to begin — because waiting for certainty usually turns “not now” into “not ever.”
- **When you face a dream that feels too risky to pursue full-time**: Pull part of that life into your current life as a side project — because small experiments increase energy and test whether the path is truly meaningful.
- **When you face a plateau and are tempted to preserve short-term comfort**: Do the harder move that makes tomorrow worse but five years from now better — because most worthwhile change has a negative first move.
- **When you face a long, difficult journey and wonder whether success is taking too long**: Reset your timeline and assume meaningful outcomes may take a decade or more — because many people quit simply by underestimating time.
- **When you face trouble staying consistent with your goals**: Use structured accountability with a coach or committed peer — because external accountability turns intentions into repeated action.
- **When you face a big goal but feel scattered day to day**: Write down your goal and three actions for the day — because this creates focus and compounds progress.
- **When you face the question of whether to quit something difficult**: Ask whether you still believe in the vision and can still see bright spots worth scaling — because temporary pain is not always a reason to stop.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: You should choose the safest, most prestigious, or highest-paying path early in your career. → **Their view**: Choose the path that energizes you most, even if it is less conventional and slower to pay off — because energy compounds into persistence and excellence.
- **Conventional**: If something is meant to work, it should show clear success relatively quickly. → **Their view**: Most worthwhile things take far longer than people expect — because real success is often a decade-scale process.
- **Conventional**: There is an easier path that avoids suffering if you make the right choices. → **Their view**: Life involves suffering either way, so choose what is worth suffering for — because meaning comes from the cause, not the absence of pain.
- **Conventional**: Optimize for what makes tomorrow easier and more comfortable. → **Their view**: Accept “worse first” as the price of growth — because the best outcomes usually require an initially painful move.
- **Conventional**: External achievement, money, and status will make you feel fulfilled. → **Their view**: The real game of life is internal — because external wins do not fix self-worth or create lasting joy.
- **Conventional**: In investing, industry selection and financial engineering are the main drivers of returns. → **Their view**: Management quality creates most of the alpha — because exceptional leaders change outcomes.
