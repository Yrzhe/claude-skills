# Mayur Kamat

**Chief Product Officer** | **N26** | Expertise: product-management, fintech, experimentation, leadership

## Bio
Mayur Kamat is the Chief Product Officer at N26, with prior product leadership roles at Binance, Agoda, Google, and Microsoft. His perspective matters because he has operated in environments where speed, scale, and regulation collide—so he’s developed a very practical view of what actually drives product impact.

He is especially known for building experimentation-driven product cultures, selecting high-leverage problems, and helping PMs grow by leaning into strengths rather than trying to become generic generalists.

## Signature Frameworks

### Hypothesis-to-data product strategy
- **When to use**: When your team can test ideas quickly and you need a reliable way to decide what to build.
- **Steps**:
  1. Form a clear hypothesis about the expected impact on user behavior, conversion, CAC, or LTV.
  2. Design a fast experiment or test to validate the idea.
  3. Use the data to decide whether to scale, iterate, or stop.
  4. Repeat rapidly so strategy emerges from what works.
- **Example**: At N26, he checks Statsig dashboards, reviews metrics and p-values, and then says, “This is working. Let’s do more of these.”

### High-leverage problem selection
- **When to use**: When there are more important problems than time and you need to decide where to focus.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the problems with the biggest upside or downside.
  2. Prioritize the few areas where a 10x effect is possible.
  3. Go deep into the details of that problem.
  4. Move on once the leverage shifts elsewhere.
- **Example**: At Whitepages, Alex Allgood literally moved his desk to the team working on SEO because it was the highest-leverage opportunity.

### Daily leadership escalation loop
- **When to use**: When operating in a fast-moving environment where waiting for normal management cadence is too slow.
- **Steps**:
  1. Run a daily leadership meeting with key decision-makers.
  2. Assign one owner to each urgent problem.
  3. Expect the owner to mobilize the needed resources immediately.
  4. Review progress every day until resolved.
- **Example**: At Binance, leadership met every day at 11 PM, including weekends and holidays, so decisions were rarely blocked for more than 24 hours.

### Strengths-based career calibration
- **When to use**: When choosing roles or development paths early in your career.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify your real strengths through assessment or self-observation.
  2. Compare how you see yourself with how others experience you.
  3. Choose roles where success depends on those strengths.
  4. Avoid roles that reward your weaknesses or force low-fit work.
- **Example**: He used psychologist-led assessment at Agoda and Ray Dalio-style strengths voting to identify where he was strongest.

### Fast-growth compounding model
- **When to use**: When deciding where to start or accelerate your career.
- **Steps**:
  1. Join a company that is growing quickly.
  2. Learn from many cycles in a short time.
  3. Let network, brand, and scope compound your growth.
  4. Prefer environments where shipping and feedback happen continuously.
- **Example**: He contrasted Microsoft, where some internship projects had not shipped years later, with Binance, where learning happened every hour because the company was shipping constantly.

### Experimentation as product science
- **When to use**: When product decisions are subjective and everyone has an opinion.
- **Steps**:
  1. Instrument the product so ideas can be tested.
  2. Use experiments to create a shared source of truth.
  3. Make decisions based on evidence, not hierarchy.
  4. Build a culture where PMs use a scientific method.
- **Example**: He said experimentation gives PMs a “science” and lets them answer critics with evidence like, “we have done this experiment six times and it has failed.”

## Core Advice
- **When you are early in your PM career**: Join a fast-growing company where you can ship and learn in short cycles — because learning, network, and reputation compound faster there.
- **When you are choosing between roles**: Pick the job with the highest overlap with your strengths — because you grow faster by leaning into what you naturally do well.
- **When you are tempted to optimize for salary early**: Don’t optimize for compensation in the first 10–15 years — because learning, fit, and enjoyment usually create more long-term upside.
- **When you are unsure about the C-suite path**: Decide early whether you truly want to be an executive or founder — because the lifestyle and identity trade-offs are real.
- **When a product decision is reversible and testable**: Move fast and let data decide — because speed beats prolonged debate when you can correct course.
- **When a product has many edge cases or geographies**: Go deep into the highest-leverage segment — because the biggest gains often come from specific bottlenecks, not broad abstractions.
- **When your team is stuck on a long project**: Break it into smaller launches and validate early — because macro conditions can change before a long project ships.
- **When you are deciding what to work on as a leader**: Focus on 10x upside or downside problems — because your time is finite and leverage matters most.
- **When your team lacks experimentation**: Invest in incentives, tools, and rituals that make experimentation the default — because trusted experiments empower PMs and improve objectivity.
- **When your calendar is packed with meetings**: Keep large open blocks of time — because high-leverage work requires space to think and act.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Product strategy is the core of PM work. → **Their view**: Strategy is overrated for most PMs; the real job is moving quickly from hypothesis to data — because fast testing makes strategy emerge from evidence.
- **Conventional**: Senior leaders should stay high-level and avoid details. → **Their view**: High-leverage work often requires deep detail, even for executives — because the biggest bottlenecks are often hidden there.
- **Conventional**: A full calendar signals importance and productivity. → **Their view**: A full calendar is a badge of shame, not honor — because open time is required for high-leverage work.
- **Conventional**: PMs should aim to become CPOs by default. → **Their view**: Not everyone should pursue the C-suite — because many people are happier and more effective as specialists or balanced operators.
- **Conventional**: Big, long-term product bets are the path to impact. → **Their view**: Avoid six-month-to-year projects when possible — because the world changes before many of them ship.
- **Conventional**: Product decisions are mostly art and intuition. → **Their view**: With experimentation, product becomes scientific — because data can override hierarchy and loud opinions.
