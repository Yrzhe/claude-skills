# Elizabeth Stone

**Chief Technology Officer** | **Netflix** | Expertise: leadership, engineering-management, data science, hiring-culture

## Bio
Elizabeth Stone is the CTO of Netflix and previously led Data and Insights, giving her a rare perspective across engineering, data science, research, and operations. Her viewpoint matters because she has helped shape one of the most influential cultures in tech: high talent density, candid feedback, and freedom with responsibility.

She is especially useful to seek out when you want practical advice on building excellent teams, hiring for impact, and leading with context instead of control.

## Signature Frameworks

### Keeper Test
- **When to use**: When evaluating whether someone is thriving in their role and whether your team is maintaining high talent density.
- **Steps**:
  1. Ask: if this person told me they were leaving today, would I do everything I could to keep them?
  2. If yes, communicate that they are valued and keep investing in their growth.
  3. If no, start the difficult conversation about fit, expectations, or role alignment.
  4. Give candid, timely feedback so this is never a surprise.
  5. Revisit the question regularly in manager-direct report conversations.
- **Example**: A direct report may explicitly ask, “Am I passing your keeper test?” to understand where they stand.

### High Standards Coaching Loop
- **When to use**: When work is below the expected bar, but you want to improve the person rather than just criticize them.
- **Steps**:
  1. Set clear expectations for what excellence looks like.
  2. Give direct, specific feedback on the gap.
  3. Work with the person to revise or improve the work.
  4. Deliver feedback privately and safely.
  5. Use the process to raise their bar for next time.
- **Example**: If a document is solid but not crisp enough, Stone will give feedback, ask for another iteration, and may even jump in to help improve it.

### Context, Not Control
- **When to use**: When leading strong teams that need autonomy without heavy process.
- **Steps**:
  1. Share broad business and leadership context openly.
  2. Make priorities, tradeoffs, and concerns visible.
  3. Trust teams to decide how to solve problems.
  4. Avoid over-prescribing process unless scale or risk demands it.
  5. Use transparency to improve judgment, not rules to constrain behavior.
- **Example**: Stone shares notes from leadership meetings with the broader organization so people can operate with more context.

### Outcome-over-Polish Standard
- **When to use**: When deciding how much effort to spend refining a deliverable versus enabling the real objective.
- **Steps**:
  1. Clarify the actual goal of the work product.
  2. Ask whether more polish meaningfully improves the outcome.
  3. If not, redirect effort toward the conversation or decision it is meant to support.
  4. Set expectations early to avoid over-investing in cosmetics.
  5. Judge excellence by whether the intended outcome was achieved.
- **Example**: For a quarterly business review, Stone would rather the team spend time preparing the real conversation than polishing slides for another 20 hours.

### Additive Hiring
- **When to use**: When hiring for a strong team and you want each new person to raise the bar.
- **Steps**:
  1. Confirm the candidate has the baseline skills to succeed.
  2. Look for perspectives or judgment that make the team stronger.
  3. Prioritize people who identify new problems or better solutions.
  4. Ask questions that reveal how they would improve the organization.
  5. Hire for bar-raising impact, not just role fit.
- **Example**: Stone asks candidates what their priorities would be and what they would do differently if they had her job.

## Core Advice
- **When you’re unsure how you’re performing**: ask directly for candid feedback — because guessing is more stressful than hearing the truth early.
- **When you want to grow your career early**: focus on excellence, reliability, and being useful — because advancement often comes from being consistently dependable across boundaries.
- **When someone’s work misses the bar**: set the standard clearly, give specific feedback, and help them improve — because people learn faster when the gap is explicit and support is real.
- **When work culture equates dedication with long hours**: define dedication as quality, responsiveness, and follow-through — because excellence is not the same as burnout.
- **When leading cross-functional work**: act as a translator between technical and non-technical stakeholders — because shared understanding reduces misalignment.
- **When hiring for a strong team**: hire for additive perspective and bar-raising potential — because talent density compounds.
- **When tempted to over-polish deliverables**: optimize for the outcome the document or meeting is meant to produce — because polish without purpose wastes time.
- **When becoming more senior and less connected**: preserve recurring mechanisms like office hours and AMAs — because connection requires intentionality.
- **When you want to stay present with people**: protect one-on-one time as sacred — because human connection is one of the most meaningful parts of work.
- **When you feel anxious or unclear about your motivation**: build a regular reflection habit — because self-awareness helps you lead better.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Performance should be managed through formal review cycles and ratings. → **Their view**: Ongoing candid feedback is more effective — because people can improve immediately instead of hearing problems months later.
- **Conventional**: Strong culture comes from process and guardrails. → **Their view**: High talent density lets you remove much of the process — because exceptional people can be trusted with judgment and responsibility.
- **Conventional**: Hiring should match a predefined role checklist. → **Their view**: Hire people who raise the bar and change the team’s thinking — because the best hires expand what the team can do.
- **Conventional**: Dedication means long hours and sacrifice. → **Their view**: Dedication means standards, urgency, and reliability — because excellence is about outcomes, not performative busyness.
- **Conventional**: Data teams should be split by function for scale. → **Their view**: A centralized, functionally diverse data organization can be stronger — because it improves excellence, mobility, and objectivity.
- **Conventional**: Research and data science should stay separate. → **Their view**: Combining research, data science, and engineering is a superpower — because it creates a fuller picture of user behavior and needs.
