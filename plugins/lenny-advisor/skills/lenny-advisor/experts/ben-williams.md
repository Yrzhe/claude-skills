## Bio
Ben Williams is VP of Product at Snyk, where he has led growth and developer experience work at one of the best-known product-led security companies. His perspective matters because he has operated at the intersection of developer workflows, growth systems, and enterprise monetization in a category many people assume cannot be product-led.

## Signature Frameworks

### Narrow-and-deep community-first wedge
- **When to use**: When launching into a broad market and you need early product-market fit with a specific adopter segment.
- **Steps**:
  1. Choose one persona, one context, and one use case.
  2. Go where that community already gathers.
  3. Lead with a sharp problem hook that creates urgency.
  4. Build a low-friction experience for that exact workflow.
  5. Validate depth of value before expanding.
- **Example**: Snyk started with Node.js developers using open source packages, met them through conferences and meetups, and asked whether they had known vulnerabilities in their apps.

### Growth loops modeling
- **When to use**: When you have early traction and need a shared model for how growth works.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the micro and macro loops driving acquisition, engagement, and monetization.
  2. Document them qualitatively so teams share the same mental model.
  3. Add quantitative analysis to find the biggest constraints.
  4. Use those constraints to set quarterly priorities.
  5. Revisit the model as the market and product evolve.
- **Example**: Snyk modeled loops like GitHub fix pull requests generating new signups, then extended the same loop to Bitbucket and GitLab.

### Cross-functional growth team design
- **When to use**: When growth work is stuck between product, engineering, and marketing.
- **Steps**:
  1. Build teams around shared KPIs.
  2. Include engineering, product, design, growth marketing, and data/decision science.
  3. Organize around outcomes like acquisition, activation, and monetization.
  4. Let teams test across multiple channels, not just product changes.
  5. Create shared experimentation and learning-review processes.
- **Example**: At Snyk, embedding growth marketers in growth teams reduced friction with R&D and enabled lightweight SEO and website experiments.

### Vision and mission framing
- **When to use**: When aligning teams around long-term direction.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define vision as the future state for users 5–10 years out.
  2. Keep it market-bound and solution-agnostic.
  3. Define mission as the incremental path toward that vision.
  4. Encode your differentiated approach in the mission.
  5. Apply it at company, group, and team levels.
- **Example**: Snyk’s growth vision was “every developer securely unleashes their creativity,” while the mission focused on frictionless self-serve adoption and expansion.

### Activation design via habit moments
- **When to use**: When defining activation and connecting early behavior to retention.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define retention by repeated core value, not logins.
  2. Choose the relevant unit of behavior, such as user or team.
  3. Find the habit moment most correlated with long-term retention.
  4. Work backward to aha moments and setup steps.
  5. Use the model to improve onboarding and activation.
- **Example**: Snyk defined team activation as fixing a vulnerability within 30 days of team creation because that predicted future retention.

## Core Advice
- **When you face a huge market and are tempted to launch broadly**: start with a narrow, depth-first wedge — because specificity makes product-market fit easier to validate.
- **When you are unsure how users will come**: design growth loops into the product from the beginning — because product-led acquisition needs intentional mechanics, not hope.
- **When selling to developers**: meet them in their existing tools and workflows — because flow is sacred and friction kills adoption.
- **When growth work is fragmented across functions**: build cross-functional growth teams with shared KPIs — because aligned ownership speeds execution.
- **When hiring for growth**: prioritize people who like speed, iteration, and measurable impact — because growth work rewards a different operating style than traditional functional work.
- **When experimentation gets too fancy too soon**: start simple — because teams need basics before advanced testing methods.
- **When experiments are producing noise**: run regular impact-and-learnings reviews — because knowledge compounds only if it is shared.
- **When analytics feel untrustworthy**: invest early in intentional event tracking and schema validation — because growth decisions are only as good as the data.
- **When self-serve monetization lags behind user growth**: check governance, reporting, and ecosystem coverage — because adoption alone may not satisfy enterprise buyers.
- **When deciding free vs. paid**: keep growth-driving features free and charge for high-cost or governance-heavy capabilities — because free should maximize adoption while paid should map to business-critical value.
- **When defining activation**: anchor it in repeated value behavior, not signups — because retention comes from habit formation.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Security software must be sold top-down by security teams → **Their view**: developer-first product-led growth can be the most effective security motion — because developers increasingly shape security outcomes.
- **Conventional**: To win a large market, support many use cases early → **Their view**: go narrow and deep first — because users care more about excellence in their exact workflow than broad checkbox coverage.
- **Conventional**: Marketing should support growth from the outside → **Their view**: embed growth marketers directly in growth teams — because it expands the experiment surface and speeds execution.
- **Conventional**: Experimentation is about shipping winning tests → **Their view**: experimentation is about generating reusable learnings — because durable growth comes from compounding knowledge.
- **Conventional**: Activation is an individual-user metric → **Their view**: activation can be team-level — because some products create value through collective behavior.
