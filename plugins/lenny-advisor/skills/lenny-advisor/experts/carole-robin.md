# Carole Robin

**Former Stanford Graduate School of Business professor; co-founder of Leaders in Tech; author of _Connect_** | **Stanford Graduate School of Business / Leaders in Tech** | Expertise: leadership, interpersonal dynamics, feedback, relationship-building

## Bio
Carole Robin taught Stanford GSB’s legendary Interpersonal Dynamics course for more than 20 years, helping generations of leaders learn how to build trust, give feedback, and have the conversations most people avoid. Her perspective matters because she focuses on the human mechanics behind high-performing teams: vulnerability, inquiry, repair, and the ability to stay connected under stress.

She now brings that work to tech leaders through Leaders in Tech, translating deep relationship skills into practical leadership behavior. If you need someone who can help you make relationships more functional, honest, and resilient, her viewpoint is especially valuable.

## Signature Frameworks

### The Relationship Continuum
- **When to use**: When assessing the quality of a relationship and deciding how much to invest in deepening it.
- **Steps**:
  1. Recognize that relationships exist on a continuum from contact/no connection or dysfunction to functional/robust to exceptional.
  2. Build the core skills needed to move a relationship from weak or dysfunctional to functional and robust.
  3. Use those same skills to decide intentionally which relationships to deepen further into exceptional ones.
- **Example**: Not every relationship needs to become exceptional, but the same skills that move a relationship from “contact and no connection” to “functional and robust” also enable a few relationships to become exceptional.

### 15% Rule
- **When to use**: When you want to be more vulnerable, disclose more, or give feedback without overwhelming yourself or the other person.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify your current comfort zone.
  2. Avoid jumping straight into the danger zone.
  3. Step about 15% outside your comfort zone into the learning zone.
  4. Observe how the other person responds.
  5. Let that become the new comfort zone and repeat gradually.
- **Example**: Say something slightly more vulnerable than usual, notice the response, and then expand from there rather than oversharing all at once.

### Three Realities / Stay on Your Side of the Net
- **When to use**: When giving interpersonal feedback, resolving conflict, or trying to avoid defensiveness.
- **Steps**:
  1. Separate the interaction into three realities: your internal reality/intent, the observable behavior, and the other person’s impact/reality.
  2. Recognize that you only know your internal reality and the observable behavior.
  3. Do not assume the other person’s motives, intent, or internal state.
  4. Describe only the observable behavior.
  5. State your own feelings and impact.
  6. Use that as the basis for a problem-solving conversation.
- **Example**: Instead of saying, “You’re not listening,” say what happened, how it affected you, and what you need next.

### Constructive Feedback Formula
- **When to use**: When you need to address behavior that is affecting trust, collaboration, or connection.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with: “When you do...” and describe specific observable behavior.
  2. Continue with: “I feel...” and name an actual feeling, not an attribution.
  3. Add: “I’m telling you this because...” or “I’m hoping the outcome is...”
  4. Move into joint problem solving rather than trying to change the person.
- **Example**: “When I speak and I’m all worked up about something and the only thing I get back from you are either a grunt or an affectless repetition of what I just said, I don’t feel heard. When I don’t feel heard, I feel hurt and distanced.”

### Talk About a Pinch Before It Becomes a Crunch
- **When to use**: When a small irritation or recurring behavior is starting to bother you but has not yet become a major conflict.
- **Steps**:
  1. Notice the small irritation early.
  2. Check whether the irritation is increasing over time.
  3. Raise it while it is still small and manageable.
  4. Use specific behavior and impact language rather than accusation.
  5. Prevent resentment from compounding into a larger rupture.
- **Example**: If someone keeps doing something mildly irritating and you never say anything, they will keep doing it and you will become more activated, making the eventual conversation much harder.

### Artful Inquiry
- **When to use**: When you want to understand someone better, repair a misunderstanding, coach someone, or avoid jumping to conclusions.
- **Steps**:
  1. Suspend judgment temporarily.
  2. Approach the conversation as a genuine quest, not an attempt to confirm your hypothesis.
  3. Ask open questions beginning with what, when, where, or how.
  4. Avoid yes/no questions and avoid “why” questions that trigger defensiveness.
  5. Use the answers to learn what is actually happening before offering advice or conclusions.
- **Example**: Ask, “What did you hear me say?” after an unexpectedly defensive reaction to uncover the gap between intent and impact.

### Repair After a Miss
- **When to use**: When a conversation goes sideways, feedback lands badly, or someone reacts unexpectedly.
- **Steps**:
  1. Pause instead of defending your intent.
  2. Ask what the other person heard or experienced.
  3. Acknowledge that their reaction makes sense given what they heard.
  4. Restate your intent more clearly.
  5. Agree on a better way to handle similar situations in the future.
- **Example**: When a spouse snaps after “Can I help you with that?”, ask what they heard; if they heard criticism, repair by clarifying and changing the approach next time.

### Six Characteristics of Exceptional Relationships
- **When to use**: When evaluating whether a relationship is becoming truly strong and resilient.
- **Steps**:
  1. Become better known by the other person.
  2. Know the other person better.
  3. Build trust that disclosures will not be used against either person.
  4. Develop the ability to be honest with each other.
  5. Learn to resolve conflict productively.
  6. Commit to each other’s learning and growth.
- **Example**: Relationships become exceptional when people do not have to hide important parts of themselves and can deal with major issues even when it feels scary.

### AFOG
- **When to use**: When processing failure, mistakes, or painful setbacks.
- **Steps**:
  1. Label the setback as an “Another Fucking Opportunity for Growth.”
  2. Ask what you learned.
  3. Extract the lesson before moving on.
  4. Treat failure as recoverable and developmental rather than identity-defining.
- **Example**: After something goes wrong, the first question is always, “So what did you learn?”

## Core Advice
- **When you need to deepen trust with someone**: Do a small, appropriate act of disclosure about 15% beyond your comfort zone — because vulnerability tends to be reciprocal and gradual expansion is safer than oversharing.
- **When you need to lead credibly under uncertainty**: Acknowledge reality and share appropriate vulnerability — because false certainty makes leaders seem less human and less trustworthy.
- **When you feel anger in conflict**: Look underneath it for fear, hurt, or sadness — because anger is often secondary and distancing.
- **When you need to give difficult feedback**: Stay on your side of the net and describe observable behavior plus your feeling — because mind-reading and accusation trigger defensiveness.
- **When a small irritation keeps repeating**: Address the pinch before it becomes a crunch — because unspoken annoyances compound into resentment.
- **When someone reacts defensively**: Ask, “What did you hear me say?” — because people often respond to what they heard, not what you meant.
- **When a tense conversation starts drifting into blame**: Ask what/when/where/how questions instead of why — because “why” often sounds accusatory.
- **When someone asks for advice**: Start with inquiry and thought partnership — because immediate advice can miss the real issue and reduce ownership.
- **When you believe you “can’t” change**: Add “yet” and focus on behavior — because behavior is more changeable than identity.
- **When failure hits**: Treat it as an AFOG — because reframing failure reduces shame and accelerates learning.
- **When others may be making assumptions about you**: Disclose more, not less — because in the absence of data, people invent stories.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Strong leaders should avoid vulnerability and project certainty at all times. → **Their view**: Appropriately vulnerable leaders are often stronger and more influential — because vulnerability humanizes leaders and builds trust.
- **Conventional**: Feelings do not belong at work. → **Their view**: Feelings are essential to leadership, motivation, and connection — because they give meaning to facts and help people feel known.
- **Conventional**: Giving feedback damages relationships. → **Their view**: Well-given feedback strengthens relationships — because it signals care and prevents resentment.
- **Conventional**: Anger is the main emotion to address in conflict. → **Their view**: Anger is often secondary; fear, hurt, or sadness matter more — because naming them creates connection.
- **Conventional**: Good leaders should have answers and give advice quickly. → **Their view**: A leader’s job is to ensure the best answer is found, not to always provide it — because inquiry builds judgment and ownership.
- **Conventional**: Sharing less about yourself gives you more control over perception. → **Their view**: Disclosing more gives you more control over your self-definition — because people otherwise make up stories.
