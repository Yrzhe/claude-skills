# Jackson Shuttleworth

**Group Product Manager, Retention Team** | **Duolingo** | Expertise: retention, growth, product-management, experimentation, habit design

## Bio
Jackson Shuttleworth leads retention product work at Duolingo, with a particular focus on streaks as a core habit-building and growth mechanic. His perspective matters because he has helped scale one of the most heavily tested retention systems in consumer product, using experimentation to turn streaks into a major driver of daily active use.

He stands out for treating retention as a product discipline, not just a marketing or notification problem. His work shows how clarity, commitment, flexibility, and celebration can be tuned scientifically to build durable user habits without cheapening the product.

## Signature Frameworks

### Unit of Use Streak Design
- **When to use**: When deciding what action should count toward a streak or habit mechanic.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the smallest meaningful action that reflects real product value.
  2. Make the streak depend on that action, not on a more abstract metric.
  3. Test whether simplifying the requirement improves retention.
  4. Avoid making the unit so small that it rewards low-intent behavior.
- **Example**: Duolingo moved from an XP-based streak to “do one lesson a day.” That worked better because a lesson is the true unit of use, while “one exercise” was too small and did not move DAUs.

### 0-to-7 Day Loss Aversion Ramp
- **When to use**: When onboarding users into a streak or habit loop.
- **Steps**:
  1. Analyze retention by streak length.
  2. Find the point where users begin to care about losing progress.
  3. Focus experimentation on getting users to that threshold quickly.
  4. Add support and flexibility early, then preserve meaning later.
- **Example**: Duolingo found retention improves sharply from day 1 to day 7, then flattens. The team responded by helping new users reach a 7-day streak, including two streak freezes and clearer streak education.

### Minimal V1, Then Iterative Expansion
- **When to use**: When testing a new feature idea.
- **Steps**:
  1. Strip the idea to the smallest testable version.
  2. Launch quickly.
  3. Measure the core hypothesis.
  4. Add complexity only after the core wins.
- **Example**: Duolingo first tested a simple message that users were more likely to finish the course if they reached a 30-day streak. After it won, the team explored goal lengths, opt-outs, and selection UI.

### Intentional Commitment UX
- **When to use**: When you want users to feel ownership over a goal.
- **Steps**:
  1. Replace passive progression with explicit choice.
  2. Use real yes/no or choose-your-goal decisions.
  3. Test whether choosing increases downstream commitment.
  4. Keep enough friction to create intentionality.
- **Example**: Changing a CTA from “Continue” to “Commit to My Goal” was a major win. Adding an opt-out button also improved performance because users were making an intentional decision.

### Flexibility Without Cheapening
- **When to use**: When adding forgiveness to a habit system.
- **Steps**:
  1. Add limited flexibility like freezes or earn-back.
  2. Test how much it helps versus trains skipping.
  3. Give more flexibility early in the habit journey.
  4. Constrain flexibility later so long streaks still feel earned.
  5. Reward perfection visibly.
- **Example**: Duolingo found that two streak freezes beat one, but three did not beat two. New users got two freezes up front, and later “Perfect Streak” rewarded users who avoided using them.

### Clarity-First Streak Communication
- **When to use**: When users may not understand how the streak works.
- **Steps**:
  1. Assume the mechanic is less clear than you think.
  2. Use copy and visuals to explain it simply.
  3. Prioritize explicitness over cleverness.
  4. Make every element reinforce the cadence and rules.
- **Example**: A line like “Start a day to extend your streak, but miss a day and it resets” was a major win. Duolingo also redesigned the UI to make the calendar and streak number more obvious.

### Celebrate the Desired Behavior
- **When to use**: When you want to deepen emotional attachment to a habit.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the emotionally important moment.
  2. Use animation, haptics, and visual treatment.
  3. Sometimes slow users down so they feel the reward.
  4. Celebrate both continuity and perfection.
- **Example**: Haptics and celebratory animations improved outcomes because they made users pause and feel good about extending the streak. Gold “Perfect Streak” treatment rewarded users who never used freezes.

### Behavior-Based Notification Timing
- **When to use**: When sending reminders tied to recurring behavior.
- **Steps**:
  1. Use actual prior behavior as the main timing signal.
  2. Send reminders before the user’s likely next session.
  3. Add a last-chance reminder only when real loss is at stake.
  4. Frame the message as protection, not just reactivation.
- **Example**: Duolingo found that reminders sent 23.5 hours after the prior session outperformed user-set reminder times. A 10 PM “streak saver” notification works because it protects something users value.

## Core Advice
- **When retention is weak and you’re thinking about streaks**: first check whether the core product is already valuable enough to bring users back — because streaks amplify existing value; they don’t replace it.
- **When deciding what counts toward a streak**: define it around the true unit of use — because the streak should reinforce meaningful behavior, not low-intent clicks.
- **When early retention is poor**: focus heavily on days 0–7 — because that’s where loss aversion begins to form and where gains are steepest.
- **When launching a new habit feature**: ship the smallest version first — because you want to validate the core mechanic before adding complexity.
- **When onboarding feels passive**: make the user explicitly commit — because intentional choice increases ownership and downstream engagement.
- **When users drop off early**: add limited forgiveness like freezes — because habits are fragile at the start.
- **When adding flexibility**: stop before it becomes too generous — because too much forgiveness devalues the streak.
- **When users seem confused**: over-invest in clarity — because even simple mechanics are often misunderstood.
- **When you want a habit to feel rewarding**: celebrate the moment — because emotional reinforcement strengthens repetition.
- **When choosing notification timing**: use revealed behavior, not stated preference — because actual behavior is a better predictor of when users will return.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Retention is secondary to core product value. → **Their view**: Retention is foundational, because users can’t benefit if they don’t come back.
- **Conventional**: Less friction is always better. → **Their view**: Some friction is good when it creates commitment and intentionality.
- **Conventional**: If an experiment wins, ship it. → **Their view**: Don’t ship wins that clutter sacred surfaces or undermine the product experience.
- **Conventional**: More flexibility always improves streaks. → **Their view**: Flexibility has a ceiling; too much trains skipping.
- **Conventional**: Simple mechanics don’t need much explanation. → **Their view**: Clarity is essential even for streaks and freezes.
- **Conventional**: User-set preferences are the best personalization signal. → **Their view**: Revealed behavior is often better.
- **Conventional**: Late-night reminders are spammy. → **Their view**: They can be welcomed if they protect something users care about.
