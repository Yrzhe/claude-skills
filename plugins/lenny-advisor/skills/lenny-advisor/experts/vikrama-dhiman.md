# Vikrama Dhiman

**Product leader** | **Gojek** | Expertise: product-management, leadership, strategy, career-development

## Bio
Vikrama Dhiman is a product leader at Gojek who is especially known for his practical thinking about PM career growth, PM craft, and cross-functional collaboration. His perspective matters because he focuses less on abstract product theory and more on what actually helps PMs grow: outputs, artifacts, operating model, and sustained skill-building.

He is particularly useful for people transitioning into product management, PMs who feel stuck, and leaders trying to build stronger product and design talent.

## Signature Frameworks

### The Three W’s for PM Career Growth
- **When to use**: Use to assess and accelerate PM growth at any stage, especially when someone feels stuck or is unsure what drives promotion.
- **Steps**:
  1. **What you produce**: the outputs and outcomes you create.
  2. **What you bring to the table**: the quality of your artifacts and the depth/breadth of your craft.
  3. **What’s your operating model**: how you communicate, collaborate, and make decisions with others.
- **Example**: A PM who ships a useful first draft for leadership review, writes a strong PRD, and helps unblock decisions across teams is demonstrating all three W’s.

### Output-to-Outcome Progression
- **When to use**: Use when developing PMs from early career to senior levels, or when coaching someone who is over-indexing on strategy before mastering execution.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with outputs: ship products, run experiments, draft briefs, and complete concrete tasks.
  2. Move to outcomes: own product areas, goals, or collaborative results with stakeholders.
  3. Keep both alive as you grow: continue producing high-quality outputs even when operating at outcome or leadership level.
- **Example**: A PM sourcing homepage content ranked the sourcing options by ease and difficulty, which was a small output that fed into the broader content strategy.

### Impact-on-Impact Craft Framework
- **When to use**: Use when a product area is successful but the PM is not getting career credit or growth from it.
- **Steps**:
  1. Demonstrate quality through artifacts such as PRDs, product notes, strategy docs, and design briefs.
  2. Show depth in execution details like pre-sprint planning, Jira story quality, and experiment design.
  3. Build across four pillars: data and metrics, design and research, technology, and strategy.
- **Example**: A PM can be on a high-impact product area but still stall if their PRDs are weak, their Jira stories are empty, or they are not contributing to strategy documents.

### The Three Tenets of Effective Stakeholder Pushback
- **When to use**: Use when a PM needs to disagree, challenge a proposal, or prevent the team from doing low-value work.
- **Steps**:
  1. Raise difficult issues without being difficult to work with.
  2. Bring up important topics without drawing importance to yourself.
  3. Get decisions made without trying to make all the decisions yourself.
- **Example**: A PM should push back on a bad idea by moving the discussion from emotional to logical ground and helping the team make a better decision.

### Skill-Axis Development for PM Transitions
- **When to use**: Use when transitioning into product management from another function or when leveling up a PM with uneven skills.
- **Steps**:
  1. Assess the core axes: data, design and research, technology, strategy, communication, collaboration, organizational skills, and community.
  2. Pick one complementary skill to your background first.
  3. After building two of the three core craft areas, move into strategy.
  4. Use strong product leadership and feedback loops to shape the transition.
- **Example**: A PM from a growth/data background was assigned a design-heavy driver redesign, while still leveraging data in the PRD and working closely with designers.

### Role-over-Title Mindset
- **When to use**: Use when clarifying what PMs are actually responsible for in ambiguous organizations or when helping teams understand the PM function.
- **Steps**:
  1. Think of product management as a role that ties together strategy, design, data, and technology.
  2. Recognize that the PM is the co-collaborator across disciplines, not the lone owner of everything.
  3. Focus on increasing the team’s overall contribution, energy, and output.
- **Example**: A PM may not be the only person who can do product work, but they are the one who has the time and mandate to connect all the pieces.

## Core Advice
- **When you are early in your PM career and trying to prove yourself**: Focus on shipping concrete outputs and being useful to the team rather than obsessing over grand strategy — because early trust comes from tangible execution.
- **When you are already senior but joining a new team or company**: Reset to an output mindset and earn trust again through hands-on contribution — because seniority does not transfer automatically across contexts.
- **When you want to stand out as a PM**: Produce high-quality artifacts like PRDs, product notes, strategy docs, design briefs, and structured Jira stories — because artifacts reveal craft and thinking.
- **When you are working on a successful product area but not getting recognized**: Make your contribution visible through the artifacts and decisions you shape, not just the product’s success — because impact alone does not always translate into career growth.
- **When you need to push back on a bad idea**: Move the conversation from emotional to logical, and focus on unblocking the team rather than winning the argument — because good pushback improves decisions without creating friction.
- **When you are tempted to focus on things outside your control**: Redirect attention to your craft, outputs, artifacts, and relationships — because those are the levers you can actually move.
- **When your growth starts to slow in mid-senior roles**: Increase your rate of change by benchmarking yourself against the best in the industry or adjacent functions — because growth stalls when you stop stretching.
- **When you feel overwhelmed by feedback on many dimensions**: Pick the highest-leverage skill gap first and improve one area at a time — because focused improvement compounds faster than scattered effort.
- **When you are transitioning into PM from another function**: Choose one complementary skill area to build first, then add a second, and only then move into strategy — because the fastest path is to leverage strengths while filling the biggest gaps.
- **When you are defining your PM identity**: Adopt enabling stories like “I am a learner” rather than rigid labels — because self-stories can become excuses for bad behavior.
- **When you are trying to grow in a new culture or team**: Practice mindful agency instead of pure high-agency behavior — because forcefulness is not always effective across different contexts.
- **When you are building PM capability over time**: Invest sustained effort and hours into your craft — because improvement is deliberate, not accidental.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Career growth in product is mainly about working on a cool, high-impact product area. → **Their view**: A great product area does not guarantee career growth; PM craft, artifacts, and operating model matter just as much or more — because strong execution and collaboration can outperform visibility.
- **Conventional**: Early-career PM advice should focus on driving impact and thinking strategically. → **Their view**: Early in your career, focus first on outputs: shipping, drafting, analyzing, and being useful — because execution builds trust and capability.
- **Conventional**: Intent is what matters most; if your intentions are good, the rest will work out. → **Their view**: Intent is not enough; your actions, behavior, and communication must reflect who you are — because people judge operating behavior, not just motives.
- **Conventional**: Product managers should primarily be strategic and delegate execution artifacts to others. → **Their view**: PMs should still produce high-quality product artifacts themselves, even at mid-senior levels — because artifacts are how craft and credibility are demonstrated.
- **Conventional**: Being a “high-agency” PM is always a positive trait. → **Their view**: High agency can become a license for rudeness or brashness; it needs mindfulness — because effectiveness without collaboration can damage the team.
- **Conventional**: You need to be a mini-CEO as a PM. → **Their view**: A PM is one part of the cogwheel, not the whole wheel — because the role is to connect disciplines, not dominate them.
