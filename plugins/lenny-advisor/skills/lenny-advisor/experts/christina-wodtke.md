# Christina Wodtke

**Lecturer at Stanford; author, speaker, and consultant on product development and OKRs** | **Stanford University** | Expertise: OKRs, product-management, strategy, leadership

## Bio
Christina Wodtke is a longtime product leader and Stanford lecturer who teaches product management and game design, with experience at LinkedIn, MySpace, Zynga, and Yahoo. She is especially known for helping teams implement OKRs in a way that actually changes behavior, not just planning docs, and for her book *Radical Focus*.

Her perspective matters because she combines strategy, product thinking, and organizational design. She is particularly useful when a team needs to turn ambition into execution without drowning in process.

## Signature Frameworks

### Mission → Strategy → Quarterly OKRs → Weekly Actions
- **When to use**: When translating a company’s long-term purpose into concrete quarterly priorities and weekly execution.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define a mission or vision that can plausibly guide the next ~5 years.
  2. Create a strategy as a strongly held hypothesis about how to win.
  3. Turn the strategy into a quarterly objective that names what matters most now.
  4. Define key results by asking, “How do we know we succeeded?”
  5. Each week, choose actions that move the team toward the outcome.
- **Example**: In a game company, Q1 is about discovering what the game is and what users find interesting, Q2 is about prototypes and validation, Q3 is about building, and Q4 is about marketing and launch.

### The “How do we know?” Key Result Design Method
- **When to use**: When an objective feels inspiring but vague, and you need measurable signals of success.
- **Steps**:
  1. Write an objective that describes the meaningful quarterly outcome.
  2. Ask repeatedly, “How do we know?”
  3. List possible signals: user testing, conversion, feasibility, revenue, reach, quality.
  4. Choose about three key results that triangulate success.
  5. Use the quarter to learn whether your measures were right.
- **Example**: For an interior design magazine, success might be measured through member conversion, weekly engagement like liking products, and qualitative panel feedback every two weeks.

### Three-Key-Result Triangulation
- **When to use**: When selecting a balanced set of key results for one objective.
- **Steps**:
  1. Include one hard quantitative metric.
  2. Include one squishier quality or delight measure.
  3. Include one metric tied to money or business impact when relevant.
  4. Keep the set small to preserve focus.
- **Example**: For a product launch, measure revenue, reach, and delight, with delight captured through Metacritic, survey data, or NPS.

### Healthy OKR Cadence
- **When to use**: When building the operating rhythm around OKRs so they stay alive.
- **Steps**:
  1. On Mondays, commit to what you’ll do this week.
  2. Use a short weekly update with confidence level, last week’s work, and next week’s plan.
  3. On Fridays, celebrate wins across the team.
  4. At quarter end, grade lightly and spend more time on retrospective learning.
  5. Set the next quarter’s OKRs quickly to avoid over-planning.
- **Example**: She notes that some CEOs saw meaningful improvement just by starting Friday celebrations before fully rolling out OKRs.

### Pilot OKRs with the Best Team
- **When to use**: When introducing OKRs for the first time or repairing a broken rollout.
- **Steps**:
  1. Choose the highest-performing multidisciplinary team.
  2. Give them guidance and let them experiment for a quarter.
  3. Have them identify where OKRs fit or clash with culture and rituals.
  4. Use their learnings as a template for two more teams.
  5. Expand gradually.
- **Example**: Tell the best team, “We’re thinking about OKRs. Can you guys see if it works?” ואז check back after three months.

### 10-Minute Key Result Brainstorm
- **When to use**: When teams default to obvious or weak measures.
- **Steps**:
  1. Spend a full 10 minutes brainstorming every possible way to measure the outcome.
  2. Expect obvious ideas first.
  3. Push through the awkward lull.
  4. Capture the weird ideas that emerge later.
  5. Use those less obvious ideas to find better measures.
- **Example**: The best measurement ideas often appear only after the obvious ones are exhausted.

### Fast Peer Review Instead of Hierarchical Approval
- **When to use**: When OKR approval cycles are slowing planning and creating bureaucracy.
- **Steps**:
  1. Draft the team’s OKRs.
  2. Send them to three adjacent teams or stakeholders.
  3. Ask for feedback within 24 hours.
  4. Incorporate feedback on feasibility or alignment.
  5. Skip heavy boss-driven approval chains.
- **Example**: A client process where teams get feedback from three relevant teams instead of formal managerial approval, speeding planning dramatically.

### Storytelling Structure for Product Leaders
- **When to use**: When presenting ideas, persuading stakeholders, or teaching concepts.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with a hook: mystery, secret, surprise, or success.
  2. Use the middle for the core message or facts.
  3. End with success and celebration.
  4. Ask trusted listeners for feedback.
- **Example**: Many TED Talks work because they are stories with facts embedded inside.

## Core Advice
- **When you face a focus problem or too many competing priorities**: Do fewer OKRs and make one big rock unmistakably important for the quarter — because spreading effort too thin creates shallow progress everywhere.
- **When you face pressure to use OKRs to fix a dysfunctional company**: Do foundational work first on strategy, empowered teams, and psychological safety — because OKRs amplify health; they don’t cure dysfunction.
- **When you face vague quarterly goals**: Keep asking, “How do we know we succeeded?” — because this turns aspiration into measurable outcomes.
- **When you face fluffy or uninspiring objectives**: Write objectives that are meaningful and motivating, not just task statements — because objectives should energize people and clarify why the work matters.
- **When you face key results that are really task lists**: Rewrite them as outcomes or evidence of progress — because tasks don’t show whether the business or user outcome improved.
- **When you face boring OKR review meetings**: Keep the meeting at the right altitude and discuss whether the strategy is working — because leaders should focus on decisions, not IC-level tracking.
- **When you face uncertainty about measuring quality, delight, or retention**: Combine hard metrics with qualitative research and retention signals — because easy numbers alone can hide bad user experience.
- **When you face a first-time OKR rollout**: Start with a small pilot on your best multidisciplinary team — because strong teams reveal fit issues and create a reusable template.
- **When you face long planning cycles for quarterly OKRs**: Optimize for speed and keep planning short — because over-planning steals time from shipping and learning.
- **When you face weak weekly execution against quarterly goals**: Establish Monday commitments and Friday celebrations — because accountability and morale both matter.
- **When you face status updates that feel useless**: Use a short weekly format: confidence level, what you did, and what you’ll do next — because it surfaces blockers and is easy to scan.
- **When you face debates about whether a stretch goal is too ambitious**: Set goals that feel uncomfortable but not doomed — because ambition should motivate, not demoralize.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: OKRs can fix execution and alignment problems on their own. → **Their view**: OKRs are a vitamin, not a medicine — because they work best in already healthy organizations.
- **Conventional**: Good OKRs require heavy structure and precision. → **Their view**: Simpler is better — because complexity creates bureaucracy and distracts from outcomes.
- **Conventional**: Status emails and rituals are overhead. → **Their view**: Weekly updates and Friday celebrations are valuable — because they reinforce focus, learning, and morale.
- **Conventional**: Precise grading is essential. → **Their view**: Retrospective learning matters more than exact scores — because fake precision wastes time.
- **Conventional**: PMs should mainly focus on users and product sense. → **Their view**: PMs fundamentally serve the business — because someone must steward economics and market fit.
- **Conventional**: You should get into product as early as possible. → **Their view**: You probably shouldn’t start in product — because engineering, design, or startup experience builds better judgment.
- **Conventional**: Scale signals a superior product culture. → **Their view**: Scale is just a tactic — because some of the healthiest cultures are small companies in narrow niches.
