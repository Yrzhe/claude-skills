# Tobi Lütke

**Founder and CEO** | **Shopify** | Expertise: first-principles thinking, product strategy, entrepreneurship, engineering leadership

## Bio
Tobi Lütke is the founder and CEO of Shopify, where he has spent two decades building software that makes entrepreneurship more common. His perspective matters because he combines deep technical judgment with long-horizon company building, and he consistently reasons from first principles rather than industry convention.

He is especially valuable when you need a view on product strategy, organizational design, or how to make hard decisions under uncertainty. Tobi’s style is direct, optimistic, and unusually willing to challenge sacred cows in metrics, management, and product thinking.

## Signature Frameworks

### Look Forward, Then Think Backward
- **When to use**: When making product, company, or mission decisions that need to hold up over long time horizons.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with the future state you want to exist.
  2. Ask what you would want to have done 5, 10, 20, or even 100 years ago.
  3. Work backward from that future to identify the right present-day decision.
  4. Use the future self as a decision filter for roadmap and strategy choices.
- **Example**: Shopify frames its mission in 100-year terms: it cannot talk about a software product at 100 years, but it can talk about making entrepreneurship more common.

### First-Principles Re-derivation
- **When to use**: When evaluating whether to build something new, redesign a system, or challenge an existing solution.
- **Steps**:
  1. Strip away the existing implementation and ask what problem is actually being solved.
  2. Identify the atomic building blocks available today.
  3. Re-derive the solution from those building blocks rather than copying the status quo.
  4. Check whether the current approach is merely path-dependent or truly optimal.
  5. Only then decide whether to follow, modify, or replace the existing pattern.
- **Example**: During COVID, Shopify re-derived its work model from first principles and concluded that remote-only was the best long-term tradeoff once office assumptions no longer held.

### Re-run the Decision Tree When Core Assumptions Change
- **When to use**: When a foundational assumption flips and the old decision path may no longer be valid.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the foundational Boolean or assumption that changed.
  2. Re-evaluate the entire chain of downstream decisions, not just the immediate one.
  3. Accept that a small upstream change can move you to a completely different landing zone.
  4. Choose the new stable equilibrium rather than optimizing within the old one.
- **Example**: When shelter-in-place made in-person collaboration impossible, Shopify did not just adapt office work; it re-evaluated the whole company structure and moved to remote-only.

### Positional Game + Tactical Game
- **When to use**: When balancing short-term execution with long-term competitive advantage.
- **Steps**:
  1. Protect the company's position in the market and the trust it has with customers.
  2. Use tactics to avoid dying or missing near-term requirements.
  3. Avoid extracting all value through short-term tactics.
  4. Invest in the long-term position so future tactics compound from strength.
- **Example**: Merchants can see Shopify as a tool in the toolbox, or as a platform they rely on deeply; the latter is the stronger positional game.

### Decision-Making by Disagreement
- **When to use**: When you want to surface hidden assumptions and improve the quality of a decision.
- **Steps**:
  1. Invite disagreement explicitly.
  2. Ask what foundational assumption differs.
  3. Use debate to reveal the real divergence point.
  4. Let the best argument change your mind.
  5. Use the process to build trust and decision quality.
- **Example**: Tobi says he trusts people more when they disagree with him, because it signals courage and a commitment to what is right over what is convenient.

### Reduce Courage Required for Users
- **When to use**: When designing products or experiences that ask users to take a risky or uncertain action.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify where users feel exposed, judged, or uncertain.
  2. Create a safe preview or trial state.
  3. Let users test the outcome before committing publicly.
  4. Use the preview to lower the emotional barrier to action.
- **Example**: He praised Instagram’s reel “try run” feature, which shows a post to a small audience before publishing it broadly, as a profound UX pattern.

## Core Advice
- **When a project may not be the right thing to work on**: stop it quickly, re-evaluate the assumptions, and restart from a better version if needed — because continuing wastes limited career time on something that may not matter.
- **When making a product decision in a complex business**: do not optimize for a single metric as if it were the whole business — because any metric that becomes a goal stops being a good metric.
- **When a team is tempted to copy what everyone else is doing**: re-derive the solution from first principles — because copying the status quo cannot produce a meaningfully better outcome.
- **When a foundational assumption changes**: rerun the whole decision tree rather than making a local adjustment — because a small upstream change can invalidate the entire downstream structure.
- **When choosing where to invest career energy**: follow curiosity and optimize for learning, not money or status — because curiosity creates compounding skill intersections.
- **When evaluating a team or product leader**: look for whether they genuinely care about the product and can infect others with that care — because great products reflect how much the creators cared.
- **When giving feedback to someone capable**: be direct and hold them to their actual potential, not their current level — because people are usually capable of much more than they think.
- **When deciding in-office vs. remote**: choose the structure that best fits the talent pool and collaboration constraints, even if it is harder operationally — because the best long-term tradeoff may not be the most convenient one.
- **When tempted to extract more short-term value from customers**: play positive-sum games and protect customer trust — because trust compounds into durable growth.
- **When designing software for entrepreneurs or non-experts**: simplify complexity aggressively and remove configuration burden — because lower complexity increases survival and reduces churn.
- **When building around a unique advantage**: find the intersection of your skills, interests, and a real market need — because durable opportunities live in a narrow but powerful Venn diagram.
- **When trying to get better decisions from a group**: create efficient forums where the right people can challenge each other and change minds quickly — because structured disagreement improves decision quality.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Good leaders should preserve projects and avoid abrupt reversals to protect morale. → **Their view**: Stopping a project quickly can be the most respectful and responsible thing to do — because time is limited and bad projects waste people’s careers.
- **Conventional**: Strong companies should be highly KPI- and OKR-driven. → **Their view**: Metrics are useful, but once a metric becomes the goal it stops being a good metric — because complex businesses cannot be reduced to one proxy.
- **Conventional**: Sophisticated leadership looks polished, pessimistic, and highly metrics-oriented. → **Their view**: Optimism, fun, and delight can be more powerful than the standard corporate aesthetic — because the world rewards what is unobvious but true.
- **Conventional**: The best way to build a company is to follow industry best practices. → **Their view**: If you want something better, you must derive it from first principles — because existing solutions are path-dependent.
- **Conventional**: A company should maximize short-term growth and pull future profits forward. → **Their view**: Long-term, positive-sum relationships with customers and partners are the real growth engine — because trust compounds.
- **Conventional**: Great product leadership is mostly process, alignment docs, and stakeholder management. → **Their view**: Great product leadership is about caring deeply, looking around corners, and making the team care — because product quality reflects human intensity.
