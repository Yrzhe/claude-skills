# Tanguy Crusson

**Head of Jira Product Discovery** | **Atlassian** | Expertise: product-management, zero-to-one, internal-incubation, b2b

## Bio
Tanguy Crusson has spent roughly a decade inside Atlassian’s product organization, repeatedly helping bootstrap new bets from scratch. His perspective matters because he has seen both sides of innovation inside a large company: how to create startup-like conditions for a new product, and how easily mature-company processes can smother it.

He is especially strong on internal incubation, customer discovery, and the practical mechanics of turning an idea into a real business without overbuilding too early. His lessons are grounded in hard-won experience from products like HipChat/Stride, Statuspage integration work, and Jira Product Discovery.

## Signature Frameworks

### Point A incubation model
- **When to use**: When a large company wants to create new products without applying the same processes, metrics, and resource expectations used for mature businesses.
- **Steps**:
  1. **Wonder**: prove there is a real problem area and a credible reason for the company to enter it now.
  2. **Explore**: validate solution direction with customers before building heavily, using prototypes and qualitative feedback.
  3. **Make**: build in stages, typically alpha then beta, while continuing to learn and iterate.
  4. **Impact**: graduate the product into a real business and monitor business impact after launch.
  5. Use formal stage-gates with leadership review to decide whether to continue, pivot, or kill the bet.
- **Example**: Jira Product Discovery moved from Wonder to Explore with research and Figma prototypes, then to Make with alpha/beta releases, and finally to Impact after launch.

### Safety Funnel
- **When to use**: When launching an early product inside an existing platform and you need to protect current customers from a bad early experience.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with a very small number of lighthouse users.
  2. Limit exposure so only a controlled group experiences the product.
  3. Iterate until the core experience is strong enough for broader use.
  4. Expand from 10 to 100 to 1,000 users as the product becomes more self-serve and reliable.
  5. Only then open the product to a wider audience.
- **Example**: Jira Product Discovery was first tested with 10 lighthouse users, then expanded to 100 and 1,000 as the team improved the experience.

### Lighthouse Users Program
- **When to use**: When you need a small, high-signal customer cohort to guide product development and create direct empathy across the team.
- **Steps**:
  1. Recruit a small set of representative customers and make them official lighthouse users.
  2. Work with them continuously over months, not as one-off research participants.
  3. Put customer conversations in front of the whole team, including engineers and designers.
  4. Use their problems and feedback as the primary input for prioritization.
  5. Scale the cohort gradually as the product proves itself.
- **Example**: The Jira Product Discovery team worked closely with 10 named customers on Zoom and Slack, and engineers used those conversations to influence planning decisions.

### Incubate, iterate, integrate
- **When to use**: When building a new experience inside an existing mature product and you need to avoid disrupting the core product too early.
- **Steps**:
  1. Incubate the idea in a separate or detached experience.
  2. Iterate on the UX and workflows with a small audience.
  3. Integrate the winning experience back into the main product once it is validated.
- **Example**: Jira Product Discovery was built as a detached experience on the Jira platform, then later moved into integration with the broader Jira ecosystem.

### Keep the flame alive
- **When to use**: When a new bet is vulnerable to being killed or deprioritized before it has enough evidence to prove itself.
- **Steps**:
  1. Send frequent, bite-sized internal updates.
  2. Show momentum with demos, metrics, and customer snippets.
  3. Make progress easy to consume so stakeholders can see the train moving.
  4. Use communication to buy time and protect the team from premature intervention.
- **Example**: Tanguy used weekly Atlas updates, demos, and short customer video snippets to keep internal support alive for Jira Product Discovery.

## Core Advice
- **When launching a new product inside a large company**: assume failure is the most likely outcome and explicitly frame the bet that way internally — because this creates scarcity, protects the team from over-investment, and prevents the broader organization from dragging the experiment into mature-company processes too early.
- **When you think a new product can reuse the same playbook that made your current business successful**: validate the underlying assumptions early instead of assuming the old success factors will transfer — because what worked before may not work in a new market or with a new audience.
- **When you are tempted to fast-follow a competitor**: anchor on your own users and research rather than reacting feature-by-feature to competitors — because competitors’ shipped features are the visible output of research and strategy from the past.
- **When you are building for a new customer segment that seems adjacent to your current one**: treat it as a distinct audience and test whether your current product really solves their needs — because adjacent does not mean identical, and HipChat’s tech-user success did not translate to business users.
- **When you are starting a bet that needs speed and autonomy**: create a super-siloed team with enough separation to move fast and avoid being swallowed by the core org — because early-stage bets need room to hack, test, and iterate.
- **When you are unsure whether to invest heavily in platform work or rewrite infrastructure**: win the problem space first; platformize later if the product proves itself — because solving product, rewrite, and platformization all at once slows the bet down.
- **When you are trying to get internal buy-in for a new product**: be very clear about what you are testing, why now matters, and what evidence would justify more investment — because leadership needs a crisp stage model, not vague optimism.
- **When your product is still early and fragile**: use a safety funnel and limit exposure to a small number of customers before scaling — because a bad early experience can be hard to recover from.
- **When you need to prove demand before building**: test interest with lightweight signals like newsletters, landing pages, and waitlists before writing code — because Jira Product Discovery got over 3,000 waitlist signups in two weeks before any code existed.
- **When your team is stuck waiting for approval or a decision**: keep moving with the smallest viable team and avoid dragging in the rest of the company too soon — because extra help often comes with process and slows the experiment down.
- **When you need the team to care deeply about customer problems**: put real customers in front of the whole team, not just the PM, and keep the same customers involved over time — because direct exposure creates empathy and better prioritization.
- **When you are trying to protect an ugly early product from being killed**: communicate weekly with a mix of data, demos, and customer stories so stakeholders can see momentum — because frequent, digestible updates reduce the urge to intervene prematurely.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Big companies should use their resources to move faster and scale new bets aggressively. → **Their view**: For early-stage innovation, you often need to create scarcity and keep the team small so the company does not over-invest too early — because extra resources usually bring process, coordination overhead, and slower learning.
- **Conventional**: A successful product should be measured with the same metrics as mature products, like MAU. → **Their view**: Early internal startups should not be judged by mature-product metrics; they need stage-specific success criteria — because low MAU can be correct when you are still validating with a lighthouse cohort.
- **Conventional**: You should closely track competitors and respond quickly to their moves. → **Their view**: Competitor watching should be occasional; the real source of strategy should be your own customer research — because competitors’ visible features are lagging indicators.
- **Conventional**: Acquisitions accelerate innovation because the buyer can plug the startup into a larger machine. → **Their view**: Acquisitions often slow things down because the hard part is people, culture, and process integration, not technology.
- **Conventional**: If a new product is not gaining traction after a few months, it should probably be killed. → **Their view**: Early products often need months of protected iteration, and the right move is to keep the flame alive with evidence and momentum — because an ugly early product can still be on the path to product-market fit.
- **Conventional**: To innovate inside a mature product, you should build directly in the core product from day one. → **Their view**: Sometimes you need to detach the experience from the core product first, then integrate later once the new UX is proven — because this protects existing customers and frees the team from legacy constraints.
