# Cameron Adams

**Co-founder and Chief Product Officer** | **Canva** | Expertise: product-management, design, leadership, go-to-market

## Bio
Cameron Adams is the co-founder and CPO of Canva, where he helped build one of the world’s most successful product-led software companies. His perspective matters because he combines design craft, product strategy, and company-building at massive scale, with a strong focus on joyful user experience, coaching-based leadership, and global growth.

## Signature Frameworks

### Giving Away Your Legos
- **When to use**: When a startup is scaling and people need to shift from doing the work themselves to building systems and leverage.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the work you currently own that has become central to your identity.
  2. Notice when doing it yourself is limiting your impact.
  3. Hand off execution or build systems to distribute the work.
  4. Reframe satisfaction around enabling others, not just personal output.
  5. Use the freed capacity to operate at a higher level.
- **Example**: An early Canva email copywriter eventually had to stop writing every email personally and instead build translation workflows and a broader team.

### Coaches Not Managers
- **When to use**: When you want to scale people development in a fast-growing company.
- **Steps**:
  1. Assign each employee a coach from the same craft area.
  2. Use the coach to assess skills and growth trajectory.
  3. Hold regular one-on-ones and development conversations.
  4. Pair coaching with 360 feedback from collaborators.
  5. Train coaches so development quality scales consistently.
- **Example**: At Canva, product managers are coached by product leaders rather than managed in a classic hierarchy.

### Joyful MVP Bar
- **When to use**: When deciding whether an early product is ready to launch, especially for consumer or prosumer tools.
- **Steps**:
  1. Understand the problem deeply through research.
  2. Don’t ship just because something works.
  3. Check whether users feel excitement, not just utility.
  4. Accept rough edges only if delight is strong.
  5. Launch when a target segment reliably reacts with enthusiasm.
- **Example**: Canva spent about a year before launch because the team wanted a product that would generate word of mouth, not just function.

### Persona Discovery Through Emotional User Testing
- **When to use**: When you have a broad vision but need the best initial ICP or wedge.
- **Steps**:
  1. Test with multiple user groups.
  2. Measure emotional intensity, not just task completion.
  3. Identify the segment with the strongest excitement.
  4. Tailor onboarding and messaging to that segment.
  5. Focus go-to-market on that wedge.
- **Example**: Canva found social media managers were the best early audience because they reacted with unusually strong excitement and had urgent needs.

### Blank-Page Onboarding Reduction
- **When to use**: When first use is intimidating or confusing.
- **Steps**:
  1. Find the first moment of fear or friction.
  2. Replace blank starts with a guided first action.
  3. Make the first action easy and surprising.
  4. Chain a few low-effort steps into a visible win.
  5. Use that win to build confidence and activation.
- **Example**: Canva replaced a blank canvas with a guided flow that had users search for a monkey, drag it onto the page, and complete a design.

### End-to-End SEO Product Loop
- **When to use**: When SEO can drive intent-based growth for a product with many use cases.
- **Steps**:
  1. Map the jobs your product solves.
  2. Match those jobs to search queries.
  3. Build landing pages that fit the intent.
  4. Route users to a relevant template or starting point.
  5. Deliver a fast, delightful product outcome.
- **Example**: For “Halloween poster,” Canva aimed to rank in Google, land users on a matching page, and guide them into a template.

### Three-Pillar AI Integration Strategy
- **When to use**: When adding AI without becoming a generic wrapper.
- **Steps**:
  1. Build proprietary AI where you have unique advantage.
  2. Partner externally for commodity capabilities.
  3. Let third parties extend the product through an ecosystem.
  4. Keep the user goal central.
  5. Judge AI by whether it improves speed and quality.
- **Example**: Canva builds some AI itself, partners with OpenAI and RunwayML, and supports AI developers through its app ecosystem.

## Core Advice
- **When you face pressure to launch an MVP quickly**: Don’t launch until the product creates real delight, not just utility — because word of mouth depends on users loving the experience.
- **When you’re unsure who your first customer should be**: Test multiple segments and choose the one with the strongest emotional reaction — because excitement reveals the best wedge.
- **When users are overwhelmed on first use**: Design onboarding as a sequence of tiny wins — because early success reduces fear and increases activation.
- **When people hold onto responsibilities too long**: Encourage them to “give away their Legos” — because scaling requires leverage, not personal ownership of everything.
- **When you need to develop people at scale**: Use coaches instead of relying only on managers — because craft-specific guidance scales better.
- **When copying product management models from other companies**: Build your own model from first principles — because culture and product type should shape the operating system.
- **When SEO is a growth channel**: Treat it as an end-to-end product loop — because traffic alone doesn’t create growth without a great post-click experience.
- **When expanding internationally**: Localize early and deeply — because global markets can reshape both growth and product direction.
- **When fundraising pressure is high**: Build toward profitability — because independence gives you leverage.
- **When adding AI because it’s trendy**: Integrate AI only where it improves user outcomes — because generic wrappers are not durable products.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Launch as fast as possible, even if the MVP is rough. → **Their view**: Wait until the product is joyful enough to create excitement. — because experience quality is part of the growth engine.
- **Conventional**: Don’t build for yourself. → **Their view**: Building for yourself can be a strength if you truly share the user’s problem. — because overlap improves judgment and speed.
- **Conventional**: Great companies need traditional managers. → **Their view**: Coaching can be better than management for scaling craft excellence. — because development is more effective when it’s specialty-based.
- **Conventional**: Copy best-practice PM models from top companies. → **Their view**: Design product management to fit your own company. — because operating models are context-specific.
- **Conventional**: AI strategy means shipping chatbot-style features fast. → **Their view**: Use AI selectively and embed it into workflows. — because durable value comes from outcomes, not novelty.
- **Conventional**: Freemium is mainly a pricing hack. → **Their view**: Freemium can be mission-aligned. — because access and business model can reinforce each other.
