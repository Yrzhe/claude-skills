# Casey Winters

**Chief Product Officer** | **Eventbrite** | Expertise: growth, product strategy, leadership, design

## Bio
Casey Winters is a longtime product and growth leader who has scaled consumer businesses at companies like Grubhub and Pinterest, and advised startups including Airbnb, Canva, Tinder, and Thumbtack. At Eventbrite, he led product management, design, research, and growth marketing, giving him a rare end-to-end view of how product, distribution, and execution fit together.

His perspective matters because he thinks about growth as a product problem, not just a marketing one. He is especially useful when teams need to balance simplicity with power, make hard trade-offs visible to executives, or scale without losing quality.

## Signature Frameworks

### Executive communication as storytelling
- **When to use**: When presenting plans, trade-offs, or strategy to executives and cross-functional leaders.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with the strategic context: what part of company strategy you are working on.
  2. Clarify the metrics you are trying to improve.
  3. State the assumptions guiding the work.
  4. Begin at the last point that is obvious to the audience, not too early and not too late.
  5. Anticipate each executive's likely questions and concerns.
  6. Pre-wire key stakeholders before the main meeting to reduce surprises.
- **Example**: Before a major review, Casey has teams role-play the CFO and CEO, predicting questions about economics, strategy, and the “why” behind the proposal.

### Perceived simplicity
- **When to use**: When a product must serve both novice and advanced users without becoming bloated or confusing.
- **Steps**:
  1. Keep the core experience simple and easy to use.
  2. Make advanced functionality discoverable only when users look for it.
  3. Avoid exposing complexity to users who will never need it.
  4. Design clear on-ramps for users who want more control or sophistication.
  5. Continuously test where complexity is helping versus confusing.
- **Example**: Eventbrite’s marketing tools let creators run simple default Facebook ads, while advanced users can opt into deeper targeting and creative controls.

### Justifying non-sexy product investments
- **When to use**: When advocating for work like performance, stability, developer velocity, UX quality, or technical debt reduction that is hard to measure directly.
- **Steps**:
  1. Align a small cross-functional leadership group first, especially PM, engineering, and design.
  2. Create custom metrics that approximate the value of the investment.
  3. Run small tests or experiments to demonstrate impact.
  4. Establish team principles so these areas are not repeatedly deprioritized.
  5. Frame the work as protecting existing value, not just creating upside.
- **Example**: Casey argues that conversion or engagement gains can erode if teams do not invest in quality and infrastructure at scale.

### Kindle strategies to fire strategies
- **When to use**: When an early-stage company is figuring out how to grow before and after product-market fit.
- **Steps**:
  1. Use non-scalable “kindle” tactics to get initial users and learn.
  2. Look for signals that reveal a scalable growth loop.
  3. Identify the scalable “fire” strategy, such as viral, content, sales, or paid loops.
  4. Once a scalable loop is unlocked, hire dedicated growth talent to amplify it.
  5. Focus growth hiring around the specific loop that is working.
- **Example**: Founders should handle early growth themselves, then hire a growth PM, salesperson, or performance marketer only after a scalable loop is proven.

### PM spectrum: innovator to executor
- **When to use**: When hiring, coaching, or leveling product managers.
- **Steps**:
  1. Recognize one end of the spectrum: idea-rich innovators who generate many concepts but may struggle to execute.
  2. Recognize the other end: strong executors who can ship well but need strategic direction.
  3. Hire for the middle when possible: people who can both shape strategy and deliver.
  4. Bias toward strong executors if forced to choose, because companies usually have more ideas than execution capacity.
  5. Invest in helping executors become more strategic through training, mentorship, and exposure to strong examples.
- **Example**: Many PMs can execute but get stuck before director level because they cannot independently write strong strategy docs.

### Ops as temporary inefficiency removal
- **When to use**: When deciding how to use product ops, marketing ops, or business ops teams.
- **Steps**:
  1. Treat ops as a way to identify inefficiencies, not as a permanent manual layer.
  2. Map the repetitive or broken process.
  3. Replace manual work with automation, software, or better process.
  4. Measure whether the need for the ops role is shrinking over time.
  5. Redeploy strong ops people into new high-value problems once inefficiencies are removed.
- **Example**: Eventbrite’s product ops team was tasked with finding inefficiencies and eliminating them through process or software, not owning manual work forever.

## Core Advice
- **When you face a high-stakes executive review or decision meeting**: Do extensive prep, anticipate every likely question, and pre-meet with key stakeholders before the main session — because important decisions are often made there, and surprises undermine confidence.
- **When you face a difficult delivery problem or impossible constraints on your team**: Escalate the issue early instead of heroically absorbing it yourself — because leaders can only help if they understand the real constraints.
- **When you face pressure to add more features while keeping the product easy to use**: Hide advanced features unless users actively seek them — because this preserves a simple core experience for most users.
- **When you face skepticism about investing in performance, stability, UX quality, or developer velocity**: Align PM, engineering, and design leaders first, then create custom metrics or small experiments to prove value — because these investments are hard to measure and easy to deprioritize.
- **When you face a mature product that is already working well**: Frame infrastructure and quality work as protecting existing gains, not just chasing upside — because conversion, engagement, and PMF can erode over time.
- **When you face the challenge of scaling a function with more people and process**: Treat ops roles as temporary mechanisms to remove inefficiency through automation and better systems — because permanent manual layers normalize broken process.
- **When you face the question of how to grow as a PM beyond execution**: Invest heavily in strategy skills, especially the ability to independently write strong strategy docs and drive decisions — because strategy is the bottleneck to senior leadership.
- **When you face early-stage growth decisions before product-market fit**: Use founder-led, non-scalable tactics to learn, but design the product with future scalable growth loops in mind — because distribution should be part of product design.
- **When you face the decision of whether to hire a dedicated growth leader**: Wait until you have unlocked a scalable growth loop, then hire specifically for that loop — because growth hires are most effective when they amplify a proven engine.
- **When you face rising acquisition costs and weaker platform targeting**: Invest in data network effects that use product usage data to improve product value and targeting over time — because first-party data becomes a durable advantage.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Leaders should handle problems themselves and avoid escalating upward unless absolutely necessary. → **Their view**: Strong leaders escalate constraints and trade-offs early — because executives need reality to make good decisions and fair judgments.
- **Conventional**: You should focus only on building product until you find product-market fit, and think about growth later. → **Their view**: Think about scalable growth loops before PMF — because a product that cannot acquire users at scale is incomplete.
- **Conventional**: Operations teams are a normal way to scale a function. → **Their view**: Ops should exist to automate itself away — because permanent manual ops layers institutionalize inefficiency.
- **Conventional**: At scale, the most important work is adding more growth on top of what already works. → **Their view**: Protecting what you have built can be just as important — because quality and performance can erode core metrics.
- **Conventional**: The best PMs are visionary idea generators. → **Their view**: If forced to choose, prefer strong executors — because companies usually have more ideas than execution capacity.
