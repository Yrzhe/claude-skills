# Claire Hughes Johnson

**Former COO** | **Stripe** | Expertise: leadership, operations, hiring-culture, go-to-market

## Bio
Claire Hughes Johnson helped scale Stripe from about 160 employees to more than 7,000 as COO, making her perspective especially valuable for leaders navigating growth, ambiguity, and organizational design. A longtime Google executive and author of *Scaling People*, she is known for turning “soft” management problems into practical operating systems for companies.

## Signature Frameworks

### Personal Operating Principles
- **When to use**: When you want to become a more effective leader or manager by understanding your own values, tendencies, and default behaviors.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify your core values by narrowing a long list down to your top 3-5.
  2. Reflect on the formative stories behind those values.
  3. Assess your work-style tendencies using tools like Myers-Briggs, DISC, or Enneagram.
  4. Translate those patterns into explicit operating principles you can share with others.
  5. Use those principles to build self-awareness and mutual awareness with your team.
- **Example**: A manager’s strong value of transparency may come from a formative family experience; understanding that story helps explain their management behavior.

### Say the Thing You Think You Cannot Say
- **When to use**: When a team is avoiding an uncomfortable truth, conflict, or observation that is blocking progress.
- **Steps**:
  1. Notice the unsaid issue or your internal “left-hand column” commentary.
  2. Detoxify the thought so it becomes constructive rather than harsh.
  3. Lead with a question instead of an accusation.
  4. Own the observation as your perception rather than stating it as fact.
  5. Open the door for others to engage with the issue.
- **Example**: In a meeting where two teams are clearly fighting over ownership, say: “I feel like there’s something we’re not talking about... I wonder if it’s the fact that these two teams both seem to have the exact same project.”

### Explorer, Not Lecturer
- **When to use**: When coaching direct reports or giving feedback, especially when you have a hypothesis but not complete certainty.
- **Steps**:
  1. Observe a pattern in someone’s behavior or work.
  2. Form a hypothesis rather than waiting for perfect evidence.
  3. Ask exploratory questions to understand their perspective.
  4. Share your observations with specific examples.
  5. Collaboratively test whether the hypothesis is true and what to change.
- **Example**: “My experience of you in that meeting was that you seemed nervous to me,” then explore what physical or verbal cues led to that impression.

### Distinguish Leadership from Management
- **When to use**: When clarifying what kind of support a team or company needs from you, especially as you scale.
- **Steps**:
  1. Separate visionary leadership work from day-to-day management work.
  2. Recognize whether you are stronger in one mode than the other.
  3. Ensure the organization has both functions covered.
  4. Create an environment where feedback can flow upward as well as downward.
- **Example**: Reid Hoffman has said he is more of a leader than a manager; a colleague’s blunt feedback about not hiring him to manage McDonald’s showed strong feedback culture.

### Come Back to the Operating System
- **When to use**: When a company or team feels chaotic, ambiguous, or unstable during growth.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define a few common rituals and practices such as goal-setting, planning, and reviews.
  2. Use those rituals consistently to create stability.
  3. Rely on shared touchstones for decision-making and execution.
  4. Avoid reinventing processes in moments of stress.
  5. Use the same basic operating model across teams to scale yourself as a leader.
- **Example**: In high-chaos moments, teams should return to known systems for launches, metrics reviews, and goal-setting instead of improvising.

### House Framework for Company Building
- **When to use**: When building the organizational infrastructure needed to scale beyond early product-market fit.
- **Steps**:
  1. Create foundational documents such as mission, long-term goals, and operating principles.
  2. Add supporting structures like planning, goal-setting, and business reviews.
  3. Build mechanicals through operating cadence: the rhythm of meetings, launches, and reviews.
  4. Keep the system lightweight but replicable across the company.
  5. Revise deliberately rather than constantly changing processes.
- **Example**: Claire compares scaling a company to building a house: without foundations, beams, and mechanical systems, the structure becomes a messy set of additions.

### Founding Documents
- **When to use**: When a startup is beginning to scale and needs shared clarity on purpose, priorities, and culture.
- **Steps**:
  1. Write a clear mission statement.
  2. Define long-term goals that explain what the company is trying to accomplish over time.
  3. Document company values or operating principles.
  4. Use these documents internally for hiring, onboarding, and decision-making, not just externally for fundraising.
- **Example**: At Stripe, “increase the GDP of the internet” became the mission, with long-term goals that included advancing developer tools.

### Operating Cadence and Business Reviews
- **When to use**: When teams need a repeatable rhythm for planning, measuring progress, and staying aligned.
- **Steps**:
  1. Set annual or semiannual targets.
  2. Use goals or OKRs to define milestones.
  3. Run regular business reviews using a consistent template.
  4. Review live dashboards instead of creating bespoke presentations where possible.
  5. Adjust cadence if reviews are too stale or too frequent to be useful.
- **Example**: Stripe used quarterly business reviews, but shifted some newer product areas to every six weeks when quarterly was too slow.

### Decision-Making Frameworks: SPADE and Type 1/Type 2 Decisions
- **When to use**: When teams are stuck, unclear on ownership, or overcomplicating decisions.
- **Steps**:
  1. Make explicit that a decision needs to be made.
  2. Clarify who the decision-maker is.
  3. Define the criteria for the decision.
  4. Assess whether the decision is irreversible or reversible.
  5. Follow a clear process and make the decision rather than letting ambiguity stall progress.
- **Example**: If someone is unsure who owns a decision, Claire’s advice is essentially: ask, and if you’re not sure, it may be you.

## Core Advice
- **When decision ownership is unclear**: act like the decision-maker by default, or explicitly ask who owns it — because ambiguity slows the company down more than imperfect decisions.
- **When a startup is growing fast**: build lightweight infrastructure early — because ad hoc systems harden into painful, unfair, and confusing processes later.
- **When giving difficult feedback**: ask a question and frame your point as an owned observation — because questions reduce defensiveness and invite dialogue.
- **When coaching without perfect evidence**: use a hypothesis-based approach — because waiting for certainty delays development.
- **When hiring early**: invest in a real interviewing process and train interviewers — because talent compounds and hiring quality is leverage.
- **When communication is breaking down**: repeat key messages across multiple channels — because no single channel reaches everyone.
- **When execution gets chaotic**: return to a few consistent rituals — because stable operating rhythms reduce cognitive load.
- **When meetings feel wasteful**: clarify purpose, decision needs, owner, and attendees — because most meeting waste comes from ambiguity.
- **When reviews feel stale**: change cadence based on freshness and progress — because too much or too little review both hurt execution.
- **When tempted to keep redesigning processes**: keep a small number of good-enough systems — because constant change creates confusion.
- **When aligning on strategy**: use offsites or dedicated planning time — because distance from daily work improves buy-in and memory.
- **When considering a COO hire**: treat it as situational and consider narrower operational roles first — because the role is not a universal fix.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Early-stage startups should focus almost exclusively on product-market fit and worry about company-building later. → **Their view**: Build lightweight operational and cultural infrastructure sooner than you think — because product-market fit is only the product, not the company.
- **Conventional**: Managers should wait for hard data before giving developmental feedback. → **Their view**: Use informed hypotheses earlier — because growth slows when managers wait for perfect evidence.
- **Conventional**: Titles help motivate people and clarify hierarchy as companies grow. → **Their view**: Be conservative with titles early — because titles reduce flexibility and can create future organizational pain.
- **Conventional**: A COO is a standard scaling hire. → **Their view**: Most companies do not need a COO — because the role is highly context-dependent and often overused as a silver bullet.
- **Conventional**: More meetings and reviews improve execution. → **Their view**: Too much cadence can slow velocity — because prep and stale updates steal time from actual work.
