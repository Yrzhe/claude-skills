## Signature Frameworks

### Outside-the-building product management
- **When to use**: When defining strategy, writing product docs, or trying to become a stronger PM.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start from the customer’s perspective, not the internal team’s perspective.
  2. Include the market and competitor perspective in every narrative.
  3. Spend most of your thinking time on what is happening outside the company.
  4. Support claims with anecdotes and data rather than internal opinions.
  5. Use the outside view to identify differentiated value the company can uniquely deliver.
- **Example**: A strategy doc begins with customer pain, market shifts, and competitor moves before discussing roadmap or execution.

### Structured qualitative research with counterfactuals
- **When to use**: When doing customer interviews, surveys, or synthesis work and you want real insight rather than noise.
- **Steps**:
  1. Right-size the sample size before starting research.
  2. Avoid leading questions that confirm what you already believe.
  3. Seek out people and sources you do not normally talk to.
  4. Look for proof that your current theory is wrong.
  5. Synthesize findings into implications, not just notes.
  6. Use LLMs to compare your strategy against customer feedback and competitor positioning.
  7. Ask specifically where your strategy does not fit what customers said.
- **Example**: Put interview transcripts into ChatGPT and ask, “Tell me where my strategy does not fit what these customers talked about.”

### Data as compass, not GPS
- **When to use**: When interpreting metrics, experiments, or product analytics.
- **Steps**:
  1. Treat data as a signal that can disprove or validate intuition, not as the answer itself.
  2. Check whether the data is representative and free of selection bias.
  3. Inspect what happened before the metric moved.
  4. Inspect what happened after the metric moved.
  5. Go one level up to see whether the metric matters to the real business goal.
  6. Only trust the result if it still makes sense after those checks.
- **Example**: A conversion lift that only applies to 2% of onboarding traffic may be statistically interesting but strategically meaningless.

### AI product context stack
- **When to use**: When building AI features or internal AI workflows.
- **Steps**:
  1. Assume the model is replaceable.
  2. Focus on the context and data you can provide to the model.
  3. Bring together first-party, third-party, timely, and structured data.
  4. Connect the relevant business rules, policies, and domain data.
  5. Optimize for the right data at the right time in the right place.
- **Example**: For an HR assistant, the useful context is not just employee records but also benefits, country-specific legal rules, and company policies.

### B2B growth operating model
- **When to use**: When building or scaling a growth team in B2B SaaS.
- **Steps**:
  1. Prove the growth function can create value at all.
  2. Find repeatable wins rather than one-off hacks.
  3. Operationalize the wins into a system.
  4. Scale across acquisition, activation, retention, engagement, expansion, and upsell.
  5. Integrate growth with product, sales, and marketing rather than operating separately.
- **Example**: A growth team moves from a “gold rush” phase of random discovery to a repeatable, embedded system that supports the whole company.

### Career bingo card
- **When to use**: When choosing your next role or planning long-term career growth.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify gaps in your experience.
  2. Choose roles that fill missing boxes.
  3. Prefer adjacent stretch roles over identical ones.
  4. Use each role to build pattern recognition across functions and company types.
  5. Aim eventually to work at the intersection of what you are good at and what is valuable to the company.
- **Example**: Shaun intentionally moved across enterprise software, Atlassian’s no-sales PLG motion, consumer insurance at Metromile, and Salesforce/MuleSoft’s enterprise distribution machine.

## Core Advice
- **When you want to become a stronger PM**: Spend most of your time thinking outside the building about customers, the market, and competitors — because the PM job is to find differentiated value in the market, not to get absorbed by internal execution or politics.
- **When you write product docs or present strategy**: Frame everything from the customer’s, market’s, and competitor’s perspective — because that framing is easier for others to understand and support.
- **When you think you are already doing enough customer research**: Use a structured process and actively seek counterexamples and unfamiliar sources — because most teams confirm existing beliefs instead of learning something new.
- **When you run interviews or surveys**: Avoid leading questions and right-size the sample size before you start — because bad research design creates false confidence.
- **When you use LLMs for research synthesis**: Ask the model where your strategy does not fit the evidence — because LLMs are most useful when pushed to reveal gaps and contradictions.
- **When you evaluate a metric or experiment result**: Check the upstream and downstream effects, then zoom out to the business goal — because a positive metric can be misleading if it is narrow or short-lived.
- **When intuition and data conflict**: Trust your intuition first, then prove it right or wrong with rigorous analysis — because wildly wrong results often indicate a data or analysis problem.
- **When building AI products**: Invest more in data access, data quality, timeliness, and structure than in model choice — because models are replaceable and context is the real advantage.
- **When designing AI workflows for internal teams**: Create a “feedback river” of customer feedback, NPS, competitor info, and usage data — because LLMs are much more useful with a broad, current stream of relevant information.
- **When your company is deciding whether to invest in PLG**: Do not treat PLG as all-or-nothing; balance it with sales-led motions — because enterprise buying does not naturally optimize for end-user success.
- **When you are building a growth team**: Move from random experimentation to repeatable systems and then to cross-functional integration — because growth succeeds when it becomes part of the company operating model.
- **When choosing your next career move**: Pick roles that stretch you into adjacent domains you do not yet know — because variety builds pattern recognition and optionality.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Product management is a mature, well-defined discipline. → **Their view**: PM is still relatively undeveloped and too random in outcomes and performance — because many PMs miss the truly value-added work of outside-in thinking and differentiated market insight.
- **Conventional**: Great PMs are mostly execution-oriented and close to delivery. → **Their view**: PMs who spend too much time on scrum, delivery, and internal politics cannot win — because the real job is finding reliable differentiated value in the market.
- **Conventional**: Data-driven means letting data tell you what to do. → **Their view**: Data is a compass, not a GPS — because data is better at disproving bad ideas than providing the answer.
- **Conventional**: AI differentiation comes mainly from better models or clever prompts. → **Their view**: The real moat is context and data management — because models are replaceable and the quality of the data you provide determines usefulness.
- **Conventional**: AI will make it easy to clone B2B SaaS apps and destroy incumbents. → **Their view**: Incumbents are more likely to get stronger — because business rules and workflows create deep lock-in.
- **Conventional**: PLG is either the future of B2B or a niche tactic. → **Their view**: PLG is important, but only as part of a balanced motion with sales and other channels — because the best businesses combine self-serve and enterprise motions.
