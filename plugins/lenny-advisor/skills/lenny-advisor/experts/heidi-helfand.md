# Heidi Helfand

**Author, consultant, and organizational coach focused on reteaming** | **Dynamic Reteaming / independent advisor** | Expertise: leadership, hiring-culture, engineering-management, strategy

## Bio
Heidi Helfand is the author of *Dynamic Reteaming* and a longtime startup operator who has worked across fast-growing SaaS companies including Expertcity, AppFolio, and Procore. Her perspective matters because she has spent years helping leaders navigate the people side of growth: reorgs, onboarding, mergers, team splits, and the messy reality that teams must change to stay effective.

She is especially valuable when organizations need to redesign teams without losing learning, autonomy, or resilience. Her work turns team change from an ad hoc crisis into a repeatable leadership capability.

## Signature Frameworks

### The Five Patterns of Dynamic Reteaming
- **When to use**: When diagnosing or planning how teams change over time in growing, shrinking, or evolving organizations.
- **Steps**:
  1. One by one: account for people joining or leaving the company.
  2. Grow and split: when a team becomes too large or work diverges, split it into two or more teams.
  3. Merging: combine two or more teams when work consolidates, companies combine, or downsizing occurs.
  4. Isolation: create a team off to the side with process freedom to pursue innovation or handle emergencies.
  5. Switching: move people between teams temporarily or permanently for learning, fulfillment, or resilience.
- **Example**: At Expertcity, Heidi used isolation when a small team was pulled off the main product to build GoToMyPC with more freedom and faster iteration, helping save the company after the original marketplace failed.

### Whiteboard Reteaming
- **When to use**: When leaders want to make a reorg or team redesign more transparent and collaborative without fully opening up self-selection.
- **Steps**:
  1. Draft a starting-point org design on whiteboards.
  2. Show team names, missions, open roles, and current people assignments.
  3. Bring people in to review the proposed structure.
  4. Invite feedback on mistakes, better placements, and personal interest in opportunities.
  5. Refine the design quickly and finalize ownership and moves.
- **Example**: At Procore, Heidi helped roll out whiteboards for an 80-person platform organization splitting from two large clusters into three, allowing people to spot design mistakes and express interest in new roles.

### Transitions: Endings, Neutral Zone, New Beginning
- **When to use**: When helping people emotionally process reorgs, reteaming, acquisitions, or any major organizational change.
- **Steps**:
  1. Acknowledge the ending: name what is changing or being left behind.
  2. Support people through the neutral zone: the uncomfortable in-between period where the future is not yet settled.
  3. Create a new beginning: paint a clear vision of the future state, its purpose, and its benefits.
- **Example**: Heidi said learning William Bridges' framework made later changes easier for her to understand because it normalized the liminal period between old and new team realities.

### RIDE Decision Clarity Framework
- **When to use**: When a team or organization needs clarity on who is involved in a change decision and how participation should work.
- **Steps**:
  1. R: identify who is requesting the change.
  2. I: identify who can give input.
  3. D: identify who is the decider.
  4. E: identify who will execute the change.
- **Example**: Heidi used RIDE, taught by former Procore chief people officer Pat Wadors, to clarify that some changes are open to team input while others, like acquisitions, are not.

### Story of Our Team
- **When to use**: When two teams merge and need to build shared context, trust, and identity.
- **Steps**:
  1. Have each team stand in order of when members joined.
  2. Create a timeline of milestones, departures, and meaningful accomplishments.
  3. Share the timelines with the newly merged team.
  4. Discuss what each team is proud of and what they learned.
  5. Use the shared history as a bridge to define the future together.
- **Example**: Heidi recommends this exercise after mergers so teams can discover each other's history, systems, and accomplishments before aligning on what comes next.

## Core Advice
- **When you face a reorg or team redesign**: Do a transparent, time-boxed planning process with a visible starting-point design rather than revealing a fully secret final answer — because transparency helps people understand opportunities, catch design mistakes, and feel agency, while time-boxing prevents endless deliberation.
- **When you face a decision about how much input people should have in a change**: Do explicit decision-role mapping using who requests, inputs, decides, and executes — because people handle change better when they know whether they are being consulted, informed, or asked to act.
- **When you face the need to incubate a new product or solve an urgent cross-functional problem**: Do an isolation team — because new bets and emergencies need faster iteration and less bureaucracy than mature product lines can usually support.
- **When you face a team that has grown too large and feels sluggish**: Do a grow-and-split once meetings drag, decisions slow down, and work diverges — because communication overhead has overtaken the benefits of staying together.
- **When you face onboarding new hires or new leaders**: Do belonging-focused onboarding and also prepare existing employees for the change — because new hires need connection from day one, but existing team members need context so they do not feel blindsided.
- **When you face the risk of key-person dependency**: Do regular pairing and switching — because knowledge redundancy creates resilience and makes future reteaming easier.
- **When you face a merged team after an acquisition or consolidation**: Do a shared-history exercise before pushing straight into execution — because teams integrate faster when they understand each other's milestones and identity.
- **When you face pressure to spread your best people across teams to improve performance everywhere**: Do not automatically break up a high-performing team — because performance often comes from chemistry, shared context, and working rhythms.
- **When you face resource planning across many projects**: Do not allocate people in tiny percentages across multiple efforts whenever possible — because context switching is expensive and multitasking degrades delivery quality.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: The best teams stay stable for long periods and should be preserved as much as possible. → **Their view**: Team change is inevitable and should be treated as a capability to build, not a failure to avoid — because fast-growing and shrinking companies naturally evolve.
- **Conventional**: Reorgs should be planned secretly by executives and announced only when finalized. → **Their view**: Many team changes work better with meaningful transparency and input from the people affected — because people closest to the work can spot flaws and identify better structures.
- **Conventional**: Silos are always bad and should be eliminated. → **Their view**: Beneficial silos can be exactly what innovation or crisis response requires — because an isolated team with process freedom can move faster.
- **Conventional**: To raise performance across the org, spread high performers evenly across teams. → **Their view**: Breaking up a great team can destroy the chemistry that made it high-performing — because team effectiveness is emergent.
- **Conventional**: Org design is mainly a top-down management exercise. → **Their view**: Teams themselves can and should sometimes influence how they evolve — because agency improves ownership, morale, and effectiveness.
