# Maya Prohovnik

**Head of Product for Podcasting** | **Spotify** | Expertise: podcast product, creator tools, product leadership, consumer growth

## Bio
Maya Prohovnik leads product for Spotify’s podcasting business and was employee #1 at Anchor, the startup Spotify acquired and turned into the core of its podcast hosting platform. Her perspective matters because she has built creator products from the ground up, with a rare mix of deep user empathy, startup speed, and willingness to challenge “best practices” when they get in the way of growth.

She is especially valuable when you need to understand how to build for emotionally invested users, how to preserve startup DNA inside a large company, and how to make strategic product calls when data is incomplete.

## Signature Frameworks

### Dogfood the creator experience
- **When to use**: When building products for creators or any user group whose workflow and emotions are hard to understand from the outside.
- **Steps**:
  1. Become a real user of the product yourself.
  2. Use the product in the most realistic way possible, including the rough edges.
  3. Feel the pain points firsthand instead of only reading feature requests or analytics.
  4. Share your experience with the team and make it part of product discussions.
  5. Turn what you learn into onboarding, education, and product decisions.
- **Example**: Maya made multiple podcasts of her own, including a fully mobile, minimally edited Big Brother podcast, to understand what it feels like to create on Anchor.

### Build things that don't scale to unlock growth
- **When to use**: When a tiny team needs to create a magical user experience or remove a major friction point quickly.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the biggest friction in the user journey.
  2. Create a manual workaround behind the scenes.
  3. Package the experience as a simple, magical one-button flow for users.
  4. Use the workaround to validate demand and drive adoption.
  5. Replace or evolve the workaround once the product has scaled.
- **Example**: Anchor used college interns to manually create Apple Podcasts accounts and submit hundreds of thousands of shows so users could press one button and feel instantly distributed.

### Mission stays fixed; product can evolve
- **When to use**: When a product is working but may not be on track to achieve the company’s larger ambition.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the long-term mission clearly.
  2. Evaluate whether the current product path can realistically reach that mission.
  3. If not, be willing to kill the current version even if users like it.
  4. Rebuild around the next best path to the mission.
  5. Keep iterating as new information arrives.
- **Example**: Anchor moved from 1.0 to 2.0 even though users loved 1.0, because the team believed it would never become big enough to democratize audio.

### Use data and gut as complementary inputs
- **When to use**: When deciding what to build, what to prioritize, or whether to make a strategic shift.
- **Steps**:
  1. Form a hypothesis.
  2. Use data to understand or validate the hypothesis.
  3. Add user research and qualitative feedback.
  4. Launch with success metrics in place.
  5. Use your gut as another data point, but explain the reasoning behind it objectively.
  6. Bring stories, anecdotes, and prior experience to persuade stakeholders.
- **Example**: Anchor’s move to support podcast export was initially resisted, but the team tested it and later saw it was the missing ingredient behind hockey-stick growth.

### Operate like a startup inside a large company
- **When to use**: When a startup team is acquired and needs to preserve speed and identity inside a bigger organization.
- **Steps**:
  1. Keep the team’s core values explicit and stable.
  2. Stay vocal about what the team is doing internally.
  3. Build relationships across the larger company.
  4. Help other teams whenever possible.
  5. Stay excited and adaptable as the parent company’s strategy shifts.
  6. Reduce unnecessary bureaucracy while accepting the complexity that comes with scale.
- **Example**: After Spotify acquired Anchor, the team kept its move-fast DNA, but later had to integrate more deeply into Spotify to avoid feeling isolated.

### Radical Candor as a management operating system
- **When to use**: When giving feedback, managing performance, or building strong cross-functional relationships.
- **Steps**:
  1. Care personally about the person.
  2. Challenge directly with specific feedback.
  3. Treat feedback as a gift rather than a confrontation.
  4. Assume underperformance is often a role-fit issue, not a character flaw.
  5. Use the conversation to help the person succeed, even if that means moving them elsewhere.
- **Example**: Maya makes her managers read Radical Candor and uses it to frame difficult conversations as support for someone’s growth.

### Daily triage with do, defer, delegate, delete
- **When to use**: When your workload is overwhelming and you need to clear mental clutter.
- **Steps**:
  1. Write everything down.
  2. At the end of the day, sort each item into do, defer, delegate, or delete.
  3. Use the list to decide what tomorrow should look like.
  4. Do not keep tasks in your head.
- **Example**: Maya uses Todoist and reviews her list every day so she can stop mentally carrying unfinished work.

### Public speaking through rehearsal and reframing
- **When to use**: When preparing for a high-stakes presentation or stage appearance.
- **Steps**:
  1. Decide not to hate public speaking.
  2. Reframe anxiety as adrenaline that helps you perform.
  3. Practice the talk many times end-to-end.
  4. Choose speaker notes that fit your style.
  5. Use eye contact, humor, and personal stories to connect with the audience.
  6. Lean into caring deeply about the topic.
- **Example**: Maya rehearses major talks at least 10 times and often writes near-verbatim speaker notes so she can deliver them confidently.

## Core Advice
- **When you build for creators or other emotionally invested users**: Become one of the users yourself and use the product in a real, imperfect way — because firsthand experience reveals emotional friction and hidden barriers that analytics and feature requests miss.
- **When your product has a major friction point that blocks adoption**: Do the unscalable manual work behind the scenes so the user experience feels magical — because a simple, low-friction experience can create outsized growth even if the internal process is messy.
- **When a product is loved but seems too small to support the company’s mission**: Be willing to kill or radically change it — because user love alone is not enough if the product cannot reach the scale required by the mission.
- **When users ask for something that seems off-strategy**: Test it instead of dismissing it outright — because small experiments can reveal that the requested feature is actually the missing growth lever.
- **When you’re deciding between data and intuition**: Treat gut feel as a legitimate data point, but explain it with evidence and stories — because people are more likely to align when your intuition is grounded in experience and user evidence.
- **When you’re leading a team inside a larger company**: Invest early in relationships and internal visibility, not just execution — because without relationships, the team can become isolated and lose a sense of belonging to the broader mission.
- **When you need to preserve startup speed after an acquisition**: Protect your core values and aggressively remove unnecessary process — because speed is part of the team’s identity, but some slowing is inevitable as coordination needs increase.
- **When someone on your team is underperforming**: Assume role mismatch before assuming laziness or incompetence — because a caring, fit-focused conversation is more productive and often leads to a better outcome for the person.
- **When your mind is overloaded with tasks**: Write everything down and triage it into do, defer, delegate, or delete — because externalizing tasks reduces stress and frees up cognitive bandwidth for actual problem solving.
- **When you’re preparing for a presentation and feel anxious**: Reframe the anxiety as useful adrenaline and rehearse until the delivery feels natural — because acceptance plus repetition turns fear into performance energy and builds confidence.
- **When you’re trying to build a creator platform**: Focus on community and repeat usage, not just feature completeness — because creators keep investing only when they feel audience connection and feedback loops.
- **When you’re hiring**: Ask candidates what podcast they would make about anything — because the answer reveals passion, curiosity, and whether they can generate ideas from their own interests.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: If users love a product and retention is strong, keep iterating on it. → **Their view**: Kill and rebuild it if it is not on track for the larger mission — because a product can be good and still be strategically too small.
- **Conventional**: Product decisions should be driven primarily by data. → **Their view**: Gut feel is also a valid form of data — because experienced intuition and user context reveal what metrics miss.
- **Conventional**: You should avoid unscalable manual work in product. → **Their view**: Do unscalable things when they create a magical experience — because early growth often requires hacks before systems exist.
- **Conventional**: After an acquisition, the startup should quickly integrate into the parent company. → **Their view**: Stay separate long enough to preserve speed, but not so long that the team becomes isolated — because integration is a trade-off.
- **Conventional**: Public speaking is mostly a natural talent. → **Their view**: It is a skill built through rehearsal and mindset — because preparation matters more than charisma.
- **Conventional**: A good manager should focus on fixing underperformance directly. → **Their view**: Underperformance is often a role-fit issue — because helping someone find the right fit is more humane and often more effective.
