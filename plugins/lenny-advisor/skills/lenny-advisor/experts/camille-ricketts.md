## Signature Frameworks

### Content-Market Fit
- **When to use**: When building a content program to drive discovery, trust, and demand rather than publishing opportunistically.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the exact audience you want to reach.
  2. Understand what that audience needs to succeed, avoid failure, or reduce anxiety.
  3. Treat content like a product, not a side project.
  4. Choose content that acts like a painkiller, not a vitamin.
  5. Create a repeatable content product that directly addresses those needs.
- **Example**: First Round Review focused on operators’ real anxieties and failures, publishing tactical stories that helped readers feel less alone and learn how to navigate difficult situations.

### Community by Company Stage and Market Type (2x2 Matrix)
- **When to use**: When deciding what kind of community investment makes sense based on whether you have product-market fit and whether your company is more enterprise or consumer-oriented.
- **Steps**:
  1. Assess whether you have product-market fit or are still searching for it.
  2. Assess whether your motion is strongly enterprise or more consumer/prosumer.
  3. If pre-PMF and enterprise, start with customer advisory boards.
  4. If post-PMF and enterprise, build champion and consultant communities.
  5. If post-PMF and more consumer/prosumer, invest in ambassadors and influencers.
- **Example**: Notion was post-PMF and closer to the consumer side early on, which made ambassadors and influencers especially effective.

### Atomic Unit of Sharing
- **When to use**: When evaluating whether community-led growth is likely to work for your product.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify whether your product creates something users naturally want to share.
  2. Check whether sharing that output signals identity, taste, competence, or aspiration.
  3. Design programs that amplify that sharing behavior.
  4. Support creators who build businesses or audiences around those shareable outputs.
- **Example**: For Notion, templates and beautifully organized workspaces were the atomic units of sharing; users wanted to show them off, which fueled community and discovery.

### Ambassador Program Design
- **When to use**: When you already see organic enthusiasm from users and want to formalize a non-transactional advocacy program.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify your best-fit and most vocal users rather than your idealized target users.
  2. Start small with a curated initial cohort.
  3. Offer special access, such as feature previews and direct conversations with leaders.
  4. Promote ambassadors’ work and support their events or initiatives.
  5. Listen continuously and evolve the program based on what participants actually want.
- **Example**: Notion started its ambassador program in 2019 with just 20 highly vocal users found on Twitter and other platforms, then gave them early feature access, AMAs with leaders, and support for events.

### Healthy Community Growth
- **When to use**: When a community is gaining momentum and you want to preserve engagement quality rather than maximizing headline size.
- **Steps**:
  1. Avoid optimizing for raw member count too early.
  2. Use a lightweight application or intake process to maintain fit and pacing.
  3. Add members in manageable cohorts.
  4. Give existing members time to welcome and integrate newcomers.
  5. Prioritize intimacy and interaction quality over vanity metrics.
- **Example**: Notion inducted ambassadors in small batches of around 20 people at a time so the community would not feel suddenly diluted or like an auditorium.

## Core Advice
- **When you face early organic enthusiasm around your product**: Do not over-index on ROI metrics immediately; first nurture the energy and learn what participants want — because early community value is often visible in qualitative signals, geographic spread, and word of mouth before it is cleanly measurable.
- **When you face the decision of whether community is right for your product**: Check whether your product has an atomic unit of sharing that users are proud to show others — because community works especially well when users can share outputs that express identity, creativity, or competence.
- **When you face the challenge of starting an ambassador program**: Begin with a small group of your most vocal, best-fit users and give them special access rather than cash incentives — because the strongest ambassadors are already intrinsically motivated.
- **When you face pressure to scale a community quickly**: Grow the community slowly in cohorts and protect interaction quality — because if a community gets too large too fast, members feel anonymous and participation drops.
- **When you face uncertainty about where your brand team’s impact shows up**: Track net new visitors to your website as a core discovery metric — because it’s a practical proxy for awareness and new interest.
- **When you face the need to make content marketing actually work**: Treat content like a product and design it around audience pain, not just topics you want to publish — because content succeeds when it solves meaningful problems for a specific audience.
- **When you face the temptation to build your community on a custom platform**: Use a tool your audience already lives in, like Slack, if that fits their workflow — because reducing friction increases participation.
- **When you face a major outage or product failure**: Communicate frequently and clearly, and remember that users are often more resilient than you fear if the product is truly valuable — because transparency preserves trust.
- **When you face the question of whether PR still matters in a direct-to-audience world**: Invest in strong press relationships alongside owned channels — because credible third-party coverage can create breakthrough legitimacy.
- **When you face pressure for a founder to post constantly on social media**: Only publish when there is something genuinely valuable to say and align the approach with the founder’s authentic style — because forced posting quotas usually produce low-value content.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Community programs should be tightly measured for ROI from the start. → **Their view**: Early community efforts should not be constrained by hard metrics before you understand the organic energy and strategic value — because some of the biggest benefits show up before clean attribution does.
- **Conventional**: The best community strategy is to grow membership as fast as possible. → **Their view**: Healthy communities often grow better slowly and intentionally — because rapid scale can mute conversation and reduce belonging.
- **Conventional**: Community means a big forum or chat space. → **Their view**: Community can take many forms, including customer advisory boards, champion networks, and consultant ecosystems — because the right structure depends on stage and business model.
- **Conventional**: Owned media and social channels have made PR much less important. → **Their view**: Traditional press still matters a great deal when done well — because credible coverage can create a step-change in awareness and trust.
- **Conventional**: Founders should post constantly to build audience and brand. → **Their view**: Founders should avoid arbitrary posting quotas and communicate in ways that fit their personality — because authenticity compounds.
