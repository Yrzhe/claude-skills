# Eoghan McCabe

**Founder and CEO** | **Intercom** | Expertise: ai-strategy, pricing, product-management, leadership

## Bio
Eoghan McCabe is the founder and CEO of Intercom, the customer communications company that reinvented itself around AI customer service with Fin. His perspective matters because he led a late-stage SaaS company through a hard AI-first transformation involving product focus, pricing redesign, and a major cultural reset.

He is especially useful when a company is facing disruption, needs to make painful strategic tradeoffs, or has to rethink how it prices and organizes around AI.

## Signature Frameworks

### Wartime AI Transformation
- **When to use**: When an established software company is plateauing, facing disruption, and incremental change is unlikely to be enough.
- **Steps**:
  1. Acknowledge the existential threat clearly and publicly.
  2. Cut costs and stop legacy spending patterns that assume old growth will return.
  3. Pick a single strategic lane instead of serving too many use cases.
  4. Commit meaningful capital to the disruptive technology rather than dabbling.
  5. Rebuild culture and incentives to match the new strategy.
  6. Accept short-term pain, including turnover and revenue tradeoffs, to create long-term viability.
- **Example**: McCabe returned to Intercom as growth was nearing zero net new ARR, declared the company needed to become a “wartime company,” cut projects and spending, focused on customer service, invested nearly $100 million in AI, and reorganized the company around Fin.

### Outcome-Based AI Pricing
- **When to use**: When pricing AI products where customers care about business results more than seats, usage complexity, or technical inputs.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the clearest customer outcome the product delivers.
  2. Anchor pricing to customer value rather than your internal cost structure.
  3. Choose a metric customers perceive as fair, simple, and predictable.
  4. Test the willingness-to-pay threshold where the value proposition still feels obvious.
  5. Let cost reduction and margin improvement be your internal optimization problem.
- **Example**: Intercom priced Fin at 99 cents per resolved support ticket, even when early costs were about $1.20 per resolution, because customers were often spending $20 to $30 per human-resolved ticket and the company wanted pricing aligned to value delivered.

### Culture as a Sharp Knife
- **When to use**: When a company has become bloated, consensus-driven, or misaligned with the intensity required for a new strategic phase.
- **Steps**:
  1. Rewrite company values to explicitly reflect the behaviors needed for the next era.
  2. Translate values into measurable performance criteria rather than slogans.
  3. Score employees on both outcomes and behavior against values.
  4. Use a consistent formula to make retention decisions rather than ad hoc manager discretion.
  5. Hire new people in the image of the desired culture.
- **Example**: McCabe rewrote Intercom's values around resilience, high standards, hard work, and shareholder value, then created quarterly scoring that combined goal performance with behavior against values; over time about 40% of employees turned over.

### First-Principles Product Strategy
- **When to use**: When teams need to reason clearly about products, initiatives, or go-to-market choices instead of copying best practices.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define who the initiative is for.
  2. Clarify the ultimate goal and the mechanism by which it creates value.
  3. List alternative mechanisms that could achieve the same goal.
  4. Define success from both the company and user perspective.
  5. Build a joined-up strategy from these underlying truths.
- **Example**: McCabe described how Intercom would break down even something like events by asking who they are for, what goal they serve, what mechanism makes them work, what alternatives exist, and how both the company and attendee define value.

### Employee-Founder-Product Fit
- **When to use**: When scaling or reshaping a company and trying to determine whether the current team matches the company you are actually building.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the type of company you want to be, including pace, hierarchy, and decision style.
  2. Assess whether current employees thrive in that environment.
  3. Recognize that mismatch is often structural, not moral.
  4. Transition out people who want a different kind of company.
  5. Recruit people whose temperament and ambition fit the new operating model.
- **Example**: McCabe argued that employees who wanted a gentler, more democratic environment would be happier elsewhere, while Intercom needed people who wanted a more intense, founder-led company.

## Core Advice
- **When you face an existential AI disruption to your business**: Do not treat AI as an optional add-on; go all in on understanding how it will disrupt your category and build around that reality — because McCabe argues AI is a platform shift on the scale of or bigger than prior computing waves.
- **When you face slowing growth and a bloated late-stage company**: Cut costs aggressively, stop prestige spending, and remove projects that do not support the new strategic focus — because urgency and capital preservation are prerequisites for a real turnaround.
- **When you face too many products, customer segments, or monetization models**: Pick a lane and simplify, even if it means walking away from meaningful existing revenue — because diluted strategy creates organizational drag and customer confusion.
- **When you face resistance to a major strategic shift**: Use decisive founder-led decision-making rather than waiting for broad consensus — because committee processes often preserve the status quo.
- **When you face customer frustration with pricing**: Simplify pricing dramatically and align it to customer outcomes, even if it requires giving up short-term revenue — because trust and clarity improve retention and long-term value.
- **When you face uncertainty about how to price an AI product**: Price from customer value, not from your current model costs — because costs will likely fall over time, while value perception drives adoption.
- **When you face a need to reshape company culture**: Make values operational by tying them to performance reviews and retention decisions — because culture changes only when behaviors have consequences.
- **When you face a talent gap in AI**: Bring in real AI experts and empower younger, AI-native builders rather than assuming existing teams will naturally adapt — because AI-native talent often works differently and moves faster.
- **When you face competition from fast-moving AI startups**: Match their intensity or hire leaders who can; do not half-commit — because startups often win through extreme effort plus AI-native workflows.
- **When you face the challenge of developing strong product leaders**: Give PMs broad ownership, expose them to complexity, and teach first-principles thinking through explicit frameworks — because this builds judgment, not just feature execution.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: AI support bots are impersonal and inferior to human service. → **Their view**: A great AI agent can be more personal than human support because it is instant, consistent, expert, and always available — because customers often prefer fast, accurate resolution over waiting for humans.
- **Conventional**: You should price AI products based on cost or preserve margins from day one. → **Their view**: You should price based on customer value and let cost be your internal problem — because adoption depends on perceived value, and costs usually improve over time.
- **Conventional**: Big company transformations should be collaborative, consensus-driven, and gentle. → **Their view**: In crisis, great companies need strong hierarchy and unilateral CEO decisions — because painful, risky decisions often require founder authority.
- **Conventional**: Protecting jobs should be the default lens for evaluating automation. → **Their view**: AI replacing repetitive digital work is broadly positive because it removes demeaning tasks and frees humans for more meaningful work — because technological progress has historically improved living standards.
- **Conventional**: Established SaaS companies can cautiously add AI features while preserving the old business model. → **Their view**: Half-measures will fail; if you want to compete in AI, you must change strategy, culture, talent, and pace of execution — because incumbents lose when they keep old operating habits.
