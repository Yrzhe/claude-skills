# Robby Stein

**VP of Product, Google Search** | **Google** | Expertise: search, consumer AI, product strategy, growth

## Bio
Robby Stein leads product for Google Search, including AI Overviews, AI Mode, ranking, and multimodal experiences like Google Lens. He previously drove major consumer growth at Instagram, helping shape products like Stories, Reels, and Close Friends.

His perspective matters because he has repeatedly taken mature products and found new growth engines inside them. He combines product intuition, user research, and metric discipline with a strong bias toward clarity and compounding improvement.

## Signature Frameworks

### Embodying relentless improvement
- **When to use**: When building or scaling a product that needs continuous progress rather than a one-time breakthrough.
- **Steps**:
  1. Exert relentless effort in a direction of positive productivity.
  2. Never be content with the current version.
  3. Keep making the product better through compounding small improvements.
  4. Push until the product crosses a tipping point and becomes clearly useful.
- **Example**: AI Mode started as a rough prototype, but a small team kept iterating until it became magical and useful.

### Jobs-to-be-done discovery
- **When to use**: When trying to understand why people use a product or why they abandon it.
- **Steps**:
  1. Ask what problem the user is actually trying to solve.
  2. Interrogate the context of use: where they were, what triggered the need, and what they were trying to accomplish.
  3. Distinguish between utility jobs and emotional jobs.
  4. Use the answer to define the product’s true essence and next direction.
- **Example**: Close Friends was not just about private posting; it was about feeling connected and getting a DM back from a trusted group.

### Design for clarity over cleverness
- **When to use**: When introducing a new feature into an existing product or designing a new interface.
- **Steps**:
  1. Use familiar patterns and primitives whenever possible.
  2. Avoid overly clever or ambiguous UI that forces users to learn new conventions.
  3. Make the feature’s purpose obvious at a glance.
  4. Only reinvent when the new behavior truly requires it.
- **Example**: Google chose the name “AI Mode” because it immediately communicates what the feature is.

### Build complementary, not replacement, product layers
- **When to use**: When adding a new format or capability to a mature product.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the core essence of the existing product.
  2. Add the new experience in a way that expands the product rather than disrupting it.
  3. Keep the new layer coherent with the existing system, but distinct enough to have its own expectations.
  4. Let the new format become a natural extension of the core product.
- **Example**: Instagram Stories did not replace the feed; it added a lower-pressure, ephemeral sharing layer.

### Instrument, diagnose, then improve
- **When to use**: When managing a mature product or deciding where to invest resources.
- **Steps**:
  1. Track retention and growth curves to see whether a feature is compounding or plateauing.
  2. Use data to identify root causes when metrics move.
  3. Segment the issue by region, device, demographic, or use case.
  4. Apply targeted fixes and then re-measure.
- **Example**: Close Friends underperformed at first, and data revealed issues like mistranslation and broken list size.

## Core Advice
- **When a mature product seems stuck**: Look for the part still growing, then use that as the seed for the next growth driver — because growth often comes from amplifying an emerging behavior.
- **When users are forcing your product to do something it doesn’t yet do**: Treat that as unmet demand and build for it directly — because user workarounds are often the clearest product signal.
- **When a new feature feels confusing inside an existing product**: Separate it into a distinct experience — because forcing it into the core product usually creates friction.
- **When deciding whether to copy a successful pattern**: Borrow the format, but adapt it to your product’s essence — because great products are often recombinations, not pure inventions.
- **When a feature is underperforming and you don’t know why**: Do root-cause analysis instead of assuming the idea is wrong — because execution details can hide the real issue.
- **When building AI products**: Use natural language and tool access instead of relying heavily on prompt hacks or fine-tuning — because modern models can often infer intent well enough.
- **When creating content for AI search or AI answers**: Focus on being the best source for the user’s actual intent — because usefulness, originality, and intent match still matter.
- **When deciding how much to invest in a feature**: Use diminishing returns and S-curve thinking — because many features need a push to reach escape velocity, but not all deserve endless investment.
- **When launching a new product inside a large company**: Start with a small team to prove conviction, then scale once there is real user pull — because early focus and later resourcing solve different problems.
- **When tempted to optimize for clever design**: Choose the simplest, most legible design — because clarity creates adoption and cleverness often creates confusion.
- **When building something big from an existing product**: Make the new capability complementary, not a replacement — because users adopt extensions more easily than disruptions.
- **When trying to understand what users really need**: Ask why they used the product in the first place and what triggered the need — because the click is less important than the context.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: AI will kill traditional search because people no longer want links and search results. → **Their view**: Core search is not dying; AI expands the number and type of questions people ask — because AI increases curiosity and informational demand.
- **Conventional**: The best product teams should stay lean and scrappy for as long as possible. → **Their view**: Some hard technical breakthroughs need more resources — because under-investing can prevent a product from ever reaching quality.
- **Conventional**: Great products must be invented from scratch. → **Their view**: Learn from the world and adopt great formats when they fit — because borrowing can be a strength if the product becomes distinctly yours.
- **Conventional**: More features and cleverness make products better. → **Their view**: Clarity and simplicity usually win — because users adopt obvious products faster.
- **Conventional**: AI products require heavy prompt engineering or fine-tuning to be useful. → **Their view**: Plain language and tool instructions are often enough — because models are becoming better at understanding intent directly.
