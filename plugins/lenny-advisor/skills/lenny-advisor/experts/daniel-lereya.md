# Daniel Lereya

**Chief Product and Technology Officer** | **monday.com** | Expertise: leadership, product-management, go-to-market, engineering-management, strategy, metrics, operations

## Bio
Daniel Lereya is the Chief Product and Technology Officer at monday.com, where he joined when the company was still around 30–40 people and helped scale it from roughly $4M ARR to over $1B ARR. His perspective matters because he has led product, technology, and operating-model changes through extreme growth, and he consistently ties execution to measurable customer and business impact.

He is especially known for pushing radical transparency, impact-driven product management, and ambitious execution systems that force teams to rethink architecture, ownership, and speed.

## Signature Frameworks

### Impact-Driven Product Management
- **When to use**: When teams are shipping a lot but it’s unclear whether the work is actually changing customer behavior or business outcomes.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the customer problem or opportunity before discussing solutions.
  2. Specify what change you want for users.
  3. Define how you will know you moved the needle using measurable indicators.
  4. Align the whole team around the impact goal, not just the PM.
  5. Evaluate solutions based on whether they increase impact, including non-feature work like discoverability, access, or go-to-market enablement.
  6. Track the relevant numbers continuously and adjust based on real-world results.
- **Example**: With monday.com’s AI Blocks, the team saw strong product feedback but low adoption. By checking daily numbers, they found most customers couldn’t access the feature yet because terms-of-service changes were pending. Instead of building more features, they opened access and reached 98% of customers within two weeks.

### Ambitious Goal Reframing
- **When to use**: When execution is efficient but not transformative, or when teams are stuck optimizing the current way of working.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify a gap between your output and what is possible in the market.
  2. Set a goal so ambitious that the current process obviously cannot achieve it.
  3. Use the goal to force a redesign of architecture, process, and ownership.
  4. Strip away assumptions and define the reusable infrastructure needed.
  5. Execute in a concentrated burst with clear ownership.
- **Example**: After seeing a competitor launch 30 new column types while monday.com had only five and each took four months to build, the team set a goal of shipping 25 columns in one month. They created a common column architecture and ran a hackathon, leading to 30 columns in production about six weeks later.

### Time-Boxed Release Traps
- **When to use**: When teams keep expanding scope, delaying launches, and over-polishing before getting customer feedback.
- **Steps**:
  1. Set a fixed time constraint tied to a real milestone or date.
  2. Treat the deadline as non-negotiable and cut scope instead of extending time.
  3. Focus only on the core value needed to learn from users.
  4. Release early, even if the product is incomplete.
  5. Use customer feedback to guide the next iteration rather than trying to predict everything upfront.
- **Example**: For a new enterprise work management offering, the team released an early alpha even though it felt premature. Customers said it needed more comprehensive value, but the team got precise feedback on what was missing, which Daniel saw as a success.

### Radical Transparency Operating System
- **When to use**: When you want employees to think like owners, spot problems early, and align around company outcomes.
- **Steps**:
  1. Share core business and product metrics broadly across the company.
  2. Make dashboards visible in the office and in internal tools.
  3. Create a daily cadence where teams review the numbers they care about.
  4. Encourage anyone in the company to question changes in metrics.
  5. Preserve access to sensitive data as the company scales, while adding governance mechanisms if needed.
- **Example**: Before going public, monday.com displayed metrics like paying accounts, churn, and signups openly in the office, even for interview candidates. After going public, they built an internal app with role-based confidential access and used 10b5 plans so PMs could still see sensitive data.

### Future-Back Planning
- **When to use**: When teams need sharper prioritization and a clearer sense of what meaningful progress should look like over a quarter or year.
- **Steps**:
  1. Imagine a future point in time, such as next quarter or next year.
  2. Describe concretely what will be different and better for customers.
  3. Reject vague statements like “better performance” unless tied to customer value.
  4. Work backward from that future state into priorities and execution.
  5. Review progress against that future-facing statement regularly.
- **Example**: At yearly kickoff, Daniel uses a slide that asks, “When I’m going to stand here in a year from now, what is going to be different for our customers?” He then starts the next year’s presentation by revisiting the prior year’s slide.

### Strategic Problem-to-Advantage Conversion
- **When to use**: When a recurring technical or operational problem threatens customer experience and could become a long-term bottleneck.
- **Steps**:
  1. Recognize repeated incidents as a strategic issue, not just an operational one.
  2. Stop treating the issue as a series of patches.
  3. Assign strong talent to solve the root problem with a long-term horizon.
  4. Design for scale far beyond current needs.
  5. Turn the solution into a product or platform advantage.
- **Example**: After repeated board performance issues as customers pushed monday.com’s tables to much larger scale, the company created MondayDB, a new underlying data infrastructure. It pulled top talent away from feature work, but later became a major enabler of enterprise-grade scale.

## Core Advice
- **When a team is shipping a lot but cannot point to one transformative outcome**: do a forcing exercise and ask what will be meaningfully different for customers next quarter — because it exposes focus problems and shifts teams from activity to impact.
- **When a competitor does something you thought was impossible**: treat it as proof the bar is achievable — because it removes the excuse that it cannot be done and forces a rethink of architecture and process.
- **When pressure is to speed up delivery**: set goals so ambitious the current way of working cannot achieve them — because real speed comes from redesigning the system, not just working longer.
- **When planning is full of vague language like “enhance” or “extend”**: require exact user change and measurable success — because vague goals hide lack of clarity.
- **When qualitative feedback is strong but adoption is weak**: inspect the full funnel and remove access, discoverability, legal, or go-to-market blockers — because the biggest lever is often making existing value reachable.
- **When teams are disconnected from metrics**: create daily metric updates and visible dashboards — because frequent exposure builds ownership and surfaces anomalies quickly.
- **When worried that sharing bad company numbers will demoralize people**: share the numbers anyway and invite everyone into the challenge — because transparency creates partnership and broadens problem-solving.
- **When teams are endlessly polishing before launch**: use a hard time box and release the smallest version that can generate real feedback — because more time often means more assumptions and overbuilding.
- **When expanding into a new product area**: consider a bold coordinated move instead of only one cautious side bet — because a bigger move can reset perception faster.
- **When recurring infrastructure issues keep returning**: treat them as strategic investments and assign top talent — because foundational reliability can become a competitive advantage.
- **When growing into a much larger leadership role**: reevaluate whether your old strengths still serve the new job — because strengths like detail mastery can become bottlenecks at scale.
- **When self-doubt shows up in a first-time role**: assume it’s normal, stay vulnerable, and define how you want to grow over the next six months — because adaptability matters more than prior title match.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Sharing too much company information will demoralize employees and create risk. → **Their view**: Radical transparency strengthens partnership and improves problem-solving — because talented people want the real picture and can help more when they see it.
- **Conventional**: To move faster, teams should push harder or work longer. → **Their view**: Real speed comes from changing how you work — because ambitious goals should force redesign, not just effort.
- **Conventional**: Product impact mainly comes from shipping more features. → **Their view**: The highest-impact move is often making existing value more accessible or enabled — because access and go-to-market can matter more than new functionality.
- **Conventional**: More time before launch leads to a better product. → **Their view**: More time often creates more assumptions and complexity — because early release produces better learning.
- **Conventional**: New products should start as cautious side bets. → **Their view**: Sometimes a bold multi-product launch is the right move — because it can create a step-change in perception and learning.
- **Conventional**: Leaders should double down on the strengths that got them promoted. → **Their view**: Your biggest strengths can become liabilities as the company scales — because the job changes and the old operating style may no longer fit.
