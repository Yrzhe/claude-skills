# Molly Graham

**Founder and leader of Glue Club; advisor to leaders navigating rapid scale and change** | **Glue Club** | Expertise: leadership, growth, hiring-culture, operations, strategy

## Bio
Molly Graham is a veteran operator who helped scale Google, Facebook, Quip, and the Chan Zuckerberg Initiative through periods of intense growth and change. Her perspective matters because she has lived the messy middle of scaling: handing off work, redesigning teams, clarifying goals, and helping people grow faster than the company around them.

She now leads Glue Club, where she helps leaders deal with ambiguity, organizational change, and the emotional side of growth. Her advice is especially useful for founders, managers, and operators who need practical tools for scaling without losing clarity or culture.

## Signature Frameworks

### Give Away Your Legos
- **When to use**: When your company is growing faster than your current role and you feel attached to work you’ve become good at.
- **Steps**:
  1. Recognize that your job is expanding as fast as the company is growing.
  2. Accept that the work you are good at will eventually need to be handed off.
  3. Pass the current “house” to someone else and move to the next level of work.
  4. Repeat the cycle as scope expands from houses to neighborhoods to cities and beyond.
  5. Treat the emotional discomfort as normal, but not as a reason to cling.
- **Example**: At Facebook, Molly said she was sometimes “giving away my job every three weeks” as the company scaled.

### Bob the Monster
- **When to use**: When change triggers fear, territoriality, rage, or imposter syndrome and you need to avoid reacting emotionally.
- **Steps**:
  1. Externalize the emotional reaction into a named character or monster.
  2. Notice when “Bob” is trying to make you act like the worst version of yourself.
  3. Do not treat the emotion as a reliable compass for action.
  4. Wait and observe whether the feeling persists beyond the initial wave.
  5. If the feeling lasts more than two weeks, discuss it with a manager, friend, or coach.
- **Example**: Molly named her monster “Bob” to represent the urge to send rage emails or grab back work she had handed off.

### J-Curve Career Growth vs. Stairs
- **When to use**: When deciding whether to take a risky role or stretch assignment that may temporarily make you worse before making you much better.
- **Steps**:
  1. Reject the default “stairs” model of incremental promotions.
  2. Choose a role that is meaningfully beyond your current expertise.
  3. Expect a fall period of roughly six to nine months where you feel incompetent.
  4. Use the fall to learn deeply and build new capabilities.
  5. Climb out of the curve with skills and scope that stairs could not provide.
- **Example**: Molly left HR at Facebook to work on mobile with Chamath Palihapitiya, spent months feeling like an idiot, then became an expert in mobile.

### Waterline Model
- **When to use**: When a team is underperforming or confused and you need to diagnose the real source of the problem.
- **Steps**:
  1. Treat the team as a boat trying to reach goals.
  2. Start diagnosis above the waterline: goals, vision, roles, and expectations.
  3. Then inspect team dynamics: decision-making, conflict, and culture.
  4. Only then move to interpersonal issues and individual behavior.
  5. Use the rule “snorkel before you scuba” to avoid over-focusing on people before structure.
- **Example**: Molly often finds that a team member’s performance issue is actually caused by unclear goals or unclear role expectations.

### Goal Setting as Communication
- **When to use**: When setting company or team goals in a fast-changing environment.
- **Steps**:
  1. Limit company goals to no more than three top-level goals.
  2. Make one goal clearly win in a tradeoff.
  3. Write goals so an intern can understand them without translation.
  4. Make tradeoffs painful enough that strategy actually forces prioritization.
  5. Assign exactly one owner to each goal.
  6. Create a follow-up process to review, learn from, and hold people accountable to goals.
- **Example**: Facebook used three goals for years: growth, engagement, and revenue, with engagement winning when priorities conflicted.

## Core Advice
- **When your company is scaling and your current work starts to feel like “your thing”**: give away the work you’ve gotten good at and move to the next pile of Legos — because the company will outgrow you if you cling to one scope.
- **When you feel rage, fear, or territoriality after a change like layering or a reorg**: name the feeling as “Bob,” do not act on it immediately, and wait to see if it persists — because the first emotional wave is normal but usually not useful.
- **When you’re considering a stretch role that scares you**: ask whether the fear is financial risk or capability fear; only take the leap if the finances are workable — because financial anxiety should be modeled explicitly, while fear of failure is often a signal to learn by trying.
- **When you are offered work that is far outside your current expertise**: take the job if it is a true stretch and you can afford the downside — because stretch roles create the fastest self-knowledge.
- **When you are in the falling phase of a J-curve and feel like a professional idiot**: lean into being a “professional idiot” and ask the dumb questions out loud — because dumb questions reveal hidden assumptions and accelerate learning.
- **When a team is struggling and people immediately blame individuals**: start by checking goals, roles, and expectations before diagnosing people problems — because most team issues come from structural confusion rather than bad intent.
- **When a direct report seems underperforming**: ask what they think their job is and what number they were hired to drive — because their mental model may differ from yours.
- **When setting goals for a company or team**: keep the top-level goals to three or fewer and make them understandable to a new hire — because goals are a communication tool, not a spreadsheet.
- **When priorities conflict and everything feels important**: make strategy hurt by explicitly choosing what will not get done — because painless prioritization is not real prioritization.
- **When a goal has multiple owners or vague accountability**: assign one owner per goal and put one name next to each number — because shared ownership often becomes no ownership.
- **When you’re tempted to promise a new hire stability, titles, or a smooth onboarding**: only promise what you can control and be explicit about uncertainty — because broken promises create resentment.
- **When you’re deciding whether to spend time on a struggling employee or a high performer**: invest disproportionately in high performers and use experiments to expand their scope — because they are the future of the company.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Career growth should be a steady climb up the ladder with predictable promotions. → **Their view**: The best careers are J-curves: jump off cliffs, fall for a while, then climb to a much higher level than stairs could reach. — because stretch roles create rapid learning.
- **Conventional**: Leaders should have all the answers. → **Their view**: A leader’s job is to get good at finding answers and bringing people together to find them. — because in fast-changing environments, nobody knows the answers in advance.
- **Conventional**: Culture can be intentionally designed through values exercises. → **Their view**: About 80% of culture is defined by the founder’s personality, and culture is what people do, not what they write down. — because actions and decisions matter more than posters.
- **Conventional**: More headcount is the solution to growth problems. → **Their view**: Growing headcount faster than 100% a year is usually a bad idea and often makes things slower. — because coordination overhead rises faster than clarity.
- **Conventional**: Escalation is a sign of failure or tattling. → **Their view**: Escalation is a management tool. — because it saves time and uses management for its intended purpose.
- **Conventional**: Hiring is the main people-management skill; firing is a separate unpleasant exception. → **Their view**: Firing is as important as hiring, and being good at it is a core leadership skill. — because misfits become barnacles that drag the company down.
