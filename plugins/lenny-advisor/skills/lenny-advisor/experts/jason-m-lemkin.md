# Jason M Lemkin

**Founder and CEO of SaaStr** | **SaaStr** | Expertise: B2B sales, go-to-market, SaaS scaling, leadership

## Bio
Jason Lemkin is the founder of SaaStr, one of the largest communities for SaaS and B2B founders and operators. He previously co-founded EchoSign, scaled it past $100M ARR, and sold it to Adobe, so his perspective comes from building real revenue engines, not just advising on them.

What makes Jason especially valuable is his insistence on matching sales strategy to actual customer behavior. He is strongest when you need blunt, practical guidance on early sales hiring, founder-led selling, and how to scale a B2B company without losing touch with customers.

## Signature Frameworks

### Founder-led sales before hiring sales
- **When to use**: When you’re early-stage and deciding whether to hire your first sales reps.
- **Steps**:
  1. Close the first 10 customers yourself as the founder.
  2. Use your advantage as a “middler” who can explain the product and market better than most reps.
  3. Observe how customers actually buy: self-serve, sales-assisted, or hybrid.
  4. Be honest about the real buying motion instead of forcing PLG if customers need help, onboarding, security reviews, or deployment support.
  5. Once sales consumes more than roughly 20% of your time, hire for leverage.
- **Example**: Jason described a company that avoided sales because it felt “icky,” then fell from $5M in revenue to under $1M after firing the sales team.

### Hire two reps, not one
- **When to use**: When making your first sales hires after founder-led sales starts working.
- **Steps**:
  1. Wait until you have enough traction to know there is a real sales motion.
  2. Hire two reps at the same time so you can compare performance.
  3. Interview a large pool, often around 30 candidates.
  4. Prioritize candidates you would personally buy your own product from.
  5. Look for maturity, relevant experience, and comfort operating with little onboarding.
- **Example**: Jason said his first great rep had been let go elsewhere and was living in his brother’s garage, but became the person who closed their first five-, six-, and seven-figure deals.

### Would I buy from them? sales hiring test
- **When to use**: When evaluating early sales hires or filtering candidates with impressive logos but unclear fit.
- **Steps**:
  1. Ignore resume prestige as the primary signal.
  2. Ask whether this person truly understands your product and customer problem.
  3. Have them demo or sell your product in the interview process.
  4. Assess whether they can explain the problem, value, objections, and next steps credibly.
  5. Choose the candidate you would trust with scarce early leads.
- **Example**: Jason warned against hiring someone just because they worked at Twilio or Cloudflare if you wouldn’t actually buy your product from them.

### Sell me this app
- **When to use**: When interviewing sales candidates, especially early AEs and sales leaders.
- **Steps**:
  1. Give the candidate time to prepare.
  2. Ask them to demo or sell your actual product, not a generic object.
  3. Expect them to research your website, explainer videos, webinars, and customer materials.
  4. Evaluate whether they frame the sale as solving a real problem rather than using manipulative tactics.
  5. Look for confidence, product understanding, and a clear next step.
- **Example**: Jason said candidates should at minimum have watched the explainer video or webinar and be able to sell the core problem the app solves.

### Two reps hitting quota before VP of Sales
- **When to use**: When deciding whether you’re ready to hire a VP of Sales.
- **Steps**:
  1. Do not hire a VP of Sales before you have at least two reps closing deals and hitting quota.
  2. Use the founder as the de facto head of sales while the motion is still being figured out.
  3. Wait until there is a repeatable script, understood objections, and some lead flow.
  4. Then hire a VP of Sales to scale from rep 3 to 300, not invent the motion from scratch.
- **Example**: Jason said hiring a VP of Sales too early is a Hail Mary that usually burns cash and time.

### 14-day customer-first interview test for leaders
- **When to use**: When interviewing VP-level leaders in sales or product.
- **Steps**:
  1. Ask what they want to do in their first 14 days.
  2. Listen for a strong desire to meet customers or prospects immediately.
  3. Reject candidates who focus first on process, dashboards, territory planning, or internal meetings.
  4. Use this as a proxy for whether they still care about the craft and the customer.
- **Example**: Jason said the best product leaders say, “Leave me alone. I’m going to go talk to 20 customers.”

### Sales feature-request budget
- **When to use**: When product and sales are fighting over roadmap priorities.
- **Steps**:
  1. Allocate sales a fixed budget each quarter, such as story points or a percentage of roadmap capacity.
  2. Review that budget weekly between the VP of Sales and VP of Product.
  3. Force-rank requests rather than reacting to the loudest deal of the week.
  4. Allow changes, but make the disruption cost explicit.
  5. Push roadmap decisions up to leadership rather than letting ICs negotiate ad hoc.
- **Example**: Jason suggested giving sales about 10% of roadmap capacity so they can choose between requests like HubSpot, SAP, or Salesforce integrations without thrashing the team.

### Rules of eight for sales org design
- **When to use**: When scaling a sales organization beyond the first few reps.
- **Steps**:
  1. Use roughly eight SDRs per manager.
  2. Use roughly eight AEs per manager/director.
  3. Add directors under the VP once direct reports approach eight.
  4. Continue layering management in groups of about eight as the org grows.
- **Example**: Jason described building the sales org around “rules of eight” as a simple scaling heuristic.

## Core Advice
- **When you’re unsure whether you need sales**: Study how your first 10–20 customers actually buy, and follow that motion honestly — because customer behavior, not ideology, should determine your GTM.
- **When you’re about to hire your first salesperson**: Close about 10 customers yourself first, then hire two reps instead of one — because founder-led sales creates the playbook and two reps give you a real comparison.
- **When you’re staring at impressive resumes**: Pick the person you would buy your own product from — because early sales success depends more on trust and product fluency than pedigree.
- **When interviewing sales candidates**: Ask them to sell your actual product after giving them time to prepare — because this reveals whether they can learn, explain, and guide a buyer.
- **When you feel pressure to hire a VP of Sales early**: Wait until at least two reps are hitting quota — because a VP should scale a working motion, not invent one.
- **When hiring a VP of Sales in an early company**: Choose someone who still wants to be in deals and with customers — because distance from the market kills judgment.
- **When setting comp for early reps**: Pay market OTE and make the first quarter ramp-friendly — because the goal is productive reps who can eventually return 3x–5x their cost.
- **When your best rep earns more than the founders**: Don’t panic — because that usually means the company is making money and equity value is compounding.
- **When product and sales fight over roadmap**: Give sales a fixed quarterly budget and review it weekly — because explicit tradeoffs are healthier than constant thrash.
- **When scaling the org**: Add management in roughly groups of eight — because it keeps span of control sane.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: If you’re product-led, you may never need sales. → **Their view**: Most B2B companies eventually need some form of sales, and many need it much earlier than founders expect — because real buyers often need help, not just a checkout flow.
- **Conventional**: Hire a VP of Sales early to figure it out. → **Their view**: Don’t hire a VP until two reps are already hitting quota — because the VP’s job is to scale, not discover.
- **Conventional**: The best early rep comes from a famous company. → **Their view**: The best early rep is the one you’d personally buy from — because early selling is about credibility and adaptability.
- **Conventional**: Sales is persuasion and closing tactics. → **Their view**: In B2B SaaS, sales is solving problems and helping buyers make the right decision — because good software is not a commodity.
- **Conventional**: Senior sales leaders should focus on process and dashboards. → **Their view**: Great leaders stay close to deals and customers — because distance from the market leads to weak judgment.
- **Conventional**: Shorter trials and aggressive terms are always better. → **Their view**: Customer-friendly trials and flexible terms can be better long term — because trust and adoption compound.
- **Conventional**: Product should be tightly weaponized for revenue. → **Their view**: Be careful not to optimize product in ways that hurt customers — because short-term monetization can damage retention and brand trust.
