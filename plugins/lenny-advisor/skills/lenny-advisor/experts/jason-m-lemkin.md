# Jason M Lemkin

**Founder and CEO of SaaStr** | **SaaStr** | Expertise: B2B sales, go-to-market, SaaS scaling, leadership

## Bio
Jason Lemkin is the founder of SaaStr, one of the largest communities for SaaS and B2B founders and operators. He previously co-founded EchoSign, scaled it past $100M ARR, and sold it to Adobe, so his advice comes from having built and sold a real SaaS business, not just observed one.

His perspective matters most when a B2B company is trying to find its true sales motion, make its first sales hires, or scale from founder-led selling into a repeatable revenue engine. He is especially useful for founders who are tempted to over-index on process, pedigree, or product-led ideology before the customer reality is clear.

## Signature Frameworks

### Founder-led sales before hiring sales
- **When to use**: When you’re early-stage and deciding whether to hire your first sales reps.
- **Steps**:
  1. Close the first 10 customers yourself as the founder.
  2. Learn how customers actually buy.
  3. Identify whether the motion is self-serve, sales-assisted, or hybrid.
  4. Be honest about customer needs like onboarding, security, and deployment.
  5. Hire for leverage once sales takes more than ~20% of your time.
- **Example**: Jason described a company that avoided sales because it felt “icky,” then fell from $5M in revenue to under $1M after firing the sales team.

### Hire two reps, not one
- **When to use**: When founder-led sales is working and you’re ready for your first sales hires.
- **Steps**:
  1. Confirm there is a real sales motion.
  2. Hire two reps at the same time.
  3. Interview a broad pool, often around 30 candidates.
  4. Prioritize people you’d personally buy from.
  5. Look for maturity and enough experience to operate with little onboarding.
- **Example**: Jason shared that one of his best early reps had been let go elsewhere and was living in his brother’s garage, yet became the person who closed their first five-, six-, and seven-figure deals.

### Would I buy from them? sales hiring test
- **When to use**: When evaluating early sales candidates with strong resumes but uncertain fit.
- **Steps**:
  1. Ignore prestige as the main signal.
  2. Ask whether they truly understand your product and customer problem.
  3. Have them demo or sell your product in the interview.
  4. Assess whether they can explain value, objections, and next steps.
  5. Choose the person you’d trust with scarce early leads.
- **Example**: Jason warned against hiring someone just because they worked at Twilio or Cloudflare if you wouldn’t actually buy your product from them.

### Sell me this app
- **When to use**: When interviewing sales candidates, especially early AEs and sales leaders.
- **Steps**:
  1. Give them time to prepare.
  2. Ask them to sell your actual product.
  3. Expect them to research your site, videos, webinars, and customer materials.
  4. Evaluate whether they solve a real problem rather than use tricks.
  5. Look for confidence and a clear next step.
- **Example**: Jason said candidates should at minimum have watched the explainer video or webinar and be able to sell the core problem the app solves.

### Two reps hitting quota before VP of Sales
- **When to use**: When deciding whether you’re ready to hire a VP of Sales.
- **Steps**:
  1. Wait until at least two reps are closing and hitting quota.
  2. Keep the founder as the de facto sales leader until then.
  3. Make sure the script and objections are becoming repeatable.
  4. Hire a VP of Sales to scale a working motion, not invent one.
- **Example**: Jason said hiring a VP of Sales too early is a Hail Mary that usually burns cash and time.

### 14-day customer-first interview test for leaders
- **When to use**: When interviewing VP-level sales or product leaders.
- **Steps**:
  1. Ask what they’d do in their first 14 days.
  2. Listen for immediate customer or prospect conversations.
  3. Reject candidates who lead with dashboards, process, or territory planning.
  4. Use this to judge whether they still care about the craft.
- **Example**: The strongest product leaders say, “Leave me alone. I’m going to go talk to 20 customers.”

### Sales feature-request budget
- **When to use**: When sales and product are fighting over roadmap priorities.
- **Steps**:
  1. Allocate sales a fixed quarterly budget.
  2. Review it weekly between VP Sales and VP Product.
  3. Force-rank requests instead of reacting to the loudest deal.
  4. Make the cost of disruption explicit.
  5. Escalate decisions to leadership, not ICs.
- **Example**: Jason suggested giving sales something like 10% of roadmap capacity so requests like HubSpot, SAP, or Salesforce integrations can be prioritized intentionally.

### Rules of eight for sales org design
- **When to use**: When scaling beyond the first few reps.
- **Steps**:
  1. Use about eight SDRs per manager.
  2. Use about eight AEs per manager/director.
  3. Add directors as direct reports approach eight.
  4. Continue layering management in groups of about eight.
- **Example**: Jason described this as a simple heuristic for when to add managers and directors.

## Core Advice
- **When you’re unsure whether you need sales**: Follow how your first 10–20 customers actually buy — because customer behavior, not ideology, determines the motion.
- **When sales is taking over your time**: Hire once it’s more than ~20% of your workload — because that’s the point where leverage matters.
- **When hiring your first reps**: Hire two, not one — because you need a comparison set, not a single data point.
- **When choosing between sales candidates**: Pick the person you’d buy from — because trust and product fluency matter more than logos early on.
- **When interviewing early salespeople**: Make them sell your real product — because it reveals preparation, understanding, and judgment.
- **When tempted to hire a VP of Sales early**: Don’t — because a VP should scale a motion, not discover it.
- **When hiring senior leaders**: Ask what they’ll do in their first 14 days — because customer contact should come before internal process.
- **When product and sales clash on roadmap**: Give sales a fixed budget — because ad hoc deal-driven requests create chaos.
- **When scaling the org**: Use the “rules of eight” — because it gives a simple, practical management structure.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: If you’re product-led, you may never need sales. → **Their view**: Most B2B companies eventually need some form of sales, often much earlier than founders expect — because real buyers often need help, trust, and coordination.
- **Conventional**: Hire a VP of Sales to figure it out. → **Their view**: Wait until two reps are already hitting quota — because a VP is for scaling, not inventing.
- **Conventional**: The best early hire has the strongest resume. → **Their view**: The best early hire is the person you’d personally buy from — because early selling is about credibility and adaptability.
- **Conventional**: Sales is persuasion and closing tricks. → **Their view**: Sales is solving problems and helping buyers make the right decision — because good software should be sold honestly.
- **Conventional**: Senior sales leaders should focus on process. → **Their view**: Great leaders stay close to customers and deals — because distance weakens judgment.
- **Conventional**: Shorter trials and tighter monetization are always better. → **Their view**: Customer-friendly terms often win long term — because trust and adoption compound.
- **Conventional**: Product should be tightly weaponized for revenue. → **Their view**: Be careful not to optimize product in ways that hurt customers — because short-term monetization can damage retention and brand.
