# Patrick Campbell

**Founder and former CEO** | **ProfitWell** | Expertise: pricing, retention, bootstrapping, team-building, growth, strategy, go-to-market

## Bio
Patrick Campbell founded ProfitWell and bootstrapped it to a $200M+ exit, building a reputation for unusually data-driven thinking on SaaS pricing, retention, and growth. His perspective matters because he combines operator experience with a first-principles, contrarian approach to company building, especially where most teams are vague: pricing, culture, and monetization.

## Signature Frameworks

### Mission Metric + Guiding Principles + Tempo Framework
- **When to use**: When building a team or scaling execution across functions and you need alignment on what good looks like.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the mission metric that matters most to the company.
  2. Set guiding principles for how the company will get there.
  3. Establish what good looks like in terms of shipping tempo for each function.
  4. Have leaders translate the company-level tempo into function-specific frameworks.
  5. Use recurring leadership conversations to close the gap between current output and expected tempo.
- **Example**: At ProfitWell, the mission metric was revenue on the platform, the guiding principle was being the most helpful brand in SaaS, and each org had explicit shipping expectations like how many launches per month or quarter.

### Problem → Cause → Solution
- **When to use**: When doing first-principles thinking, strategy work, or diagnosing a messy problem.
- **Steps**:
  1. State the problem clearly.
  2. Break the problem into its underlying causes.
  3. Rank causes by magnitude and importance.
  4. Map solutions to the biggest causes first.
  5. Use the framework to align teams and make the approach actionable.
- **Example**: For a growth problem in a metrics product, the causes included low willingness to pay, accuracy challenges, and market stance; the solution became freemium plus pay-for-performance products.

### Quarterly Pricing Improvement Loop
- **When to use**: When managing SaaS pricing and monetization over time.
- **Steps**:
  1. Track revenue per customer as the core pricing KPI.
  2. Pick one pricing change to test each quarter.
  3. Use a pricing committee to make decisions and reduce ambiguity.
  4. Consider levers such as price, packaging, add-ons, discounts, localization, and freemium.
  5. Measure the impact and repeat every quarter.
- **Example**: Patrick recommended setting a recurring quarterly calendar invite to force one pricing action every three months.

### Strategic vs Tactical Retention
- **When to use**: When trying to reduce churn after product-market fit.
- **Steps**:
  1. Separate strategic retention work from tactical retention work.
  2. Use product strategy to improve ICP fit, time to value, roadmap, and core features.
  3. Fix tactical churn drivers like payment failures, cancellation flows, offboarding, and term optimization.
  4. Use cancellation data to identify salvage opportunities.
  5. Route tactical retention ownership to finance or operations when product is focused elsewhere.
- **Example**: Patrick said tactical retention can account for 25-40% of churn and can often be improved in about two months with better payment recovery and cancellation flows.

### Customer Development Operating System
- **When to use**: When you need better positioning, product decisions, or growth efficiency.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define ICPs or buyer personas.
  2. Commit to a regular cadence of customer conversations or surveys.
  3. Use research to understand how customers perceive the problem, the product, and the market.
  4. Combine qualitative insight with other business data before making decisions.
  5. Repeat continuously rather than treating research as a one-time project.
- **Example**: He suggested a simple target like 10 non-sales customer conversations per month or one well-designed survey.

### Middle-of-Funnel Pool Building
- **When to use**: When top-of-funnel and bottom-of-funnel efficiency are getting worse and you need more durable demand.
- **Steps**:
  1. Create a pool of people who are aware of you and interacting with your product or content.
  2. Use freemium or product-led entry points to let prospects engage on their own timeline.
  3. Build inbound media like podcasts and video series to keep people in the middle of the funnel.
  4. Use the product itself as content and proof.
  5. Let timing mature naturally before pushing to conversion.
- **Example**: ProfitWell used freemium plus niche podcasts like Pricing Page Tear Down and Boxed Out to build an audience that later converted when the timing was right.

## Core Advice
- **When defining company values**: Make each value include a real trade-off and explicitly state who the company is and is not for — because values only matter if they create clear decision boundaries.
- **When hiring or interviewing**: Pull culture and operating trade-offs into the interview process early — because it is better for both sides to know upfront whether the company’s way of working fits.
- **When team members have conflict**: Teach and expect the most charitable interpretation or assume good intent before escalating — because it reduces unnecessary friction and keeps people focused on solving problems.
- **When deciding whether to bootstrap or raise money**: Choose the funding model that matches the business model and market size — because prestige is irrelevant if the path to scale does not fit.
- **When unsure if a startup is venture-scale**: Ask whether the business can plausibly reach about $1B in annual revenue — because if not, it may be a great business but not a VC business.
- **When thinking about pricing**: Do one meaningful pricing action every quarter — because pricing improves through consistent iteration and most teams ignore it.
- **When choosing a pricing lever**: Start by getting the value metric right — because it drives fairness, expansion, and lower churn more than most other choices.
- **When pricing politics are blocking progress**: Start with a straightforward annual price increase if the product is healthy and NPS is acceptable — because it forces the organization to confront data and messaging.
- **When trying to reduce churn**: Treat payment failures, cancellation flows, and offboarding as first-class retention work — because these issues can represent a large share of churn and are often fixable quickly.
- **When a customer clicks cancel**: Ask why they are leaving and what they liked about the product — because you capture churn reasons and interrupt cancellation momentum.
- **When building product or GTM strategy**: Do regular customer development and maintain ICPs or personas — because companies that do this tend to have better NPS, willingness to pay, funnel efficiency, LTV/CAC, and retention.
- **When you need more efficient demand generation**: Invest in the middle of the funnel with freemium, product-led engagement, and inbound media — because top and bottom funnel efficiency are getting worse.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Team is everything, so the main job is to keep everyone happy. → **Their view**: Team is about alignment to the mission and clear standards for behavior and output — because vague values create accommodation culture and low accountability.
- **Conventional**: Bootstrapping is the superior path for most founders. → **Their view**: Bootstrapping can be a mistake if the goal is to build a very large business — because capital can help you move faster when the market supports it.
- **Conventional**: Pricing should be revisited only when there is a major reason. → **Their view**: Pricing should be touched every quarter, with yearly increases for healthy products — because monetization is one of the core growth levers.
- **Conventional**: Retention is mostly a product problem. → **Their view**: A large chunk of churn is tactical and operational — because payment recovery, cancellation flows, and offboarding can materially reduce churn.
- **Conventional**: Don’t focus on competitors; just focus on the customer. → **Their view**: Competitor awareness is essential in dense markets — because customers are already comparing alternatives.
- **Conventional**: Top-of-funnel is the main growth battleground. → **Their view**: The middle of the funnel is the biggest opportunity now — because a pool of engaged prospects converts better over time.
