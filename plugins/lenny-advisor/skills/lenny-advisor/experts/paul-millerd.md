# Paul Millerd

**Author, writer, podcaster, and founder of StrategyU** | **StrategyU / Pathlesspath.com** | Expertise: career design, work philosophy, personal reinvention, creator economy

## Bio
Paul Millerd is the author of *The Pathless Path*, a book about redefining work, success, and belonging outside the default career script. He writes, podcasts, and teaches around unconventional career paths, helping people explore work that feels energizing and sustainable.

His perspective matters because he doesn’t just critique traditional career advice — he has lived through the uncertainty of stepping off the “normal” path and building a more self-directed one. He’s especially useful for people who feel successful on paper but disconnected in practice.

## Signature Frameworks

### The Default Path vs. Pathless Path
- **When to use**: When you want to evaluate whether your career and life are being driven by conscious choice or inherited scripts.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the default story you were taught about success and work.
  2. Notice the implicit contracts you've made with yourself about how much you must work.
  3. Ask what trade-offs and costs your current path creates.
  4. Decide whether you are opting in consciously or just following the script.
  5. If desired, remix the path rather than abandoning everything.
- **Example**: A friend realized he was getting three of his four priorities from his job and used that awareness to plan a future change instead of quitting immediately.

### Work Mindfulness Experiment
- **When to use**: When you feel stuck, numb, or unsure what kind of work actually fits you.
- **Steps**:
  1. Block off a few hours during a workday.
  2. Leave without a destination or do an activity from childhood.
  3. Pay attention to what emotions come up, especially guilt or resistance.
  4. Notice what activities energize you versus drain you.
  5. Use those signals to infer what work and identity scripts you may be carrying.
- **Example**: Paul suggests taking three hours during a workday to go for a walk or play basketball, paint, or an instrument and observe what emerges.

### Ship, Quit, and Learn
- **When to use**: When exploring new work directions without wanting to commit to a big irreversible leap.
- **Steps**:
  1. Create the smallest version of the thing you want to test.
  2. Set a clear limit or end point up front.
  3. Ship it quickly.
  4. Treat the project as an experiment designed to be quit.
  5. Use the experience to learn what gives you energy and what you should avoid.
- **Example**: Paul started his podcast by committing to five episodes, then continued because the conversations energized him.

### Energy Audit for Career Decisions
- **When to use**: When deciding whether to pursue a side project, job, or business idea.
- **Steps**:
  1. Track how you feel before and after each activity.
  2. Separate activities that energize you from those that de-energize you.
  3. Do more of the energizing work and less of the draining work.
  4. Use repeated energy signals as evidence of what to pursue.
  5. Avoid creating a job for yourself that you hate.
- **Example**: Lenny realized advising calls drained him while writing energized him, so he shifted toward writing.

### Fear Setting and Cost of Inaction
- **When to use**: When fear is preventing you from making a career or life change.
- **Steps**:
  1. Write down the fears you have about the change.
  2. Think through how you could mitigate each fear.
  3. Ask what the cost of not acting is.
  4. Reframe fear as something to be managed rather than eliminated.
  5. Take action even if the fear remains.
- **Example**: Paul realized that not hiring help would drain his creative energy, which was a bigger risk than the fear of creating a job for someone else.

### Create Slack to Enable Reinvention
- **When to use**: When you need room to explore new work without blowing up your finances or responsibilities.
- **Steps**:
  1. Reduce expenses or lower your cost of living.
  2. Create a runway or budget for exploration.
  3. Use sabbaticals, contract work, grants, or other flexible arrangements.
  4. Protect time for reflection and experimentation.
  5. Let the exploration reveal the next path.
- **Example**: Paul lowered his cost of living in Asia to about $1,000/month so he could explore writing and other creative work.

## Core Advice
- **When you feel trapped in a career script**: Ask whether you are consciously choosing your path or just accepting the default — because many people are following inherited expectations without examining the trade-offs or implicit contracts they have made with themselves.
- **When you want to understand your relationship to work**: Take three hours during a workday to leave your normal routine and do something from childhood or a walk without a destination — because disrupting the workday reveals guilt, resistance, and buried assumptions about what work means to you.
- **When you are unsure what kind of work fits you**: Track what energizes you after each activity and do more of that, while reducing what drains you — because energy is a reliable signal of alignment.
- **When you think you need a huge leap to change careers**: Run small experiments first, then quit or expand based on what you learn — because Paul’s path emerged from side experiments like writing, coaching, and a five-episode podcast test.
- **When you are afraid of leaving a stable job**: Use fear setting: list fears, think through mitigations, and compare them to the cost of inaction — because naming the downside of staying put often makes the current situation look riskier than it first appears.
- **When you need time to explore but have bills and responsibilities**: Create a runway by lowering expenses, moving somewhere cheaper, or using a sabbatical/contract arrangement — because a smaller burn rate makes experimentation possible without requiring a dramatic all-or-nothing move.
- **When you are considering a sabbatical**: Aim for at least 6–8 weeks, and ideally around three months, to fully unwind and reconnect with yourself — because it takes weeks just to unwind from work mode before deeper clarity emerges.
- **When you are worried about what others will think of your unconventional path**: Prepare a simple, socially legible explanation for what you are doing — because a “boomer compatible story” or saying you’re “tinkering” can reduce friction while you explore.
- **When you are tempted to create a job for yourself that you don’t enjoy**: Protect your time and say no to work that doesn’t fit your energy or interests — because the pathless path only works if you avoid recreating the same draining structure in a new form.
- **When you are deciding whether to hire or delegate**: Hire people who are energized by the work, even if they are not the obvious choice on paper — because motivated people tend to do better work.
- **When you are trying to make money from creative work**: Focus on sustainability and intrinsic fit rather than maximizing short-term income — because work that fits you can become more durable and eventually more lucrative.
- **When you are afraid of regret**: Treat inaction as the bigger risk and remember that people usually regret what they didn’t do — because mistakes can be corrected, but missed opportunities are harder to recover.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: A successful career should be a smooth, upward trajectory in a stable full-time job. → **Their view**: Most people are already on a pathless path because life and work are constantly disrupted — because health, relationships, layoffs, inflation, and company changes constantly reshape reality.
- **Conventional**: The goal of work exploration is to find a job, build a business, or hit a measurable outcome. → **Their view**: The real goal is to find work you want to keep doing — because liking the work is more durable than chasing an external endpoint.
- **Conventional**: A sabbatical is a luxury or a risky detour that most people cannot afford. → **Their view**: A three-month sabbatical is more attainable than people think and often reveals important truths — because companies are often open to it and people can usually find a way.
- **Conventional**: Leaving a full-time job is irresponsible or shameful. → **Their view**: The shame is largely a social script — because many people feel better after testing an unconventional path, even if they return to traditional work.
- **Conventional**: You should maximize income and prestige when choosing work. → **Their view**: Coming alive matters more than getting ahead — because extrinsic outcomes did not deliver much for Paul, while internal energy did.
- **Conventional**: Podcasting is too crowded to be worth starting. → **Their view**: There is still plenty of room, and the best reason to podcast is the creative act itself — because the medium is valuable even if it never makes money.
