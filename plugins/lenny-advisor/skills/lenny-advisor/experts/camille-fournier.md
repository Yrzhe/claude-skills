# Camille Fournier

**Technology executive and author; former CTO of Rent the Runway** | **Independent / formerly Rent the Runway** | Expertise: engineering-management, leadership, product-management, operations

## Bio
Camille Fournier is a highly respected engineering leader and author of *The Manager’s Path*, known for practical, grounded advice on how technical organizations actually work. Her perspective matters because she has led at the highest levels of engineering while staying unusually focused on the human systems around software: trust, ownership, product thinking, and operational discipline.

She is especially valuable when teams are struggling with PM-engineering friction, platform team design, or the transition from strong IC to credible manager. Camille’s advice tends to be direct, anti-theoretical, and shaped by real organizational tradeoffs.

## Signature Frameworks

### How PMs can be less annoying to engineers
- **When to use**: When product managers want to build stronger trust and collaboration with engineering teams
- **Steps**:
  1. Share credit visibly instead of acting like the sole owner of wins.
  2. Respect technical details even if you do not personally need to understand every implementation nuance.
  3. Avoid becoming a constant middle-person in technical conversations; connect the right people directly when needed.
  4. Invite engineers into ideation so they have creative ownership over the product, not just the implementation.
- **Example**: An engineer is frustrated because a PM keeps relaying questions back and forth between stakeholders and the team. Camille would advise the PM to connect the stakeholders directly with the engineer, acknowledge the engineer’s contribution publicly, and bring them into early product brainstorming.

### Staged system evolution instead of full rewrites
- **When to use**: When a legacy system is painful to work with and teams are proposing a rewrite
- **Steps**:
  1. Question whether the system truly needs major change or is merely annoying to engineers.
  2. Identify the specific subsystem or capability that actually needs improvement.
  3. Create a staged migration plan around bounded components rather than replacing everything at once.
  4. Account explicitly for migration complexity, legacy support, undocumented logic, and user/data transition costs.
  5. Continue delivering business value while evolving the system incrementally.
- **Example**: Instead of rewriting an entire web framework, a team improves the recommendation system behind a stable API and migrates piece by piece.

### Technical mastery before management
- **When to use**: When an engineer is considering moving into engineering management but wants to remain technically credible
- **Steps**:
  1. Stay hands-on long enough to develop deep technical mastery that feels durable and reusable.
  2. Do not move into management the first time the role is offered if you still have significant growth left as an IC.
  3. Once in management, stay technically relevant by asking good questions rather than dictating implementation details.
  4. Maintain a network of strong hands-on engineers and keep learning through them.
- **Example**: Camille suggests a rough heuristic closer to 10 years of meaningful coding experience in total, though the exact timing depends on the person’s path.

### Focused work through constraint and delegation
- **When to use**: When leaders or ICs feel overworked, unfocused, or trapped in reactive busyness
- **Steps**:
  1. Force time boundaries, such as logging off at a fixed hour or protecting weekends.
  2. Audit what actually got done and what mattered.
  3. Regularly remove low-value work instead of using long hours to avoid prioritization.
  4. Delegate more, even though teaching others takes more time upfront.
  5. Build habits of concentrated focus during work hours.
- **Example**: Log off at a set time every day for a week, then review what truly moved forward. The constraint reveals what work was actually important.

### Building effective platform teams
- **When to use**: When a company is creating or scaling a platform engineering function
- **Steps**:
  1. Treat platforms as products, not just infrastructure maintenance.
  2. Staff platform teams with software engineers, systems/SRE expertise, and product managers.
  3. Define success in terms of outcomes such as cycle-time reduction, launch enablement, scale, or cost efficiency.
  4. Work closely with internal users to understand real problems instead of building whatever seems technically interesting.
  5. Prepare for long-running projects, migrations, and heavy stakeholder management.
- **Example**: A platform team fails because it builds tooling based on internal assumptions rather than user pain. Camille would push for PM support, customer discovery with internal teams, and outcome-based metrics.

## Core Advice
- **When PMs and engineers are tense**: Share credit, involve engineers in ideation, and respect technical detail — because engineers want recognition, ownership, and empathy for complexity.
- **When you are acting as a relay between stakeholders and engineers**: Stop over-functioning as the middle-person and connect people directly — because telephone-style communication loses nuance and wastes time.
- **When a legacy system feels unbearable**: Prefer staged evolution over a rewrite — because migration risk, hidden logic, and parallel support are usually underestimated.
- **When you are deciding whether to become a manager**: Wait until technical skill feels deeply internalized — because durable mastery creates credibility and confidence later.
- **When you worry about losing technical relevance**: Stay close to strong engineers and ask good questions — because leadership is about judgment, not personally coding everything.
- **When your calendar is packed with one-on-ones**: Keep the essential ones, but cut the rest — because many peer/stakeholder 1:1s are unscalable and often mask real disagreement.
- **When overwork feels unavoidable**: Set hard stopping points and cut low-value work — because constraints force prioritization.
- **When your team depends too much on you**: Delegate earlier, even if it slows you down — because delegation is how leaders scale others and themselves.
- **When a platform team is underperforming**: Give concrete product feedback and build relationships with the parts that work — because platform teams often need clearer customer problems, not just more engineering effort.
- **When deciding whether to create a platform team**: Wait for repeated cross-team bottlenecks or scale pain — because centralization only pays off when many teams are solving the same problem repeatedly.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: More one-on-ones are always better for alignment and relationship building. → **Their view**: Many one-on-ones beyond direct reports and your manager are wasteful and unscalable — because they consume time linearly and can hide disagreement.
- **Conventional**: When a system is painful, rewrite it from scratch. → **Their view**: Full rewrites are usually a trap; staged evolution is better — because migration and legacy complexity are routinely underestimated.
- **Conventional**: Strong managers should move into leadership early. → **Their view**: Engineers should stay hands-on longer and build real mastery first — because deep technical grounding creates lasting credibility.
- **Conventional**: Platform teams are mostly infrastructure or DevOps functions. → **Their view**: Platform engineering should be treated as product development and include PMs — because internal users need product thinking, not just tooling.
- **Conventional**: Working longer is the clearest path to high performance. → **Their view**: Overwork often substitutes for prioritization — because constraints force sharper choices and better delegation.
