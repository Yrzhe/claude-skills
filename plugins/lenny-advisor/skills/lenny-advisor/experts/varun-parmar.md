# Varun Parmar

**Chief Product Officer** | **Miro** | Expertise: product strategy, collaboration software, product-led growth, cross-functional product org design

## Bio
Varun Parmar is the Chief Product Officer at Miro and previously served as SVP and CPO at Box, with more than two decades of experience building collaboration and productivity products. His perspective matters because he combines product strategy with operating rigor: he cares deeply about speed, quality, empathy, and how product decisions change a company’s competitive position.

He is especially valuable for teams building collaborative, multi-user products, or any org trying to align product, design, engineering, analytics, and marketing around a shared operating model.

## Signature Frameworks

### AMPED cross-functional product operating model
- **When to use**: When you want product, design, engineering, analytics, and product marketing to operate as one integrated team rather than separate functions.
- **Steps**:
  1. Organize the product org as AMPED: analytics, marketing, product, engineering, and design.
  2. Embed product marketing deeply inside each stream/domain.
  3. Use cross-functional perspectives to challenge assumptions about user needs, enterprise requirements, and positioning.
  4. Share work broadly so non-product stakeholders can see hypotheses, designs, and solutions.
  5. Use product reviews to connect the dots across functions and maintain a holistic view.
- **Example**: At Miro, product marketing is part of the AMPED structure so teams can catch issues like weak positioning or missing enterprise requirements before launch.

### Design Sprint validation loop
- **When to use**: When building a new experience or zero-to-one concept and you need fast validation before investing heavily.
- **Steps**:
  1. Frame the problem and hypothesis.
  2. Run a five-day sprint with a small group.
  3. Mock up the concept quickly.
  4. Turn the concept into a prototype.
  5. Test it with users and gather insights.
  6. Iterate based on what you learn.
- **Example**: Miro used a Design Sprint to validate Talktrack and learned users wanted collaboration, not just communication, which led them to synchronize board movement with the audio/video recording.

### Competition-aware product chess
- **When to use**: When operating in a crowded market where every release changes your relative position.
- **Steps**:
  1. Assume every release by you or a competitor changes the score.
  2. Evaluate the competitor's unfair advantage: product quality, distribution, pricing, packaging.
  3. Clarify your unique value in the customer's mind.
  4. Invest in capabilities that reinforce differentiation.
  5. Package and position the product so customers understand how it fits in their stack.
- **Example**: Varun described product releases as “making chess points” against competitors and said Miro must clearly show why it is different from adjacent tools.

### Deliver customer value faster with high quality
- **When to use**: When you need a simple operating principle that aligns execution, measurement, and culture.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define customer value as something that is actually used by customers.
  2. Measure cycle time from idea to shipped solution to metric movement.
  3. Benchmark speed across teams and project sizes.
  4. Classify shipped work as high quality or not high quality.
  5. Use the rubric in performance, rewards, and team evaluation.
- **Example**: Miro uses this exact sentence as the product org motto and ties it to performance reviews, cycle-time tracking, and design quality triage.

### Rolling roadmap with fixed-confidence windows
- **When to use**: When enterprise customers want predictability but the business still needs room to adapt to market changes.
- **Steps**:
  1. Maintain a six-month rolling roadmap.
  2. Update the roadmap every three months.
  3. Set higher confidence for the first three months.
  4. Set lower confidence for the next three months.
  5. Allow teams to pivot based on customer feedback, competition, or technology shifts.
- **Example**: Miro targets 80% confidence for the first three months of the roadmap and 50% for the following three months.

### Three-horizon investment allocation
- **When to use**: When balancing core execution, adjacent bets, and long-term innovation.
- **Steps**:
  1. Allocate most resources to horizon one, the core business.
  2. Reserve a meaningful share for horizon two, adjacent opportunities in the next 12–36 months.
  3. Keep a smaller but real allocation for horizon three, next-generation bets three to five years out.
- **Example**: Varun described Miro's rough allocation as 70% horizon one, 20% horizon two, and 10% horizon three.

## Core Advice
- **When your team works across geographies and functions**: Ask questions that uncover how people reached a decision, what insights informed it, and what market or customer evidence shaped it — because empathy is how distributed teams build shared understanding and avoid siloed decisions.
- **When you are building a new feature or experience**: Validate the hypothesis quickly with a small sprint, prototype, and user feedback before scaling investment — because fast validation reduces the risk of building the wrong thing.
- **When a product team is moving slowly**: Treat blockers as the primary problem and make it the PM's job to surface them early so leadership can remove them quickly — because slowness usually comes from unresolved technical, organizational, or priority roadblocks.
- **When you need to keep a persona-based org from becoming fragmented**: Use cross-functional product teams and shared product reviews to force holistic thinking across user experience, enterprise needs, and positioning — because specialization can create local optimization.
- **When you are deciding what to prioritize in a competitive market**: Evaluate each bet through the lens of how it changes your position relative to competitors and whether it strengthens your unique value — because customers compare alternatives continuously.
- **When you want to improve execution speed across teams**: Measure cycle times by stage, compare teams, and share the data openly so teams can learn from each other — because visibility creates a feedback loop for faster execution.
- **When quality is hard to define in abstract terms**: Use examples and binary classification of great vs not great to build shared intuition before trying to codify every attribute in a document — because people often understand quality better by seeing concrete examples.
- **When you are balancing innovation with maintenance**: Adjust the mix by team context, but keep a baseline allocation for architecture, tech debt, and platform scaling work — because different teams have different constraints.
- **When you need to align enterprise and self-serve motions**: Treat them as two channels of one company, use product marketing as a bridge, and design a clear handoff from self-serve adoption to sales engagement — because internal competition hurts both growth and customer experience.
- **When you want to create stronger team accountability**: Separate the leadership role from the stream-leader role and define accountability as the leadership job and improvement as the stream job — because this dual lens clarifies responsibility.
- **When you want to build trust in leadership**: Invite direct feedback openly, including on yourself, and frame disagreements as questions rather than accusations — because vulnerability and empathetic questioning make honesty easier.
- **When you want to accelerate product-led growth**: Invest in viral use cases, templates, and community contributions that naturally bring new users into the product — because Miro grows when one user invites many others.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Don’t worry about the competition; just focus on the customer. → **Their view**: Competition is the biggest variable in whether a company accelerates or decelerates — because customers compare products against alternatives, and competitor quality, distribution, pricing, and packaging shape growth.
- **Conventional**: Move slow to go fast. → **Their view**: In innovation-heavy markets, you should try to be the first to hit the brick wall and learn faster than everyone else — because speed creates earlier learning and better course correction.
- **Conventional**: Quality should be defined through detailed written standards. → **Their view**: A simpler great-vs-not-great classification system can be more effective — because examples and calibration often build intuition better than long documents.
- **Conventional**: Product and sales should be separate motions with clear boundaries. → **Their view**: In a PLG company, product and sales are two channels serving the same customer and should be tightly connected — because the self-serve to sales handoff must be deliberately designed.
- **Conventional**: Roadmaps should be fixed commitments. → **Their view**: Roadmaps should be confidence-weighted and rolling — because predictability matters, but rigidity blocks adaptation.
