# Jason Fried

**Co-Founder and CEO** | **37signals** | Expertise: bootstrapping, product design, leadership, operations

## Bio
Jason Fried is the co-founder and CEO of 37signals, the company behind Basecamp and HEY. He’s one of the clearest voices in software for building a profitable, independent business without venture capital, and for running a calm, focused company with small teams and tight constraints.

His perspective matters because it challenges much of startup orthodoxy: he argues that most software companies do not need VC, that profit matters more than growth theater, and that product work is better when it is shaped, time-boxed, and allowed to stay small.

## Signature Frameworks

### Shape Up
- **When to use**: When building software features or products and you want to avoid endless projects, overplanning, and large-team coordination overhead.
- **Steps**:
  1. Shape the work upfront into a clear concept rather than a detailed spec.
  2. Set an appetite for the work instead of estimating how long it will take.
  3. Cap individual projects at a maximum of six weeks.
  4. Assign very small teams, typically one designer and one programmer.
  5. Let the team decide the tasks and implementation details as they go.
  6. Trade concessions during execution when reality differs from the original concept.
  7. At the deadline, ship what is done; unfinished work that is still unresolved should usually be dropped.
- **Example**: At 37signals, features for Basecamp or HEY are typically built by two people with a maximum six-week cycle, and if work is still on the “uphill” side of the hill chart at the deadline, it usually dies instead of being extended.

### Appetites over Estimates
- **When to use**: When teams routinely miss deadlines or let projects expand indefinitely because estimates are unreliable.
- **Steps**:
  1. Decide how much time you're willing to spend on a problem.
  2. Treat that time budget as fixed.
  3. Design the solution to fit the budget rather than expanding the budget to fit the solution.
  4. Reduce scope until the simplest effective version fits.
- **Example**: Instead of asking how long a feature will take, they might decide they only have a one-week or six-week appetite and then shape the feature to fit that constraint.

### Six-Week Cycle + Two-Week Cooldown
- **When to use**: When teams need sustainable pace, regular shipping rhythm, and recovery time between focused product pushes.
- **Steps**:
  1. Work in six-week cycles on shaped projects.
  2. Avoid calling them sprints to prevent a perpetual exhaustion mindset.
  3. After each cycle, take a two-week cooldown.
  4. Use cooldown for bug fixes, cleanup, small unscheduled improvements, and shaping the next cycle.
- **Example**: After each six-week cycle at 37signals, teams enter a two-week cooldown where they finish loose ends, fix bugs, and leadership shapes the next batch of work.

### Stay Small as Long as You Can
- **When to use**: When starting a company with limited resources or trying to preserve flexibility, profitability, and simplicity.
- **Steps**:
  1. Keep costs as low as possible.
  2. Do as much work yourself as you can before hiring.
  3. Avoid unnecessary spending on branding, offices, and custom work.
  4. Use simple off-the-shelf tools to get started.
  5. Delay complexity and headcount until truly necessary.
- **Example**: Fried advises founders to avoid spending on branding or offices, use tools like Squarespace, and hold off on hiring because every additional person adds cost and complexity.

### Negative Visualization
- **When to use**: When deciding whether to pursue a risky product or business move and fear of failure is blocking action.
- **Steps**:
  1. Imagine the worst realistic outcome.
  2. Assess whether you can live with that downside.
  3. Make peace with the downside before proceeding.
  4. Move forward only if the downside is survivable.
- **Example**: For the ONCE product line, Fried says they asked what the worst case would be if it flopped, and concluded they would have spent six months exploring something interesting that they could afford to try.

## Core Advice
- **When you face the decision of whether to raise venture capital for a software business**: Do a hard check on whether you truly need capital for physical or upfront costs; if not, try bootstrapping first — because software businesses often have low startup costs and high margins, so outside capital can create pressure to chase only massive outcomes and skip many viable, profitable business paths.
- **When you face a new product idea and limited resources**: Do as much as possible yourself and stay small for as long as you can before hiring — because the main thing you control early is cost, and every additional person adds payroll, coordination, and risk if the hire is wrong.
- **When you face projects that keep dragging on and demoralizing the team**: Do fixed-length cycles with a maximum time budget and cut scope to fit the budget — because long-running projects are demoralizing and often expand to fill available time.
- **When you face pressure to promise delivery dates far in advance**: Do less promising and more sharing of what you're currently working on — because promises create artificial pressure and often backfire, especially vague commitments like “by the end of the year.”
- **When you face a desire to adopt a new operating model like Shape Up**: Do a low-stakes pilot on a noncritical project before rolling it out broadly — because big process changes fail when introduced all at once.
- **When you face hiring for product or design roles**: Do a paid project and then ask candidates how they would evolve the work if they had more time — because this reveals taste, judgment, instinct, and whether they can both execute clearly and riff productively beyond the initial brief.
- **When you face decisions with incomplete data**: Do ask people what they think and how something feels, not only what they can prove — because most business decisions are judgment calls shaped by experience, taste, and tacit knowledge.
- **When you face the temptation to spend because you have money**: Do preserve constraints instead of removing them with excess hiring and marketing spend — because constraints sharpen judgment and force teams to learn how to make money.
- **When you face a crowded market with many similar products**: Do build an alternative rather than trying to outdo competitors on every dimension — because trying to beat competitors feature-for-feature is expensive and defensive.
- **When you face fear about trying something new that may fail**: Do explicitly define the worst-case outcome and decide if you can live with it — because once the downside is understood and acceptable, it becomes easier to act without being paralyzed by vague anxiety.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: The best startups raise venture capital and grow as fast as possible. → **Their view**: Most founders should consider bootstrapping because venture only works for a tiny number of outliers and removes many viable success paths — because VC-backed companies are forced into a narrow “go huge” outcome, while bootstrapped companies can succeed at many smaller but still meaningful scales.
- **Conventional**: Revenue growth and startup metrics are the main indicators of business health. → **Their view**: Profit matters more than revenue, and many common startup metrics are distractions — because a company can generate lots of revenue and still go broke.
- **Conventional**: Bigger teams help companies move faster and do more. → **Their view**: Small teams with tight constraints often produce better work with less waste — because large teams create layers, meetings, complexity, and support burdens.
- **Conventional**: Good management requires long-term plans, goals, OKRs, and KPIs. → **Their view**: Long-term planning is often fantasy; it is better to rethink priorities every six weeks and stay close to current reality — because the further out you plan, the less you know.
- **Conventional**: Business decisions should be data-driven and justified analytically. → **Their view**: Most important decisions are judgment calls, so intuition and gut deserve more respect — because humans do not make decisions like machines.
- **Conventional**: Competition means outperforming rivals and conquering markets. → **Their view**: Companies should aim to provide alternatives, not wage war on competitors — because war metaphors create a destructive mindset.
- **Conventional**: Subscription SaaS is the inevitable best model for business software. → **Their view**: There is room for high-quality, pay-once software products as an alternative to endless subscriptions — because many customers prefer ownership, lower long-term cost, and control.
- **Conventional**: Starting a company is the hard part and deserves the most celebration. → **Their view**: Staying in business is much harder than starting, so “stay ups” matter more than startups — because launching is relatively easy compared with surviving years of execution.
