# Christian Idiodi

**Partner** | **Silicon Valley Product Group** | Expertise: product-management, leadership, product discovery, coaching

## Bio
Christian Idiodi is a partner at Silicon Valley Product Group, where he helps companies improve product management, leadership, and product operating models. His perspective matters because he combines strong product discovery instincts with a deep focus on coaching people to become better decision-makers, not just better framework users.

He is especially known for pushing teams to get close to customers, solve real problems in the field, and build trust through competence. He also brings a community-builder’s lens from his work developing product communities in Africa.

## Signature Frameworks

### Four Risks of Product Discovery
- **When to use**: Use when evaluating whether a product idea is worth building and when clarifying how product, design, and engineering should work together.
- **Steps**:
  1. Assess value risk: will customers buy, choose, or use this?
  2. Assess usability risk: can users actually use it successfully?
  3. Assess feasibility risk: can we build it with the available skills, technology, and time?
  4. Assess viability risk: does it work for the business?
  5. Work as a product trio/team to uncover a solution customers love and the business can support.
- **Example**: Christian emphasizes that product managers are especially accountable for value and viability—making sure the team is solving a real customer problem and that the solution makes sense for the business.

### Reference Customer Discovery
- **When to use**: Use when creating a new product or entering a new problem space and you need the clearest signal of product-market fit.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify a specific customer segment with a real, urgent problem.
  2. Get out of the building and immerse yourself in the customer’s environment.
  3. Work directly with customers to discover and deliver a solution at the same time.
  4. Iterate until customers love the solution enough to put their reputation on the line for it.
  5. For B2B, aim for 6–8 reference customers; for B2C, aim for 15–25.
  6. Do not customize endlessly for outliers; look for one solution that many target customers want.
- **Example**: Christian says he won’t launch a consumer app until he has around 25 five-star reviews from real users who genuinely love it.

### Pressure-Cooked Discovery
- **When to use**: Use when you want to learn quickly whether a problem is real and how to solve it, especially in ambiguous early-stage work.
- **Steps**:
  1. Find people who clearly have the problem.
  2. Go to where the problem happens rather than relying on abstract interviews.
  3. Stay with the problem long enough to observe real behavior and constraints.
  4. Try to solve the problem directly, even manually.
  5. Use what happens in reality—not what people say—to refine the product.
- **Example**: In a high-volume hiring story, Christian and his team physically visited McDonald’s, malls, and employers, manually recruited candidates, observed no-shows, and then designed software based on what they learned.

### Teach Me / Help You Trust-Building Technique
- **When to use**: Use when a new PM or leader needs to build trust and credibility quickly with influential stakeholders.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the loudest or most influential person in the organization.
  2. Ask them to teach you how the business works, or volunteer to help them if they are too busy.
  3. Spend time observing them in meetings and day-to-day work.
  4. Learn the sources of their competence and influence.
  5. Leverage the relationship to build trust, context, and visibility across the company.
  6. Keep learning so others see your competence compound over time.
- **Example**: Christian recommends shadowing a highly influential leader for a week so trust transfers and the new hire learns what actually drives influence.

### Practice Before Promotion
- **When to use**: Use when preparing someone for management or leadership so they are not promoted into incompetence.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define what the next-level role actually does in practice.
  2. Let the person perform parts of that role before they receive the title.
  3. Create safe practice environments where mistakes are low-risk.
  4. Coach them on those behaviors while they are still protected.
  5. Promote them only after they have demonstrated they can do the job.
- **Example**: Christian tells aspiring directors to “go be a director” first—practice the work with coaching—because he wants to promote people to do the job, not to learn the job.

### Collaborative Problem-Solving Reps
- **When to use**: Use when someone lacks strong coaching or wants to break into product management and needs practical skill development.
- **Steps**:
  1. Find low-risk environments where teams solve problems together.
  2. Volunteer in nonprofits, community organizations, churches, or side projects.
  3. Observe how strong people collaborate, communicate, and make decisions.
  4. Practice solving problems with others repeatedly.
  5. Use these reps to build judgment, humility, and pattern recognition.
- **Example**: Christian advises aspiring PMs to join “pickup games” for collaborative problem solving, because product skill comes from repeated practice, not theory alone.

## Core Advice
- **When you face distrust or dislike as a PM**: become the most competent person on the customer, business, data, and product dimensions — because trust follows judgment and competence.
- **When you are new to a company and need credibility fast**: ask the most influential person to teach you, or help them — because trust and context transfer through proximity.
- **When you are trying to figure out what to build**: work directly with reference customers until they will recommend the product publicly — because reputation-backed commitment is stronger than polite feedback.
- **When you are building a B2B product from scratch**: don’t call it ready until you have roughly 6–8 reference customers — because that’s a stronger signal than a few happy conversations.
- **When you are building a B2C product from scratch**: aim for 15–25 reference customers before launch — because consumer products need visible social proof and repeated love.
- **When customers say they like a product but adoption is unclear**: ask for a testimonial, review, or referral — because action reveals true value.
- **When discovery feels abstract or slow**: solve the problem manually in the real environment first — because reality exposes constraints faster than detached research.
- **When customers request many different features**: build the minimum common solution for the target segment — because over-customization destroys product clarity.
- **When you are coaching someone to improve**: create practice environments, not just feedback — because people improve through reps with correction.
- **When someone is preparing for a management promotion**: have them do parts of the job before the title — because promotion should validate readiness, not create it.
- **When you do not have a strong manager or coach**: seek collaborative problem-solving environments outside your job — because product judgment is built through repeated team reps.
- **When hiring PMs**: give candidates a live problem and watch how they think — because real-time collaboration reveals more than rehearsed answers.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: People dislike product managers because the role is unnecessary or annoying. → **Their view**: Most people dislike PMs because they haven’t worked with competent ones — because strong PMs create clarity and better decisions.
- **Conventional**: Usability testing and positive feedback are enough to validate a product. → **Their view**: Value is the most overlooked risk — because users can like something and still not buy, choose, or keep using it.
- **Conventional**: Many discovery frameworks and artifacts will tell you what to build. → **Their view**: The best discovery is immersion and direct problem solving with customers — because reality beats abstraction.
- **Conventional**: Training alone will improve managers and employees. → **Their view**: Training without practice is insufficient — because skill comes from coached reps.
- **Conventional**: Promotion is how people grow into leadership. → **Their view**: People should practice leadership before promotion — because you should promote them to do the job, not learn it.
- **Conventional**: Polished interviews prove product competence. → **Their view**: Collaborative problem solving in real situations is the real test — because product work is lived, not performed.
