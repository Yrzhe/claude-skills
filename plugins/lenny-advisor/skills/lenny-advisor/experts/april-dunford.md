# April Dunford

**Author, positioning consultant, and former VP of Marketing at multiple B2B tech startups** | **April Dunford Consulting** | Expertise: positioning, go-to-market, segmentation, B2B sales

## Bio
April Dunford is one of the most influential voices in B2B tech positioning. She spent roughly 25 years leading marketing, product, and sales at seven startups, and has helped around 200 companies clarify how they win, who they win with, and how to tell that story consistently.

Her perspective matters because she treats positioning as a practical business system, not a branding exercise. If a company has confused prospects, weak pipeline, or inconsistent sales messaging, April is the expert to consult.

## Signature Frameworks

### The 5-Part Positioning Framework
- **When to use**: When a product is underperforming, the market is confused, teams are misaligned, or you need to sharpen how you win against alternatives.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define competitive alternatives: identify what you must beat to win, including the status quo and shortlist competitors.
  2. List differentiated capabilities: identify features, functions, services, or company capabilities you have that alternatives do not.
  3. Translate capabilities into value: ask “so what?” for each capability and group the benefits into 2-3 differentiated value themes.
  4. Identify best-fit customers: determine which customer or company characteristics make buyers care deeply about that value.
  5. Choose market category: frame the product in a category context that makes the differentiated value obvious.
- **Example**: Help Scout competed not just with Zendesk, but with shared inboxes and email. Its differentiated value was more human, relationship-oriented support, which resonated with e-commerce and DTC brands that saw support as a growth driver.

### Sales Narrative Mapping from Positioning
- **When to use**: After positioning is defined and you need a repeatable story for sales, marketing, product, and leadership.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start from the customer’s worldview and problem framing.
  2. Explain why the problem matters now using evidence or market shifts.
  3. Show how existing alternatives frame the problem incorrectly or solve for the wrong objective.
  4. Introduce your differentiated value in that context.
  5. Equip the buyer to justify why your approach is the right choice.
- **Example**: Help Scout’s narrative starts with customer service as a growth driver, then contrasts that with alternatives that treat support as a cost center.

### Positioning Diagnosis via Sales Calls
- **When to use**: When you suspect weak positioning but don’t have a single metric to prove it.
- **Steps**:
  1. Listen to early sales calls, especially first calls.
  2. Look for confusion signals like prospects asking reps to restart.
  3. Watch for incorrect comparisons to the wrong category or incumbent.
  4. Notice value skepticism, where prospects think they understand the product but don’t see why anyone would pay for it.
  5. Use these patterns to identify whether the issue is understanding, category framing, or value articulation.
- **Example**: Prospects saying, “Could you just back up to the beginning?” or “I get it, you’re just like Salesforce,” or “I can do that in a spreadsheet.”

### Early-Stage Positioning as Thesis, Not Truth
- **When to use**: When a startup is pre-PMF or very early with only a handful of customers.
- **Steps**:
  1. Document an internal positioning thesis based on current assumptions.
  2. Keep external positioning broad rather than over-constraining it.
  3. Watch which customer types repeatedly respond, buy, and succeed.
  4. Look for patterns in company characteristics and buying context.
  5. Tighten positioning only once a clear repeatable pattern emerges.
- **Example**: Her fishing-net analogy: launch as a net for “big fish,” then see whether the market reveals it’s actually best for grouper or another segment.

### B2B Segmentation Before Personas
- **When to use**: When building go-to-market strategy and deciding who sales and marketing should target.
- **Steps**:
  1. Segment the market by company-level characteristics, not just individual buyer traits.
  2. Use firmographics and operational signals like size, budget, stack, team structure, or workflow maturity.
  3. Identify the segment where your differentiated value wins consistently.
  4. Prioritize the champion persona within that segment.
  5. Arm the champion to sell internally to other stakeholders later.
- **Example**: Target companies with a creative team larger than three people, a certain budget, and a specific software package.

## Core Advice
- **When pipeline is sluggish, prospects are confused, or churn appears after “successful” sales**: do a positioning review before assuming the product is the problem — because weak positioning can hurt every stage of the funnel.
- **When founder, marketing, sales, product, and CS disagree on how to describe the product**: run positioning as a cross-functional exercise with the CEO in the room — because positioning is also an alignment problem.
- **When asked to define your value proposition**: start with the competitive alternatives you must beat — because that forces differentiation instead of generic benefits.
- **When many deals are lost as “no decision”**: position against the status quo, not just named competitors — because spreadsheets, manual work, and inertia are often the real rival.
- **When sales effectiveness is weak after positioning work**: turn positioning into a tested sales narrative — because sales needs a story they can actually tell.
- **When judging homepage or messaging quality**: evaluate whether qualified prospects understand and get excited — because broad audiences are not the target buyer.
- **When launching early with limited data**: keep positioning loose externally while maintaining an internal thesis — because early assumptions are usually partly wrong.
- **When deciding when to tighten positioning**: do it once you see a repeatable pattern in who buys, why they buy, and when the pitch works — because that’s when the market is telling you the truth.
- **When planning B2B GTM**: segment by company characteristics first, then focus persona work on the champion — because company context determines fit.
- **When selling into a complex buying committee**: optimize first for the champion, then equip them to sell internally — because the champion is the gatekeeper.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: If a product is not performing, the product itself is probably the issue. → **Their view**: Often the real problem is positioning, not the product — because a good product can still be misunderstood, miscategorized, or aimed at the wrong buyers.
- **Conventional**: Positioning can be judged by a homepage or broad audience comprehension. → **Their view**: The only meaningful test is whether qualified prospects understand and want to buy — because outsiders are not the intended audience.
- **Conventional**: Start positioning by listing customer benefits or why people love the product. → **Their view**: Start with competitive alternatives, including the status quo — because generic benefits don’t help buyers choose.
- **Conventional**: Early startups should tightly define positioning from day one. → **Their view**: Early positioning should be treated as a hypothesis and kept somewhat loose — because the market still needs to reveal the best segment.
- **Conventional**: Build detailed personas for every stakeholder in the buying committee. → **Their view**: The champion persona matters most; most other persona work is overdone — because the champion drives the evaluation and internal consensus.
- **Conventional**: Competition means similar vendors. → **Their view**: The biggest competitor is often the status quo — because many deals are lost to inertia, not another software company.
