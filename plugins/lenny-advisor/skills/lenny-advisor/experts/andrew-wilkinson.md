# Andrew Wilkinson

**Co-founder and CEO** | **Tiny** | Expertise: strategy, go-to-market, operations, ai-strategy

## Bio
Andrew Wilkinson is the co-founder and CEO of Tiny, a holding company that acquires and operates profitable internet and consumer businesses for the long term. His perspective matters because he has both built and bought many companies, giving him a rare view into what makes businesses durable, what makes founders effective, and where AI can create real leverage.

He is especially useful when you need a practical, non-hype view on startup ideas, business quality, hiring, and founder productivity. Wilkinson tends to favor simple, profitable, moaty businesses over flashy venture narratives.

## Signature Frameworks

### Fish Where the Fish Are
- **When to use**: When choosing a startup idea or market to enter, especially as a first-time founder.
- **Steps**:
  1. List markets or problems you are naturally drawn to.
  2. Avoid categories that attract lots of founders simply because they seem fun or glamorous.
  3. Look for niches with real customer pain but relatively low founder competition.
  4. Prioritize markets where customers already spend meaningful money to solve the problem.
  5. Use your own background and unfair advantages to identify where you can compete effectively.
- **Example**: Instead of crowded ideas like cafes, bars, or project management tools, Wilkinson points to less glamorous opportunities like funeral homes, pest control, or software that helps people fill out government assistance forms.

### Start With Baby Weights
- **When to use**: When deciding what kind of business to start early in your entrepreneurial career.
- **Steps**:
  1. Choose a simple business model with few moving parts.
  2. Aim for a fast first win that gives you confidence and feedback.
  3. Avoid highly regulated, capital-intensive, or hyper-competitive categories at the beginning.
  4. Build entrepreneurial skill through repetition before attempting very hard businesses.
- **Example**: He compares a first-time founder trying to build “the next great AI company” to trying to deadlift 300 pounds on day one. His own first win was a simple web design agency where he only needed to sell clients and deliver work.

### Business vs. Job Test
- **When to use**: When evaluating whether an idea will create leverage or just create self-employment.
- **Steps**:
  1. Ask whether the business can scale beyond your own labor.
  2. Identify the functions you personally dislike doing.
  3. Design the business so those tasks can be delegated quickly.
  4. Build enough lead flow and margin to hire operators or specialists.
  5. Move yourself toward the parts of the business you enjoy most.
- **Example**: In a pressure washing business, Wilkinson told his student partner that if the company reached scale, he would not need to wash driveways himself and could focus on sales or marketing instead.

### Passion Adjacent Profit Search
- **When to use**: When you want to work in an area you care about without getting trapped in a bad business model.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with an industry or hobby you genuinely care about.
  2. Study the economics of the obvious business models in that space.
  3. Reject the parts of the industry with poor returns or structural weakness.
  4. Look for adjacent businesses with moats, better margins, or stronger demand.
  5. Invest or build where passion and economics overlap.
- **Example**: Wilkinson loved movies but found film financing unattractive, then later bought Letterboxd as a better way to participate in the film world. Similarly, his coffee interest led to buying AeroPress rather than opening a cafe.

### Moat-First Acquisition Framework
- **When to use**: When evaluating businesses to buy or hold long term.
- **Steps**:
  1. Look for businesses that are hard to mess up.
  2. Assess whether the company has a moat such as brand, network effects, or switching costs.
  3. Prefer businesses with loyal customers and durable demand.
  4. Avoid businesses held together by a few fragile people or processes.
  5. If management is strong, leave the company largely alone after acquisition.
- **Example**: Tiny bought Letterboxd because its social graph and community create network effects, and bought Serato because of its passionate user base and deep hardware integration.

### Hire Fully Formed, Not for Potential
- **When to use**: When hiring leaders or key operators in a business.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the actual job to be done and the growth path required.
  2. Listen for what candidates naturally believe and have repeatedly done before.
  3. Hire people whose instincts already match the business need.
  4. Do not assume you can coach someone into a fundamentally different operating style.
  5. If you start seriously wondering whether to fire someone, treat that as a strong signal.
- **Example**: Wilkinson hired a CEO for a business that needed organic marketing, but the candidate kept reverting to enterprise sales because that was his background. Wilkinson says CEOs are like an elephant and the board is the rider: they go where they want to go.

### Lazy Leadership / Teflon for Tasks
- **When to use**: When founders are overloaded with work they dislike or that should be systematized.
- **Steps**:
  1. List recurring tasks that drain your energy.
  2. Ask how each task can be delegated, automated, or eliminated.
  3. Use AI agents and workflows for repetitive inbox, calendar, and research work.
  4. Keep yourself focused on the highest-value activities you are uniquely good at.
  5. Continuously redesign your role to avoid becoming the bottleneck.
- **Example**: Wilkinson uses Lindy to triage email, identify urgent messages, draft replies, update calendars, and prep meeting briefs, replacing much of what a full-time assistant used to do.

## Core Advice
- **When you are choosing a startup idea as a first-time founder**: do something simpler and less crowded than your dream “big swing” idea — because early success builds confidence, pattern recognition, and momentum.
- **When a market looks exciting and crowded**: do a competition check and bias toward boring, underloved niches — because crowded markets compress margins and make differentiation harder.
- **When you are unsure whether a problem is worth solving**: use AI to estimate customer economics and willingness to pay — because a problem is only attractive if the buyer has enough pain and budget.
- **When an idea seems fun but may trap you operationally**: do a business-vs-job test — because many small businesses are just jobs in disguise unless they can scale beyond your own labor.
- **When you love an industry but the obvious business model is weak**: search for adjacent businesses with better moats and margins — because you can still work in a field you care about without choosing the worst economics inside it.
- **When facing a venture-backed incumbent**: assess whether you are playing the same game — because smaller, focused businesses can win by serving a narrower audience intentionally.
- **When hiring leaders or key employees**: hire for demonstrated fit for the exact job — because people tend to repeat the patterns and strategies they already know.
- **When you keep doubting an employee**: act quickly — because persistent doubt is usually predictive, and delaying prolongs stress and underperformance.
- **When repetitive admin work is eating your time**: automate aggressively with AI before hiring more support — because AI can now handle triage, summaries, reminders, research, and drafting cheaply.
- **When you worry AI will replace you**: become fluent with AI tools now — because near-term winners are likely to be people who use AI to move faster and create leverage.
- **When you feel persistent anxiety or mental noise**: get a serious medical evaluation — because money and success often do not fix internal chemistry or mental health issues.
- **When you feel distracted or “broken” despite high achievement**: explore ADHD screening and treatment — because diagnosis can improve both performance and quality of life.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: The best startup ideas are big, exciting, and obviously ambitious. → **Their view**: Boring, niche, and underappreciated ideas are often better businesses — because exciting categories attract too much competition.
- **Conventional**: Founders should pursue the biggest market possible from day one. → **Their view**: First-time founders should start with “baby weights” — because entrepreneurial skill compounds through reps.
- **Conventional**: If you love an industry, start the obvious business in that space. → **Their view**: Find the profitable niche within it — because the visible business model is often the weakest one.
- **Conventional**: Bootstrapped businesses are inherently small and inferior. → **Their view**: Bootstrapped businesses can become very large and create better founder outcomes — because profitable, moaty businesses can scale without venture capital.
- **Conventional**: Hire for potential and coach people into the role. → **Their view**: Hire people who are already fully formed for the job — because people usually revert to their natural strengths.
- **Conventional**: More money and status will make you happier. → **Their view**: Wealth does little to fix anxiety or unhappiness driven by internal patterns — because comparison and mental health issues persist at every wealth level.
- **Conventional**: Psychiatric medication should be avoided if possible. → **Their view**: For some people, SSRIs or ADHD treatment can be life-changing — because treating a real condition can be more rational than chasing external success.
