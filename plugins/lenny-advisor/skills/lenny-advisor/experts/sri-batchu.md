# Sri Batchu

**Head of Growth** | **Ramp** | Expertise: growth, B2B go-to-market, metrics, operations

## Bio
Sri Batchu leads growth at Ramp and has previously held leadership roles in operations and growth at Opendoor and Instacart. He is especially valuable for teams that want a rigorous, systems-oriented view of growth: how to sequence channels, translate metrics across functions, and build a high-velocity operating cadence.

His perspective matters because he combines analytical discipline with practical GTM experience. He thinks in frameworks, uses data to connect local team work to company outcomes, and is unusually opinionated about what actually drives scalable B2B growth.

## Signature Frameworks

### B2B Channel Sequencing Ladder
- **When to use**: When building a B2B growth engine from scratch or deciding what channels to invest in next.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with founder-led sales so the early team learns how to sell.
  2. Hire the first couple of salespeople.
  3. Add low-cost targeted marketing like content, community, and small events.
  4. Layer in PR once you have something interesting to say.
  5. Only then invest in paid and brand marketing.
  6. Start SEO around the same time as paid, once you have enough domain authority and customer understanding.
- **Example**: Ramp’s preferred progression is founder-led sales, then sales hires, then content/community/events, then PR, then paid and brand, with SEO starting around the paid-marketing stage.

### North-Star Metric Translation Layer
- **When to use**: When multiple teams need to optimize different local metrics but the company needs one shared planning currency.
- **Steps**:
  1. Choose one company-level north-star metric that is intuitive and tied to value creation.
  2. Give each team a local metric they can directly influence.
  3. Build a translation layer that maps local metric movement into the north-star metric.
  4. Use the translation layer for planning, resource allocation, and reporting.
  5. Treat the translation as directional, not exact, and avoid over-optimizing marginal decisions.
  6. Refresh the translation factors periodically as new data arrives.
- **Example**: At Instacart, teams had local metrics like checkout speed, but finance/data translated those changes into monthly active orders; at Ramp, growth teams translated work into dollars of SQL pipeline.

### Fail Conclusively, Not Just Fast
- **When to use**: When running growth experiments or testing a hypothesis that could be expensive or slow to validate.
- **Steps**:
  1. Design the test so it can actually answer the hypothesis.
  2. Maximize the treatment effect when sample size is limited.
  3. Use as many relevant tactics as needed to test the full strategy.
  4. If the test fails, ensure it failed for a clear reason and not because the execution was too weak.
  5. If it works, run a second test to isolate which components matter most.
- **Example**: For account-based marketing, Sri recommends touching target accounts in as many meaningful ways as possible in one test, rather than trying only a few tactics and concluding too early that the strategy does not work.

### Growth as a Portfolio of Bets
- **When to use**: When a company has moved beyond a single dominant acquisition channel and needs a repeatable growth system.
- **Steps**:
  1. Maintain a diversified set of channel bets.
  2. Allocate more budget to channels that are efficient and scalable.
  3. Continuously improve efficiency across all channels.
  4. Use data and experimentation to compare ROI across channels.
  5. Shift spend toward owned and earned media where possible.
- **Example**: Ramp does not rely on one magical channel; instead it runs a diversified portfolio across outbound sales, paid marketing, field, SEO, lifecycle, and other channels.

### MECE Problem Decomposition
- **When to use**: When diagnosing a business problem or brainstorming solutions and you want to avoid missing key causes.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the problem clearly.
  2. Break it into mutually exclusive, collectively exhaustive buckets.
  3. Continue decomposing each bucket until the root causes are visible.
  4. Use the structure to ensure you have covered all plausible explanations.
  5. Turn the decomposition into a formula when possible.
- **Example**: When revenue growth slowed, Sri suggested decomposing it into revenue per user, number of customers, acquisition, activation, retention, and product-level revenue sources.

### Build a Growth Engine Around Culture and Cadences
- **When to use**: When designing a growth organization and deciding whether team structure or operating system matters more.
- **Steps**:
  1. Set a simple north-star metric, usually one or at most two.
  2. Create a culture of data-driven hypotheses.
  3. Run fast MVP-style experiments for both product and non-product work.
  4. Use rituals and cadences to keep teams aligned.
  5. Prioritize speed first, then quality, then cost.
- **Example**: Sri argues that team structure is often a red herring; what matters more is the operating cadence, shared metrics, and a culture that can execute quickly and learn fast.

## Core Advice
- **When you are building B2B demand generation from zero**: Start with founder-led sales, then add sales hires, then low-cost targeted marketing, and only later scale paid and brand — because channels generally get more expensive as you move down the stack, and they become more effective as you learn more about customers.
- **When you need a company-wide growth metric that multiple teams can influence**: Use one intuitive north-star metric and create translation layers from each team’s local metric into that shared currency — because it makes prioritization, resource allocation, and cross-team planning much easier without forcing every team to optimize the same lagging metric directly.
- **When a growth team is tempted to optimize CAC alone**: Measure payback period using contribution margin instead of obsessing over CAC reduction — because lower CAC can attract worse customers; payback period keeps the focus on value created relative to acquisition cost.
- **When you are unsure whether a growth idea is worth repeating**: Design the experiment to fail conclusively, not just quickly — because a weak test can create false negatives and cause teams to retry the same bad idea for years.
- **When sample sizes are small in B2B experiments**: Maximize the treatment effect by using all relevant tactics in the test — because B2B often lacks large M, so the best way to learn is to make the intervention strong enough to reveal whether the hypothesis is valid.
- **When your growth org is getting bigger and more complex**: Use a common currency for planning and reporting across teams — because it helps compare opportunities across channels and decide where to add budget or engineering resources.
- **When you are deciding whether to build a tool in-house or buy it**: Buy external tools for non-proprietary work and reserve in-house builds for truly strategic automation — because building everything internally slows velocity and wastes engineering effort on problems that off-the-shelf tools already solve well.
- **When you want to improve team velocity**: Shorten decision cycles and make time visible in days, not quarters — because Ramp reinforces urgency by tracking days since founding and by pushing people to do today what could be done today.
- **When your team is overloaded and reactive**: Use calendar blocking and calendar audits to align time spent with priorities — because people effectively ship their calendars, so auditing them helps ensure focus time is protected and priorities are reflected in the schedule.
- **When hiring for a specialized role**: Target companies that are one to two stages ahead of you and source candidates from the best teams in that function — because those people have seen the stage of growth you need and usually bring the right craft and operating experience.
- **When you want to find overlooked hiring sources**: Use public data to identify companies that are unusually effective in the function you need, then recruit from those teams — because traffic-source data can reveal which companies are strong at email marketing or other channels.
- **When you are deciding how to pay top performers**: Be willing to pay 10X operators 10X compensation — because small teams of exceptional people can outperform larger teams of mediocre people, and differentiated pay helps attract and retain elite talent.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Investor recommendations and cap-table relationships are not a major growth lever. → **Their view**: Putting founders, operators, and customers on the cap table can be a real early growth strategy — because those people become early customers, advocates, and sources of credibility.
- **Conventional**: Growth teams should be organized primarily by function and team structure. → **Their view**: Team structure is often a red herring; culture, rituals, and cadences matter more — because a strong operating system with simple metrics and fast experimentation matters more than the exact org chart.
- **Conventional**: CAC is the best way to judge acquisition efficiency. → **Their view**: Payback period is a better metric than CAC or even LTV/CAC — because CAC ignores value created, while LTV/CAC is too assumption-heavy and can hide bad retention assumptions.
- **Conventional**: Growth experiments should be run as small, narrow tests to isolate one variable. → **Their view**: For big, expensive B2B bets, you should often test the full strategy at once — because partial tests can incorrectly suggest the strategy does not work.
- **Conventional**: Compensation should mostly be compressed and standardized for fairness. → **Their view**: Companies should widen compensation differences to reward exceptional operators disproportionately — because differentiated pay is a tool for building talent density.
- **Conventional**: PR is mostly a vanity channel with limited growth value. → **Their view**: PR can be a meaningful growth machine if the company has genuinely interesting news and uses the moment thoughtfully — because well-timed PR and fundraising announcements can create real top-of-funnel impact.
