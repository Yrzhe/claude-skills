# Timothy Davis

**Head of Performance Marketing** | **Shopify** | Expertise: performance marketing, paid growth, attribution, creative strategy

## Bio
Timothy Davis leads performance marketing at Shopify and has advised companies like Pinterest, LinkedIn, Redfin, Eventbrite, and IBM. His perspective matters because he combines hands-on channel expertise with a strong operating mindset: he cares less about vanity metrics and more about whether paid media is producing real, incremental business results.

He is especially useful when you need a practical view on how to test channels, structure a performance marketing team, and decide what data actually deserves attention. His approach is highly tactical, data-driven, and built for scale.

## Signature Frameworks

### Signs of Life Testing
- **When to use**: When evaluating a new paid channel or deciding whether a platform is worth scaling.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with your own customer data and upload it to the platform.
  2. Build the most tightly matched audience first, such as a 1% lookalike.
  3. Run a small-budget test to see whether the platform produces any meaningful signal.
  4. Evaluate early engagement and conversion signals rather than expecting immediate scale.
  5. If there is a positive signal, expand audience breadth and build a fuller campaign around it.
  6. If there is no signal, treat it as learning rather than failure and move on or iterate.
- **Example**: Test Meta with a 1% lookalike audience first, then expand to 2-4%, 5-7%, and beyond only if the initial test shows life.

### Forward Thinking, Backwards Planning
- **When to use**: When deciding which channels to enter, how to scale them, or how to build a team around paid growth.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the end state you want, such as always-on performance in a channel or a target spend level.
  2. Work backward to identify the milestones required to get there.
  3. Choose the first channel, ad unit, or creative format that can realistically support the first milestone.
  4. Set explicit thresholds for moving from test to BAU, such as conversion volume or lift.
  5. Iterate through each milestone until the channel is ready to be treated as a core growth engine.
- **Example**: For an emerging channel, define success as 1,000 conversions per month at a certain spend and lift, then work backward to the first creative and test structure.

### Signal Over Noise Operating Model
- **When to use**: When hiring, analyzing accounts, or deciding what metrics to optimize.
- **Steps**:
  1. Clarify the campaign objective first: awareness, consideration, or conversion.
  2. Ignore vanity metrics unless they directly support the objective.
  3. Identify the few metrics that best indicate progress toward the goal.
  4. Use platform diagnostics and reports to find the true bottleneck.
  5. Focus optimization on the signal, not the flood of available data.
  6. Train the team to ask what matters for the business outcome before acting on metrics.
- **Example**: If the goal is conversions, focus on clicks, conversion rate, and target quality rather than impressions or reach.

### Ops Cadence Management
- **When to use**: When running a performance marketing team that needs consistency, accountability, and repeatability.
- **Steps**:
  1. Create a spreadsheet of recurring workstreams and buckets such as finance, performance, structure, keywords, and ad copy.
  2. Break each bucket into specific tasks like search query reports, negatives, and brand vs. non-brand analysis.
  3. Assign a cadence to each task: weekly, biweekly, or monthly.
  4. Use the cadence as a shared source of truth for the team and cross-functional partners.
  5. Spot-check execution regularly to ensure the work is actually happening.
  6. Adjust the cadence as the team and spend scale.
- **Example**: Track keyword granularity weekly, while other items may be biweekly or monthly, all in one shared spreadsheet.

### Incrementality-Adjusted Measurement
- **When to use**: When spend is large enough that you need to know whether paid media is truly incremental.
- **Steps**:
  1. Use multi-touch attribution as a directional view, not the final answer.
  2. Run GeoX or Conversion Lift tests to measure true incrementality.
  3. Use the test results to calculate an incrementality-adjusted factor.
  4. Apply that factor by platform or region to refine efficiency estimates.
  5. Use the results to inform future budget allocation and channel strategy.
- **Example**: Use lift tests to discover that a brand campaign is mostly capturing existing demand, then reduce spend accordingly.

### Team Build-Out by Milestone
- **When to use**: When transitioning from agency support to an in-house performance marketing team.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with a generalist growth marketer who can operate across channels.
  2. Add a creative person once the channel needs faster iteration and tighter brand alignment.
  3. Add a dedicated data scientist when the volume and complexity justify deeper analysis and incrementality work.
  4. Use workload and meeting-load calculations to determine when the team is actually in the red.
  5. Hire only when there is enough real work to justify the headcount.
- **Example**: Start hiring conversations around $50K/month spend, but only add headcount when the team’s workload exceeds available days in the quarter.

## Core Advice
- **When deciding whether to invest in paid growth at all**: Start with paid search first, then expand into Meta and video channels if the audience and creative support it — because search captures existing intent and is the most universal starting point.
- **When deciding which channels deserve budget**: Look at where users are already finding you in analytics and turn that channel up rather than guessing — because existing traffic sources are the strongest clue for efficient paid amplification.
- **When testing a new platform**: Use your own customer data to build the tightest lookalike audience first, then widen only after you see a signal — because the closest audience gives the cleanest read on platform fit.
- **When a platform test is inconclusive or fails**: Treat it as learning, not failure, and avoid overreacting to a small test — because weak results often reflect creative or execution mismatch, not channel impossibility.
- **When unsure whether creative is the problem**: Run controlled tests and talk directly to users in focus groups — because metrics show behavior, but not the emotional reason behind it.
- **When choosing between Google, Meta, LinkedIn, and emerging channels**: Start with Google Search, then Meta, and only then consider LinkedIn unless you are clearly enterprise-led — because search is the broadest intent channel and LinkedIn is expensive but powerful for high-value B2B.
- **When advertising on YouTube or other video surfaces**: Lead with emotion, comedy, or happiness — because memorable emotional creative drives later action better than purely informational ads.
- **When entering a market where demand is not yet obvious**: Use display-style awareness media rather than search — because search captures demand, while awareness creates it.
- **When your product is not operationally ready for a market**: Do not scale paid aggressively — because ads without conversion readiness waste spend and can damage perception.
- **When working with an agency**: Use agencies to get started, but plan to transition in-house as spend and complexity grow — because internal teams can go deeper and move with more context.
- **When hiring the first performance marketer**: Hire for data judgment and signal detection, not just platform mechanics — because the hard part is knowing what data matters and what to do next.
- **When your team is overloaded**: Use workload and meeting-time calculations before adding headcount — because many teams are overcommitted, not understaffed.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Paid growth is only for certain companies, while others should focus on SEO, sales, or word of mouth. → **Their view**: Paid is for everyone, at least at the baseline level of paid search — because search is user-driven and increasingly central across platforms.
- **Conventional**: If a platform test fails, the channel probably does not work. → **Their view**: A failed test often means the creative, audience, or execution was wrong — because platform context and user behavior differ.
- **Conventional**: More impressions and top-of-page presence are the main goals in search ads. → **Their view**: Reaching the right user matters more than being number one — because impression share can be ego marketing, while click share and relevance are more meaningful.
- **Conventional**: Attribution models tell you which channels deserve credit. → **Their view**: Attribution is incomplete without incrementality testing — because users may have converted anyway.
- **Conventional**: Agencies are the best long-term way to run performance marketing. → **Their view**: Agencies are useful for starting, but in-house teams usually outperform over time — because they can go deeper and own the context.
- **Conventional**: You should hire more people as soon as work starts to feel busy. → **Their view**: Don’t hire until you’ve proven you’re truly in the red — because many teams can reclaim time by cutting low-value work.
