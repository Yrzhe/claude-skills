# Anton Osika

**Co-founder and CEO** | **Lovable** | Expertise: ai-strategy, product-management, go-to-market, leadership

## Bio
Anton Osika is the co-founder and CEO of Lovable, an AI product-building platform that turns natural-language prompts into working software. He previously created GPT Engineer, a widely adopted open-source project for LLM-powered app generation, and has firsthand experience scaling an AI-native product to extraordinary revenue with a very small team.

His perspective matters because he sits at the intersection of product taste, technical execution, and AI leverage. Anton is especially useful when you need a point of view on building AI-native products, prioritizing fast-moving roadmaps, and hiring for high-ownership startup teams.

## Signature Frameworks

### Minimum Lovable Product -> Lovable Product -> Absolutely Lovable Product
- **When to use**: When defining product quality bars and deciding how far to push beyond a basic MVP.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with a minimum lovable product rather than a merely functional MVP.
  2. Iterate until the product feels genuinely lovable to users.
  3. Keep raising the quality bar toward an absolutely lovable product.
- **Example**: Anton says the company name came from his internal product jargon: build a “minimum lovable product,” then a “lovable product,” then an “absolutely lovable product.”

### Clear Prompting and Debugging Loop
- **When to use**: When using AI agents to build software and the output is close but not correct.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with a simple prompt to get an initial version quickly.
  2. Be highly specific about the desired behavior or UI change.
  3. If the result is wrong, describe exactly what is working and what is not.
  4. Use chat mode or direct editing to clarify, inspect, and correct the output.
  5. Repeat until the product behaves as intended.
- **Example**: In the Airbnb demo, Anton first prompted “Airbnb clone,” then asked for a purchase flow. When the AI created a “Book now” flow instead of “Buy now,” he corrected it by directly editing the UI text and explained that users should avoid vague feedback like “it doesn’t work.”

### Biggest Bottleneck First Prioritization
- **When to use**: When a fast-moving product team has many requests and limited bandwidth.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the single biggest user or product bottleneck.
  2. Focus intensely on solving that problem rather than spreading effort across many initiatives.
  3. Use user conversations, feature requests, and observed friction to validate the bottleneck.
  4. Ship improvements quickly.
  5. Reassess and move to the next biggest bottleneck.
- **Example**: Anton describes Lovable’s prioritization as a “very, very simple algorithm”: find the biggest problem, solve it hard, then pick the next one. He cites current initiatives like making the system more agentic and earlier focus areas like login, persistence, and Stripe.

### Engineering-Led Weekly Planning
- **When to use**: When building a highly technical AI product where the best product decisions are tightly coupled to technical constraints.
- **Steps**:
  1. Maintain a shared board of major problems and rank them.
  2. Run weekly planning to decide what the team will focus on.
  3. Ship during the week with a bias toward iteration and speed.
  4. Hold demos to review what shipped and align the team.
  5. Keep only a short, flexible roadmap and expect it to change.
- **Example**: Anton says the team uses weekly planning, a big FigJam board of ranked problems, and weekly demos. He notes that one recent week was explicitly focused on bug fixes and polish.

### Work Simulation Hiring
- **When to use**: When hiring for elite startup environments where speed, ownership, and collaboration matter more than polished interview answers.
- **Steps**:
  1. Screen for people who deeply care about what they build.
  2. Probe prior work for signs of obsession, detail orientation, and initiative.
  3. Give candidates a hard, unfamiliar problem to reason through.
  4. Run a work simulation for at least a day, often up to a week.
  5. Evaluate how they think, collaborate, and operate in the real environment.
- **Example**: Anton says Lovable often has candidates join a work simulation for at least a day and often a full week, in addition to discussing past work and solving unorthodox problems.

### End-to-End AI Product Design
- **When to use**: When deciding how to apply AI to a product instead of bolting AI onto an existing workflow.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start from the full user experience you want to create.
  2. Identify where AI meaningfully improves the end-to-end flow.
  3. Design the product around that integrated experience.
  4. Avoid forcing customers to retrofit AI into an existing system unless the workflow naturally supports it.
- **Example**: Anton shares that at his earlier startup, building an API for personalized learning was hard to adopt because customers had to retrofit it into their products. His lesson was to start with the end-to-end product experience and then add AI where it solves specific problems.

## Core Advice
- **When you face a blank page with AI product-building tools**: Do the simplest possible first prompt to get a working starting point, then iterate from there — because a fast initial version creates momentum and gives you something concrete to refine instead of overthinking upfront requirements.
- **When you face an AI output that is wrong or incomplete**: Do a precise explanation of what you expected, what worked, and what failed instead of saying “it doesn’t work” — because AI systems respond much better to explicit, structured feedback than vague dissatisfaction.
- **When you face confusion about why an AI tool is behaving a certain way**: Do a conversation with the tool in chat mode and ask how it works, what you’re missing, and what to try next — because interrogating the system helps you both solve the immediate issue and build mental models for future use.
- **When you face the need to become more valuable in an AI-shaped job market**: Do a full week-long project using AI tools to solve a real end-to-end problem — because hands-on repetition around a concrete outcome is the fastest way to move into the top tier of practical AI users.
- **When you face hiring decisions for modern product teams**: Do hiring for generalists with multiple overlapping skills, not narrow specialists alone — because as AI lowers the cost of execution, leverage shifts toward people who can combine product taste, user understanding, design sense, and technical judgment.
- **When you face too many feature requests and roadmap ideas**: Do ruthless prioritization around the single biggest bottleneck first — because concentrated effort on the highest-friction problem creates more user value than diluted progress across many initiatives.
- **When you face building an AI-native company with a small team**: Do heavy internal use of AI tools for coding, experimentation, and even improving your own product — because using AI throughout the workflow compounds team leverage and helps a small team operate at outsized scale.
- **When you face uncertainty about how to structure a fast-moving team**: Do in-person collaboration where possible, especially high-bandwidth moments like shared lunches and spontaneous discussion — because fast iteration benefits from unstructured communication, cross-pollination, and quick course correction that are harder to replicate asynchronously.
- **When you face the temptation to commercialize a cool AI capability directly**: Do product design from the end-to-end user experience first, then insert AI where it meaningfully improves the flow — because users adopt complete solutions to real problems more readily than standalone AI components that require painful retrofitting.
- **When you face the challenge of attracting exceptional startup talent**: Do explicit signaling about the intensity, urgency, and ambition of the mission — because a strong filter repels people seeking comfort and attracts those energized by difficult, high-impact work.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: MVPs should be as minimal as possible; polish can come later. → **Their view**: Aim for a minimum lovable product, not just a minimum viable one. — because Anton believes products win because people love them, and that emotional quality should be designed in from the start rather than deferred.
- **Conventional**: The most important future skill is deep specialization, especially in engineering. → **Their view**: Generalists with broad skill coverage will become more valuable than narrowly specialized contributors. — because AI handles more execution, so the highest-leverage people can bridge product, design, architecture, user understanding, and implementation.
- **Conventional**: To build AI products, add AI features onto existing products or expose AI through APIs. → **Their view**: Start with the end-to-end product experience and design AI into it from the beginning. — because retrofitting AI into existing workflows creates adoption friction; users want complete solutions, not extra technical plumbing.
- **Conventional**: Remote-first is the default best setup for modern software teams. → **Their view**: In-office collaboration can be a major speed advantage for small, intense product teams. — because high-bandwidth communication, quick corrections, and even lunch conversations materially improve decision-making and execution speed.
- **Conventional**: The main value of AI coding tools is making engineers more productive. → **Their view**: The bigger opportunity is enabling the 99% of people who do not code to build software directly. — because the highest impact is not marginal productivity gains for developers, but unlocking entrepreneurship and product creation for non-technical people.
