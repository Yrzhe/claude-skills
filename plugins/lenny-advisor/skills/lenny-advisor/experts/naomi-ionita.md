# Naomi Ionita

**Partner / VC** | **Menlo Ventures** | Expertise: pricing, product-led growth, monetization, growth systems

## Bio
Naomi Ionita is a partner at Menlo Ventures focused on early-stage SaaS from seed to Series B. She brings a rare operator’s perspective from leading product growth and monetization at Evernote and Invoice2go, which makes her especially sharp on pricing, packaging, and the systems that turn product usage into revenue.

Her perspective matters most when a company has product traction but is still figuring out how to monetize it without hurting growth. She is especially useful for founders building PLG products, freemium models, or any SaaS business where pricing is tightly linked to usage and customer segmentation.

## Signature Frameworks

### Match Price to Value
- **When to use**: When designing or revisiting pricing for a SaaS product, especially if you want pricing to scale with customer usage and segment value.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the value metric: the unit of value customers get from the product.
  2. Choose a pricing model that naturally escalates as usage or value increases.
  3. Use the value metric to understand which customer segments you are serving.
  4. Package tiers so different segments can self-select based on willingness to pay.
  5. Revisit whether the metric still aligns with how customers derive value over time.
- **Example**: Usage-based models like API calls, messages sent, terabytes stored, or words written; Evernote’s research showed avid users got far more value than casual users, which justified different plans.

### Day One vs Day 100 Feature Packaging
- **When to use**: When deciding what belongs in free, starter, or premium tiers.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the core utility that gets users to the aha moment on day one.
  2. Keep that core utility free if it helps habit formation and top-of-funnel adoption.
  3. Identify advanced features that only become valuable after deeper usage or scale.
  4. Move those advanced features into paid or higher-tier plans.
  5. Map pricing and packaging to the user journey over time.
- **Example**: A feature that matters only once a team has more data or more collaborators should be reserved for a pro or enterprise tier, rather than cluttering the free plan.

### Cross-Functional Pricing Committee
- **When to use**: When setting initial pricing or iterating on monetization in a startup.
- **Steps**:
  1. Form a cross-functional group rather than leaving pricing to one team.
  2. Include product, data, growth, sales, finance, and rev ops as relevant.
  3. Talk to customers through surveys and interviews.
  4. Rank features by importance using must-have / nice-to-have / not-necessary or point-allocation methods.
  5. Measure willingness to pay using structured pricing research.
  6. Iterate pricing and packaging based on the findings.
- **Example**: At PLG companies, Naomi described product and data teams owning pricing experiments; at enterprise SaaS companies, sales, finance, and rev ops also need to be involved.

### Pricing and Packaging Rebalancing
- **When to use**: When a simple price increase is not enough and you need to improve monetization holistically.
- **Steps**:
  1. Assess the full customer journey from individual user to team to enterprise.
  2. Rebalance free, starter, pro, and enterprise offerings.
  3. Adjust feature gates, quotas, and value metrics together.
  4. Use pricing changes as part of a broader monetization strategy, not in isolation.
  5. Test the impact on conversion, upgrade rate, retention, and ARPU.
- **Example**: At Invoice2go, Naomi’s team doubled the upgrade rate from starter to pro while also increasing the pro price by about 30% by rethinking what belonged in each tier.

### Modern Growth Stack
- **When to use**: When building or buying tools to replace internal growth infrastructure and scale product-led growth.
- **Steps**:
  1. Use data infrastructure to make customer and product data accessible across the company.
  2. Enable workflows that connect product, growth, sales, finance, and operations.
  3. Automate repetitive growth tasks like experimentation, billing, personalization, and monetization.
  4. Measure impact in hard ROI terms: cost reduction and revenue generation.
  5. Prefer purpose-built tools over building everything in-house when possible.
- **Example**: Reverse ETL tools like Hightouch or Census, experimentation tools like Eppo, and billing tools like Orb or Metronome were cited as parts of the stack.

## Core Advice
- **When launching a B2B product and thinking about monetization**: start charging earlier rather than waiting too long, even if you use a free beta or freemium model — because charging creates real feedback loops and validates willingness to pay.
- **When your free product is driving habit formation and top-of-funnel growth**: keep the core aha-moment free, but design the paid path from the start — because freemium works best when it shepherds users into paid plans.
- **When users only pay because they feel guilty about usage**: raise prices or add tiers — because guilt-based conversion usually means the free version is too generous.
- **When you have both casual users and power users**: create multiple plans that reflect different value levels — because one premium tier leaves money on the table.
- **When deciding what belongs in free versus paid tiers**: put day-one utility in free and reserve day-100 or scale-dependent features for paid — because advanced features are more valuable after deeper engagement.
- **When choosing a pricing model**: prefer a hybrid model with a subscription base plus usage or quota component — because pure usage-based pricing is often hard for buyers to budget.
- **When setting initial pricing without much market data**: use customer interviews, feature ranking, and Van Westendorp-style willingness-to-pay questions — because structured research reduces guesswork.
- **When testing pricing changes on a public pricing page**: run controlled experiments, ideally by geography or other constrained segments — because pricing tests are hard to isolate.
- **When evaluating whether a pricing change worked**: measure long-term churn, not just short-term conversion or ARPU — because bad pricing can look good early and fail later.
- **When your product can spread from individual use to team or enterprise adoption**: optimize for growth first and monetize later at the individual level — because wall-to-wall adoption can create much larger upside.
- **When enterprise buyers are negotiating and you suspect you are underpriced**: ask for more than you think you can get, and accept some deal loss — because price resistance reveals willingness to pay.
- **When your team is still building pricing infrastructure in-house**: consider buying modern tools for experimentation, billing, metering, and reverse ETL — because purpose-built tools speed monetization and save engineering time.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Wait until the product is mature before charging users. → **Their view**: Start monetizing early; waiting too long leaves money on the table and weakens your pricing signal — because early payment is a key PMF signal.
- **Conventional**: A single premium tier is enough for most startups. → **Their view**: One premium tier is often a mistake — because casual users and power users have very different willingness to pay.
- **Conventional**: Set pricing once and move on. → **Their view**: Pricing should be revisited every 6 to 12 months — because product value and customer segments evolve.
- **Conventional**: Pure usage-based pricing is the future of SaaS. → **Their view**: Most companies should use a hybrid model — because buyers want predictability and budgetability.
- **Conventional**: Maximize revenue from every user as early as possible. → **Their view**: For multiplayer PLG products, under-monetize individuals to drive adoption and monetize teams later — because network effects can create larger long-term value.
- **Conventional**: Pricing changes are mainly about raising prices. → **Their view**: The biggest gains usually come from rebalancing pricing and packaging holistically — because tier design and feature gates matter as much as price.
