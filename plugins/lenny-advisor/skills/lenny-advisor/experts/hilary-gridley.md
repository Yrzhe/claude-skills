## Bio
Hilary Gridley is the Head of Core Product at Whoop, with prior senior product leadership experience at Big Health and Dropbox. Her perspective matters because she combines strong product taste with unusually practical frameworks for emotional resilience, team behavior change, and AI adoption.

She is especially useful when the problem is not “what should we build?” but “how do we help people think, act, and work better?” Her advice is grounded in real operating pressure: stakeholder management, leadership alignment, and building habits that actually stick.

## Signature Frameworks

### Take a Punch
- **When to use**: When you or someone on your team feels criticized, embarrassed, misunderstood, or worried that an important stakeholder now has a negative impression of them.
- **Steps**:
  1. Notice the emotional reaction and name the fear underneath it.
  2. Identify the narrative you're afraid the other person now believes about you.
  3. Do not litigate the past or try to defensively correct the record.
  4. Ask: what is one small action I can take next that demonstrates the opposite of that narrative?
  5. Take that action quickly to restore agency and move forward.
- **Example**: After laughing when Whoop's CTO suggested ketamine tracking, Gridley felt humiliated and worried she looked dismissive of serious health issues. Instead of explaining herself defensively, she quickly researched another emerging public health issue—sports betting—and sent a thoughtful follow-up note building on the CTO's idea.

### Behavioral Activation for Work
- **When to use**: When someone is spiraling, stuck in negative thinking, burned out, or waiting to feel better before taking action.
- **Steps**:
  1. Recognize the downward spiral or negative thought loop.
  2. Reject the assumption that you must feel better before acting.
  3. Choose a very small action that can interrupt the spiral.
  4. Act first, even if motivation is low.
  5. Use the resulting momentum or mood improvement to continue.
- **Example**: Instead of waiting to feel better before responding to a text, exercising, or engaging, you act first because action is what changes the mood.

### Build Mental Models of How Leaders Think
- **When to use**: When teams are slow, over-dependent on approvals, confused by leadership decisions, or unable to anticipate stakeholder reactions.
- **Steps**:
  1. Pay close attention in meetings to what leaders say and the “note behind the note.”
  2. Capture verbatim comments and your interpretation of why they said them.
  3. Share recurring patterns with your team through notes, Slack updates, or team meetings.
  4. Translate feedback into principles, risk tolerances, and decision criteria.
  5. Help the team act from an understanding of how leaders think, not just what they said once in a document.
- **Example**: When Whoop CEO Will says products should “feel like the future,” Gridley interprets that as elegant, low-cost, high-impact touches—like using a member’s own data in explanations—to make the experience feel magical and futuristic.

### Magic Questions
- **When to use**: When you need to understand how another person thinks, especially a leader, legal/compliance partner, or manager, without relying on vague open-ended explanations.
- **Steps**:
  1. Turn your question into a concrete statement of what you think is true.
  2. End with “Do you agree?” or “Is that right?”
  3. Observe what they say yes to, no to, or refine.
  4. Use their corrections to infer their actual mental model.
  5. Repeat to calibrate your judgment over time.
- **Example**: Instead of asking legal teams to explain the rules abstractly, ask scenario-based questions like, “If the copy said X and the flow worked like Y, would that be okay?” to tease out the real decision logic.

### Habit Formation via Consistency, Friction Reduction, and Reward Loops
- **When to use**: When trying to change team behavior, such as increasing AI adoption, encouraging healthier work habits, or building new skills.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with very small, easy actions that can be done daily.
  2. Optimize for consistency over intensity.
  3. Reduce friction by avoiding high-stakes or deadline-driven first use cases.
  4. Design reward loops that are immediate, emotional, and powerful.
  5. Publicly reinforce desired behaviors so people feel great when they do them.
- **Example**: For AI adoption, Gridley created “30 Days of GPT” with one tiny task per day, often unrelated to core work at first, so people could build the habit without deadline pressure.

### What If I’m Wrong?
- **When to use**: When you strongly disagree with a leader's decision or feedback and need to respond productively.
- **Steps**:
  1. Pause your instinct to poke holes in the decision.
  2. Ask what would have to be true for the other person to be right.
  3. Infer the hidden context, pressure, or insight they may be acting on.
  4. If you still disagree, explain their rationale fairly to your team.
  5. Then execute well enough to learn whether the decision works.
- **Example**: When she disagrees with leadership, she still tries to explain to her team why the decision makes sense from that leader’s perspective, even if she personally would have chosen differently.

## Core Advice
- **When you face criticism or fear that someone important now thinks poorly of you**: Do one small follow-up action that demonstrates the opposite of the negative narrative you're worried about — because evidence changes perception better than argument.
- **When you get tough feedback and feel the urge to explain why it's unfair**: Let yourself feel bad briefly, but move on quickly instead of litigating the past — because defensiveness usually deepens the spiral.
- **When your team is afraid to speak up in meetings**: Teach them how to take a punch so they become less afraid of saying the wrong thing — because fear, not lack of ideas, is often the blocker.
- **When your organization feels slow because too many people need to review or approve everything**: Invest in helping people understand how leaders think, not just what the strategy document says — because shared context reduces dependency.
- **When you disagree with a leader's decision**: First try to understand what hidden insight or context would make their view reasonable before pushing back — because this improves judgment and credibility.
- **When you want to understand someone's thinking more accurately**: Use statement-based questions like “Here’s what I think is true—do you agree?” instead of broad open-ended questions — because concrete scenarios reveal real mental models.
- **When you're trying to drive AI adoption on your team**: Start with tiny, low-friction daily uses that are fun or easy, not high-stakes work tasks — because habits form through repetition and reward.
- **When you're trying to build any new team habit**: Design the reward loop so the behavior feels immediately and emotionally rewarding — because people repeat what feels good right away.
- **When you want your team to have sustainable performance rather than burnout**: Publicly reward behaviors like self-care, hobbies, and healthy boundaries, not just heroic overwork — because teams copy what gets praised.
- **When someone on your team seems depleted or stuck**: Ask what they do for joy and help them identify daily activities that restore them — because energy and performance are linked.
- **When you're worried AI will reduce entry-level learning opportunities**: Use AI to compress feedback loops and increase reps, rather than preserving inefficient old training models — because practice and judgment-building can accelerate.
- **When you're learning a skill that normally develops slowly on the job**: Build or use AI simulations that generate repeated practice scenarios and instant feedback — because reps matter more than waiting for rare real-world cases.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: If someone has the wrong impression of you, you should explain yourself and correct the record. → **Their view**: Don’t litigate the impression; counter-program it with action — because action is more persuasive and less defensive.
- **Conventional**: To change team behavior, you teach the skill, assess it, and enforce accountability. → **Their view**: Treat behavior change as habit formation with friction reduction and reward loops — because people adopt what is easy, repeated, and rewarding.
- **Conventional**: Organizational slowness is mainly a process or approvals problem. → **Their view**: It’s often a transparency and communication problem — because people lack context and mental models of how leaders think.
- **Conventional**: Your job as a product leader is to influence the CEO toward your vision. → **Their view**: Your job is first to understand the CEO’s vision and operationalize it — because leverage comes from execution, not ego.
- **Conventional**: AI threatens junior talent development because entry-level grunt work is how people learn. → **Their view**: AI can improve learning by dramatically increasing reps and shortening feedback loops — because the old model was inefficient.
- **Conventional**: The best product people naturally gravitate to prestigious, familiar product spaces. → **Their view**: Strong product craft should be applied to harder, less glamorous problems — because that’s where it matters most.
