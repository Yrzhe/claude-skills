# Howie Liu

**Co-founder and CEO** | **Airtable** | Expertise: ai-strategy, product-management, leadership, design

## Bio
Howie Liu is the co-founder and CEO of Airtable, where he has spent more than a decade building and evolving the company’s no-code platform. His perspective matters because he is not just talking about AI transformation in theory—he is actively trying to reorient a large, established software company around an AI-native future.

What makes him distinctive is his insistence that legacy companies must “refound” themselves for the AI era, not merely bolt on features. He combines strategic thinking with unusually deep product and UX involvement, making him especially useful for leaders navigating platform shifts, product reinvention, and org design.

## Signature Frameworks

### Refound the company for AI
- **When to use**: When an established software company is trying to adapt its product and org to the AI era.
- **Steps**:
  1. Ask: if we were founding this company today with the same mission, how would we build it in a fully AI-native way?
  2. Evaluate whether existing assets are true advantages or legacy baggage.
  3. Identify the durable primitives or building blocks from the current product that can accelerate the AI-native vision.
  4. If the current company cannot credibly execute the mission better than a fresh startup could, consider selling and starting the next version.
- **Example**: Airtable’s no-code primitives become reliable building blocks for AI agents creating business apps, giving it an advantage over a pure from-scratch vibe-coding approach for enterprise use cases.

### Fast thinking / slow thinking org design
- **When to use**: When a company needs to ship AI capabilities quickly while still investing in foundational infrastructure and enterprise-scale systems.
- **Steps**:
  1. Separate work into a fast-thinking group focused on near-weekly AI shipping and experimentation.
  2. Create a slow-thinking group focused on deliberate, longer-horizon infrastructure and scale investments.
  3. Use the fast group to create top-of-funnel excitement and discover new use cases.
  4. Use the slow group to turn early adoption into durable retention, expansion, and enterprise readiness.
- **Example**: Airtable split EPD so its AI platform group could ship rapidly, while another group handled slower work like HyperDB and large-scale data infrastructure.

### Play, prototype, then eval
- **When to use**: When exploring new AI product capabilities or form factors that are still ambiguous and not yet well-defined.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with open-ended experimentation and “play” rather than rigid specs.
  2. Build functional prototypes instead of decks or PRDs to understand the real UX.
  3. Try many prompts, workflows, and use cases to discover where the product actually works well.
  4. Once the form factor and target use cases are clearer, define evals to measure and improve quality systematically.
- **Example**: For custom code generation, first test many scenarios to see what feels useful, then introduce evals after the team understands the right use cases and quality bar.

### Collapse role silos into full-stack operators
- **When to use**: When teams need to move faster in AI product development and traditional handoffs are slowing execution.
- **Steps**:
  1. Raise the baseline expectation that each function understands adjacent disciplines.
  2. Encourage PMs, designers, and engineers to become minimally competent in the other two areas.
  3. Reduce dependency chains by enabling individuals to prototype, demo, and iterate more independently.
  4. Apply the same principle beyond product to marketing, sales, and other functions.
- **Example**: PMs become PM-prototypers with design sensibility, designers understand technical constraints, and AEs can demo AI products without relying entirely on sales engineers.

### IC CEO / chief tastemaker
- **When to use**: When the product is undergoing major reinvention and leadership cannot rely on high-level abstraction alone.
- **Steps**:
  1. Stay deeply engaged with the product and underlying AI tools on a daily basis.
  2. Cut low-value recurring meetings to create time for hands-on exploration and timely discussions.
  3. Use the product yourself aggressively, even wastefully, to uncover strategic insights.
  4. Lead through product taste and direct exposure to what is technically and experientially possible.
- **Example**: Howie describes himself as likely Airtable’s highest inference-cost user, using AI to analyze sales transcripts, generate insights, and pressure-test product possibilities firsthand.

## Core Advice
- **When you face an AI platform shift that threatens your existing product**: Do a clean-slate exercise and ask how you would pursue the same mission if you were starting today as a fully AI-native company — because this reveals whether your current assets are real advantages or just baggage.
- **When you face slow execution on AI initiatives**: Create a dedicated fast-moving team with permission to ship frequently and experiment aggressively — because AI product discovery depends on rapid iteration.
- **When you face uncertainty about what AI can actually do for your product**: Use the tools yourself repeatedly, not just through reports — because AI capabilities are experiential and intuition comes from direct use.
- **When you face a culture that treats AI adoption as a side task**: Explicitly give people permission to play, cancel meetings, and explore — because curiosity-driven exploration produces better ideas than checkbox adoption.
- **When you face debates stuck in docs, decks, or PRDs**: Build prototypes instead of presentations — because AI UX is hard to evaluate abstractly.
- **When you face pressure to optimize AI costs too early**: Spend the compute on high-value work anyway — because the ROI can be far higher than the raw inference bill suggests.
- **When you face role boundaries that slow product work**: Cross-train PMs, designers, and engineers — because AI rewards broad competence and fewer handoffs.
- **When you face a novel AI feature idea and want to improve quality**: Start with open-ended exploration, then add evals later — because early evals can prematurely narrow the solution space.
- **When you face the challenge of learning new AI skills mid-career**: Build small real projects — because practical building develops intuition much faster than passive study.
- **When you face leadership drift away from the product details**: Reduce standing meetings and reclaim time for product work — because reinvention requires direct engagement with what’s changing.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: As companies scale, CEOs should step away from the details and manage through layers, process, and high-level metrics. → **Their view**: In periods of AI-driven reinvention, CEOs need to act more like ICs and stay close to product details — because the key decisions span UX, technology, and business model.
- **Conventional**: Organizing teams by feature area or business unit is the best way to scale product development. → **Their view**: AI work should be split into fast-thinking and slow-thinking modes — because experimentation and infrastructure have different tempos.
- **Conventional**: You should tightly control AI costs and avoid expensive inference-heavy workflows. → **Their view**: Spend compute aggressively on high-value problems — because the ROI can be extraordinary.
- **Conventional**: Evals are the right starting point for building AI products. → **Their view**: Start with vibes and open-ended experimentation — because you need to discover the form factor before measuring it.
- **Conventional**: Specialization is the path to effectiveness; everyone should stay in their lane. → **Their view**: AI rewards role collapse and broad competence — because fewer handoffs mean faster iteration.
- **Conventional**: Legacy companies can add a few AI features and update messaging to stay relevant. → **Their view**: Most companies need to be effectively refounded for AI — because this shift changes the product at a fundamental level.
