# Jake Knapp and John Zeratsky

**Authors of *Sprint* and *Make Time*; co-founders and venture investors** | **Character Capital** | Expertise: product design, product strategy, time management, startup acceleration

## Bio
Jake Knapp and John Zeratsky are former Google and Google Ventures leaders who helped popularize the Design Sprint as a way to rapidly test product ideas and reduce startup risk. They later co-authored *Make Time*, bringing the same structured, experimental mindset to personal productivity and attention management.

Their perspective matters because they combine product thinking, behavioral design, and startup execution. They are especially useful when you need a practical system for focusing attention, designing better days, or validating a risky product idea quickly.

## Signature Frameworks

### Make Time
- **When to use**: When you feel busy and reactive, but want to consistently make time for meaningful work or life priorities.
- **Steps**:
  1. Choose a daily Highlight: decide what you want the highlight of your day to be.
  2. Use Laser tactics: create barriers to distraction so you can focus on the highlight.
  3. Use Energize tactics: support attention with sleep, exercise, food, and environment.
  4. Reflect: review what worked, what did not, and adjust through small experiments.
- **Example**: Jake set “prepare for this podcast” as his highlight, blocked focus time for it, then reflected when distraction derailed the first block and a family sledding moment became the true highlight of the day.

### Daily Highlight
- **When to use**: At the start of each day or the night before, when deciding what matters most today.
- **Steps**:
  1. Ask: What would give me the most satisfaction today?
  2. Ask: What would bring me the most joy today?
  3. Ask: What is the most urgent thing I need to get done today?
  4. Write the chosen highlight down and place it somewhere visible.
  5. Protect 60-90 minutes for it, ideally when your energy is strongest.
- **Example**: John’s highlights are often project-oriented work like writing, decks, or portfolio support, but sometimes the right highlight is cooking dinner with friends or going for a run.

### Design Your Day
- **When to use**: When your calendar feels like something happening to you instead of a tool you control.
- **Steps**:
  1. Treat your calendar as a canvas, not just a record of meetings.
  2. Block time for focus work before others can claim it.
  3. Template recurring time buckets for different kinds of work.
  4. Include real-life activities too, such as exercise, meals, and transitions.
  5. Review what actually happened and refine the template over time.
- **Example**: John protects the first half of Monday, Wednesday, and Thursday for focus work, while Tuesday and Friday mornings are reserved for meetings.

### Busy Bandwagon + Infinity Pools
- **When to use**: When you feel trapped in constant busyness and compulsive checking of apps, email, or feeds.
- **Steps**:
  1. Recognize the busy bandwagon: the cultural pressure to always seem busy and responsive.
  2. Identify infinity pools: endlessly replenishing sources of content like email, social feeds, news, and streaming.
  3. Notice how the two reinforce each other into reactive behavior.
  4. Change defaults by adding friction and reducing access.
  5. Re-center your day around intentional priorities instead of reactive loops.
- **Example**: They describe email as one of the hardest infinity pools to control because it feels productive while still pulling attention into endless replenishment.

### Laser / Barrier-Based Focus
- **When to use**: When willpower is not enough to resist distraction during focused work.
- **Steps**:
  1. Remove distracting apps from your phone.
  2. Log out of distracting websites on your computer.
  3. Add friction with two-factor authentication or browser extensions.
  4. Physically separate yourself from distraction sources when possible.
  5. Reset expectations with others so you do not feel pressure to respond instantly.
- **Example**: John stays logged out of Twitter and LinkedIn, uses a Chrome extension to remove the LinkedIn feed, and relies on the login screen as a speed bump that interrupts automatic checking.

### Reflect as Scientific Method
- **When to use**: When habits or productivity tactics are inconsistent and you need a sustainable way to improve.
- **Steps**:
  1. Treat each day as an experiment, not a verdict on your worth.
  2. Check whether your highlight happened.
  3. Identify what helped or blocked it.
  4. Adjust one variable at a time, such as timing, environment, or distraction barriers.
  5. Repeat without self-judgment.
- **Example**: Jake refined his daily calendar template after realizing his assumptions about breakfast, dog walks, and morning routines were inaccurate.

### Design Sprint
- **When to use**: When a team needs to quickly test a product idea, reduce risk, or get unstuck on product-market fit questions.
- **Steps**:
  1. Clear space for a focused, structured sprint week.
  2. Turn abstract ideas into a concrete prototype quickly.
  3. Test the prototype with customers within five days.
  4. Use customer reactions to evaluate strategy and next steps.
  5. Repeat or adapt based on what you learn.
- **Example**: Jake described how an early focused week on what became Google Meet transformed a stalled 20% project by building a prototype and putting it in front of colleagues instead of debating plans endlessly.

## Core Advice
- **When you face a day full of reactive work and feel like your important work never gets done**: Do choose one daily highlight and protect 60-90 minutes for it — because a single meaningful win can make a day feel successful, even if the rest is messy.
- **When you face constant distraction from social apps, email, or news**: Do remove the most distracting apps from your phone and log out of them on desktop — because willpower is unreliable and friction interrupts automatic checking.
- **When you face pressure to respond instantly to messages and email**: Do reset expectations with an email signature, autoresponder, or direct conversation — because reducing others’ expectations also reduces your own guilt.
- **When you face an overflowing inbox that keeps generating more work**: Do slow your inbox by checking and replying less frequently — because fast replies accelerate the loop and create more incoming messages.
- **When you face a calendar that feels imposed on you**: Do use your calendar as a design tool by blocking focus time and scheduling routines — because time goes where the calendar directs it.
- **When you face repeated failure to stick with a productivity tactic**: Do treat it as an experiment and return to the system instead of judging yourself — because sustainable improvement comes from iteration, not perfection.
- **When you face low energy and poor focus during your highlight time**: Do prioritize sleep and exercise before chasing advanced optimization tactics — because attention depends on physical energy.
- **When you face compulsive phone use at night or first thing in the morning**: Do keep your phone out of the bedroom — because it prevents the phone from becoming the default last and first activity of the day.
- **When you face a need to stay informed without getting trapped in the news cycle**: Do consume news in slower, higher-signal formats — because important information will still matter later.
- **When you face a risky startup idea with major behavior change or trust barriers**: Do run a design sprint to prototype and test the concept in days — because customer reactions are more valuable than internal debate.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Productivity is about getting faster and more efficient at handling everything in front of you. → **Their view**: Productivity should start with choosing what matters most and changing defaults so that it happens — because inboxes, meetings, and feeds expand endlessly.
- **Conventional**: If you are disciplined enough, you can resist distraction with willpower. → **Their view**: Willpower is not enough; you need environmental friction and system design — because apps and feeds are engineered to be frictionless and compelling.
- **Conventional**: A good day means getting a lot done. → **Their view**: A good day may only need one meaningful highlight — because most days are messy and one intentional moment can anchor satisfaction.
- **Conventional**: You need to stay constantly up to date on news, messages, and social feeds. → **Their view**: Really important information will find you; you can safely step back — because constant checking creates stress and attention residue.
- **Conventional**: Calendars are a necessary evil and mostly a place where meetings get dumped. → **Their view**: Your calendar should be an active design tool — because proactive allocation creates leverage.
- **Conventional**: Teams should think longer and plan more before building. → **Their view**: Teams should prototype and test quickly, often within five days — because customer reactions beat internal debate.
