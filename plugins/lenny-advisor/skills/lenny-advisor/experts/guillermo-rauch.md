# Guillermo Rauch

**Founder and CEO** | **Vercel** | Expertise: ai-strategy, design, engineering-management, product-management

## Bio
Guillermo Rauch is the founder and CEO of Vercel, the company behind v0 and Next.js, and a longtime open-source builder whose tools shape how modern web products are made. His perspective matters because he sits at the intersection of product, design, and infrastructure, and he has a clear point of view on how AI changes who can build software and how fast teams can ship.

## Signature Frameworks

### Experience-first AI product building
- **When to use**: When you want to turn an idea into a working product quickly, especially for prototypes, interfaces, or new product concepts.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start by describing the end-user experience you want, not the implementation details.
  2. Let the AI generate a first version of the product or interface.
  3. Iterate by giving direct feedback on what should change in the experience, style, or behavior.
  4. Only go deeper into code or infrastructure once the product direction feels right.
  5. Deploy and test the result in a real environment.
- **Example**: He described building a flight radar app on a flight by asking v0 to build “the best flight radar on the planet,” then refining the design and only later connecting it to a real flight data API.

### Increase exposure hours
- **When to use**: When trying to improve product taste, design judgment, and product quality.
- **Steps**:
  1. Spend more time watching people use your product.
  2. Use great products yourself and study their interaction details.
  3. Ship work publicly and observe how people respond.
  4. Review customer demos and live usage regularly.
  5. Use what you learn to refine product decisions and develop better judgment.
- **Example**: Vercel’s internal principle of “increasing exposure hours” includes watching customers use products, inviting live demos, and using customer products personally to sharpen judgment.

### AI coaching and iteration loop
- **When to use**: When an AI-generated product output is close but not right, or when you're stuck during generation.
- **Steps**:
  1. Treat the AI like a collaborator rather than a one-shot generator.
  2. Give simple, direct feedback on what is wrong or what should change.
  3. If the current path is failing, explicitly tell it to try a different approach.
  4. Use screenshots, style references, or named design tokens to steer it.
  5. Repeat until the output matches the intended product experience.
- **Example**: Rauch said he often gets unstuck by telling v0, “Try something else,” and showed how he iterated from a Supreme-style contact form to a more serious Charles Schwab-style version.

### Escape hatches for AI-native development
- **When to use**: When AI gets you most of the way there but you need reliability, debugging, or deeper control.
- **Steps**:
  1. Use AI to generate the initial product and code.
  2. Inspect the generated code when needed.
  3. If the AI gets stuck, move into manual or hybrid workflows.
  4. Use other tools or models to reason about specific issues.
  5. Return to the AI workflow once the blocker is resolved.
- **Example**: Rauch shared that a design engineer at Luma Labs copied code from a deeply iterated v0 project into ChatGPT o1 to solve a problem after getting stuck.

### Community-first prompting
- **When to use**: When starting from a blank page is hard or when you want to accelerate with proven starting points.
- **Steps**:
  1. Browse community-created projects for relevant starting points.
  2. Fork an existing project instead of prompting from scratch.
  3. Modify the fork with prompts to fit your use case.
  4. Reuse and improve patterns contributed by others.
  5. Share your own improved version back to the community.
- **Example**: v0 Community works like a compounding system where users fork shared apps and iterate on them, similar to Figma files or open source.

## Core Advice
- **When you face a blank page and don't know how to prompt an AI builder**: Do not start from scratch; browse community examples, fork one, and modify it with prompts — because a strong starting point reduces writer’s block and gives the model concrete structure.
- **When you face weak or generic AI-generated design output**: Give the model stylistic references and meaningful design tokens like “neobrutalist,” “minimalist,” or “newspaper-like” — because AI responds better to recognizable concepts than vague requests for “better design.”
- **When you face an AI output that is close but not right**: Treat the model like a collaborator and give direct feedback such as “make it more serious,” “make it pop,” or “try something else” — because iterative coaching often unlocks better alternatives faster than technical instructions.
- **When you face uncertainty about what skills to build for an AI-heavy future**: Learn how systems work conceptually — math, software fundamentals, infrastructure, and technical vocabulary — because understanding the underlying symbolic systems helps you steer AI better.
- **When you face the challenge of improving your product taste**: Increase your exposure hours by using great products, watching users interact with your product, and shipping often — because taste is built through repeated exposure and feedback.
- **When you face product decisions made in isolation from users**: Create many in-product opportunities for feedback and route it directly to the team — because tight feedback loops accelerate learning, especially in AI products.
- **When you face a complex AI-generated project that gets stuck**: Use escape hatches: inspect the code, narrow the task, or hand it to another model/tool — because current AI systems do better on scoped tasks and hybrid workflows preserve momentum.
- **When you face pressure to over-specify implementation details to AI**: Focus first on describing the user experience and desired outcome — because the model may know better implementation patterns than you do.
- **When you face the temptation to keep product work siloed by function**: Enable designers, PMs, marketers, and sales teams to build and ship artifacts directly with AI tools — because AI expands who can contribute meaningfully to product creation.
- **When you face the need to build high-quality products with limited resources**: Use AI to create realistic, interactive artifacts instead of static specs or mockups — because interactive artifacts reveal edge cases and UX issues earlier.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: AI will mainly help engineers code faster, while product managers and designers still rely on engineers to ship. → **Their view**: AI will make many product roles more full-stack, enabling designers, PMs, marketers, and others to directly build and ship production-quality software — because many software tasks are translation tasks AI can handle.
- **Conventional**: Taste is innate—you either have it or you don't. → **Their view**: Taste is a skill that can be developed through exposure, observation, and repeated contact with real users and great products — because “increasing exposure hours” builds judgment.
- **Conventional**: Learning to code deeply is the main path to staying relevant in software. → **Their view**: Knowing how systems work conceptually matters more than mastering every low-level specialization — because AI will absorb many narrow coding tasks.
- **Conventional**: AI is a separate category that companies should talk about distinctly from software. → **Their view**: AI will become synonymous with software, and eventually we should stop treating it as separate — because it will be embedded in every part of product creation.
- **Conventional**: The best way to build software is to start with specs, implementation plans, and code structure. → **Their view**: Start with intent and experience first; let code be the output rather than the starting point — because product direction should lead implementation.
