# Mihika Kapoor

**Product leader / design-engineering PM hybrid** | **Figma** | Expertise: zero-to-one product development, product vision, product design, team culture

## Bio
Mihika Kapoor is a product leader at Figma who specializes in building new products from scratch at the intersection of product, design, and engineering. Her perspective matters because she has repeatedly turned ambiguous ideas into tangible products by combining strong vision, visual thinking, and cross-functional collaboration.

Before Figma, she founded Design Nation, a nonprofit focused on democratizing design education, and led products at Meta across commerce and creators. That mix of mission-driven work, platform-scale product experience, and zero-to-one building gives her a distinctive point of view on how products actually get made and adopted.

## Signature Frameworks

### Vision through cross-pollination
- **When to use**: When defining a new product direction or aligning a team around a future state
- **Steps**:
  1. Start from a user insight or observed behavior that reveals a real opportunity.
  2. Bring research, design, and engineering together early instead of working in a linear handoff.
  3. Use prototypes and visual artifacts to make the future tangible.
  4. Anchor the vision in feasibility so the team believes it can actually be built.
  5. Communicate the vision as a concrete future world, not just a feature list.
- **Example**: For FigJam, she started from brainstorms as a highly democratic meeting format and expanded that into a broader vision of making meetings more collaborative and participatory.

### Pain point → solution → proof point pitch
- **When to use**: When you need to get internal buy-in for a new idea
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the pain clearly and make it emotionally legible.
  2. Show the solution visually, ideally with a prototype or mock.
  3. Add proof points such as user testimonials, usage data, or observed behavior.
  4. Repeat the sequence so the audience feels the problem and the future state together.
- **Example**: She described vision pitches at Figma as combining pain point, solution, and proof point rather than relying on a traditional text-heavy memo.

### A-idea before research
- **When to use**: When entering user research or early product exploration with incomplete information
- **Steps**:
  1. Form an opinion early instead of starting from zero.
  2. Make sure the idea is strong enough to provoke reaction, even if it is wrong.
  3. Use user feedback to upgrade the idea from good to great.
  4. Be willing to kill the idea or pivot if external signal contradicts it.
- **Example**: Yuhki advised her to go into research with at least an A- idea so user conversations can refine it to an A+ rather than leaving the team at a B.

### Keep the flame alive
- **When to use**: When driving a zero-to-one initiative through ambiguity, skepticism, or long timelines
- **Steps**:
  1. Treat yourself as the keeper of the idea’s momentum.
  2. Continuously create visibility in the right forums.
  3. Use demos, launches, and company moments to re-energize the organization.
  4. Translate setbacks into learning and forward motion.
  5. Keep the idea emotionally alive until it can stand on its own.
- **Example**: She pushed a rough new product into Figma’s Sales Kickoff keynote with a demo so the company would see and feel the product’s future.

### Build hype through staged exposure
- **When to use**: When a product is early but needs momentum, feedback, and organizational belief
- **Steps**:
  1. Show the product in high-visibility forums before it feels fully finished.
  2. Let people interact with staging or beta versions early.
  3. Use demos and surprise moments to create emotional pull.
  4. Turn internal excitement into external excitement through marketing, sales, and community moments.
- **Example**: For FigJam’s one-year anniversary, the team hid new features as birthday presents in an Easter egg hunt, turning a launch into a playful celebration.

### Culture as trust infrastructure
- **When to use**: When building a high-performing cross-functional team
- **Steps**:
  1. Create rituals that help people know each other beyond work.
  2. Make appreciation and play part of the team’s operating system.
  3. Use culture to strengthen trust so hard moments are handled collaboratively.
  4. Invest in relationships because roadmaps will change but team cohesion compounds.
- **Example**: She created the “hot seat” game at a PM offsite and the “Figgies” awards to help the team bond and feel seen.

## Core Advice
- **When you need to create a compelling product vision**: Build it with users, designers, and engineers together, and make it visual with prototypes rather than only writing a memo — because cross-functional input produces a stronger, more feasible vision, and visuals help people feel the future instead of just reading about it.
- **When you want people to buy into a new idea**: Present it as pain point, solution, and proof point in one artifact — because people respond more strongly to a concrete, emotionally legible story than to abstract strategy language.
- **When you are unsure whether your idea is good enough to test**: Go in with an A- idea and let research sharpen it — because people react more to an opinion than to a blank slate, and even a wrong idea creates useful feedback.
- **When a zero-to-one project starts losing momentum**: Act as the keeper of the flame and keep creating moments of visibility — because early products need active stewardship to survive skepticism and ambiguity.
- **When your team is not excited about a project yet**: Use company-wide forums, demos, and launch moments to make the product feel real — because seeing the product in action creates emotional belief and can convert passive observers into advocates.
- **When you want to build hype for an early product**: Make the product visible before it is perfect and let people experience it early — because early exposure generates feedback, ownership, and momentum that a polished but hidden product cannot.
- **When you are trying to get a new initiative off the ground without formal authority**: Ask people directly to join you and build momentum one yes at a time — because agency matters more than title; people often help if you simply make the ask.
- **When you work with strong opinions on a team**: State your confidence level explicitly and invite disagreement — because clear confidence labels prevent others from over-indexing on your opinion and make room for real debate.
- **When giving or receiving feedback**: Make feedback continuous and two-way, and act on it quickly — because feedback works best as an ongoing exchange, not a rare event, and visible action builds trust.
- **When you need to understand what users or teammates really want**: Ask directly about motivations, not just preferences — because motivations explain behavior and help you tailor both product decisions and collaboration styles.
- **When your product is changing or your priorities get reset**: Treat change as normal and pivot with enthusiasm instead of grief — because in fast-moving companies, adaptability is a competitive advantage and helps teams stay resilient.
- **When you are trying to make a product feel more real in a pitch**: Use small, scrappy changes to existing surfaces instead of trying to build the whole thing — because a tiny but concrete change can communicate the idea better than a large speculative build.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Product vision should be written first and then handed off through a linear process from research to design to engineering. → **Their view**: The strongest vision comes from cross-pollination across functions, with research, design, and engineering shaping the idea together from the start. — because a linear process creates weaker ownership and less innovation; collaboration produces a more believable and differentiated future.
- **Conventional**: You should wait until a product is polished before showing it broadly. → **Their view**: Show early prototypes and rough products in high-visibility forums to build belief and gather signal. — because visibility creates momentum, feedback, and ownership; hiding the product delays learning and enthusiasm.
- **Conventional**: Hackathons are mainly for engineers. → **Their view**: Anyone can use a hackathon to pitch and advance an idea, even if they cannot build it themselves. — because the key is agency and momentum; you can recruit collaborators and use the event to turn an idea into reality.
- **Conventional**: Strong conviction is risky because it can shut down collaboration. → **Their view**: Strong conviction is useful if you explicitly communicate confidence levels and stay open to being wrong. — because people need an opinion to react to, but they also need psychological safety to disagree and refine the idea.
- **Conventional**: Culture work is a nice-to-have that distracts from real product work. → **Their view**: Culture is core product infrastructure because it builds trust, durability, and team cohesion. — because roadmaps change, but strong relationships keep teams aligned and resilient when things get hard.
- **Conventional**: A PM should focus only on their assigned scope. → **Their view**: Your scope is the world; if you care about an idea, you should look for where it fits inside the company or whether it belongs elsewhere. — because great opportunities often sit outside the narrow project boundary, and career direction should follow where your conviction lives.
