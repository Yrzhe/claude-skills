## Signature Frameworks

### Customer-Led Growth Journey Mapping
- **When to use**: When a SaaS company wants to improve growth by aligning acquisition, onboarding, activation, retention, and expansion around customer value rather than funnel stages.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify your best customers: paying, happy, low-maintenance customers who signed up recently enough to remember life before your product.
  2. Research those customers to understand their problem context, trigger moments, evaluation criteria, anxieties, desired outcomes, and what they value most.
  3. Prioritize one customer job to lead with based on willingness to pay, urgency of problem, retention/expansion potential, ease of serving, and any unfair market advantage.
  4. Map the customer journey through the lens of value delivery, typically across struggle, evaluation, and growth phases.
  5. Define key milestones such as problem, interest, first value, value realization, continued value, and growth/expansion.
  6. Assign a KPI to each milestone so you can measure whether customers are progressing or falling off.
  7. Audit current messaging, onboarding, and product experience against the journey map to find misalignments.
  8. Design proactive and reactive experiences, such as onboarding checklists, emails, and win-back flows, to move customers to the next value milestone.
- **Example**: With SparkToro, her team researched best customers, identified a priority customer job, mapped the journey, defined milestone KPIs like first search, repeated searches, list creation, and exports, then used that to improve onboarding and double trial-to-paid conversion.

### Best Customer Research for Jobs-to-Be-Done
- **When to use**: When you need to understand which customer segment and job to prioritize, especially before changing positioning, messaging, onboarding, or go-to-market strategy.
- **Steps**:
  1. Select best customers who are successful with the product and recently converted.
  2. Survey or interview them about what life was like before the product, what triggered their search, what alternatives they considered, what mattered most in a solution, and what they can do now that they couldn't before.
  3. Look for patterns in struggles, motivations, desired outcomes, and product value moments.
  4. Cluster responses into distinct customer jobs.
  5. Choose the job to prioritize based on commercial and strategic criteria.
- **Example**: For SparkToro, they used surveys instead of interviews, identified two customer jobs, and chose the marketer/service-provider-oriented job over the more data-purist job because SparkToro had a stronger unfair advantage there.

### Milestone KPI Model
- **When to use**: When teams have traffic and conversion data but lack a clear way to measure activation, value realization, retention, and re-engagement.
- **Steps**:
  1. Break the customer journey into a small number of milestones.
  2. For each milestone, define the customer behavior that proves value has been reached.
  3. Tie that behavior to a measurable KPI, often based on meaningful product usage.
  4. Use those KPIs to trigger proactive nudges and reactive win-back interventions.
- **Example**: At SparkToro, one milestone KPI was performing a first search; another was completing five or more searches plus creating at least one list; a later milestone included repeated searches, list usage, and using export functionality.

### Voice-of-Customer Messaging Guide
- **When to use**: When rewriting website copy, onboarding emails, product messaging, or positioning and you want messaging grounded in customer language rather than internal opinions.
- **Steps**:
  1. Collect customer language from research.
  2. Identify the hierarchy of what customers value most.
  3. Translate those insights into a messaging guide covering value proposition, competitive advantages, value themes, emotional benefits, functional benefits, and supporting product attributes.
  4. Use the guide as the baseline for website copy, campaigns, onboarding emails, and handoff to copywriters or other teams.
- **Example**: Laudi described producing 5-7 page messaging guides that teams can use across marketing and onboarding; SparkToro used this kind of guidance to build onboarding checklists and emails.

## Core Advice
- **When free or trial users are not converting to paid**: Do customer research with your best recent customers first, then redesign onboarding around the specific features and moments that create value for that ideal customer job — because conversion usually improves when the right users reach the right value moments faster.
- **When growth metrics feel vague or business-centric**: Do a customer-centered journey map with milestone KPIs tied to value delivery instead of relying only on funnel metrics — because funnels flatten customer differences and miss what happens after acquisition.
- **When there is disagreement about the ideal customer**: Do research with paying, happy, low-maintenance customers who signed up in the last 3-6 months and use their patterns to choose a priority customer job — because recent successful customers remember the pre-purchase context clearly.
- **When you are tempted to market to multiple segments at once**: Do one customer job really well first, then expand to adjacent jobs later — because clear positioning and product experience beat broad but weak relevance.
- **When onboarding drop-off or one-time logins are a problem**: Do proactive onboarding plus reactive win-back flows based on whether users hit milestone KPIs — because generic reminders won’t recover users who never reached first value.
- **When messaging debates are driven by internal opinions**: Do messaging based on voice-of-customer language and the hierarchy of what customers say matters most — because mirroring customer language increases resonance and trust.
- **When research feels too slow**: Do lightweight but focused surveys with best customers and use the results to make decisive choices within a few weeks — because clarity is often faster than endless debate.
- **When your website attracts the wrong users**: Do positioning and messaging work that speaks to the customer’s pre-product struggle, desired outcome, and value criteria — because better-fit users improve downstream conversion even without product changes.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Funnels, pirate metrics, MQLs, and SQLs are the standard way to manage growth. → **Their view**: These models are often harmful because they center the business instead of the customer and fail to capture value delivery in recurring-revenue SaaS — because they ignore the problem stage and stop at acquisition.
- **Conventional**: Growth problems should be solved by optimizing aggregate conversion metrics and funnel leaks. → **Their view**: Start by identifying the ideal customer job and optimize for the right customer reaching value — because better-fit customers can improve downstream conversion more than generic funnel tweaks.
- **Conventional**: Personas are the best foundation for marketing and product decisions. → **Their view**: Jobs-to-be-done is more useful because demographics often hide the real motivations and desired outcomes — because similar people can have very different needs.
- **Conventional**: Product onboarding is mainly a product team responsibility. → **Their view**: In freemium or self-serve contexts, onboarding should often be treated as a marketing responsibility or shared cross-functionally — because freemium is a marketing tool and ownership gaps hurt activation.
- **Conventional**: Founders already know their customers well enough. → **Their view**: Founders still need ongoing customer research — because markets change and important knowledge should not stay trapped in one person’s head.
