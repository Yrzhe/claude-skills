## Signature Frameworks

### Product Market Pricing Fit
- **When to use**: Before launch and throughout product development, to validate whether customers will pay for the product, not just like it.
- **Steps**:
  1. Pitch the product value before launch as if you were selling it.
  2. Ask whether customers would pay for it, not whether they like it.
  3. If they say no, ask why to uncover missing value, wrong positioning, or weak segment fit.
  4. Use the feedback to change features, packaging, or target customer.
  5. Repeat as the product evolves.
- **Example**: Porsche tested the Cayenne concept with customers before building it, then refined details like cup holders and transmission choices in car clinics.

### Willingness-to-Pay Conversation Methods
- **When to use**: When you need to estimate value, price thresholds, or purchase likelihood without asking “what should I charge?”
- **Steps**:
  1. Use relative anchors, like comparing against Salesforce or another known product.
  2. Ask acceptable, expensive, and prohibitively expensive price points.
  3. Use purchase-probability scales at different prices.
  4. Run most/least exercises to identify value-driving features.
  5. Use trade-off and simulated buying questions to reveal decision rules.
  6. Ask “why?” after each answer.
- **Example**: Rahul Vohra used acceptable/expensive/prohibitively expensive questions to land on Superhuman’s $30 price point.

### Segmentation by Needs and Willingness to Pay
- **When to use**: When defining target customers, pricing tiers, packaging, or roadmap priorities.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify differences in needs, not just demographics.
  2. Group customers by what they value and will pay for.
  3. Build different offers for each segment.
  4. Choose the first segment based on size, willingness to pay, and fit.
  5. Tailor messaging, sales motion, and roadmap to that segment.
  6. Expand later with differentiated offers.
- **Example**: Eventbrite moved from one product to three plans for casual hosts versus professional organizers.

### Leaders, Fillers, and Killers Packaging
- **When to use**: When designing bundles, tiers, or add-ons.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify leader features that more than half of customers strongly want.
  2. Identify filler features that help the bundle feel complete.
  3. Identify killer features that only a small minority wants.
  4. Bundle leaders and fillers together.
  5. Keep killers as add-ons or separate offers.
- **Example**: A burger, fries, and Coke can be bundled as a Happy Meal, while coffee may be better as an add-on.

### Pricing Model Selection
- **When to use**: When deciding between subscription, usage-based, freemium, hybrid, or another model.
- **Steps**:
  1. Determine whether value is ongoing or episodic.
  2. Check whether usage is predictable or variable.
  3. Assess whether customers want transparency and fairness.
  4. Verify that usage can be measured cleanly.
  5. Choose the pricing metric first, then the structure.
  6. Test alternatives with break-even or scenario exercises.
- **Example**: Michelin moved from per-tire pricing to per-mile pricing; Segment moved from API-based pricing to monthly tracked users.

### Behavioral Pricing and Framing
- **When to use**: When you want to improve conversion or ARPU without changing the product.
- **Steps**:
  1. Set prices around psychological thresholds.
  2. Use good-better-best tiers to steer customers.
  3. Add a decoy tier to make the target plan look better.
  4. Frame prices in smaller units, like per day instead of per month.
  5. Use anchoring and compromise effects.
  6. Avoid overstuffing the entry tier.
- **Example**: A company changed from $49/$79/$149 to $99/$199/$299, added a decoy tier, and increased MRR and ARPU by 30%+ without changing the product.

## Core Advice
- **When designing a new product or feature**: start willingness-to-pay conversations before launch and use them to shape the product, not just the price — because if customers won’t pay, the issue is often product design or segment fit.
- **When customers say they like your product**: ask whether they would pay a specific price, then ask why — because likeability is not the same as willingness to pay.
- **When estimating pricing**: use relative questions, thresholds, and purchase-probability scales instead of asking “what should I charge?” — because direct price questions produce bad data.
- **When prioritizing a roadmap**: prioritize by willingness to pay, not internal opinion or technical ease — because value is usually concentrated in a small set of features.
- **When you think you have segmentation**: check whether you can actually act differently for each segment — because if every segment gets the same treatment, it’s not real segmentation.
- **When launching early-stage products**: pick one segment first and productize for that segment — because trying to serve everyone usually resonates with no one.
- **When packaging a product**: separate leader, filler, and killer features — because bundling low-demand features can reduce willingness to pay.
- **When choosing a pricing model**: choose based on usage patterns, value timing, and measurability — because fashion is a bad reason to pick subscription or usage pricing.
- **When in a downturn**: create a lower-cost de-featured alternative instead of discounting — because discounting resets market expectations downward.
- **When customers push back on price**: use non-pricing levers first, like more value or better terms — because you can often preserve headline price.
- **When pitching a product**: lead with benefits and outcomes, not features — because customers buy value.
- **When setting tiers**: use thresholds and decoys to steer choice — because people are shaped by framing and comparison.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Pricing should be decided after the product is built and launched. → **Their view**: Do willingness-to-pay work before launch; in many cases, price before product. — because you want to discover weak demand before wasting time and money.
- **Conventional**: Pricing belongs in finance. → **Their view**: Pricing should sit in product. — because pricing is a product-design input, not just a control function.
- **Conventional**: Segmentation is mostly personas or demographics. → **Their view**: Real segmentation is based on needs, value, and willingness to pay. — because two similar-looking customers can value the same product very differently.
- **Conventional**: The main pricing question is how much to charge. → **Their view**: How you charge is often more important than how much. — because the pricing metric can unlock value that a simple price increase cannot.
- **Conventional**: Usage-based pricing is the default future for SaaS. → **Their view**: Usage pricing only works when usage, value, and measurement align. — because subscription or hybrid can be better when predictability and fairness matter.
- **Conventional**: Good-better-best is always smart. → **Their view**: Overstuffed entry tiers train customers to buy cheap. — because the middle tier only works if the entry tier is not too generous.
