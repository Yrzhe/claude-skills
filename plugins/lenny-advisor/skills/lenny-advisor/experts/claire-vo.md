# Claire Vo

**Chief Product Officer** | **LaunchDarkly** | Expertise: product-management, leadership, ai-strategy, go-to-market, engineering-management, operations

## Bio
Claire Vo is a multi-time product leader, former founder, engineer, designer, and marketer who has led product at companies like Color, Optimizely, and LaunchDarkly. Her perspective matters because she operates at the intersection of product, engineering, design, and revenue, and she has a rare bias toward action: she thinks in org design, speed, and leverage, not just roadmaps.

She is also the creator of ChatPRD, which gives her a practical, hands-on view of how AI is changing product work. Claire is especially valuable when you need a perspective on scaling without slowing down, expanding scope across functions, or building a stronger product operating model.

## Signature Frameworks

### Career Agency Through Org Problem-Solving
- **When to use**: When you want to grow into a new role, expand scope, or earn a promotion without sounding self-focused.
- **Steps**:
  1. Decide clearly what you want your next role to be.
  2. Tell your manager explicitly what role you want and why.
  3. Identify a real organizational problem or gap that role would solve.
  4. Design a concrete proposal, including org structure, responsibilities, and why you are the right fit.
  5. Make it easy for leadership to say yes by showing how the company benefits, not just you.
- **Example**: When the head of marketing left, Claire drew an org chart with herself leading a combined product and marketing/growth organization, proposed the management structure and job description, and got the role.

### Zone of Genius Audit
- **When to use**: When you're overloaded, unsure what work energizes you, or trying to shape your role around your strengths.
- **Steps**:
  1. Review your calendar over the last month or quarter.
  2. List the major activities you spent time on.
  3. Sort them into buckets: hated it, fine, loved it, and would happily spend all your time on it.
  4. Reduce time spent in the bottom buckets.
  5. Increase time spent in the top bucket and identify what only you do exceptionally well.
- **Example**: Claire described her own zone of genius as traversing across functions like product, engineering, design, data, operations, and revenue, while also moving between strategy and details.

### One-Click-Faster Clock Speed
- **When to use**: When an organization is moving too slowly or defaulting to big-company pace and calendar-driven execution.
- **Steps**:
  1. Set the expectation that leaders should bring timelines in by one iteration.
  2. Translate timelines down one level: year to half, half to quarter, quarter to month, month to week, week to today.
  3. Do not let recurring meetings determine decision timing.
  4. Push for decisions on real timelines based on information needed, not calendar cadence.
  5. Maintain a fast personal SLA so leadership does not become the bottleneck.
- **Example**: Claire tells leaders that if they think something should be done this year, it should be done this half; if this half, then this quarter.

### High-Bar Leadership via Clear Expectations and Feedback
- **When to use**: When trying to raise talent density, improve leadership quality, or address underperformance quickly.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the bar with specific, measurable leadership expectations.
  2. Avoid vague ladders like “manages stakeholders well” and instead make expectations tractable.
  3. Normalize candid feedback as part of team culture.
  4. Tell people clearly when they are not meeting expectations.
  5. If fit is not improving, act quickly to protect team health and performance.
- **Example**: Claire told two leaders who were failing to collaborate that if they did not change immediately, they could not remain in the organization. Both improved, and one became a highly effective manager.

### CPTO Operating Model
- **When to use**: When product, engineering, design, and related R&D functions need tighter alignment and a single accountable leader.
- **Steps**:
  1. Bring product and engineering under one leader who is technically credible.
  2. Optimize for what is best for the organization, not for individual functions.
  3. Treat product, engineering, and design as one builder organization.
  4. Use the structure to improve CEO leverage and create a single owner for R&D investment.
  5. Ensure the leader can handle architecture, org design, operations, and incident accountability.
- **Example**: Claire described comparing the PRD and GitHub during reviews and emphasized that a CPTO must understand both business outcomes and how software gets built.

### AI Skill Adaptation for PMs
- **When to use**: When product managers want to stay ahead of AI-driven changes to their role.
- **Steps**:
  1. Experiment directly with AI tools rather than observing from a distance.
  2. Use AI to automate lower-value drafting and synthesis work.
  3. Study AI-native products through outside-in teardowns.
  4. Build familiarity with non-deterministic product design, including error states and evaluation.
  5. Shift your identity from document producer to builder of the right things, quickly and effectively.
- **Example**: Claire built ChatPRD first as a prompt, then a GPT, then a standalone app to understand both how AI changes PM work and how to build AI-native products.

## Core Advice
- **When you face uncertainty about your career path**: Define your next role clearly, then tell your manager what you want and ask for partnership — because clarity creates direction and explicit communication helps others support you.
- **When you face a desire for promotion**: Frame the conversation around a business problem the new role solves, not your personal desire for advancement — because promotions are easier to justify when they create organizational leverage.
- **When you face limited upward mobility in product**: Look left and right across adjacent functions like marketing, growth, engineering, or operations — because broader scope often comes from lateral expansion.
- **When you face overload from being good at many things**: Identify your zone of genius and shape your role toward work that energizes you — because sustainable performance comes from doing more of what only you do exceptionally well.
- **When you face a slow-moving organization**: Set a one-click-faster expectation on timelines and refuse to let recurring meetings dictate decision speed — because calendars often create artificial slowness.
- **When you face becoming a bottleneck as a leader**: Maintain a fast personal SLA on decisions, approvals, and responses — because your responsiveness sets the pace for the whole org.
- **When you face underperformance or team conflict**: Give direct, unambiguous feedback about what is not working, what must change, and the consequences — because clear feedback is kinder and more effective than vague hints.
- **When you face pressure to act like a big company as you scale**: Remind the organization it can still operate like a startup — because scale often brings unnecessary bureaucracy.
- **When you face anxiety about AI replacing PM work**: Use AI to automate drafting, synthesis, and routine communication while investing more in judgment and influence — because AI raises the value of strategic thinking.
- **When you face wanting to learn AI product skills without deep engineering ability**: Study AI products through teardowns, use no-code tools, and experiment in areas that feel fun — because hands-on learning builds intuition fast.
- **When you face a need to create opportunities that do not obviously exist**: Assume the organization is more bendable than it appears, then engineer your way into the right room — because initiative often creates openings.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Later-stage companies need leaders who teach them to operate like big companies. → **Their view**: They often need leaders who remind them they can still operate like startups. — because scale often introduces avoidable slowness and bureaucracy.
- **Conventional**: Promotion conversations should focus on whether someone deserves the next level. → **Their view**: They should focus on what problem the company needs solved and why that role should exist. — because organizations promote more effectively when the role creates leverage.
- **Conventional**: Being kind in management means softening difficult feedback. → **Their view**: Being clear is kinder than being soft. — because vague feedback delays improvement and hides consequences.
- **Conventional**: PMs should stay in their lane and rely on specialists for adjacent work. → **Their view**: There are no solid lanes; strong builders should cross boundaries. — because cross-functional fluidity creates better leaders and faster execution.
- **Conventional**: Sales-led companies are inferior to product-led companies. → **Their view**: Sales-led companies can build great products and great businesses. — because commercial orientation does not conflict with product craft.
- **Conventional**: A PM’s value comes from personally generating ideas and turning them into specs. → **Their view**: PM value is shifting toward choosing the right things, moving quickly, and delivering outcomes. — because AI can absorb more drafting and synthesis work.
