# Crystal Widjaja

**Chief Product Officer** | **Kumu** | Expertise: growth, product-management, metrics, go-to-market

## Bio
Crystal Widjaja is a product and growth leader best known for building early growth at Gojek and later serving as CPO at Kumu. Her perspective matters because she combines scrappy experimentation with rigorous analytics, especially in consumer products where growth depends on understanding user behavior, not just tracking vanity metrics.

She is particularly strong on Southeast Asian consumer markets, where operational creativity, trust-building, and channel design often matter as much as product features. If you need someone who thinks in funnels, loops, and real-world constraints, her viewpoint is highly practical.

## Signature Frameworks

### Scrappy Wizard-of-Oz Experimentation
- **When to use**: When you need to validate a feature, value proposition, or funnel idea before investing engineering resources.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the user experience you want to test.
  2. Identify the minimum manual or no-code way to simulate that experience.
  3. Run the test with a small but real user sample.
  4. Measure user behavior and conversion, not just opinions.
  5. Only build the real feature after the manual test validates demand.
- **Example**: At Gojek, Crystal tested a subscription concept by putting 100 drivers into a WhatsApp group, having them pitch riders during trips, manually crediting vouchers in the backend, and deducting payment from driver balances—without building the feature first.

### Physics of Growth Model
- **When to use**: When founders need to understand where growth comes from and which levers are realistic to pull.
- **Steps**:
  1. Map the current physics: market, product, business model, and channels.
  2. Identify what is already working in the current system.
  3. Find the biggest constraint or underused lever within those existing physics.
  4. Change one variable at a time rather than making multiple assumptions.
  5. Measure whether the change improves the loop or funnel without breaking the model.
- **Example**: At Gojek, they realized drivers themselves were an underused growth channel. They incentivized drivers to sell GoPay top-ups to riders during trips, which became 60% of acquisition for that product.

### Pre-Conversion Constraint Analysis
- **When to use**: When retention or conversion is weak and teams are tempted to optimize broad top-line metrics without understanding the cause.
- **Steps**:
  1. Avoid starting with retention as the only target.
  2. Identify the step immediately before conversion or repeat usage.
  3. Determine what psychological or functional barrier exists at that step.
  4. Design interventions that remove that specific barrier.
  5. Measure whether solving the barrier improves downstream conversion and retention.
- **Example**: For GoFood, the issue was not just conversion but trust in unfamiliar restaurants. Gojek used Facebook-connected friend purchase data to show that a friend had ordered from a merchant, doubling the likelihood of trying a new restaurant.

### Insight-Driven Instrumentation
- **When to use**: When analytics efforts produce dashboards and metrics but fail to generate actionable decisions.
- **Steps**:
  1. Instrument events with rich contextual properties, not just event names.
  2. Capture the user experience conditions around each action.
  3. Segment behavior by those properties to understand who did what under which conditions.
  4. Turn observations into hypotheses about why behavior happened.
  5. Use those hypotheses to change product, marketing, or operations.
- **Example**: Instead of only tracking that a map screen loaded, Crystal recommends tracking properties like number of visible drivers, city, surge pricing, pickup location, minimum fare, and voucher status so teams can understand why booking conversion changes.

### Retention Benchmarking by Product Frequency
- **When to use**: When evaluating whether a product has real product-market fit versus early vanity growth.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the natural usage frequency of the product (weekly, monthly, etc.).
  2. Measure cohort retention at the first relevant interval.
  3. Look for retention to flatten within 2-3 frequency periods.
  4. For free products, aim for roughly 60% retention at scale.
  5. For very early friends-and-family users, expect much higher retention, near 80%.
- **Example**: Crystal said early Gojek had 60-70% retention because it solved a major real-world problem, which gave her confidence the company would succeed if the product simply kept working.

## Core Advice
- **When you face uncertainty about whether a new feature will work**: Do a scrappy manual experiment before building the feature — because real user behavior is the best data you can get, and early ideas are cheap to test with no-code or operational hacks.
- **When you face a very small user base and think experimentation is impossible**: Do run experiments even with samples as small as 30 users — because the trend signal is often directionally useful even if precision is lower; 30 real data points are far better than zero.
- **When you face poor retention and don’t know what to improve**: Do analyze the step immediately before conversion or repeat usage instead of optimizing retention directly — because users make decisions through sequential events, so fixing the barrier right before the key action often improves retention more effectively than targeting retention abstractly.
- **When you face low activation from landing pages or app opens**: Do revisit your copy and message the user’s pain point rather than just your solution — because if users bounce before experiencing the product, the issue is often framing and resonance, not the product itself.
- **When you face long, painful user paths to conversion**: Do identify the longest high-intent path and shorten it first — because if users are already trying to complete the journey, reducing friction there creates a more certain win than inventing a new feature.
- **When you face churn caused by temporary user circumstances**: Do add pause or snooze options instead of forcing users to cancel — because a temporary problem should have a temporary solution; this preserves the relationship and reduces costly reacquisition later.
- **When you face confusion around a novel product concept**: Do explain it using a familiar mental model or visual metaphor — because users adopt new products faster when they can map them to something they already understand.
- **When you face analytics dashboards full of numbers but few decisions**: Do instrument user journeys with contextual properties that explain why behavior happened — because measurements alone are observations; insights require context that lets teams act differently.
- **When you face hiring for an early growth role**: Do hire someone with strong statistical intuition and test them with an experiment-design case study — because growth decisions are easy to get wrong if someone misreads ratios, sample sizes, or selection bias.
- **When you face pressure to create a growth team from scratch**: Do first identify specific growth gaps and hire someone to tackle those, rather than expecting a new hire to invent the whole growth model — because growth operates within existing product and market physics, so the first hire should solve known constraints rather than start from a blank slate.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: You need large sample sizes before experiments are worth running. → **Their view**: Even experiments with around 30 users are worth doing early on. — because the directional trend usually remains useful even if statistical precision is lower, and early-stage teams benefit more from learning something than waiting for perfect rigor.
- **Conventional**: Retention is the main metric to optimize directly. → **Their view**: Retention is too vague on its own; teams should focus on the specific step before conversion or repeat usage. — because retention is an outcome of earlier user decisions, so solving the immediate barrier before the key action is more actionable and often more effective.
- **Conventional**: Analytics means tracking KPIs and dashboarding top-line metrics. → **Their view**: Tracking KPIs without contextual instrumentation is mostly entertainment, not analytics. — because metrics only become useful when they generate insights that change behavior in the real world.
- **Conventional**: Growth teams should build scalable systems from the start. → **Their view**: Early growth should rely heavily on scrappy, non-scalable tactics to validate ideas first. — because manual tests, operational hacks, and fake-door experiences can validate demand faster and cheaper than building full systems prematurely.
- **Conventional**: A great first growth hire should come in and figure out the whole growth strategy. → **Their view**: The company should already know its biggest growth gaps; the first growth hire should focus on those specific problems. — because it is unrealistic and inefficient to expect one person to infer the entire growth model from scratch while also executing.
