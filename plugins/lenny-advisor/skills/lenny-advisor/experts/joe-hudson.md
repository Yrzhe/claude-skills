# Joe Hudson

**Executive coach; founder/teacher at Art of Accomplishment** | **Art of Accomplishment** | Expertise: leadership, decision-making, emotional intelligence, team effectiveness

## Bio
Joe Hudson coaches leaders at some of the world’s most ambitious companies, including OpenAI, SpaceX, and Apple. His perspective matters because he blends practical leadership advice with emotional, somatic, and spiritual tools—helping people not just perform better, but relate differently to themselves, their teams, and their work.

He is especially useful for leaders who are smart, driven, and stuck in patterns of self-criticism, conflict avoidance, or low enjoyment. His core belief: many “performance” problems are actually emotional and relational problems in disguise.

## Signature Frameworks

### Experiment with the critical voice
- **When to use**: When you notice repetitive self-criticism, fear, or an inner voice that claims it is necessary for productivity.
- **Steps**:
  1. Recognize the critical voice as inaccurate or incomplete rather than authoritative.
  2. Stop trying to suppress or control it directly.
  3. Choose a new response to the voice as an experiment.
  4. Try different responses each day: compassion, humor, disbelief, curiosity.
  5. Observe how your relationship to the voice changes.
- **Example**: Respond to the inner critic with: “Oh, I see that you’re really scared. Don’t worry. I’m right here with you. I got you.”

### Emotional inquiry
- **When to use**: When you feel stuck, overwhelmed, reactive, or disconnected from what you’re feeling.
- **Steps**:
  1. Notice the emotion or body sensation present.
  2. Approach it with curiosity, like a child examining a toad.
  3. Explore it somatically: what it feels like, where it lives, how it changes.
  4. Welcome and love the emotion rather than merely tolerating it.
  5. Notice what happens when you resist it versus allow it.
  6. If needed, let it move through sound, expression, or body movement.
- **Example**: Emotional inquiry is a “somatic mental experiencing of an emotional experience in your body.”

### Reverse-engineer problems through avoided emotions
- **When to use**: When recurring conflicts, patterns, or life problems keep repeating despite your efforts to fix them.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the recurring problem.
  2. Ask what feeling you are trying not to feel.
  3. Identify the protective behavior you use to avoid that feeling.
  4. See how that behavior creates the outcome you fear.
  5. Practice becoming willing to feel the avoided emotion directly.
  6. Respond from truth rather than emotional defense.
- **Example**: If someone avoids shame by becoming defensive, they may start the fight and create more shame.

### Question the assumption
- **When to use**: When you are attached to a self-story, identity, or problem statement that feels fixed and limiting.
- **Steps**:
  1. State the problem clearly.
  2. List the assumptions required for it to be true.
  3. Examine each assumption for accuracy and inherited meaning.
  4. Ask whether the real issue is shame or resistance rather than the surface behavior.
  5. Let the story loosen as assumptions weaken.
  6. Act from the clearer understanding.
- **Example**: Unpack “I am an asshole to my girlfriend” by questioning what “asshole” means and whether the real issue is shame.

### Enjoy it 10% more
- **When to use**: When work feels draining, boring, effortful, or inefficient.
- **Steps**:
  1. Pause in the middle of the activity.
  2. Ask: “How do I enjoy this 10% more right now?”
  3. Don’t start by changing the external environment.
  4. Let your body adjust naturally: breathe, relax, settle, become present.
  5. Notice whether energy and quality improve.
- **Example**: While listening to a podcast, ask how to enjoy it 10% more; you may naturally breathe deeper and hear differently.

### Five personal principles
- **When to use**: When you want clearer decision-making, more consistency, and less reactivity.
- **Steps**:
  1. Draft no more than five principles that would create the life you want.
  2. Define each principle clearly, including what it is and is not.
  3. Test them for five days in real situations.
  4. Refine based on what works.
  5. Use them to make decisions automatically under pressure.
- **Example**: Principles like “embrace intensity,” “connection first,” and “everything is in iteration.”

### Five-star meetings
- **When to use**: When a team has too many meetings, low energy, hidden issues, or poor execution.
- **Steps**:
  1. Treat meetings and decisions as the atomic structure of the company.
  2. Aim to make every meeting a five-star meeting.
  3. Notice which meetings feel dead, draining, or avoidant.
  4. Use those meetings as diagnostic signals.
  5. Redesign meetings around honesty, engagement, and usefulness.
  6. Reduce unnecessary meetings as quality improves.
- **Example**: A creator’s YouTube meetings “sucked”; once he committed to making them enjoyable, performance improved dramatically.

### Seven-minute gratitude with another person
- **When to use**: When you want a simple daily practice to increase well-being and reduce scarcity thinking.
- **Steps**:
  1. Find another person each day.
  2. Spend at least seven minutes going back and forth expressing gratitude.
  3. Speak from felt gratitude, not a mental list.
  4. Savor the bodily experience as you speak.
  5. After a couple of weeks, apply it to areas of lack.
- **Example**: Joe and his wife practiced gratitude around money while in debt, shifting identity from scarcity to abundance.

## Core Advice
- **When you face a harsh, repetitive inner critic**: Don’t try to silence it; experiment with compassion, humor, or disbelief — because changing your relationship to the voice creates freedom.
- **When you face overwhelm**: Look for emotions you are not feeling or expressing — because overwhelm is often unprocessed emotional energy, not just too much work.
- **When you face recurring conflict in work or relationships**: Ask what feeling you are trying to avoid — because avoidance often creates the exact outcome you fear.
- **When you face a problem that feels fixed or identity-based**: Question the assumptions underneath it — because many “facts” are really inherited stories.
- **When you face boring, draining, or repetitive work**: Ask, “How do I enjoy this 10% more right now?” — because enjoyment increases energy and efficiency.
- **When you face difficult decisions as a leader**: Create and test a short list of personal principles — because principles reduce hesitation under pressure.
- **When you face team dysfunction or unclear organizational problems**: Audit your meetings and aim for five-star experiences — because meetings reveal the real health of the company.
- **When you face scarcity around money, time, love, or success**: Practice seven minutes of spoken gratitude with another person daily — because it shifts identity from deficiency to sufficiency.
- **When you face self-improvement pressure and shame**: Replace “I should improve” with curiosity and experiments — because shame-based improvement stalls growth.
- **When you face fear of speaking truth to authority**: Focus less on the exact words and more on the emotional place you’re speaking from — because people respond to the energy behind the truth.

- **Conventional**: Good decisions are purely logical. → **Their view**: Emotions are central to decision-making — because damaged emotional processing impairs even simple choices.
- **Conventional**: Avoid painful emotions and optimize external conditions. → **Their view**: Avoidance creates the very outcomes you fear — because internal resistance shapes external behavior.
- **Conventional**: Self-improvement is how you become worthy. → **Their view**: Authenticity and self-understanding beat shame-driven improvement — because “shoulds” create stagnation.
- **Conventional**: Enjoyment comes from doing more pleasant things. → **Their view**: Learn to enjoy what you’re already doing — because that’s the fastest path to more energy and effectiveness.
- **Conventional**: Meetings are a necessary burden. → **Their view**: Meetings should be five-star experiences — because they are the atomic structure of company health.
