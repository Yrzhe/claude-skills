# Naomi Gleit

**Head of Product** | **Meta** | Expertise: product-management, growth, leadership, operations

## Bio
Naomi Gleit is Meta’s Head of Product and one of the company’s longest-serving leaders, having joined Facebook as employee #29. Her perspective matters because she helped build and scale the systems behind Facebook’s growth, and she combines product intuition with unusually strong operational rigor, documentation discipline, and cross-functional execution.

She is especially valuable when a team needs clarity in messy situations: ambiguous growth problems, overloaded projects, or cross-functional decisions with many stakeholders. Naomi’s style is highly structured, practical, and contrarian in the best way.

## Signature Frameworks

### Understand, Identify, Execute
- **When to use**: When you need to drive growth or solve a complex product problem and don’t yet know the highest-leverage lever.
- **Steps**:
  1. Instrument the data so you can see where users drop off and where the biggest opportunities are.
  2. Understand the problem by looking at the full funnel and the key metrics that correlate with the outcome.
  3. Identify the most important levers or barriers to change.
  4. Execute by building product changes that remove friction or improve the key behavior.
- **Example**: The Facebook growth team spent January 2009 instrumenting registration and onboarding flows before deciding what to optimize.

### Extreme Clarity
- **When to use**: When teams are misaligned, meetings are going in circles, or a project has too many moving parts.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the facts everyone agrees on.
  2. Make the options explicit and spell out trade-offs.
  3. Use canonical nomenclature so everyone uses the same words for the same concepts.
  4. Use visuals in meetings and edit them in real time to capture decisions.
  5. Document decisions and next steps immediately so there is no ambiguity after the meeting.
- **Example**: In a meeting, Naomi will edit the projected slide live to show which option was chosen and what the next steps are.

### Canonical Everything
- **When to use**: When onboarding to a new project or trying to reduce confusion across many docs, chats, and stakeholders.
- **Steps**:
  1. Create one canonical doc that serves as the source of truth.
  2. List the discrete work streams and assign a single-threaded owner to each.
  3. Define the process, canonical meetings, canonical email list, and canonical chat channel.
  4. Include canonical nomenclature and canonical visuals so people share the same vocabulary and mental model.
  5. Link out to all supporting docs from the canonical doc rather than scattering information across many places.
- **Example**: Naomi insists that every project has one canonical doc that links to all other docs and clearly shows owners, work streams, and process.

### School Pyramid Simplification
- **When to use**: When a project is too complex for the team to align on or when stakeholders are operating at very different levels of understanding.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start at a kindergarten-level explanation of the problem.
  2. Build the elementary, high school, college, and PhD layers of understanding in sequence.
  3. Identify the basic building blocks before adding complexity.
  4. Use the simplification process to reveal the most important issues and decisions.
- **Example**: Naomi described joining a project where others were operating at a PhD level and she had to create the kindergarten curriculum first.

### Traffic-Light Decision Matrix
- **When to use**: When a team needs to compare multiple options in a decision meeting.
- **Steps**:
  1. Lay out the options as rows.
  2. Choose evaluation criteria as columns.
  3. Color-code each cell red/yellow/green based on how each option performs.
  4. Add rationale inside the cells so each function can own its perspective.
  5. Use the matrix to quickly see which option has the best overall profile.
- **Example**: Naomi uses a traffic-light table to compare three options across legal, policy, privacy, and product criteria.

### PM as Conductor
- **When to use**: When explaining the PM role or when leading a cross-functional product team.
- **Steps**:
  1. Treat the team like an orchestra with many instruments.
  2. Ensure each function plays its part correctly.
  3. Make sure the team is unified, in harmony, and at the right tempo.
  4. Stay out of the spotlight; the PM’s job is to coordinate, not be the star.
  5. Elevate the experts on the team and help them perform at their best.
- **Example**: Naomi literally used to give people conductor wands and metronomes when the team was smaller.

## Core Advice
- **When you believe a company is a rocket ship and you want to work there**: Do everything you can to get any seat on the rocket ship first, even if it is not your ideal role — because getting in early creates optionality.
- **When you want a role that is not being offered to you yet**: Volunteer to do the work informally before you formally ask for the job — because proving capability makes the case obvious.
- **When you are trying to understand what matters most in growth**: Instrument the funnel before you optimize it — because without data you cannot know where the biggest drop-offs or opportunities are.
- **When retention is the real growth lever**: Focus on the behavior that predicts retention, not just acquisition volume — because the strongest growth comes from improving the core habit.
- **When a product has a key activation moment**: Pick one clear activation milestone and rally the team around it — because extreme clarity matters more than perfect precision.
- **When onboarding is not obvious to new users**: Design onboarding to remove friction and guide users to the product’s core value fast — because activation depends on getting to the “aha” moment quickly.
- **When a project feels messy and everyone has a different answer**: Create one canonical doc and make it the source of truth — because scattered information creates repeated confusion.
- **When a project is stuck in ambiguity or conflict**: Separate people/process issues from strategy/execution issues before debating the strategy — because bad execution can make a good strategy look bad.
- **When you need to run a productive meeting**: Send a pre-read and agenda at least 24 hours in advance, and end with documented decisions and next steps — because context and closure save time.
- **When you need to compare multiple choices in a decision meeting**: Use a structured matrix with options as rows and criteria as columns, color-coded red/yellow/green — because trade-offs become visible at a glance.
- **When you are a PM who is too consensus-driven**: Block time to think and develop your own first-party point of view — because PMs cannot outsource judgment.
- **When you want to improve performance at work and in life**: Treat exercise, sleep, food, and alone time as non-negotiable inputs to performance — because sustained output depends on basic human maintenance.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Growth is mainly driven by marketing, comms, or acquisition tactics. → **Their view**: The product itself is the biggest lever for growth, so growth should be a product and engineering function — because product changes to onboarding, registration, and core behavior often drive retention more than campaigns.
- **Conventional**: A growth team deserves most of the credit for a company’s growth. → **Their view**: A lot of growth success may simply reflect strong product-market fit — because removing friction is easier when demand already exists.
- **Conventional**: The best way to solve a problem is to add more process and more meetings. → **Their view**: Process should exist to create speed and clarity, not for its own sake — because structure should reduce wasted time, not add it.
- **Conventional**: PMs should be the loud boss or CEO of the team. → **Their view**: PMs are more like conductors who coordinate experts and elevate others — because the team’s best work comes from harmony, not domination.
- **Conventional**: Pros and cons lists are a sufficient way to make decisions. → **Their view**: Flat lists are too hard to parse; structured visuals and traffic-light matrices are better — because they make trade-offs legible quickly.
- **Conventional**: To solve age-safety issues online, every app should independently verify age. → **Their view**: The operating system or device layer should provide age information to apps with user consent — because it is simpler, more privacy-preserving, and easier for parents and developers.
