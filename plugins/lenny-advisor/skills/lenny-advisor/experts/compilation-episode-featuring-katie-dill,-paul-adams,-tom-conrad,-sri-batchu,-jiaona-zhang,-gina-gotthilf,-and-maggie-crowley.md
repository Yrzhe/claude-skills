# Compilation Episode: Katie Dill, Paul Adams, Tom Conrad, Sri Batchu, Jiaona Zhang, Gina Gotthilf, and Maggie Crowley

**Various product, design, growth, and leadership roles** | **Airbnb, Intercom, Quibi, Ramp, Webflow, Duolingo, Toast, and others** | Expertise: leadership, product-management, growth, design, strategy, go-to-market

## Bio
This is a compilation episode, not a single-expert interview, but it surfaces a rare cross-section of hard-won lessons from leaders in product, design, and growth. The shared perspective is unusually practical: trust-building, experimentation discipline, product strategy, resilience, and avoiding expensive execution traps.

If you want a viewpoint that cuts across functions, this episode is especially useful when you’re leading change, deciding whether to ship or rewrite, or trying to tell the difference between a bad product and a bad business model.

## Signature Frameworks

### Come in listening, not swinging
- **When to use**: When taking over a new team or leading organizational change
- **Steps**:
  1. Assess the situation, but avoid acting too quickly on secondhand input.
  2. Listen deeply to team members’ goals, motivations, and concerns.
  3. Identify whether trust has been earned before pushing major changes.
  4. Bring people along with the change instead of imposing it on them.
  5. Adjust operating style quickly based on feedback.
- **Example**: Katie Dill joined Airbnb and pushed change quickly, then got a difficult intervention from half the design team and HR. She realized the core issue was lack of trust, shifted to listening-first leadership, and within months the team had the best engagement scores in the company.

### Ship fast, ship early, ship often
- **When to use**: When building products in uncertain environments where learning speed matters more than perfect upfront certainty
- **Steps**:
  1. Make bold bets rather than only incremental improvements.
  2. Ship earlier than feels comfortable.
  3. Use releases to learn, not to prove perfection.
  4. Incorporate feedback quickly and iterate fast.
  5. Balance speed with quality standards so teams are not embarrassed by what they ship.
- **Example**: Paul Adams described Intercom’s principle of embracing failure through fast shipping, arguing that if you make big bets, many will be wrong, so the organization must learn quickly rather than avoid risk.

### Fail conclusively
- **When to use**: When running growth experiments, especially in B2B where sample sizes are small and inconclusive tests create repeated wasted effort
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the hypothesis at the strategy level, not just the tactic level.
  2. Design the experiment so it can produce a clear yes or no learning.
  3. Maximize sample size where possible.
  4. If sample size is limited, maximize treatment effect by combining the strongest plausible interventions.
  5. If the full-force version fails, stop retrying the same idea repeatedly.
  6. If it works, then optimize by removing or refining components.
- **Example**: Sri Batchu used this approach for account-based marketing at Ramp, recommending that teams test the strategy with many coordinated touches rather than half-heartedly trying a few tactics and then revisiting the same idea every time a new executive arrives.

### Problem-first product thinking
- **When to use**: When teams are tempted to jump to solutions or react to competitors
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with the user problem, not the feature idea.
  2. Separate the underlying need from the proposed implementation.
  3. Check whether the problem aligns with the company’s strategic strengths.
  4. Validate unit economics early rather than assuming scale will fix them.
  5. Solve the specific user pain with the cheapest effective intervention.
- **Example**: Jiaona Zhang said Airbnb Plus was too solution-first and competitor-driven. Instead of asking how to inspect and manage inventory, Airbnb should have focused on the real problem: helping guests know what quality to expect, potentially through reviews, lockboxes, or cleaning partnerships.

### A side / B side career framing
- **When to use**: When evaluating your career progress or comparing yourself to others’ visible success
- **Steps**:
  1. Recognize that public narratives usually highlight only wins.
  2. Surface the hidden setbacks, layoffs, failures, and detours.
  3. Treat low points as moments, not permanent identity.
  4. Keep building the story and extracting lessons from each phase.
  5. Use resilience and storytelling to convert messy experiences into future opportunities.
- **Example**: Gina Gotthilf contrasted her impressive “A side” highlights with her “B side” of depression, dropping out, visa issues, layoffs, and failed paths before eventually helping scale Duolingo.

### Avoid the rewrite trap
- **When to use**: When a team proposes rewriting or redesigning a core product from scratch
- **Steps**:
  1. Treat rewrites as a red flag, especially for mature core systems.
  2. Do discovery before committing.
  3. Write a clear one-pager with requirements and rationale.
  4. Do technical and design research to understand true scope.
  5. Beware sunk cost fallacy once timelines slip.
  6. Prefer incremental evolution over side-by-side rewrites where possible.
- **Example**: Maggie Crowley described a rewrite that was estimated at six months but dragged on for two and a half years, never reached proper feature parity, and was ultimately a bad project driven by arrogance and skipped discovery.

### Evaluate the equation, not just the product
- **When to use**: When assessing whether a business can succeed beyond product quality alone
- **Steps**:
  1. Model the business as an equation: investment in, returns out, over time.
  2. Identify which variables product execution can improve.
  3. Test whether the core economics work before assuming iteration will save it.
  4. Distinguish a broken product from a broken business model.
  5. Avoid overcommitting to businesses where the math requires unrealistic capital or scale.
- **Example**: Tom Conrad said Quibi’s deeper issue was not just execution but the underlying math: a bespoke premium content library likely needed far more than $2 billion to achieve enough subscriber acquisition and retention.

## Core Advice
- **When you face resistance after joining a new team**: Do a listening tour before driving major change, and focus first on earning trust — because teams will resist even correct changes if they do not trust your intent or feel included in the process.
- **When you face pressure to make a big strategic product move because competitors are doing something similar**: Do a problem-first analysis and verify the move fits your company’s strengths and unit economics — because competitor-driven, solution-first bets often push companies into areas where they lack capabilities or where the economics never work.
- **When you face repeated inconclusive growth experiments**: Do a stronger test that maximizes treatment effect so the idea can fail conclusively — because weak tests create ambiguous results, which causes teams to rerun the same ideas for years without real learning.
- **When you face a low-volume B2B experimentation environment**: Do fewer but bolder experiments rather than many tiny optimizations — because B2B companies rarely have enough sample size for subtle changes to register, so larger interventions are more likely to generate learnable outcomes.
- **When you face fear of shipping imperfect work**: Do early releases with clear learning goals while maintaining a minimum quality bar — because fast iteration compounds learning, but teams still need standards so speed does not erode trust in the product.
- **When you face a proposal to rewrite a core product or system from scratch**: Do discovery, scope validation, and alternatives analysis before committing—and usually avoid the rewrite — because rewrites are routinely underestimated, attract sunk-cost thinking, and often fail to deliver enough business value relative to the effort.
- **When you face a major failure or public embarrassment**: Do your best to recover in the moment and keep perspective afterward — because even severe failures are often survivable, and adapting in real time can turn a disaster into a manageable story rather than a defining collapse.
- **When you face a business that seems promising because the product is good**: Do the math on the business model separately from product quality — because great execution cannot rescue a fundamentally broken equation of acquisition, retention, cost structure, and required capital.
- **When you face discouragement from career setbacks, layoffs, or false starts**: Do reframe them as part of your B side and keep building toward the next chapter — because careers are long, and many visible successes are built on hidden periods of failure, confusion, and reinvention.
- **When you face uncertainty about whether an experiment or feature is actually compelling**: Do dogfooding before drawing conclusions from the data — because using the product yourself can reveal obvious flaws that metrics alone may not contextualize.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: New leaders should move fast and decisively to fix obvious problems. → **Their view**: A new leader should first earn trust and listen deeply before pushing change — because even necessary changes fail when the team feels acted upon rather than included.
- **Conventional**: Failure in experiments means the team underperformed. → **Their view**: Failure is acceptable; the real failure is not learning — because most experiments will not work, especially in growth, so the goal is to produce conclusive learning rather than avoid misses.
- **Conventional**: If you build a delightful product and iterate well, the rest will take care of itself. → **Their view**: A company is also a math problem, and no amount of product iteration can fix a broken core equation — because some businesses fail due to economics, capital requirements, or retention assumptions.
- **Conventional**: A full rewrite is the cleanest way to modernize old product code. → **Their view**: Avoid rewrites; they are usually traps — because they are chronically underestimated, delay value, and often become long-running sunk-cost projects.
- **Conventional**: Visible career success reflects a mostly upward trajectory. → **Their view**: Most careers are full of hidden B-side failures, detours, and setbacks — because people share polished highlight reels, which obscures how much resilience and reinvention actually drive long-term success.
- **Conventional**: Competitive urgency is a strong motivator for product innovation. → **Their view**: Products built from fear of competitors often go wrong — because fear-driven efforts focus on reacting rather than deeply understanding user needs, leading to poor products and bad leadership behavior.
