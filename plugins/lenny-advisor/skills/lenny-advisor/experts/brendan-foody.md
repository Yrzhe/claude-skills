# Brendan Foody

**CEO and co-founder** | **Mercor** | Expertise: ai-strategy, go-to-market, hiring-culture, operations, product-management

## Bio
Brendan Foody is the CEO and co-founder of Mercor, a labor marketplace that supplies highly skilled experts to AI labs for evals and post-training work. His perspective matters because he identified expert-written evals as a major bottleneck in frontier AI progress and built a fast-growing company around solving it.

He thinks about AI less as a model problem and more as a measurement, workflow, and talent problem. That makes him especially useful when you need to understand how AI actually gets adopted in real businesses, not just how it is benchmarked in theory.

## Signature Frameworks

### Evals as PRDs for AI models
- **When to use**: When building, improving, or deploying AI systems where the model is the product and success criteria are unclear.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the capability you want the model to perform in a real-world workflow.
  2. Have domain experts specify what good looks like in a rubric, verifier, or test.
  3. Use that eval to measure model performance across experiments.
  4. Use the same success criteria in reinforcement learning or post-training to reward better model behavior.
  5. Reuse the eval externally as proof of capability for customers or buyers.
- **Example**: A lawyer creates a rubric for what a correct contract redline should include, and that rubric both scores the model and guides post-training.

### Enterprise AI value-chain eval design
- **When to use**: When an enterprise wants to understand whether AI can automate meaningful parts of its business.
- **Steps**:
  1. Map the company’s core value chain or major workflows.
  2. Identify the outputs customers actually care about in each workflow.
  3. Create a systematic test for whether AI can produce those outputs at acceptable quality.
  4. Measure performance repeatedly rather than relying on anecdotes.
  5. Use the results as the prerequisite for broader AI adoption across the business.
- **Example**: An architecture firm tests whether AI can produce architecture diagrams at the quality required by end customers.

### Find pull, don’t force product-market fit
- **When to use**: When searching for a scalable startup opportunity in a fast-moving market.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with a thesis about how the world is changing.
  2. Watch for leading indicators of new demand rather than optimizing only within stagnant incumbent markets.
  3. Notice where customers are surprisingly easy to sell and urgency is unusually high.
  4. Stay open-minded about the exact product form the opportunity takes.
  5. Double down once market pull is obvious and demand exceeds your capacity.
- **Example**: Mercor shifted from general hiring automation to serving AI labs after early signals from xAI and weak incumbent support for expert talent.

### Hiring strategy by company stage
- **When to use**: When deciding how to balance hiring quality versus speed during startup growth.
- **Steps**:
  1. In the earliest stage, hire very patiently and maintain an extremely high bar.
  2. Use the first hires to establish talent density and cultural standards.
  3. Once demand clearly exceeds capacity and the market vacuum is obvious, increase hiring speed.
  4. Accept some variance while preserving a strong bar.
  5. Continue optimizing for output and ownership rather than rigid process.
- **Example**: Mercor was very patient with its first 10 hires, but Foody believes it likely moved too slowly from roughly 10 to 100 people once demand was clear.

## Core Advice
- **When you face uncertainty about how to apply AI in your company**: build systematic evals for your core value chain before rolling out AI broadly — because you cannot improve or trust AI in production unless you can measure whether it performs the work that matters to customers.
- **When a workflow seems promising but inconsistent**: create a domain-expert rubric or verifier first, then use it to evaluate and improve the model — because the bottleneck is often defining success clearly enough for iteration.
- **When assessing talent in an AI-native world**: allow candidates to use AI tools in interviews and work tests — because the relevant skill is using AI effectively, not avoiding it.
- **When career uncertainty comes from AI automation**: lean into fields with elastic demand and learn to use AI to amplify your output — because higher productivity can increase total demand rather than shrink it.
- **When startup traction feels weak and forced**: listen harder for market pull and focus on customers that are surprisingly easy to sell — because huge businesses usually come from urgent problems, not marginal use cases.
- **When making early startup hires**: move slowly and keep the bar unusually high at first, then speed up once demand is undeniable — because early talent density shapes the company, but under-hiring later can become the bigger mistake.
- **When pressure builds to invest in marketing before customers love the product**: prioritize customer obsession and product/service quality first — because word of mouth and expansion are stronger when the experience is exceptional.
- **When starting a new idea or side project**: build the simplest version now and talk to customers quickly — because initiative is often the main barrier, and AI has lowered the cost of testing ideas.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: AI progress is mostly about bigger models and more pre-training data. → **Their view**: the real bottleneck is evals and post-training data defined by experts — because many valuable capabilities depend on clear success criteria, not just more internet text.
- **Conventional**: AI will mainly destroy jobs and reduce the role of humans in the economy. → **Their view**: AI is creating a major new category of work around evals, RL environments, and expert feedback loops — because humans are needed to define, verify, and improve model behavior.
- **Conventional**: Companies should prevent candidates and students from using AI so they can measure raw ability. → **Their view**: assessments should allow AI use and measure what people can accomplish with tools — because the real skill is tool leverage, not tool avoidance.
- **Conventional**: The safest career move is to avoid fields exposed to AI automation. → **Their view**: lean into elastic fields where AI increases total output and demand — because productivity gains can expand the market.
- **Conventional**: Startups should always hire slowly and cautiously. → **Their view**: hire carefully at first, but once demand is overwhelming, moving too slowly is a major mistake — because under-hiring can cap growth.
- **Conventional**: Superintelligence is imminent within a few years. → **Their view**: the road is longer and will come through many domain-specific eval and post-training improvements — because current models still need expert-guided capability building.
