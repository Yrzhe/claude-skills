# Ayo Omojola

**Chief Product Officer** | **Carbon Health** | Expertise: product strategy, design, leadership, go-to-market, fintech, regulated products

## Bio
Ayo Omojola is the Chief Product Officer at Carbon Health and previously helped build and scale Cash App and co-create Cash Card at Square. His perspective matters because he combines consumer product taste with deep operational rigor, especially in regulated environments where assumptions are often wrong and execution details matter.

He is especially valuable when you need to build something differentiated in a crowded market, launch a startup-like effort inside a larger company, or navigate complex systems by going all the way to first principles.

## Signature Frameworks

### Different and Better
- **When to use**: When building a new product in a crowded market and trying to stand out in a way users actually care about.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify how existing products solve the problem today.
  2. Define how your product is meaningfully different, not just superficially novel.
  3. Ensure the difference is also better for the end user, not merely premium or more expensive.
  4. Validate that the improvement matters in a domain large enough to support a business.
  5. Align product, economics, shipping, and messaging so the differentiation is legible in-market.
- **Example**: At Cash App, the clearest differentiator versus Venmo was instant access to money: “Try and send me a dollar that I can use now,” which only Cash App could do at the time.

### Startup Within a Startup
- **When to use**: When launching a new business line or product inside a larger company without letting bureaucracy or excess resourcing kill focus.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with a very small team.
  2. Keep the team senior and tightly knit so trust is high and communication overhead is low.
  3. Firewall the team from conflicting priorities in the parent organization.
  4. Force discipline around milestones and value creation before adding headcount.
  5. Fight for each additional resource so team growth tracks actual traction.
- **Example**: Cash App started with roughly 11 to 12 people and stayed small for a long time, reaching real scale and business success before growing headcount significantly.

### Go to the End of the Problem
- **When to use**: When working on complex, regulated, or operationally constrained products where surface-level answers are often wrong or incomplete.
- **Steps**:
  1. Do not accept the first expert answer as final.
  2. Investigate the underlying system directly until someone on the team truly understands it.
  3. Test assumptions against real-world constraints, vendors, regulations, or data.
  4. Keep probing every inconsistency until you know what is actually true.
  5. Hold the execution owner accountable for becoming the expert on the details.
- **Example**: While building Cash Card, Ayo visited card manufacturing factories, learned how cards were physically made, and explored thousands of combinations of materials and laser engraving settings to create a differentiated product.

### Trust but Verify Constraints
- **When to use**: When teams cite regulations, contracts, or legacy processes as reasons something cannot be improved.
- **Steps**:
  1. Ask for the exact reason a constraint exists.
  2. Distinguish between hearsay and actual contractual or regulatory obligations.
  3. Clarify what specifically breaks if you do not follow the current approach.
  4. Assess the real consequence: fines, legal risk, operational failure, or just habit.
  5. Only preserve constraints that are truly binding; redesign around the rest.
- **Example**: Ayo described pushing teams beyond “X person said we can't” toward concrete explanations like contractual obligations and explicit consequences, especially when poor assumptions were creating worse patient experiences.

### Long-Horizon Recruiting
- **When to use**: When hiring exceptional people who may not be ready to join immediately.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify people you want to work with before you have an immediate opening.
  2. Be explicit that you would like to work together someday.
  3. Learn what they want now, even if it is not your role or company.
  4. Help them through introductions, advice, or opportunities with no immediate expectation of return.
  5. Stay in touch so that when timing changes, you are a trusted option.
- **Example**: Ayo said many hires joined months after first contact, and one took nearly a year and a half; he operationalizes this by helping people get what they want before trying to recruit them.

## Core Advice
- **When you face a crowded market with entrenched competitors**: Do the work to define a product that is both different and better in a way that matters to users — because novelty alone is easy and often useless.
- **When you face the challenge of launching a new initiative inside a big company**: Do start with a small, senior, tightly knit team and delay headcount growth until the product proves itself — because small trusted teams move faster and avoid fake progress.
- **When you face ambiguous expert opinions or operational complexity**: Do keep digging until someone on the team truly understands the system end to end — because tenure is not the same as expertise.
- **When you face organizational claims that something “can't be done” because of regulation or process**: Do ask for the exact contractual, legal, or operational consequence of changing the approach — because many constraints are inherited assumptions.
- **When you face hiring needs for unusually high-output teams**: Do actively recruit founders and other non-traditional candidates instead of relying only on standard resume filters — because conventional pipelines miss exceptional builders.
- **When you face the problem of hiring great people who are not ready to move now**: Do build relationships early and help them get what they want before asking them to join you — because people choose the timing.
- **When you face healthcare go-to-market complexity**: Do get extremely crisp about the specific use case and the exact decision-maker you need to reach — because network access and precision matter.
- **When you face messy data or instrumentation in a complex operation**: Do re-measure, re-instrument, and inspect inconsistencies instead of trusting existing fields and dashboards — because systems are often not designed for the optimization you now care about.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: If your product is better than the competition, that is enough to win. → **Their view**: Being better is not enough; it must also be different, and the difference must matter to the user — because incremental improvement rarely creates a compelling reason to switch.
- **Conventional**: Big-company incubations should get lots of resources to maximize their chance of success. → **Their view**: New internal ventures should stay small and resource-constrained for longer — because excess headcount can create fake momentum and organizational tax.
- **Conventional**: Experts and long-tenured operators usually know the right answer. → **Their view**: You should be skeptical of expert answers until the underlying system is truly understood — because people often repeat assumptions from other contexts.
- **Conventional**: The best hires come through standard recruiting filters and recognizable pedigrees. → **Their view**: Some of the highest-value hires are non-traditional candidates, especially former founders who get filtered out too early — because resume pipelines optimize for familiarity, not capability.
- **Conventional**: Healthcare startups win mainly by having the best product. → **Their view**: In healthcare, network access often matters as much as or more than product merit — because distribution and decision-maker access are critical.
