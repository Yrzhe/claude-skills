# Brian Tolkin

**Head of Product and Design** | **Opendoor** | Expertise: product-management, operations, go-to-market, pricing, strategy

## Bio
Brian Tolkin is the Head of Product and Design at Opendoor and an early Uber employee who grew from operations into product. His perspective matters because he has built and scaled products in businesses where software alone is not enough — the real advantage comes from tightly integrating product, operations, pricing, and field execution.

He is especially valuable for teams working in messy, real-world systems like marketplaces, logistics, mobility, and real estate, where the best answers often come from combining customer proximity with scalable technology.

## Signature Frameworks

### Product + Operations Twin-Turbine Model
- **When to use**: When building businesses where software and real-world operations are both core to the customer experience.
- **Steps**:
  1. Treat product and operations as complementary systems, not competing functions.
  2. Create strong bidirectional feedback loops between field teams and centralized product/engineering teams.
  3. Use operations to learn quickly, gather qualitative insight, and handle edge cases.
  4. Invest engineering effort where technology creates the most leverage.
  5. Gradually automate repeatable operational work as scale increases, while redeploying ops talent to the next bottleneck.
- **Example**: At Uber, local teams controlled surge parameters and manually onboarded drivers early on, while product and engineering focused scarce resources on dispatch and pricing systems. Over time, onboarding and other workflows became more automated.

### Product Operations Feedback Loop
- **When to use**: When a centralized product team serves many distributed markets or operational teams and communication is weak.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify where the feedback loop is broken in both directions: rollout to markets and market insight back to product.
  2. Create a dedicated function that sits close to product but is accountable to operations.
  3. Translate product changes into effective market launches and operational adoption.
  4. Bring structured field feedback into product planning and prioritization.
  5. Continuously refine the loop as the company scales globally.
- **Example**: At Uber, product operations bridged San Francisco-based product teams and globally distributed operations teams so new features could launch effectively and market insights could shape future features.

### Scale Through Operational Iteration, Then Automate
- **When to use**: When a process works manually at low volume but begins to break under growth.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with a manual, high-touch process to learn what matters.
  2. Increase throughput incrementally with lightweight operational changes.
  3. Watch for the point where operational improvements no longer scale.
  4. Identify the repetitive bottleneck that technology can solve best.
  5. Automate that bottleneck and free the team to work on the next constraint.
- **Example**: Uber driver onboarding evolved from 1:1 in-person sessions, to small groups, to larger classes with video, and eventually to automated credential validation once manual review no longer scaled.

### Jobs to Be Done as a Product Review Lens
- **When to use**: When teams need to better understand customer motivation and context, especially in products where employees are not frequent users themselves.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with the customer's broader problem, not just the company's desired action.
  2. Describe the context around the customer decision, including off-product behavior.
  3. Use product review templates that include problem statement and jobs to be done.
  4. Challenge narrow or company-centric definitions of the job.
  5. Let the framework become cultural language rather than rigid template compliance.
- **Example**: At Opendoor, a team might define the job as “get an offer from Opendoor,” but Brian pushes toward the broader customer job, such as “price discovery” or reducing uncertainty in the home-selling journey.

### Product Review Framework
- **When to use**: To improve product quality, create accountability, and align teams without turning reviews into punitive meetings.
- **Steps**:
  1. Clarify that the goals are accountability and, more importantly, making the product better.
  2. Have teams pre-fill a review document before the meeting.
  3. Structure the review around context, problem, potential solution, risks/premortem, and success metrics.
  4. Tailor the review to the stage of work, from ideation to pre-ship.
  5. Keep the live discussion small, ideally under 10 people, while sharing artifacts broadly.
- **Example**: At Opendoor, teams sign up for weekly review slots, and the resulting documents become useful artifacts for broader alignment and onboarding.

### Low-Volume Experimentation Decision Framework
- **When to use**: When transaction volume is too low for fast, statistically significant A/B tests.
- **Steps**:
  1. Run a power analysis before launching an experiment.
  2. Decide whether the expected runtime and minimum detectable effect are acceptable.
  3. If needed, accept longer-running experiments for strategically important questions.
  4. Use alternative methods to build conviction: customer interviews, observational analysis, diff-in-diff, geo segmentation, twin cities, lower confidence thresholds, or long-term holdouts.
  5. If precision is unattainable, make the call with judgment and set up post-launch feedback loops.
- **Example**: At Opendoor, some experiments may need six months to be useful, while others require alternative approaches because low funnel volume makes standard A/B testing impractical.

## Core Advice
- **When you face a business with both software and heavy real-world operations**: build mutual respect and tight feedback loops between product and operations — because operations teams are closer to customers and can iterate faster in the field, while product teams can scale solutions through technology.
- **When you face many possible product investments in an ops-heavy company**: search deliberately for where technology has the highest leverage, and explicitly choose not to invest elsewhere yet — because engineering resources are usually scarcer than operational resources early on.
- **When you face a manual process that is starting to break under growth**: do the process manually first, iterate operationally, and only automate once you understand the bottleneck clearly — because manual operations teach you what matters.
- **When you face product reviews that feel tense or performative**: make the goal improving the product, not judging the team — because the people closest to the problem have the deepest context.
- **When you face the challenge of giving feedback as a senior leader**: signal whether your input is a question, context, or suggestion rather than an implicit mandate — because senior voices can accidentally override team ownership.
- **When you face a customer problem in a domain you don't personally experience often**: use jobs-to-be-done thinking to understand the broader context and motivations — because intuition is weaker in infrequent-use products.
- **When you face low sample sizes and want to run experiments**: do a power analysis first and be honest about whether the experiment can answer the question — because underpowered tests create false precision.
- **When you face a product decision that cannot be tested rigorously**: use judgment, but pair it with customer conversations and a post-launch feedback loop — because informed intuition is better than pretending the data is conclusive.
- **When you face intense pressure as a leader during launches or crises**: stay visibly calm — because reflecting stress onto the team usually makes outcomes worse.
- **When you face lots of incoming ideas and feedback from across the company**: write everything down in a visible backlog before deciding what matters most — because it preserves context and helps separate signal from noise.
- **When you face PM hiring for a specialized business**: hire for person-problem fit rather than generic PM profiles — because different problems require different strengths.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Product and operations compete for control, and strong product companies should minimize ops involvement. → **Their view**: Product and operations are complementary engines that work best in harmony — because ops provides speed, customer proximity, and local nuance.
- **Conventional**: Every important product decision should be validated with a standard A/B test. → **Their view**: If the experiment is underpowered or impractical, use other methods or judgment — because low-volume businesses can’t afford false precision.
- **Conventional**: Frameworks like jobs to be done should be applied rigidly everywhere. → **Their view**: Frameworks are tools, not dogma — because the value is in the thinking, not compliance.
- **Conventional**: In stressful moments, leaders should project urgency and intensity. → **Their view**: Leaders should stay calm — because tension makes teams think less clearly.
- **Conventional**: Competition should be the central focus when a large rival enters your market. → **Their view**: Stay customer-focused — because the real competition is often the default behavior, not the rival.
