# Kevin Aluwi

**Co-founder and former CEO** | **Gojek** | Expertise: consumer products, brand strategy, operations, marketplace

## Bio
Kevin Aluwi co-founded Gojek and helped grow it from a motorcycle-taxi service into one of Southeast Asia’s most important consumer super apps. His perspective matters because he built in a market with weak infrastructure, intense local competition, and fast-changing consumer behavior—so he learned how product, brand, and operations work together when the usual Silicon Valley playbook doesn’t.

## Signature Frameworks

### Build a brand that reinforces the product’s real value proposition
- **When to use**: When you need to differentiate in a crowded consumer market and can’t rely on discounts or features alone.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the product personality and the core association customers should have with the company.
  2. Ensure every touchpoint reflects that personality: copy, app design, ads, and service behavior.
  3. Create visible, real-world artifacts that connect the brand to the service itself.
  4. Lean into cultural behaviors and local norms that naturally align with the product.
  5. Use brand to create identity and loyalty, not just recall.
- **Example**: Gojek put branded helmets and jackets on drivers so people saw the service in action while stuck in traffic, making the brand a physical reminder of how Gojek helped.

### Use operations as a competitive advantage when technology is immature
- **When to use**: When the market lacks infrastructure, talent, or technical primitives needed for a clean software-only solution.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the customer problem that cannot wait for a perfect technical solution.
  2. Create manual or physical operations to bridge the gap.
  3. Run the operational workaround long enough to serve customers and learn.
  4. Replace the workaround with scalable infrastructure when the ecosystem catches up.
- **Example**: Gojek created cash booths with vaults so drivers could withdraw earnings before digital payments and bank integrations were available.

### Compete with hard things, not just moats
- **When to use**: When competitors can copy features but not easily copy operational commitment or local execution.
- **Steps**:
  1. Choose problems that are genuinely hard but valuable to customers.
  2. Invest in the operational burden required to solve them.
  3. Signal commitment through actions that competitors are unlikely to match.
  4. Turn the hard-to-replicate effort into customer trust and loyalty.
- **Example**: Gojek hired private security to protect drivers from motorcycle-taxi mafia attacks, building trust with drivers and making the service harder to displace.

### Build products around a unifying mental model
- **When to use**: When expanding from one service into multiple adjacent services.
- **Steps**:
  1. Find the single concept customers already associate with the company.
  2. Only add services that fit naturally under that concept.
  3. Test whether users understand the connection without heavy education.
  4. Avoid adding services that require a new mental model unless you are ready to re-educate the market.
- **Example**: Gojek’s ride-hailing, package delivery, and food delivery all fit the idea of a driver, but massage services confused users because they expected the driver to perform the massage.

### Hire and learn by doing the job yourself first
- **When to use**: When talent is scarce and you need to understand what excellence looks like before hiring.
- **Steps**:
  1. Take on key roles personally in the early days.
  2. Experience the work directly to understand its constraints and failure modes.
  3. Use that experience to define what good performance looks like.
  4. Hire people who can do the job orders of magnitude better than you.
- **Example**: Kevin wrote ad copy, ran performance marketing, and even drove for Gojek to understand the work before hiring specialists.

### Localize strategy to the market’s unique behavior
- **When to use**: When building outside major tech hubs or in markets with distinct user behavior and infrastructure.
- **Steps**:
  1. Study the local behavior that incumbents from other markets may miss.
  2. Design product and branding around that local reality.
  3. Avoid copying a foreign company’s playbook without adaptation.
  4. Use local insight to create both product and distribution advantages.
- **Example**: Gojek was built around Indonesian motorcycle taxis, which made branding on helmets and jackets effective in a way that car-centric ride-hailing brands could not replicate.

## Core Advice
- **When customers don’t understand your multi-product offering**: Anchor the company around one simple unifying concept before adding adjacent services — because cross-sell only works when users can mentally group the services together.
- **When brand is treated as a nice-to-have**: Treat brand as a core growth lever alongside product, not as an afterthought — because great brands create identity-level loyalty that survives beyond discounts and feature parity.
- **When a relevant feature has low engagement**: Run user research to test whether people even know it exists before changing the feature itself — because awareness can be the bottleneck, not product quality.
- **When the brand needs to feel local and relatable**: Make the brand part of everyday culture through copy, humor, and culturally native product behavior — because cultural fit drives trust and memorability.
- **When field workers or drivers are visible in public**: Use physical brand artifacts that also demonstrate the service in action — because the brand becomes proof of the product, not just decoration.
- **When infrastructure gaps block payouts or transactions**: Build temporary operational workarounds instead of waiting for perfect infrastructure — because customers need service now, not later.
- **When fraud or third-party clones are hard to eliminate quickly**: Consider copying the most valuable user-facing features into your own product — because sometimes the fastest way to neutralize a clone is to remove its advantage.
- **When local talent and funding are scarce**: Get good at remote work early and recruit from deeper talent pools outside your headquarters market — because access to talent can matter more than geography.
- **When a product team moves slowly**: Make the accountable person the decider — because clear ownership reduces ambiguity and speeds execution.
- **When you may need to hire for a role you don’t fully understand**: Do the role yourself long enough to learn the work and define excellence — because firsthand experience improves hiring judgment.
- **When supply-side work has hidden labor and inconvenience**: Design compensation and product rules around the real work, including waiting time and multi-stop complexity — because the economics must match the actual effort.
- **When adoption can be explosive**: Prioritize product-market fit over polished strategy and be ready for very fast scaling — because unmet needs can create extreme growth if the market is ready.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Super apps naturally create lower CAC, higher retention, and easy cross-sell across services. → **Their view**: Those benefits often don’t pan out in practice, and super apps can create major UX and education problems — because users need a unifying mental model or each new service feels unrelated.
- **Conventional**: Brand is a soft, secondary concern compared with product and growth. → **Their view**: In consumer businesses, product and brand are the two most important things, and brand can be decisive for survival — because loyalty can outlast discounts and feature parity.
- **Conventional**: Startups should focus on the simplest scalable solution and avoid heavy operations. → **Their view**: In underdeveloped markets, doing the hard operational thing is often the right move and can be a durable advantage — because infrastructure gaps make “simple” solutions impossible.
- **Conventional**: Moats are the key strategic question. → **Their view**: Moats are not durable; instead, focus on doing hard things that create customer value and are difficult for competitors to match — because operational commitment is harder to copy than a slide-deck moat.
- **Conventional**: Product teams should be communal and decisions should emerge from the best ideas in the room. → **Their view**: Everyone should contribute ideas, but one accountable decider should own the final call — because clarity speeds execution.
- **Conventional**: A ride-hailing company should behave like a pure platform and leave driver safety to contractors. → **Their view**: If drivers are central to the business, the company should directly protect them — because trust on the supply side is a strategic asset.
