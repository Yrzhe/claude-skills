## Signature Frameworks

### Systems Thinking via Stocks and Flows
- **When to use**: When you need to understand a complex operational problem and identify where the real bottleneck is.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the key stocks that accumulate over time.
  2. Identify the flows that move things between stocks.
  3. Build a simple model of how the system evolves over time.
  4. Compare the model to reality and look for mismatches.
  5. Use the mismatch to refine your mental model.
  6. Stop modeling once the model is good enough and take action.
- **Example**: In a hiring pipeline, candidates flow from sourced to recruiter screens to hiring manager screens to offers; if offers are not being extended, the bottleneck may be manager conviction rather than sourcing.

### Rumelt Strategy Framework
- **When to use**: When defining or debugging strategy for a team, function, or company.
- **Steps**:
  1. Diagnose the current reality and constraints.
  2. Choose guiding policies that address the diagnosis.
  3. Translate those policies into concrete actions.
  4. Write the strategy down so it can be debugged and improved.
- **Example**: At Uber, the strategy of running only in company-owned data centers enabled rapid expansion into China despite cloud constraints.

### Engineering Strategy Through Constraints
- **When to use**: When a team is tempted to expand tools, scope, or options in ways that dilute focus.
- **Steps**:
  1. Choose a standard kit or default operating model.
  2. Constrain tool and platform sprawl.
  3. Direct engineering energy toward the company’s highest-value problems.
  4. Revisit the constraint only when the business case is strong.
- **Example**: Stripe’s Ruby monolith strategy kept engineers focused on user-facing innovation instead of supporting many languages and frameworks.

### Measure, Then Educate
- **When to use**: When executives want engineering metrics but the data is imperfect.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with a measurable proxy rather than waiting for perfect measurement.
  2. Use the metric primarily for diagnosis, not judgment.
  3. Explain what the metric misses and where it can mislead.
  4. Iterate the measurement as stakeholders become more sophisticated.
- **Example**: DORA-style metrics can show that deployments are slow, but they do not by themselves prove whether a team is good or bad.

### Write What You Want to Write
- **When to use**: When building a long-term writing practice alongside a demanding job.
- **Steps**:
  1. Write about topics that genuinely energize you.
  2. Avoid writing to a schedule or for audience demand alone.
  3. Prefer work that overlaps with your current job or thinking.
  4. Publish consistently rather than endlessly polishing drafts.
- **Example**: Will stopped working on an infrastructure engineering book when it no longer had energy and moved on.

### Shared-Constraint Performance Calibration
- **When to use**: When EM/PM or cross-functional tension is driven by misaligned incentives.
- **Steps**:
  1. Treat the paired leaders as peers.
  2. Calibrate their performance together.
  3. Evaluate them against the same overall team outcomes.
  4. Account for exceptions only when one person is clearly underperforming.
- **Example**: At Carta, Will and the CPO calibrate EM and PM performance together so both understand they share incentives.

## Core Advice
- **When your engineering team seems slow or ineffective**: Talk to the engineers directly and ask what is blocking them — because engineers usually know whether the team is effective and can often point to the real bottlenecks.
- **When you are tempted to keep analyzing a problem indefinitely**: Set a point where the model is good enough and then ship the improvement — because over-measuring becomes a substitute for action.
- **When your team wants to adopt new tools, languages, or infrastructure**: Default to the standard kit you already have unless there is a strong strategic reason to change — because constraint reduces sprawl and keeps focus on the company’s most important problems.
- **When strategy feels vague or nonexistent**: Write it down, even if it is imperfect — because a written strategy can be debugged and improved.
- **When you are choosing what to write or create**: Prioritize topics that genuinely interest you over topics you think will perform well — because sustained output depends on energy.
- **When you want writing to help your career**: Produce two or three excellent artifacts instead of starting a high-frequency newsletter — because career value comes from a few strong pieces that show depth.
- **When you are trying to build a long-term audience**: Publish consistently and do not wait for perfection — because the biggest risk is quitting.
- **When PMs and EMs are in conflict**: First understand each other’s real incentives and constraints before arguing for a solution — because many conflicts come from hidden constraints, not bad intent.
- **When a PM and EM are struggling to align on execution**: Assume there may be a compromise that satisfies both sides if you dig deeply enough — because what people say they want is often not what they actually need.
- **When executives ask for a single productivity number for engineering**: Use imperfect metrics as a starting point and educate stakeholders about what they do and do not mean — because metrics are mainly for diagnosis and shared understanding.
- **When defining company values**: Make them honest, applicable, and reversible — because values should reflect how the company actually behaves and help make decisions.
- **When you are stressed about a decision**: Ask whether anyone will remember it in six months — because many urgent decisions are not strategically important.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Engineers should be shielded from hard business problems and protected from accountability. → **Their view**: Treat engineers like peers and adults; give them real problems and hold them accountable — because coddling engineers is bad for growth and organizational efficiency.
- **Conventional**: Systems thinking is the best way to solve complex problems if you apply it rigorously. → **Their view**: Systems thinking is useful for learning, but harmful when the model is treated as more real than reality — because the gap between model and reality is where learning happens, but eventually you must act.
- **Conventional**: A good strategy should be ambitious, differentiated, and inspiring. → **Their view**: The best strategy is often boring and constraining — because scarce energy should go to the company’s real priorities.
- **Conventional**: Engineering productivity should be measured with a precise, universal dashboard. → **Their view**: Most metrics are imperfect diagnosis tools, not truth — because waiting for perfect measurement leads to measuring nothing.
- **Conventional**: Values should describe aspirational ideals like integrity, quality, or customer focus. → **Their view**: Useful values must be honest about how the company actually operates — because identity values rarely help teams choose between real tradeoffs.
- **Conventional**: Writing success comes from optimizing for audience demand and publishing on a strict schedule. → **Their view**: Writing is more sustainable when you write what energizes you and publish most of what you create — because energy and consistency matter more than trend-chasing.
