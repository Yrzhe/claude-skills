# John Cutler

**Product Evangelist** | **Amplitude** | Expertise: product-management, leadership, strategy, metrics

## Bio
John Cutler is a widely followed product thinker, writer of *The Beautiful Mess*, and former Product Evangelist at Amplitude. He coached hundreds of product leaders and teams across startups and large enterprises, giving him a rare view into what separates merely functional product organizations from truly high-performing ones.

His perspective matters because he focuses less on “best practices” in the abstract and more on how product work actually happens inside real companies: messy, constrained, political, and context-dependent. He is especially valuable when teams need to connect strategy, measurement, and execution without falling into framework worship.

## Signature Frameworks

### North Star Framework
- **When to use**: When a team needs shared direction, a way to connect strategy to measurement, or alignment on long-term value creation.
- **Steps**:
  1. Clarify the game your product is playing and the business model behind it.
  2. Build a qualitative model of how the product creates value for users and the business.
  3. Choose a North Star metric that reflects that value.
  4. Identify input metrics or drivers that influence the North Star.
  5. Use the framework to guide prioritization and tradeoffs.
- **Example**: Amplitude evolved its North Star work through workshops, customer success learnings, and the North Star Playbook, and many teams now refer to “Amplitude’s North Star framework.”

### Data-Informed Product Loop
- **When to use**: When a team wants to improve decision-making by connecting strategy, qualitative thinking, measurement, execution, and learning.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with a strategy.
  2. Develop qualitative models of how the product creates value.
  3. Add measurement to those models.
  4. Prioritize where to focus.
  5. Design bets or experiments.
  6. Measure the impact of what shipped.
  7. Feed the learning back into strategy and prioritization.
- **Example**: A team may have a strong strategy, but if it cannot ship bets, measure impact, or learn from outcomes, the strategy never becomes real.

### Reps in Transformation
- **When to use**: When a large or legacy company wants modern product practices but cannot transform everything at once.
- **Steps**:
  1. Create smaller pods or protected areas with more autonomy.
  2. Let those teams complete the full ship-learn loop.
  3. Use frameworks as job aids, not the destination.
  4. Adapt the frameworks to the company’s context.
  5. Expand from proven local practices.
- **Example**: He recommends creating “little bastions” where teams can get reps instead of trying to copy Silicon Valley operating models wholesale.

### Frameworks as Job Aids, Not End Goals
- **When to use**: When teams are tempted to copy a famous company’s process because it looks like the secret to success.
- **Steps**:
  1. Treat the framework as a learning tool.
  2. Use it to structure conversations and keep the team on track.
  3. Observe where it breaks in your context.
  4. Modify or reinvent it.
  5. Judge success by better decisions, not framework compliance.
- **Example**: Teams often read about how Figma or another admired company works and mistake adopting the framework itself for the goal.

### Leadership Self-Awareness Pyramid
- **When to use**: When leaders need to understand how their worldview shapes culture, decisions, and management style.
- **Steps**:
  1. Recognize when you know little about your own assumptions.
  2. Become aware of your preferences and defaults.
  3. Notice that others think differently.
  4. Acknowledge that other views can be valid.
  5. Integrate diverse perspectives without losing your style.
- **Example**: A leader may assume everyone is motivated by money or manager quality, when in reality different people are driven by different things.

## Core Advice
- **When deciding between process and people**: Do an honest self-assessment first and build a coherent leadership approach around what you genuinely believe — because consistency between values, style, and operating model matters more than imitation.
- **When stuck in a slow, bureaucratic environment**: Create smaller loops by documenting options, assumptions, risks, success criteria, and learning — because you often have more room to practice product thinking than you think.
- **When leading a large-company transformation**: Create small pods where teams can get real reps shipping and learning — because product skill comes from repeated cycles, not abstract training.
- **When tempted to copy a famous company’s framework**: Treat frameworks as job aids and adapt them to your context — because success depends on structural realities, not just visible rituals.
- **When overwhelmed by product advice**: Focus on just-in-time learning and getting around the loop of strategy, modeling, measurement, shipping, and learning — because knowledge without practice creates anxiety, not skill.
- **When improving team performance**: Check whether structure, incentives, architecture, and funding actually support the strategy — because talented people struggle in incoherent systems.
- **When unsure what to measure**: Measure only enough to reduce uncertainty for the next important decision — because measurement should support decisions, not become the work itself.
- **When starting analytics work**: Start small with a limited set of events and learn from real usage — because teams usually don’t know their true needs upfront.
- **When hiring or evaluating product talent**: Break vague labels like “product sense” into teachable skills — because clearer competencies make coaching and hiring more effective.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: High-performing product teams all look similar and follow the same best practices. → **Their view**: Dysfunctional teams are often similar, but high-performing teams can succeed in very different ways — because coherence matters more than conformity.
- **Conventional**: The best way to improve is to copy elite Silicon Valley frameworks and rituals. → **Their view**: Frameworks should be adapted as context-specific job aids — because visible process artifacts are not the source of success.
- **Conventional**: Product success is mostly about hiring exceptional individuals or heroic leaders. → **Their view**: Structure, context, and coherence often matter more — because talented people fail in mismatched systems.
- **Conventional**: Product mindset is a mysterious innate trait. → **Their view**: It can be decomposed into learnable skills — because vague labels hide teachable capabilities.
- **Conventional**: Slow, top-down companies can’t really practice good product management. → **Their view**: You can still run meaningful loops and build skill — because constraints rarely eliminate all room for judgment.
- **Conventional**: More measurement is always better. → **Their view**: You only need enough measurement to reduce uncertainty for the next decision — because analytics is a means, not an end.
