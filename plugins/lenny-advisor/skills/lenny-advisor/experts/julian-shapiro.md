## Signature Frameworks

### Product-Led Acquisition
- **When to use**: When designing or evaluating a product’s growth model and looking for low-cost, scalable acquisition built into product usage itself.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify whether normal product usage naturally causes users to bring in other users.
  2. Look for debt-settling mechanics, where recipients must join to claim value.
  3. Look for conversation-based mechanics, where people must join to access a social or business conversation.
  4. Look for billboarding opportunities, where product usage is inherently visible or shared publicly.
  5. Look for user-generated content loops, where users create content that spreads the product off-platform or via SEO.
  6. Prioritize roadmap features that strengthen these native acquisition loops rather than relying only on paid or SEO channels.
- **Example**: PayPal and Venmo grow when a sender pays someone who must create an account to receive the money; Calendly grows because users must share their scheduling links publicly.

### State Building for Retention
- **When to use**: When trying to improve retention, increase switching costs, and make a product more defensible over time.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify what valuable state users can accumulate inside the product over time.
  2. Design that state to become more useful as it grows, so users get compounding value.
  3. Make the state hard or impossible to transfer elsewhere.
  4. Encourage users to invest in that state through repeated actions like reviews, followers, connections, or integrations.
  5. Use the accumulated state to deepen product dependence and reduce churn.
- **Example**: eBay sellers build thousands of reviews they cannot transfer elsewhere, making them unlikely to leave the platform; YouTubers build subscriber audiences they cannot export.

### Novelty Framework for Writing
- **When to use**: When writing content that needs to capture attention, feel insightful, and drive engagement.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define novelty as information that is new, significant, and not easily intuited by the reader.
  2. Use specific novelty types such as counterintuitive ideas, counter-narrative claims, shock-and-awe facts, or elegant articulation.
  3. Draft the piece and identify the lines that make readers say “whoa.”
  4. Map where novelty appears and remove or compress low-interest sections between those moments.
  5. Increase the density of novelty so the piece maintains momentum.
- **Example**: Julian cites his tweet “Reading many books is the most socially accepted vanity metric for adults” as counter-narrative novelty because it challenges a widely accepted belief.

### Writing Quality = Novelty × Resonance
- **When to use**: When editing writing to make it both insightful and memorable, not just informative.
- **Steps**:
  1. First focus on novelty: find the core ideas that are surprising or eye-opening.
  2. Then add resonance through examples, analogies, metaphors, and stories.
  3. Treat draft one as the novelty draft.
  4. Treat draft two as the resonance draft.
  5. Balance concision with enough storytelling to make the ideas stick.
- **Example**: A highly novel idea without resonance becomes dry trivia; resonance is what makes the novelty memorable.

### Objective + Motivation Topic Selection
- **When to use**: When deciding what to write about and how to choose topics you will actually finish.
- **Steps**:
  1. Choose a clear objective for the piece, such as proving the status quo wrong, articulating what others are thinking, contributing original research, or telling an emotional story.
  2. Pair that objective with a strong personal motivation, such as getting something off your chest, solving a nagging problem, or geeking out on an obsession.
  3. Use the objective as the standard for knowing when the piece is complete.
  4. Use the motivation to sustain effort through the writing process.
- **Example**: Writing pieces to “open people’s eyes” or “articulate something everyone’s thinking but no one is saying,” then pairing that with a personal urge to get something off his chest.

### Creativity Faucet
- **When to use**: When stuck creatively, facing blank-page paralysis, or trying to generate original ideas in writing or other creative work.
- **Steps**:
  1. Assume the first ideas that come out will be bad.
  2. Write out the bad ideas instead of resisting or judging them.
  3. Keep going until the “wastewater” is emptied from the pipe.
  4. Notice what makes the early ideas weak.
  5. Iterate from weak imitation toward stronger and more original ideas.
  6. Trust that good ideas come after the bad ones are exhausted.
- **Example**: Julian says creators often stop after a few weak ideas, but Neil Gaiman, Ed Sheeran, and John Mayer keep going until the bad ideas are emptied and the better ones emerge.

## Core Advice
- **When choosing a startup growth strategy**: Favor product-led acquisition over channel-first thinking — because the cheapest growth comes from product usage that naturally recruits more users.
- **When tempted to add a referral program**: Prefer native loops over bolted-on incentives — because reward-driven referrals often produce weaker intent and less durable growth.
- **When planning a roadmap**: Ask whether a feature helps users settle debts, join conversations, billboard the product, or create shareable content — because these are recurring acquisition patterns with compounding effects.
- **When retention is weak**: Build valuable state inside the product — because users are less likely to leave when they would lose reputation, audience, social graph, or accumulated work.
- **When assessing defensibility**: Look for both cheap acquisition and sticky retention — because durable companies usually win on both loop quality and switching costs.
- **When writing feels flat**: Increase novelty density — because readers stay engaged when every few lines deliver something surprising or non-obvious.
- **When editing a draft**: Separate novelty from resonance — because insight and memorability are different jobs.
- **When choosing a topic**: Pair a clear objective with personal motivation — because one tells you what success looks like and the other keeps you going.
- **When creatively blocked**: Write through the bad ideas — because good ideas often emerge only after weak ones are exhausted.
- **When building an audience**: Optimize for “mind followers,” not just follower count — because original thinking creates deeper loyalty than curation or gimmicks.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Product-led growth mainly means self-serve SaaS without sales. → **Their view**: Product-led acquisition is the better concept — because the real advantage is when product usage itself brings in more users.
- **Conventional**: Referral programs are a strong default growth tactic. → **Their view**: They’re often less durable than native loops — because they can attract reward-seekers instead of true believers.
- **Conventional**: A moat is any sticky advantage. → **Their view**: Most “moats” are just retention mechanisms — because true moats are rare and usually come from things like regulation or scientific breakthroughs.
- **Conventional**: Great writing is mainly elegant prose and storytelling. → **Their view**: Novelty is the backbone — because without new, significant ideas, writing is forgettable.
- **Conventional**: Bad first ideas mean you’re not creative. → **Their view**: Bad ideas are necessary — because they help your brain identify what weak ideas look like and move toward better ones.
- **Conventional**: Reading many books signals intelligence. → **Their view**: It can be a vanity metric — because consumption matters less than applying ideas and making useful things.
