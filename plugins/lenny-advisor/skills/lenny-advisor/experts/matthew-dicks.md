# Matthew Dicks

**Author, storyteller, and elementary school teacher** | **Storyworthy / elementary school teaching** | Expertise: storytelling, public speaking, business communication, humor

## Bio
Matthew Dicks is a 59-time Moth Story Slam winner, nine-time Grand Slam champion, and the author of *Storyworthy*. He also teaches elementary school, which gives him a rare combination of stage-tested storytelling instincts and a deep understanding of how people actually learn, remember, and connect.

His perspective matters because he treats storytelling as a practical business skill, not an art reserved for performers. He helps people and companies become more memorable, persuasive, and human by focusing on the moments that change how an audience feels.

## Signature Frameworks

### The Five-Second Moment Story Structure
- **When to use**: When telling a true story about yourself, your company, or your product and you want it to feel meaningful rather than like a chronological recap.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the singular moment of transformation or realization.
  2. Work backward to gather the context that makes that moment land.
  3. Build the story so the audience experiences the same flip in thinking.
  4. Keep the majority of the story focused on context, not the moment itself.
  5. End on the exact moment where the change happens.
- **Example**: In Matthew’s Eileen story, the payoff lands when the student says, “First of all, I don’t like that cheeky smile of yours,” revealing trust and readiness for a bigger challenge.

### Story Quality Checklist
- **When to use**: When deciding whether a story is worth telling in a talk, pitch, or meeting.
- **Steps**:
  1. Make sure the story contains a real change.
  2. Ensure it passes the dinner test: it should sound like something you’d naturally tell at dinner.
  3. Make it your story, or center it on your own experience and vulnerability.
  4. Start as close to the end as possible.
  5. Remove unnecessary chronology, filler, and artificial dialogue.
- **Example**: Instead of a long vacation recap from Aruba, Matthew would start at the dessert table when the meaningful realization happened.

### Stakes-Building Toolkit
- **When to use**: When you need to keep an audience wondering what happens next in a story, pitch, or presentation.
- **Steps**:
  1. Plant an elephant early: signal that something important is at stake immediately.
  2. Use a backpack: tell the audience your plan so they emotionally carry your hopes and dreams.
  3. Use breadcrumbs: reveal hints without giving away the full picture.
  4. Use an hourglass: slow down time at the moment of highest tension.
  5. Use a crystal ball: forecast a plausible bad future to create worry.
- **Example**: In the Eileen story, Matthew opens by saying he worries about calling her to the board because she lacks confidence, then later predicts she might cry in front of the class if he gets it wrong.

### Speaking with Adjacency
- **When to use**: When you need a story to explain a business idea, product, or feature that feels hard to make intuitive.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the theme, meaning, or message you want the audience to feel.
  2. Find a story from your life that carries the same emotional logic.
  3. Do not force content-to-content matching.
  4. Snap the analogy into place only at the end so the audience realizes the connection.
  5. Use the story to create the desired feeling, then map that feeling back to the business point.
- **Example**: A scientist selling better-sized tubes tells a story about grocery-store apples and family preferences, then snaps it back to the idea that customers deserve the exact tools they need.

### Homework for Life
- **When to use**: When you want a reliable system for generating stories, preserving memories, and training yourself to notice meaningful moments.
- **Steps**:
  1. Every day, identify at least one moment worth remembering.
  2. Write it down in a spreadsheet or notes system with the date.
  3. Do not worry if it is not yet a full story; capture the moment anyway.
  4. Review your day in chunks if needed: morning, afternoon, evening.
  5. Over time, use the accumulated moments as a story bank and a lens for noticing more.
- **Example**: Matthew records moments like a student’s quip, a family interaction, or a surprising thought; over time, these become stories or parts of stories.

### The Four Story-Engagement Levers
- **When to use**: When speaking in business, on stage, or in any setting where you need to hold attention.
- **Steps**:
  1. Create stakes so the audience cares what happens next.
  2. Introduce surprise so the audience gets a satisfying reveal.
  3. Build suspense so the audience stays in anticipation.
  4. Use humor to differentiate yourself and keep attention.
- **Example**: Matthew cites Steve Jobs as a master of surprise and uses humor plus suspense in business talks to keep audiences engaged.

## Core Advice
- **When a story feels too long or meandering**: Start as close to the end as possible and cut everything that doesn’t serve the central change — because the shortest version is usually the best version.
- **When a business presentation risks sounding generic**: Add a personal detail about yourself that makes you human and memorable — because people connect to people, not corporate monoliths.
- **When a feature, product, or concept is hard to explain**: Find an adjacent story with the same theme instead of forcing a literal analogy — because theme-based storytelling creates a powerful snap of understanding.
- **When an audience may not care yet**: Plant an elephant immediately by signaling that something important is at stake — because attention begins with worry.
- **When a story needs momentum**: Tell the audience your plan before executing it — because once people know what you hope for, they can root for you.
- **When a story reaches a tense moment**: Slow down and linger with details instead of rushing — because an hourglass effect increases suspense.
- **When a story needs a memorable opening**: Begin with location and action — because location activates imagination and action signals movement.
- **When a business audience is skeptical or bored**: Use humor, especially nostalgia or “one of these things is not like the other” structure — because humor differentiates you and improves recall.
- **When you worry about running out of stories**: Keep a daily story log instead of relying on memory — because ordinary life contains far more story material than you think.
- **When you feel stage fright before speaking**: Focus on preparation and remember that most nervousness happens before you start — because rehearsal and recordings reduce uncertainty.
- **When you face an unfamiliar opportunity**: Say yes first, then decide later whether to keep going — because the best opportunities often appear only after you step through the door.
- **When a room may not be listening**: Assume nobody wants to hear you and earn attention through stakes, surprise, suspense, and humor — because attention is not automatic.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Business communication should be polished, data-heavy, and professionally detached. → **Their view**: Stories beat charts and facts because people remember imagery and change, not raw information — because humans are wired for narrative.
- **Conventional**: A good business speaker should avoid making it about themselves. → **Their view**: Strategic self-insertion is essential — because a small personal detail creates trust, personality, and connection.
- **Conventional**: Tell the full chronological story with all the context. → **Their view**: Start at the end and strip away most chronology — because the audience only needs enough context to understand the change.
- **Conventional**: Great stories require extraordinary events. → **Their view**: The best stories often come from tiny, ordinary moments — because the real action is usually internal.
- **Conventional**: Corporate speakers should avoid humor unless they’re sure it will land. → **Their view**: Humor is one of the best ways to stand out — because even a missed joke won’t ruin a strong story.
- **Conventional**: You should say no to protect your time. → **Their view**: Saying yes is often the better default — because unexpected opportunities can lead to major growth.
