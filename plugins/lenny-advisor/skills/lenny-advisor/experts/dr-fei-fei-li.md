# Dr. Fei-Fei Li

**Co-founder of World Labs; Professor at Stanford University; Co-director of Stanford Human-Centered AI Institute** | **World Labs / Stanford University** | Expertise: ai-strategy, computer vision, robotics, human-centered AI

## Bio
Dr. Fei-Fei Li is one of the most influential figures in modern AI, best known for creating ImageNet, the dataset that helped ignite the deep learning revolution. Her perspective matters because she combines frontier technical credibility with a deeply human-centered view of how AI should be built, governed, and used.

Today, she is pushing the field beyond language-only systems toward world models and spatial intelligence through World Labs. That makes her especially valuable for anyone trying to understand where AI is actually limited today, and what the next major breakthroughs may require.

## Signature Frameworks

### Modern AI's three-ingredient recipe
- **When to use**: When explaining what enabled the current wave of AI progress or evaluating whether a new AI system has the core ingredients to scale.
- **Steps**:
  1. Assemble large-scale training data relevant to the task.
  2. Use neural network architectures capable of learning patterns from that data.
  3. Apply sufficient compute, especially GPUs, to train at scale.
  4. Iterate on all three together rather than treating any one ingredient as sufficient.
- **Example**: ImageNet plus Geoff Hinton's team's neural network and two NVIDIA GPUs in the 2012 ImageNet Challenge sparked modern AI.

### Human-centered AI development
- **When to use**: When building, deploying, or governing AI systems that will affect people, jobs, institutions, or society.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start from the premise that AI is created by people and impacts people.
  2. Act as a responsible individual at every point in development, deployment, and application.
  3. Ensure human dignity and agency remain central to product and policy decisions.
  4. Include broader stakeholders beyond Silicon Valley, including policymakers, workers, and communities.
- **Example**: She argues that artists, farmers, nurses, and others should all have a role in shaping how AI is used.

### World model / spatial intelligence approach
- **When to use**: When language alone is insufficient and systems need to create, reason about, navigate, or act within 3D environments.
- **Steps**:
  1. Represent the world as spatially structured environments rather than only text or flat video.
  2. Enable generation of worlds from prompts such as text or images.
  3. Allow interaction and navigation within those worlds.
  4. Use the resulting world representation for reasoning, planning, design, simulation, or robotics.
- **Example**: Marble lets users prompt a world and then navigate through it, supporting VFX, game development, robotic simulation, and psychology research.

### Mission-first career decision-making
- **When to use**: When choosing jobs, teams, or major career moves in fast-moving fields like AI.
- **Steps**:
  1. Anchor on curiosity and the scientific or product north star you care about.
  2. Prioritize mission alignment and the quality of people you'll work with.
  3. Be intellectually fearless about joining or building something new.
  4. Avoid over-optimizing for every minor variable or downside scenario.
- **Example**: She describes leaving a near-tenure position at Princeton for Stanford, joining Google to work with top researchers, and starting World Labs to move faster on spatial intelligence.

## Core Advice
- **When you face major uncertainty about AI's impact on society**: do your part as a responsible participant in AI and actively care about how it is developed and deployed — because outcomes depend on human choices, not destiny.
- **When current models struggle because they lack grounding in the physical world**: invest in spatial intelligence and world models — because many capabilities require 3D reasoning, not just better text prediction.
- **When building a robotics product**: don’t assume the standard “more data and compute” playbook will work unchanged — because robots need action data and synthetic environments, not just passive internet-scale text.
- **When deciding where to work or what to build next**: optimize for mission, team, and impact — because long-term success comes more from meaningful work and strong collaborators than from perfect variable-by-variable optimization.
- **When launching an AI product before all use cases are known**: put it in users’ hands early — because real adoption reveals unexpected and valuable applications.
- **When facing a frontier problem that seems too ambitious**: pursue the overlooked bottleneck directly — because solving the missing infrastructure can unlock progress for an entire field.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: AI's future is mostly determined by the technology itself, and people can only react to it. → **Their view**: AI's impact is fundamentally up to us — because AI is a human-created tool whose consequences depend on responsible choices.
- **Conventional**: AGI is a clear scientific milestone and the main lens for progress. → **Their view**: AGI is more of a marketing term than a scientific one — because the field’s real goal is simply AI that can think and do useful things.
- **Conventional**: Scaling current models with more data and compute is enough to reach human-level intelligence. → **Their view**: Scaling helps, but major new innovations are still required — because current systems still fail at spatial reasoning, abstraction, creativity, and emotional intelligence.
- **Conventional**: Video generation is the next frontier for visual AI. → **Their view**: Spatial intelligence and world models matter more — because the real challenge is reasoning about and acting in 3D worlds.
- **Conventional**: AI will mainly replace human work across professions. → **Their view**: Everyone should have a role in AI, and the goal should be augmentation without loss of dignity — because human agency should remain central.
- **Conventional**: Big breakthroughs are usually attributable to a single genius. → **Their view**: AI progress is the result of generations of researchers and collective effort — because no one could have gotten here alone.
