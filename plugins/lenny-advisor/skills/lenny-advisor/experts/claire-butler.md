# Claire Butler

**Head of go-to-market and bottom-up growth motion, including community events, social advocacy, and Figma for Education** | **Figma** | Expertise: go-to-market, bottom-up growth, positioning, community-led growth

## Bio
Claire Butler was Figma’s 10th employee and first marketing hire, joining before launch while the company was still in stealth. She helped shape Figma’s launch, positioning, brand, and the bottom-up go-to-market motion that turned individual users into internal champions inside large organizations.

Her perspective matters because she helped build one of the clearest examples of product-led, community-led enterprise growth from the inside. Claire is especially valuable when you need to understand how to earn trust with technical users, spread adoption organically, and convert product love into enterprise expansion.

## Signature Frameworks

### Bottom-up GTM via IC love and internal champions
- **When to use**: When your product is highly valuable to individual contributors, especially technical practitioners who use the tool daily and can influence adoption inside larger organizations.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify a core IC audience that gets standalone value from the product.
  2. Build deep product love through quality, craft, and direct user feedback.
  3. Make the product easy to try and easy to share with teammates.
  4. Let usage spread organically inside organizations through collaboration.
  5. Convert passionate users into internal champions who push adoption upward.
  6. Bring in sales later to unblock procurement, security, and enterprise rollout rather than to create initial demand.
- **Example**: At Figma, designers adopted the product first, often on free or credit-card plans, and later came to Figma asking for help getting company-wide approval once usage had already spread internally at companies like Microsoft.

### Four-part playbook for getting ICs to love your product
- **When to use**: When launching a new product to a skeptical, high-expectation practitioner audience that resists traditional marketing.
- **Steps**:
  1. Build credibility with technically deep content and authentic experts, not generic marketing.
  2. Build the product with users by talking to them constantly and fixing issues quickly.
  3. Show up where the audience already is instead of trying to pull them into your own channels too early.
  4. Be transparent and human so users trust the company behind the product.
- **Example**: Figma published technical posts on topics like vector networks and grids, hired a designer advocate from its user base, used Twitter as its main community channel, and had engineers directly answer Intercom chats and fix bugs.

### Signal over metrics for early product-market fit
- **When to use**: When your product is early, sample sizes are tiny, and optimization metrics are too noisy to be meaningful.
- **Steps**:
  1. Ignore small percentage lifts that do not indicate real user pull.
  2. Look for strong qualitative signals like excitement, repeated usage, and willingness to switch.
  3. Focus on milestone progression: get one user, keep them using it, get another team, then get someone to pay.
  4. Use direct observation and anecdotes as valid evidence in the earliest stages.
- **Example**: Claire said they knew Figma was ready to launch when designers would literally take the laptop out of Dylan’s hands during demos because they wanted to try the product themselves.

### Advocate-led credibility layer across marketing, product, and sales
- **When to use**: When your buyers and users are technical practitioners who trust peers far more than marketers or salespeople.
- **Steps**:
  1. Hire advocates who are real practitioners and passionate users of the product.
  2. Embed them across marketing, product feedback, and sales conversations.
  3. Use them to create credible content, gather nuanced feedback, and explain the product in practitioner language.
  4. Scale the role by product line and region as the company grows.
- **Example**: Figma’s designer advocates joined sales calls to explain workflows and best practices; internally this became known as the “Tom Factor” because deals were more likely to close when advocate Tom Lowry joined.

### Turn the biggest blocker into the adoption wedge
- **When to use**: When a missing capability is preventing enterprise expansion and can become a strategic reason to upgrade.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the operational blocker stopping broader adoption.
  2. Study the workflows and community around that blocker deeply.
  3. Build product features, content, and events around solving it.
  4. Tie the solution to higher-tier packaging and enterprise value.
- **Example**: Design systems were initially a major blocker for Figma in larger organizations. Figma leaned into design-systems meetups, content, Schema, and product capabilities until design systems became a major reason to upgrade from Pro to Org or Enterprise.

### Scale trust through transparent public communication
- **When to use**: When the company faces outages, controversy, or any moment where user trust is at risk.
- **Steps**:
  1. Acknowledge the issue directly and quickly.
  2. Explain what happened in concrete terms.
  3. Share what is being fixed and how recurrence will be prevented.
  4. Use real people, not just the brand account, to answer questions publicly.
- **Example**: After repeated downtime, Figma published public postmortems. After the Adobe acquisition announcement, the team hosted an open Twitter Space with leaders taking live questions rather than hiding behind a corporate statement.

## Core Advice
- **When you face an early-stage product-market-fit question with very little data**: Do not over-index on small optimization metrics; look for strong user pull, repeated usage, and emotionally strong signals instead — because at tiny scale, percentage changes are noisy and can mislead you.
- **When you face a skeptical technical audience**: Use practitioner-led, technically deep content and avoid fluffy marketing language — because technical users have a high bullshit meter and trust detailed product thinking from peers.
- **When you face the challenge of getting your first users to stick**: Do things that do not scale, including hands-on support and immediate bug fixing — because every early user matters disproportionately.
- **When you face low awareness and users are unlikely to join your owned channels**: Go to the channel where your community already exists and build relationships there — because people will not come to you before they know or trust you.
- **When you face the need to spread a product inside organizations**: Make sharing and collaboration easy before payment gates kick in — because aggressive paywalls can choke off the behavior that creates enterprise demand later.
- **When you face enterprise sales conversations with practitioner buyers**: Bring credible product advocates into the sales process alongside account reps — because practitioners trust someone who has used the product in their role.
- **When you face a blocker to enterprise adoption**: Turn that blocker into a strategic focus area across product, content, and community — because the blocker often points to the highest-value expansion wedge.
- **When you face outages, backlash, or trust-damaging events**: Communicate transparently in public, with real people answering real questions — because trust compounds when users feel the company is honest and accountable.
- **When you face pressure to layer on traditional top-down GTM as you scale**: Protect the bottom-up motion that got you there while adding new motions carefully — because it is easy to break the organic engine that created demand.
- **When you face uncertainty about joining an early-stage startup**: Look for an idea that clicks logically, trusted believers around it, and a founder whose conviction makes you believe obstacles will be overcome — because conviction and product intuition matter before certainty exists.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Early-stage teams should measure everything and optimize funnels from the start. → **Their view**: You cannot optimize your way to product-market fit — because early numbers are too noisy to reveal true user pull.
- **Conventional**: Marketing should lead with polished messaging about benefits like collaboration and efficiency. → **Their view**: For technical audiences, traditional marketing language hurts credibility — because practitioners respond to technical depth and product craft.
- **Conventional**: Sales should drive enterprise adoption from the top down by targeting executives first. → **Their view**: The most efficient path can be bottom-up — because practitioners can create internal demand before procurement enters.
- **Conventional**: As companies scale, they should rely more on brand-managed communication and less on direct, messy public interaction. → **Their view**: Even at scale, companies should stay human and transparent — because trust is built in hard moments.
- **Conventional**: The right free-tier design is mainly about maximizing immediate conversion to paid. → **Their view**: If your product grows through collaboration, free-tier design should prioritize spread before monetization — because sharing drives enterprise pull.
