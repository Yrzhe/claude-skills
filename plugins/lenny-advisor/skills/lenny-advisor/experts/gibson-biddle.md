# Gibson Biddle

**Product leadership teacher, writer, and workshop facilitator** | **Ask Gib / independent; formerly Netflix and Chegg** | Expertise: product strategy, roadmap prioritization, product leadership, product culture

## Bio
Gibson Biddle is a veteran product leader best known for serving as VP of Product at Netflix and CPO at Chegg before moving into teaching and advising product teams full-time. His perspective matters because his frameworks were forged in high-stakes operating environments, where strategy had to be tested against customer behavior, business economics, and organizational reality.

He is especially valuable when teams need clarity on what to build, how to prioritize, and how to align leadership around product direction. Biddle’s style is practical, experimental, and unusually candid about tradeoffs.

## Signature Frameworks

### DHM product strategy framework
- **When to use**: Use when defining or evaluating product strategy, major bets, or feature ideas to ensure they create customer value, defensibility, and business value.
- **Steps**:
  1. Ask whether the idea will delight customers, ideally in a meaningfully better or 10x-improved way.
  2. Ask whether it creates a hard-to-copy advantage such as brand, personalization/data, network effects, switching costs, or economies of scale.
  3. Ask whether it enhances margin or business performance through retention, growth, monetization, or better investment efficiency.
  4. Quantify tradeoffs with experiments where possible, especially the tension between delight and margin.
  5. Treat strategy as a set of hypotheses and test them one by one rather than assuming a perfect master plan.
- **Example**: At Netflix, the “Perfect New Release Test” gave a test group next-day access to new-release DVDs. It slightly improved retention, but the value created was estimated at about $1 million while the extra inventory cost was about $5 million, so it was not rolled out broadly.

### GEM prioritization framework
- **When to use**: Use when leadership or product teams are misaligned on what should matter most in a given stage of the company.
- **Steps**:
  1. Force-rank Growth, Engagement, and Monetization.
  2. Define what each means in your business context.
  3. Choose a concrete metric for each, especially an engagement metric that proxies for product quality.
  4. Get leadership alignment on the ranking before making roadmap decisions.
  5. Revisit the ranking as the company stage changes.
- **Example**: At Chegg, Biddle brought the CEO and CFO together because one wanted “grow, baby, grow” while the other wanted to slow down until the business model was proven. They aligned on Growth first, Engagement second, Monetization third.

### SWAG-first strategy development
- **When to use**: Use when entering a new company, tackling a fuzzy strategic problem, or trying to accelerate alignment without overanalyzing.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with a SWAG: a “stupid wild-ass guess” about the right strategy or prioritization.
  2. Write down your initial point of view quickly rather than waiting for perfect information.
  3. Share it one-on-one with knowledgeable stakeholders to refine it.
  4. Iterate based on feedback from people closer to the details.
  5. Socialize the improved version more broadly once the thinking is sharper.
- **Example**: When joining a new company, Biddle would give himself two weeks to draft a product strategy, then refine it through one-on-one conversations before sharing it company-wide several weeks later.

### Personal board of directors
- **When to use**: Use throughout your career to make better decisions, learn faster, and get perspective on role, company, and strategy choices.
- **Steps**:
  1. Build a community of peers by staying in touch with former colleagues and industry contacts.
  2. Identify potential mentors without formally asking them to “be your mentor.”
  3. Find ways to be useful to those people first.
  4. Use the network to compare notes, get data, and pressure-test decisions.
  5. Lean on the board for career choices, strategic questions, and blind-spot reduction.
- **Example**: Biddle used his board to evaluate whether companies like Electronic Arts, Netflix, and Chegg were good bets, and to get tactical answers such as the right level of investment in mobile versus desktop.

### High-stakes vs. low-stakes decision framing
- **When to use**: Use when teams are overreacting to decisions or struggling to judge whether a risky move is acceptable.
- **Steps**:
  1. Estimate the magnitude of the upside or downside relative to the size of the business.
  2. Ask whether the decision is reversible.
  3. Classify it as a two-way door or one-way door.
  4. Move faster on low-stakes, reversible decisions.
  5. Reserve heavier process and caution for high-stakes, irreversible decisions.
- **Example**: On Netflix auto-canceling inactive users, Biddle framed the move as relatively low stakes because the revenue impact was small relative to Netflix’s scale and the policy was reversible.

## Core Advice
- **When you face a strategic product decision**: Do a DHM check: ask whether the idea delights customers, is hard to copy, and improves the business — because this prevents teams from chasing ideas that are merely interesting or easy to copy.
- **When you face customer requests that seem obviously valuable**: Do an experiment to measure actual retention or business impact instead of trusting stated preference alone — because customers may strongly ask for something that produces only a tiny behavioral change.
- **When you face roadmap clutter from niche features**: Do a reach check and kill “two percenters” that only serve a tiny fraction of users unless they are strategically critical — because low-usage features add complexity and hidden maintenance costs.
- **When you face leadership disagreement about what to prioritize**: Do a forced ranking of Growth, Engagement, and Monetization and align on the order before debating features — because many roadmap conflicts are really unresolved executive disagreements about company stage.
- **When you face a new role or a fuzzy strategic problem**: Do a fast SWAG first, then refine it through one-on-one feedback rather than waiting for a perfect answer — because a rough point of view accelerates learning and alignment.
- **When you face important career decisions**: Do the work to build a personal board of directors made up of peers and mentors — because trusted external perspectives help you see around corners.
- **When you face the challenge of becoming a stronger PM early in your career**: Do everything you can to optimize for learning and become expert in your area before chasing title progression — because deep learning compounds more than premature ambition.
- **When you face the transition from IC PM to manager or leader**: Do more hiring and recruiting work, even before it is formally your job — because leadership leverage comes heavily from attracting and replacing talent.
- **When you face a packed PM workday**: Do begin the day with intent, minimize meetings, spend time with customers, and balance doing with thinking — because these habits keep PMs focused on real leverage.
- **When you face uncertainty about your career path**: Do treat your career like a product: form hypotheses, run experiments, learn, and iterate boldly — because career progress is rarely linear.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: If customers loudly ask for a feature, you should build it. → **Their view**: Even highly requested features should be tested because they may create only marginal retention or business value — because stated demand does not equal meaningful impact.
- **Conventional**: More features are better if some users love them. → **Their view**: Small-reach features often should be removed rather than celebrated — because “two percenters” create complexity and hidden costs.
- **Conventional**: Advertising is an obvious monetization lever for a large consumer product. → **Their view**: Sometimes you should kill ads to preserve simplicity and focus on the capability that truly differentiates you — because focus can be more valuable than a generic revenue stream.
- **Conventional**: Every product decision feels high stakes and deserves heavy process. → **Their view**: Many product decisions are low-stakes, reversible two-way doors and should be made faster — because teams waste time overdramatizing reversible choices.
- **Conventional**: You should formally ask someone to be your mentor. → **Their view**: Don’t ask directly; instead, identify helpful people and become useful to them first — because natural relationships are more durable.
- **Conventional**: Your employer is responsible for managing your career progression. → **Their view**: You are responsible for your career, and you should actively design it — because career growth requires deliberate experimentation and external perspective.
