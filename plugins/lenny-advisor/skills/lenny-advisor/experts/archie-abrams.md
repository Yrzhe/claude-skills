# Archie Abrams

**VP of Product, Head of Growth** | **Shopify** | Expertise: growth, experimentation, go-to-market, product strategy, metrics, pricing, leadership

## Bio
Archie Abrams leads product and growth at Shopify across product, design, engineering, data, ops, and growth marketing. His perspective matters because he operates at the intersection of experimentation and long-term product strategy, where many growth teams overfit to short-term metrics and miss the real business impact.

He is especially notable for Shopify’s unconventional growth philosophy: optimize for long-term cohort GMV, use long-term holdouts, and balance data with strong founder-led product judgment. If you want a view on growth that is rigorous but not blindly metric-driven, Archie is a strong reference point.

## Signature Frameworks

### Long-term cohort value experimentation
- **When to use**: When short-term conversion or activation metrics may not reflect true business impact, especially in products with long feedback loops and power-law outcomes.
- **Steps**:
  1. Run an experiment and evaluate the short-term result quickly enough to keep shipping velocity high.
  2. Preserve a holdout or cohort assignment so you can revisit the same users months later.
  3. Track downstream business outcomes over 3, 6, 9, and 12+ months, not just immediate conversion.
  4. Measure success on long-term cohort value such as GMV, gross profit, or total value created, rather than local funnel lift alone.
  5. Use the long-term readouts to recalibrate which short-term metrics are actually predictive.
- **Example**: Shopify closes experiments after a few weeks but keeps cohort assignment intact and automatically sends experiment owners updated results at 3, 6, 9, and 12 months. Abrams said 30-40% of experiments that look positive short term show no long-term GMV lift.

### Optimize for absolute outcomes, not local conversion rates
- **When to use**: When multiple teams own different funnel stages and local KPI optimization risks creating perverse incentives.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define success as the total number of users who reach a meaningful outcome, not the percentage conversion at one step.
  2. Check whether improving a local rate is actually being achieved by making the previous step harder.
  3. Encourage teams to reduce friction earlier in the funnel, even if that lowers conversion rates.
  4. Evaluate whether more people ultimately reach activation, success, or value creation in absolute terms.
- **Example**: If a team owns signup-to-activation, it can improve its conversion rate by making signup harder. Shopify instead pushes teams to think about the total number of merchants activated, even if conversion percentages worsen.

### Monetary friction reduction
- **When to use**: When early-stage users face financial barriers that prevent them from trying, persisting, or reaching product value.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the monetary barriers that block users early, such as trial length, upfront price, failed payments, or missing credits.
  2. Test ways to reduce those barriers through trials, incentives, credits, or pricing changes.
  3. Measure whether reduced friction expands access to high-potential users rather than just attracting low-intent users.
  4. Validate impact over the long term to see whether the intervention creates more successful customers.
- **Example**: At Shopify, lowering early monetary friction sometimes unlocked entrepreneurs who otherwise would have given up. Abrams noted that reducing friction can causally give someone more time to make their business work.

### Hybrid self-serve plus sales journey design
- **When to use**: When a company has both product-led and sales-led motions and users should be able to move between them.
- **Steps**:
  1. Avoid forcing users to choose upfront between self-serve and sales.
  2. Create pathways from self-serve into sales and from sales back into product-led flows.
  3. Rebuild attribution and LTV models so value is not lost when users switch motions.
  4. Use incrementality testing to understand which channels truly drive outcomes across both motions.
- **Example**: Shopify previously had separate self-serve and sales funnels. Abrams described moving toward hybrid journeys where a merchant might arrive via self-serve, then talk to sales, which required reworking attribution and LTV models.

### Founder-taste-led product governance
- **When to use**: When building foundational product bets where long-term product quality and technical architecture matter more than short-term KPI movement.
- **Steps**:
  1. Anchor product decisions in a long-term vision rather than quarterly metric targets.
  2. Use data and qualitative input as inputs, not as the sole decision-maker.
  3. Establish a small set of leaders with strong product and technical taste who review and approve what ships.
  4. Debate not only what to build, but how to build it so the architecture preserves future optionality.
- **Example**: In Shopify core product, KPIs are effectively banned. Abrams described how Tobi and group leads review projects deeply, including technical implementation details like how CSV importers should be architected.

## Core Advice
- **When short-term experiment results look strong**: do long-term follow-up on the same cohorts and judge success on downstream business value, not just immediate lift — because many wins are pull-forward effects or low-value users.
- **When teams optimize separate funnel stages**: set goals around absolute numbers reaching the end state instead of local conversion rates — because rate-based goals create incentives to constrict the prior step.
- **When retention pressure is high in a power-law business**: optimize for total cohort value and long-term revenue contribution, even if some users churn early — because a few breakout successes can outweigh many failures.
- **When an experiment is neutral**: ship the version your team believes is better if your intuition says it helps users — because metrics may not yet capture downstream value.
- **When onboarding drop-off is high**: collect more information during signup and use it to personalize guidance — because better context enables more relevant setup and support.
- **When early users hesitate because of price or commitment**: test reductions in monetary friction like trials, incentives, or credits — because financial barriers can block high-potential users from getting enough runway.
- **When you have both PLG and sales motions**: design hybrid journeys instead of forcing an either/or choice — because users often want flexibility across motions.
- **When attribution is messy**: prioritize incrementality testing over multi-touch attribution — because incrementality is closer to causal impact.
- **When you want to remove metrics from product teams**: only do it if you have strong founder or leadership taste and high standards — because without that, teams drift without accountability.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Great SaaS companies should minimize churn and maximize retention for every customer. → **Their view**: Shopify intentionally lowers barriers to entry even if that increases churn — because the winners in each cohort create outsized value.
- **Conventional**: Conversion rates are the best way to manage funnel performance. → **Their view**: Absolute numbers reaching meaningful outcomes are often better — because local rate optimization can hide harmful tradeoffs.
- **Conventional**: If an experiment wins on short-term metrics, it’s a real win. → **Their view**: A large share of short-term wins disappear over time — because many only pull forward behavior or attract low-value users.
- **Conventional**: Discounts and reduced pricing mostly attract lower-quality users. → **Their view**: Reducing monetary friction can unlock high-potential users — because a small financial break can give them enough runway to succeed.
- **Conventional**: Every product team should have KPIs and OKRs. → **Their view**: For core product, KPIs can be counterproductive — because strong taste and long-term vision can be better guides.
- **Conventional**: The most important product question is what to build and how fast to ship it. → **Their view**: How you build it, especially the technical architecture, can matter even more — because architecture determines future strategy.
- **Conventional**: Users should be routed cleanly into either self-serve or sales-led funnels. → **Their view**: The best experience is often a hybrid journey — because forcing an upfront choice is artificial.
