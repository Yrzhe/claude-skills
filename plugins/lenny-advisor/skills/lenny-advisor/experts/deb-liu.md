# Deb Liu

**CEO** | **Ancestry** | Expertise: product-management, leadership, career development, growth

## Bio
Deb Liu is a longtime product leader turned CEO who previously served as VP of Product at Facebook, where she helped build major businesses including Marketplace, mobile direct-response ads, games, and payments. Her perspective matters because she has repeatedly scaled zero-to-one products inside large organizations while also thinking deeply about career strategy, leadership, and how introverts can lead effectively.

## Signature Frameworks

### PM your career like a product
- **When to use**: When you're making career decisions without a clear plan, or trying to intentionally shape your long-term growth.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define what success looks like in 5 years.
  2. Write a career “spec” with desired outcomes, milestones, skills, and experiences.
  3. Create metrics for your career, like you would for a product.
  4. Evaluate each role or opportunity against whether it moves you toward that goal.
  5. Break big aspirations into smaller steps and sequence them over time.
- **Example**: A founder wanted to join the board of a Fortune 100 company. Deb advised her to learn the board path, meet current board members, and break the 10-year goal into smaller steps.

### Balance learning and impact
- **When to use**: When choosing roles or projects and trying to avoid either stagnation or constant low-impact learning.
- **Steps**:
  1. Take roles where you can have strong impact using existing strengths.
  2. Then deliberately move into situations where you're a beginner again.
  3. Alternate between mastery and stretch roles over time.
  4. Carry lessons from prior roles into new contexts.
- **Example**: Deb often took jobs she wasn’t fully qualified for, mastered them, and then moved into another area where she had to learn again.

### Zero-to-one inside a large company
- **When to use**: When incubating a new product or business inside a large organization.
- **Steps**:
  1. Choose spaces where others are not focused; zig when others zag.
  2. Start with minimal resources and avoid excessive visibility early.
  3. Talk to customers or adjacent partners to identify urgent unmet needs.
  4. Iterate rapidly through multiple versions rather than over-planning.
  5. Protect the team from over-scrutiny while the product is still fragile.
  6. Treat innovation as a portfolio with a high expected failure rate.
- **Example**: For Facebook’s first direct-response mobile ad product, Deb’s team worked outside the main ads org, tested five or six versions, nearly got shut down multiple times, and eventually built a billion-dollar business in about 18 months.

### Growth is a game of inches
- **When to use**: When trying to grow an existing product and deciding between incremental optimization and big bets.
- **Steps**:
  1. Generate a large list of growth hypotheses.
  2. Prioritize and test them in batches or sprint cycles.
  3. Expect most ideas to fail and treat that as learning.
  4. Stack many small wins to create meaningful compounding growth.
  5. Improve the team’s throughput and hit rate over time.
- **Example**: On payments growth, Deb’s team kept a list of around 100 hypotheses and worked through them in batches. She also cited a Facebook example where adding a “create an ad” link next to ads became a major growth driver.

### 30-60-90 day onboarding plan
- **When to use**: When starting a new role, especially in a new company or team.
- **Steps**:
  1. First 30 days: listen and learn through a structured listening tour.
  2. Document what you’re hearing and summarize the state of the union.
  3. Identify key problems, wishes, and organizational dynamics.
  4. Next 30 days: align stakeholders on the problems and future direction.
  5. Final 30 days: begin executing with a few quick wins and a clear plan.
  6. Share the plan with your manager and stakeholders so expectations are explicit.
- **Example**: When Deb joined Ancestry, she spoke with over 60 people in 30 days, took extensive notes, reflected back what she heard, and used that to align on priorities before pushing into execution.

### Bias interrupters for introvert-inclusive leadership
- **When to use**: When meetings and decision-making processes favor the loudest voices over the best thinking.
- **Steps**:
  1. Collect opinions and votes offline in a document before discussion.
  2. Require every participant to contribute a view.
  3. Go around the room so each person has equal airtime.
  4. Ask functional leaders not just for their function’s stance, but what they personally would do.
  5. Design processes that reward thoughtfulness, not just speed of speaking.
- **Example**: Deb used offline voting documents and round-robin input in leadership meetings so introverts and processors had equal voice before extroverts dominated the conversation.

## Core Advice
- **When you face uncertainty about your next career move**: Do a written career spec with a 5-year destination, milestones, and decision criteria — because intentionality prevents drifting into roles that don’t build toward your goals.
- **When you face a stretch role you don’t fully qualify for**: Lean into curiosity, humility, and passion for the problem — because people who keep learning often outperform today’s experts over time.
- **When you face a PM interview without direct PM experience**: Show deep passion for the product, customer, and problem space — because authentic product insight and customer empathy matter more than memorizing PM jargon.
- **When you face failure, rejection, or hard feedback**: Treat it as input for adaptation and bounce back quickly — because resilience is what turns setbacks into stronger judgment.
- **When you face a denied promotion or missed role**: Reshape the role you have into the role you wish you had — because agency often creates better outcomes than waiting for permission.
- **When you face the opportunity to build a zero-to-one product inside a big company**: Expect a high failure rate, seek patience over attention, and protect the team from over-scrutiny — because fragile ideas need room to iterate.
- **When you face an early-career choice between core product work and a risky innovation bet**: Learn core product skills first, then take a deliberate big swing later — because fundamentals give you a stronger base for risk.
- **When you face discomfort speaking up as an introvert**: Treat communication as a learnable skill and practice it — because advocacy builds credibility, resources, and momentum.
- **When you face resistance to self-promotion**: Reframe it as educating others about your team’s work — because sharing progress is service, not vanity.
- **When you face the challenge of building a writing or public-sharing habit**: Write what you repeat and create accountability with a manager or peer group — because repeated advice is worth codifying.
- **When you face pressure to find one huge growth lever**: Build a pipeline of many small experiments — because compounding incremental wins usually beat waiting for a magic bullet.
- **When you face a new role and feel pressure to prove yourself immediately**: Spend the first month listening deeply and aligning on priorities before pushing major changes — because diagnosis should come before treatment.
