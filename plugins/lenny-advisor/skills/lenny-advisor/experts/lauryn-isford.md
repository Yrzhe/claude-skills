# Lauryn Isford

**Head of Growth** | **Airtable** | Expertise: growth, onboarding, activation, PLG

## Bio
Lauryn Isford led growth at Airtable after earlier growth roles at Meta/Facebook and Blue Bottle. She brings a rare mix of analytical rigor and customer empathy to growth work, especially around onboarding, activation, and product-led growth systems.

Her perspective matters because she does not treat growth as “test everything and optimize the metric.” Instead, she focuses on building the right growth system for the product, using experimentation selectively, and making sure onboarding and activation actually help users reach value.

## Signature Frameworks

### Experiment selectively, not by default
- **When to use**: When deciding whether a product change should be A/B tested or shipped directly.
- **Steps**:
  1. Ask whether the goal is precise metric measurement or risk mitigation.
  2. If the change is dramatic and risky, use experimentation to reduce downside.
  3. If the change is clearly beneficial and the metric precision won’t change the decision, skip the experiment.
  4. Use customer research, mocks, and product rigor to build conviction before shipping.
  5. Reserve experimentation time for higher-leverage work instead of testing everything.
- **Example**: Airtable shipped a new Forms feature that let submitters request a copy of their submission without running an A/B test, because customer analysis already showed it was valuable and the team expected it to be net good even if it shifted signup mix.

### Three-part onboarding system
- **When to use**: When improving activation for a complex self-serve or collaborative product.
- **Steps**:
  1. Create guided onboarding that reduces cognitive load and helps users make first progress quickly.
  2. Add personalization so users start with a relevant use case rather than a generic one.
  3. Provide ongoing education that helps users progress from beginner to intermediate to advanced.
  4. Retire or reduce reliance on passive tooltips when they are too weak for the product complexity.
  5. Use a portfolio of incremental and larger changes rather than a single tactic.
- **Example**: Airtable combined an immersive guided onboarding wizard, personalized use-case selection, and ongoing education via “The Mole” to drive a 20% lift in activation rate.

### Activation metric decomposition
- **When to use**: When defining or improving an activation metric for onboarding or growth.
- **Steps**:
  1. Choose a north star activation metric that strongly correlates with long-term retention.
  2. Break the metric into its component parts to identify the real levers.
  3. Add supporting metrics that capture adjacent outcomes the main metric misses.
  4. Use the metric set to understand tradeoffs between sophistication, retention, and collaboration.
  5. Treat the main activation metric as an output, not the only thing the team can directly move.
- **Example**: Airtable used week-four multi-user active as the core activation metric, then added week-two/week-four retention and a “Build” sophistication score to understand whether onboarding changes improved collaboration, retention, or product sophistication.

### PLG funnel: join → evaluate → upgrade → expand
- **When to use**: When structuring a product-led growth strategy or organizing growth work.
- **Steps**:
  1. Join: help users enter the product through acquisition or invitation.
  2. Evaluate: help users determine whether the product delivers value for their needs.
  3. Upgrade: convert users to paid or higher-value usage once they see premium value.
  4. Expand: increase adoption across more people or teams inside an account.
  5. Revisit the funnel over time as the business and opportunity shift.
- **Example**: At Airtable, acquisition mapped to join, activation to evaluate, monetization to upgrade, and later an expansion motion emerged as larger companies adopted the product more broadly.

### Reverse trial / freemium hybrid
- **When to use**: When deciding how to package self-serve access and premium value in a PLG business.
- **Steps**:
  1. Offer a free trial so users can experience premium value and advanced features.
  2. Also offer a free forever plan so users can keep using the product without immediate payment.
  3. Use the trial window to showcase the full product and create aha moments.
  4. Delay monetization pressure when the long-term goal is broad adoption and trust.
  5. Choose the model based on your philosophy about user growth versus immediate revenue.
- **Example**: Lauryn described Airtable’s ideal as letting users try the product for free, then showing premium capabilities during the trial while still preserving a free plan for long-term adoption.

## Core Advice
- **When a product change is clearly valuable and the experiment would only add measurement overhead**: ship it without an A/B test and use post-launch analysis instead — because if the decision won’t change based on a small metric delta, experimentation can waste engineering, analytics, and PM time.
- **When making a dramatic or risky product change**: use experimentation primarily as risk mitigation — because experiments are most useful when the downside of a bad launch is meaningful.
- **When onboarding a complex product with high cognitive load**: use a guided, immersive onboarding flow that asks questions and shows progress visually — because scaffolding helps users get to value faster than passive tips.
- **When designing onboarding for a broad user base**: start with one generic onboarding experience that covers most use cases, then personalize later — because a single well-designed flow can solve the majority of cases.
- **When segmenting onboarding users**: segment by learning style and building style, not just by job title or department — because how someone learns often predicts onboarding needs better than persona labels.
- **When users are new to a product**: teach only what they need now; defer advanced features to ongoing education — because early feature dumping increases confusion.
- **When defining activation**: pick a metric that is tightly correlated with long-term retention, even if it is hard to reach — because a lower activation rate can be better if it identifies users who truly stick.
- **When your activation metric is too broad or opaque**: decompose it into its component behaviors and add supporting metrics — because breaking the metric apart reveals which levers matter.
- **When choosing between user-level and workspace-level activation metrics**: match the metric to your product’s pricing and adoption model — because collaborative products often need account/workspace metrics.
- **When a north star metric starts to feel stale**: be willing to change it after enough stability — because the biggest business opportunity can shift over time.
- **When onboarding teams are tempted to push premium features too early**: optimize for customer value first, not packaging or monetization priors — because forcing premium features into onboarding can hurt learning.
- **When a SaaS product cannot be fully self-serve**: find some way for users to experience value without a human present — because even partial self-serve can create aha moments and reduce dependence on sales or CS.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Every meaningful product change should be A/B tested. → **Their view**: Many changes should be shipped directly if the team already has strong conviction and the experiment would not materially improve the decision — because experiments are expensive and often unnecessary.
- **Conventional**: Growth teams should optimize for the biggest measurable metric lift and prove impact through experiments. → **Their view**: Growth teams should be judged by doing the right thing for customers and the business, even when that is not neatly captured by an experiment — because customer-centered judgment matters.
- **Conventional**: Onboarding should teach users about all the cool features as early as possible. → **Their view**: Onboarding should focus on what the user needs now, not the full feature set — because early feature dumping increases cognitive load.
- **Conventional**: A single activation metric should define success forever. → **Their view**: North star metrics should evolve as the business matures — because the highest-leverage opportunity changes.
- **Conventional**: Higher activation rates are always better. → **Their view**: A lower activation rate can be healthier if it is more tightly correlated with long-term retention — because it filters for durable value.
- **Conventional**: Onboarding should mirror pricing and packaging, pushing premium features to drive conversion. → **Their view**: Onboarding should not be dictated by monetization structure if that harms the user’s ability to learn and succeed — because customer need comes first.
