# Jeremy Henrickson

**Chief Product Officer** | **Rippling** | Expertise: product-management, leadership, strategy, go-to-market

## Bio
Jeremy Henrickson is the Chief Product Officer at Rippling and previously served as Chief Product Officer at Coinbase, where he helped lead product through hypergrowth and high uncertainty. His perspective matters because he has repeatedly built and scaled products in environments where speed, complexity, and ambiguity collide.

He is especially valuable for leaders who need to preserve startup-like velocity inside a larger company, build platform-based products that compound over time, and make strong decisions without perfect information.

## Signature Frameworks

### Small Teams with Clear Missions
- **When to use**: When scaling product development, launching new products, or preserving speed as the company grows.
- **Steps**:
  1. Break company goals into narrowly scoped missions.
  2. Assign a small, highly capable team to each mission.
  3. Minimize horizontal communication overhead.
  4. Give teams direct access to senior leadership for fast feedback.
  5. Expand the team only after scope and launch needs are clear.
- **Example**: At Rippling, a team of about four people built the time and attendance product in roughly nine months, focusing only on that product and integrating with the broader suite only where needed.

### Founder-Type Product Incubation Model
- **When to use**: When starting a new product line inside a multi-product company.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with a one-page view of the opportunity.
  2. Choose an entrepreneurial engineer who can think like a product owner.
  3. Pair them with a designer who understands the platform and UX patterns.
  4. Have the lead learn the platform and talk to prior internal founders.
  5. Add 2–4 engineers for the zero-to-one team.
  6. Build toward launch in 6–9 months.
  7. Pressure-test with a DRI like the CEO or product leader.
  8. Scale only if complexity demands it.
- **Example**: Rippling’s pattern for new products is one entrepreneurial engineer, one design partner, a few engineers, and regular reviews with Jeremy or CEO Parker Conrad.

### Design for the Most Complex Use Case First
- **When to use**: When building foundational products or platforms that must scale across many segments, geographies, or edge cases.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the hardest realistic use cases.
  2. Understand the simple case, but model the complex one too.
  3. Make decisions that can absorb future complexity.
  4. Skip advanced cases in v1 if needed, but avoid blocking architecture.
  5. Use this to prevent expensive rewrites later.
- **Example**: For global payroll, Rippling designed for six very different countries up front instead of cloning the U.S. system country by country, making most of the system reusable platform logic.

### Go and See
- **When to use**: When leaders need clarity on priorities, domain mechanics, or what really matters.
- **Steps**:
  1. Don’t rely only on summaries.
  2. Go directly to the source: users, regulations, systems, code, or operations.
  3. Walk all the way to ground on the hardest part.
  4. Use firsthand understanding to guide decisions.
  5. Repeat often enough that depth becomes cultural.
- **Example**: In global payroll, Rippling leaders dug directly into country-specific payroll and tax rules, which exposed unexpected differences and reshaped the system design.

### Imperatives
- **When to use**: When too many teams are pursuing too many priorities and leadership needs company-wide focus.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the limited set of cross-company priorities.
  2. Force-rank them and draw a clear line below them.
  3. Communicate what is on the list and what is not.
  4. Require each team to incorporate them into plans.
  5. Use them to align without fully centralizing prioritization.
- **Example**: Rippling used “imperatives” to define the handful of priorities every product and engineering team had to account for.

## Core Advice
- **When you face rapid growth and operational chaos**: stabilize immediate issues while keeping a clear six-month view — because short-term fixes should support long-term scale.
- **When you face slowing velocity as your company scales**: organize work into small teams with clear missions — because smaller teams decide faster and own outcomes more directly.
- **When you face repeated complexity across teams**: invest in a clear internal platform with strong interfaces — because shared infrastructure reduces cognitive load and duplicated work.
- **When you face uncertainty about what is really blocking execution**: dive deep personally into the hardest problem area — because real constraints show up in the details.
- **When you face pressure to ship an MVP quickly**: design the architecture around the most complex important use cases — because simplistic assumptions are expensive to unwind.
- **When you face international expansion decisions**: start earlier than you think you need to — because localization and local context are harder than expected.
- **When you face a need to move faster as a company**: make top-level priorities explicit, including what is not a priority — because clarity forces tradeoffs.
- **When you face a new domain that requires specialist knowledge**: learn it firsthand before delegating fully — because product leaders need their own judgment, not just expert summaries.
- **When you face hiring decisions for PMs in ambiguous environments**: test how candidates react to changing assumptions and incomplete information — because question quality reveals product judgment.
- **When you face early-career growth as a PM**: stay humble and treat the job as continuous discovery — because product work exists where the answer is not yet known.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Start with the simplest MVP possible and expand later. → **Their view**: Design for the most complex important use case first — because speed without architectural foresight creates painful rewrites.
- **Conventional**: Leaders should stay high level and delegate details. → **Their view**: Leaders should go into the trenches regularly — because firsthand exposure reveals the real constraints and opportunities.
- **Conventional**: Scaling requires more process and standardized frameworks. → **Their view**: Use only enough process to frame good decisions — because rigid process can replace judgment and slow teams down.
- **Conventional**: International expansion can wait until the domestic business is mature. → **Their view**: Expand earlier than you think — because the learning curve is longer and more specialized than expected.
- **Conventional**: Specialists should own specialist domains while product leaders stay generalist. → **Their view**: Product leaders should first become deeply informed themselves — because strong product decisions require direct understanding.
