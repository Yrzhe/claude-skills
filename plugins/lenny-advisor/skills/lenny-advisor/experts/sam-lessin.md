# Sam Lessin

**Partner at Slow Ventures** | **Slow Ventures** | Expertise: etiquette, venture capital, product management, startup strategy

## Bio
Sam Lessin is a venture capitalist, former VP of Product at Facebook, and two-time founder who brings an unusually practical lens to founder behavior. His perspective matters because he treats etiquette not as social polish, but as a real operating skill that affects trust, deal flow, and how people respond to you in high-stakes rooms.

He stands out for connecting social calibration with startup outcomes: how you enter a room, ask questions, dress, email, and exit can materially change whether people want to work with you.

## Signature Frameworks

### Low-heart-rate etiquette
- **When to use**: When entering high-stakes social or business settings where trust and composure matter.
- **Steps**:
  1. Arrive early enough to have buffer time and avoid rushing in flustered.
  2. Take a beat before entering so your body language signals calm rather than scarcity.
  3. Show up with an abundance mindset: this is one opportunity, not your only shot.
  4. Focus on giving more than taking and on building a relationship rather than extracting value.
  5. Use etiquette to make others feel relaxed, respected, and safe to trust you.
- **Example**: At a VC holiday party, instead of rushing around trying to meet everyone, show up calm, early, and composed so people feel you are someone they can work with.

### Ping-pong conversation
- **When to use**: When networking, meeting new people, or trying to keep a conversation engaging without dominating it.
- **Steps**:
  1. Ask questions to invite the other person in.
  2. Answer with your own perspective so the exchange is reciprocal.
  3. Avoid long monologues or six questions in a row.
  4. Add a point, story, or idea that gives the other person something to react to.
  5. Aim for a back-and-forth flow where both people contribute.
- **Example**: Instead of giving a four-minute startup spiel, ask a question, respond to their answer, and keep the exchange moving like a game of ping pong.

### Leave-them-wanting-more
- **When to use**: When meeting someone important, attending events, or trying to create future follow-up opportunities.
- **Steps**:
  1. Keep the interaction concise and interesting.
  2. Share enough to be memorable, but not so much that you exhaust the conversation.
  3. Excuse yourself gracefully before the interaction turns stale.
  4. End in a way that makes the other person want another conversation later.
- **Example**: At a conference, have a strong 5-minute conversation and then leave so the person thinks, “I’d like to talk to them again,” rather than feeling they already got your whole life story.

### Match the room
- **When to use**: When dressing, speaking, or behaving in unfamiliar social or professional environments.
- **Steps**:
  1. Observe the room’s norms before acting.
  2. Match the expected level of formality without overdoing it.
  3. Use language and vocabulary that fit the audience.
  4. Ask when unsure rather than guessing and risking a mismatch.
- **Example**: If you’re unsure whether an event is business casual or formal, ask someone rather than showing up in a tuxedo or underdressed.

### Respectful exit protocol
- **When to use**: When leaving meetings, dinners, or social gatherings.
- **Steps**:
  1. Recognize when the conversation or meeting is naturally ending.
  2. Stand up and leave gracefully rather than making a production of it.
  3. Thank the host or relevant person briefly.
  4. Follow up later with a short thank-you note if appropriate.
- **Example**: At a large party, an Irish goodbye can be the best option: quietly leave after thanking the host instead of doing a dramatic round of farewells.

## Core Advice
- **When you enter a room for a meeting or event**: Arrive early enough to have buffer time, but not so early that you become awkwardly visible — because rushing in late raises your heart rate and projects scarcity.
- **When you are late**: Apologize simply and move on without making a big deal of it — because over-explaining increases tension and wastes time.
- **When you meet someone new**: Repeat their name back and use a firm but not crushing handshake — because it signals effort and focus.
- **When someone else is late to meet you**: Do not shame them or make them feel bad; just continue smoothly — because turning lateness into a moral issue damages the interaction.
- **When you are in conversation**: Ask questions, but make sure the exchange is reciprocal and not an interrogation — because conversation should feel like ping pong, not extraction.
- **When you want to make a strong impression**: Leave them wanting more rather than telling your whole story — because curiosity creates future follow-up.
- **When you meet a famous or powerful person**: Treat them normally, avoid sycophancy, and do not ask for contact details too early — because calm respect is more memorable than overeagerness.
- **When you are unsure about dress code**: Ask directly and aim to dress one level up from the room — because it signals respect without trying too hard.
- **When choosing what to wear**: Prioritize fit and cleanliness over brand or price — because fit is what people notice most.
- **When dining with others**: Tip generously, don’t order the most expensive item by default, and let others set the tone before you order — because social calibration matters.
- **When you are sending email**: Keep it short, proofread carefully, and respond quickly with at least an acknowledgment — because brevity and speed signal respect.
- **When you are in a meeting or on Zoom**: Turn your camera on, close your closet, make your bed, and clean up after yourself — because small signs of care reduce friction and build trust.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Only the product matters; etiquette is irrelevant for founders. → **Their view**: Etiquette materially affects trust, partnerships, and deal outcomes — because in a world where software is commoditized and trust is scarce, how you show up can decide who wants to work with you.
- **Conventional**: The best networking strategy is to maximize contact collection and push hard in every room. → **Their view**: Show up calm, abundant, and relationship-oriented rather than transactional — because low-heart-rate confidence beats desperate energy.
- **Conventional**: Calendly and fully self-serve scheduling are the modern default. → **Their view**: The less senior or less busy person should do the work of fitting into the other person’s schedule — because scheduling is a status signal.
- **Conventional**: AI startups are the obvious place to invest and build right now. → **Their view**: Seed investors in companies branded as AI companies are likely to lose a lot of money — because AI is better as infrastructure inside real businesses than as the business itself.
- **Conventional**: More self-expression in communication is always better, including emojis and highly customized digital style. → **Their view**: Emojis and heavily customized emoji packs can read as overly familiar or unserious in business contexts — because clear, simple communication is more legible.
