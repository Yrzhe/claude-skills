## Signature Frameworks

### Pre-training vs. Post-training model improvement
- **When to use**: Use this to understand where frontier AI gains are coming from and where a company can add value in the AI stack.
- **Steps**:
  1. Recognize that pre-training absorbs broad human knowledge from internet-scale corpora.
  2. Identify when pre-training gains are asymptoting because most public knowledge has already been ingested.
  3. Shift focus to post-training, where models improve with targeted, high-quality domain data.
  4. Use supervised fine-tuning, preference data, trajectories, multimodal data, and rubrics/evals.
  5. Test whether each pipeline improves capability in a specific domain.
- **Example**: Labs once focused on “sucking up everything that was on the internet,” but now gains increasingly come from post-training in coding, math, law, finance, and science.

### Expert-driven data creation loop
- **When to use**: Use this when models are strong on general tasks but weak in advanced or specialized domains.
- **Steps**:
  1. Recruit true domain experts, not generalist labor.
  2. Have experts intentionally break the model on hard domain problems.
  3. Capture the correct answer and the reasoning path, not just the final output.
  4. Evaluate whether the data is high quality and likely to improve the model.
  5. Scale only after early experiments show measurable gains.
- **Example**: In GPQA-style work, experts break the model, provide the ground truth, and identify exactly which reasoning steps fail.

### Trajectory data collection
- **When to use**: Use this when you want models to learn workflows, tool use, and end-to-end task execution.
- **Steps**:
  1. Observe a human expert solving a real task from start to finish.
  2. Capture the full environment: screen, mouse, tools, and narration.
  3. Record how the expert handles roadblocks and switches systems.
  4. Convert the interaction into structured training data.
  5. Use it to improve workflow execution and tool-using behavior.
- **Example**: Capturing “your screen, your mouse” and voiceover while someone completes accounting or another professional workflow.

### Quality-volume-speed triad for AI data operations
- **When to use**: Use this to prioritize and evaluate an AI data business serving frontier labs.
- **Steps**:
  1. Prioritize quality first.
  2. Build volume in scarce expert domains like physics, chemistry, and mathematics.
  3. Optimize for speed so researchers can test hypotheses quickly.
  4. Create internal evaluation and post-training capabilities to estimate usefulness.
  5. Stay tightly aligned with researchers as needs evolve.
- **Example**: Model builders care most about quality, then volume, then speed, and may run several bets in parallel before scaling the winner.

### Separate startup inside the company
- **When to use**: Use this when incubating a zero-to-one business inside a mature company.
- **Steps**:
  1. Assign a dedicated leader with founder-level attention.
  2. Create a separate team with its own engineering, design, ops, finance, onboarding, and recruiting.
  3. Move people full-time with no split responsibilities.
  4. Set a distinct culture, pace, compensation model, and expectations.
  5. Run flat, with clear DRIs and a metrics-heavy cadence.
  6. Add integration points only after momentum exists.
- **Example**: Handshake AI sat in a separate part of the office, had separate teams and onboarding, and Garrett spent 80%+ of his time on it.

### Audience-access moat in human data
- **When to use**: Use this to assess defensibility in expert-sourcing or human-in-the-loop AI businesses.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with a trusted audience rather than buying supply through recruiters and ads.
  2. Use rich profile and performance data to target the right experts.
  3. Design an expert-first experience that respects contributor status.
  4. Train contributors well so they succeed and return.
  5. Leverage retention and repeat participation to improve economics and reliability.
- **Example**: “The only moat in human data is access to an audience,” versus competitors relying on LinkedIn outreach and performance marketing.

## Core Advice
- **When a major AI platform shift could disrupt or accelerate your business**: inventory the unique audience, trust, and distribution assets you already own — because those assets may be repurposed into a new AI-native business.
- **When improving AI systems in advanced domains**: use expert-led data creation — because generalist labor is no longer enough for frontier gains.
- **When unsure whether a new AI data pipeline is valuable**: run small experiments first — because frontier labs only scale pipelines that show measurable improvement.
- **When launching a zero-to-one initiative inside a mature company**: create a fully separate org — because mature-business processes slow early-stage learning.
- **When execution is dragging inside a larger organization**: lead in founder mode — because speed and direct ownership matter more than delegation in the early phase.
- **When building a supply-side marketplace in scarce talent**: reduce CAC through trust and owned distribution — because paid acquisition is expensive and weakens the economics.
- **When expert contributors have low retention or poor output quality**: build an expert-first experience — because professionals do better work when treated like professionals.
- **When demand is unusually hot and time-sensitive**: move urgently and verify quality repeatedly — because temporary market windows reward speed and rigor.
- **When worried AI will eliminate entry-level jobs**: make younger workers AI-native — because employers are seeing leverage, not just displacement.
- **When hiring for a chaotic internal startup**: set expectations explicitly — because not everyone thrives in high-ambiguity, high-pace environments.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: AI data labeling is mostly commoditized low-cost labor. → **Their view**: The highest-value human data work has shifted to true experts in advanced domains — because frontier gains now come from specialized failure cases, not simple labeling.
- **Conventional**: The moat in AI data businesses is tooling, process, or scale. → **Their view**: The real moat is access to a trusted audience — because owned distribution beats expensive recruiting.
- **Conventional**: A new business inside a mature company should share teams and infrastructure for efficiency. → **Their view**: It should be separated almost completely at the start — because shared systems create conflicting priorities and slow decisions.
- **Conventional**: AI will mainly reduce opportunities for young workers. → **Their view**: Young people may have an advantage because they are AI-native — because they can use the tools as leverage faster than incumbents.
- **Conventional**: Synthetic data will replace most human-generated training data. → **Their view**: Human-in-the-loop data will remain essential for a long time — because labs still need humans to expose failures and capture real workflows.
