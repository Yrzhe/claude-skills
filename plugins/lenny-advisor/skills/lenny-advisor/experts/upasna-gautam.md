**Product Manager** | **CNN** | Expertise: news product management, content management systems, mindfulness, cross-functional leadership

## Bio
Upasna Gautam leads product work on CNN Digital’s content management system, helping journalists and editors write, publish, and onboard onto a new platform. Her perspective matters because she operates in one of the most chaotic product environments possible: breaking news, shifting priorities, and highly specialized users who cannot pause their work for product process.

## Signature Frameworks

### Four-touchpoint editorial intake system
- **When to use**: When you need a repeatable way to gather feedback and manage requests from fast-moving stakeholders with unpredictable schedules.
- **Steps**:
  1. Run weekly demo days to preview features, show workflows, and evangelize the product.
  2. Hold working sessions to recreate user workflows and gather collaborative feedback.
  3. Conduct breaking news dress rehearsals to stress test the platform under realistic conditions.
  4. Offer office hours for open troubleshooting, questions, and ongoing feedback.
- **Example**: CNN’s product team uses weekly demo days, working sessions, breaking news dress rehearsals, and office hours to manage intake from journalists and editors.

### Buffer-based planning for chaotic environments
- **When to use**: When your users can interrupt planned work at any moment and you need to preserve momentum despite frequent pivots.
- **Steps**:
  1. Plan with explicit buffers for expected disruptions.
  2. Size the buffer based on the scope and phase of the work.
  3. Reassess quickly when the situation changes.
  4. If the buffer is no longer needed, move immediately to the next phase.
- **Example**: For onboarding, CNN may build in a buffer of a day, a week, or even a month depending on the team and the amount of coordination required.

### Mindful communication loop
- **When to use**: When stakeholder conversations are emotional, high-stakes, or prone to reactivity.
- **Steps**:
  1. Pause before reacting.
  2. Listen fully without trying to control the other person.
  3. Extract the root cause behind the complaint or request.
  4. Translate the issue into the listener’s language rather than your own jargon.
- **Example**: When a journalist says a feature is not working, Upasna listens first, then identifies the root cause, and avoids technical language they don’t use.

### Embedded product discovery with engineering partners
- **When to use**: When technical feasibility and user needs need to be aligned early to avoid handoff friction.
- **Steps**:
  1. Bring the tech lead and engineers into discovery from the start.
  2. Include them in user testing, design jams, and planning sessions.
  3. Teach the team the “why” behind the work, not just the requirements.
  4. Use that shared context to improve feasibility decisions and execution.
- **Example**: CNN’s tech lead joins user testing sessions and editorial planning so engineers can better understand journalist needs and define the implementation with more clarity.

### Long-cycle onboarding with staged validation
- **When to use**: When migrating users from a legacy system to a new platform with many workflow differences.
- **Steps**:
  1. Train users before testing.
  2. Run multiple collaborative working sessions to recreate workflows.
  3. Address friction and feedback before onboarding.
  4. Only onboard once the team is ready and the workflow is validated.
- **Example**: CNN often runs three to six two-hour working sessions before onboarding a team to the new content management platform.

## Core Advice
- **When you face unpredictable stakeholder interruptions during planned research or testing**: Build backup plans and explicit buffers into every schedule — because in breaking-news environments, planned sessions are frequently interrupted.
- **When your users have urgent, competing priorities and cannot serve your process**: Adapt your process to their reality instead of expecting them to adapt to yours — because journalists’ mission always takes precedence over product meetings.
- **When you need to onboard users to a new workflow or platform**: Use a long, staged onboarding cycle with training, testing, dialogue, and feedback before launch — because complex migrations require repeated validation and trust-building.
- **When you need to gather feedback from different user groups with distinct workflows**: Run separate working sessions for each user segment rather than treating them as one audience — because different newsroom teams have wildly different content workflows.
- **When you are introducing a major platform change in a legacy organization**: Use smart repetition through demos and show-and-tells to evangelize the new product — because repeated exposure helps people understand and trust the system.
- **When you are planning for a high-variance project**: Stay firm on the goal but flexible on the process — because the objective should remain stable, but the path must adapt.
- **When your team is working on a critical platform with production incidents**: Create layered support and escalation paths so the product team is not always the first line of defense — because support teams can absorb and route incidents, reducing stress and improving response quality.
- **When you need to make decisions quickly under uncertainty**: Use mindfulness to see the situation objectively, decide fast, and move on — because overthinking slows response in chaotic environments.
- **When you want to improve communication as a PM**: Listen more than you speak and converse in the language of the listener — because effective communication depends on their vocabulary, assumptions, and emotional state.
- **When you are trying to build trust with a historically fragmented stakeholder group**: Increase the frequency and consistency of direct conversations early in the process — because regular contact reduces assumptions and bridges gaps.
- **When you want to start meditating but feel too busy**: Turn an existing daily habit, like brushing your teeth, into a short mindfulness practice — because meditation can start with a few minutes of full presence, not a special setup.
- **When you are hiring for a high-agency product role**: Ask candidates what would not exist without their initiative — because it reveals whether they can point to concrete outcomes they created.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Product managers should thrive in ambiguity. → **Their view**: In news, PMs must thrive in chaos — because breaking news can instantly override planned work and requires constant pivots.
- **Conventional**: PMs should have the answers and drive decisions. → **Their view**: A PM’s power comes from listening, pausing, and knowing where to find the answers — because trust and clarity come from understanding the real problem.
- **Conventional**: User research and testing should be tightly scheduled and executed as planned. → **Their view**: In breaking-news organizations, research plans must be designed to fail gracefully — because the news cycle can interrupt them at any moment.
- **Conventional**: Engineers should be brought in after product and design define the solution. → **Their view**: Engineers should be embedded in discovery from the start and treated as partners — because early involvement improves feasibility and execution.
- **Conventional**: Meditation requires long sessions, special tools, or a major lifestyle change. → **Their view**: Meditation can start with a few minutes of full presence during something you already do every day — because awareness matters more than ritual.
- **Conventional**: Product management in news is a mature, standardized discipline. → **Their view**: It is still in its infancy in many newsrooms and often has to be invented on the fly — because many organizations lack dedicated product and tech talent.
