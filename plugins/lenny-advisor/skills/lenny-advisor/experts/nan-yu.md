# Nan Yu

**Head of Product** | **Linear** | Expertise: product-management, go-to-market, design, strategy

## Bio
Nan Yu leads product at Linear, where he helps build fast, high-quality B2B software that users actually enjoy using. His perspective matters because he combines strong product taste with a deep respect for real customer workflows, and he treats product, engineering, sales, and marketing as one connected system.

He is especially valuable when teams need to decide what to build, how to shape it, and how to launch it without degrading the core user experience.

## Signature Frameworks

### Fast-first, learn-early product development
- **When to use**: When building a new feature or product and you want to maximize learning without sacrificing quality.
- **Steps**:
  1. Set a rough time budget for the work.
  2. By about 10% of the allotted time, build a workable version that tests the key hypothesis internally.
  3. Use the first version only as a directional bet, not a polished final answer.
  4. Release to a small internal circle first, then gradually expand to beta users.
  5. Use each expansion to validate or invalidate assumptions before investing further.
- **Example**: For a feature expected to take a month, Linear aims to have something working in the first week so they can test whether the core idea is right.

### Extreme-variants exploration
- **When to use**: When the right product choice is unclear and you need to discover options beyond obvious middle-ground solutions.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the trait you want to optimize or explore.
  2. Build the most extreme version in one direction.
  3. Build the most extreme version in the opposite direction.
  4. Roll out versions to users when safe enough to learn from real behavior.
  5. Compare the extremes to find the balanced solution that best fits reality.
- **Example**: For draft saving, Linear tested an unsafe version that never asked to save and a super-safe auto-save version, then landed on a hybrid: interrupt on brand-new drafts, auto-save existing drafts.

### Emotion-first customer discovery
- **When to use**: When customers ask for a feature but the real problem is unclear.
- **Steps**:
  1. Keep digging past the stated request.
  2. Ask what bad outcome the customer is trying to avoid.
  3. Find the emotional moment where they felt pain or embarrassment.
  4. Translate that feeling into a product problem.
  5. Design the solution to remove that bad feeling, not just satisfy the literal request.
- **Example**: A customer saying they need a ship date feature may really be trying to avoid a marketing conflict or the embarrassment of false precision, so Linear built flexible target dates instead of forcing exact dates.

### Reality-matching feature evaluation
- **When to use**: When deciding whether to build a requested feature or how to shape it.
- **Steps**:
  1. Tie the request to a specific real user, not a hypothetical persona.
  2. Check whether the proposed solution matches how people actually work.
  3. Look for misalignment between elegant design and messy reality.
  4. If the request is broad, identify concentrated use cases rather than solving the long tail.
  5. Build the smallest native solution that fits the real workflow.
- **Example**: For Customer Requests, Linear found that many custom-field requests were really about tracking requests from specific customers like Walmart, so they built automatic customer attribution instead of generic custom fields.

### Double-triangle product collaboration
- **When to use**: When product decisions affect both the building side and the selling side of the company.
- **Steps**:
  1. Treat product management as a bridge between engineering/design and sales/marketing.
  2. Use customer discovery to originate the right language and message.
  3. Have product marketing craft release language and change logs.
  4. Have sales validate the message in the field.
  5. Use the feedback loop between product, marketing, and sales to refine both product and positioning.
- **Example**: At Linear, a full-time product marketer works with PMs on release language, while sales validates whether the wording resonates with customers.

## Core Advice
- **When you face a new feature with uncertain value**: Build a workable version in the first 10% of the planned time and test the core hypothesis immediately — because early working software reveals whether your assumptions are right before you waste time polishing the wrong thing.
- **When your team feels pressure to make the first version perfect**: Treat the first version as a directional bet, not the final answer — because removing perfectionism lets engineers move quickly and validate assumptions without getting blocked by polish.
- **When a feature request seems to come from buyers or managers rather than end users**: Prioritize the IC workflow and reject requests that make day-to-day work worse for the people actually using the product — because if the core user disengages, reporting and buyer satisfaction won’t save the product long term.
- **When you hear a broad feature request from many customers**: Dig until you find the specific person and specific workflow behind the ask — because specific real users reveal the actual problem and prevent you from building a generic solution that fits nobody well.
- **When customers ask for customization to support reporting**: Look for a native product solution that preserves the IC experience instead of adding manual fields and workflows — because manual customization often degrades data quality and creates friction for the people doing the work.
- **When a customer says they need a feature but the request feels vague**: Ask what bad feeling or embarrassing moment they are trying to avoid — because the emotional trigger often points more directly to the real product opportunity than the literal request does.
- **When you are deciding between a few plausible product options**: Explore extreme versions of the idea to expand the search space before settling on a compromise — because the best solution is often outside the initial set of obvious middle-ground choices.
- **When a product decision depends on how people actually work**: Use real examples from named users and real workflows to ground the decision — because reality is often messier than elegant abstractions, and the product has to work in that mess.
- **When your company is debating whether to add a feature that helps managers but hurts contributors**: Be especially skeptical of features that improve reporting at the cost of making IC workflows worse — because those features create long-term product bloat and reduce adoption by the people who do the actual work.
- **When you are building B2B software**: Treat the software as teaching users a way of working, not just solving a task — because adopting software often means adopting the best practices embedded in the product.
- **When you are interviewing for a PM role**: Figure out the hiring manager’s burning problem and position yourself as the solution to that specific job-to-be-done — because hiring becomes a binary choice between the person who solves the problem and everyone else in the field.
- **When you want to stand out in a PM interview**: Act like you already have the job and ask what you would ask a teammate, including OKRs and current priorities — because that shows practical thinking and helps you understand the real problem the team needs solved.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Speed and quality are in tension; you can only get two of good, cheap, and fast. → **Their view**: There is often no trade-off between speed and quality; high competence enables both. — because experts move fast because the work is second nature, and fast iteration is what produces better products.
- **Conventional**: B2B products must add customization and reporting features to satisfy enterprise buyers. → **Their view**: Many customization and reporting requests should be rejected because they harm the core user experience. — because features that make reporting easier for managers often create friction, bad data, and resentment for ICs.
- **Conventional**: PMs should focus mainly on product, engineering, and design collaboration. → **Their view**: PMs should also be deeply involved in sales and marketing as part of their core job. — because in B2B, product decisions and messaging are tightly linked, and PMs often know the customer language best.
- **Conventional**: You should estimate carefully before committing to deadlines. → **Their view**: Linear does almost no estimating; instead, they ship early and use the first 10% of time to create a working version. — because early working software gives more useful information than detailed estimates and helps teams make real go/no-go decisions.
- **Conventional**: Product discovery is mostly about goals, jobs-to-be-done, and rational use cases. → **Their view**: The emotional pain behind a request is often more important than the stated functional need. — because understanding the moment someone felt bad reveals the real problem and leads to better product design.
- **Conventional**: A tool is just a tool; users can adopt it without changing how they work. → **Their view**: Buying B2B software means buying into a way of working and a set of practices. — because the defaults and workflows in the software shape organizational behavior and competency.
