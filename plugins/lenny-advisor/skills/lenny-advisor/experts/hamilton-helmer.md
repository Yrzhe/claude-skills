# Hamilton Helmer

**Author of 7 Powers; strategy thinker and investor** | **Strategy Capital** | Expertise: strategy, competitive advantage, business economics, go-to-market

## Bio
Hamilton Helmer is one of the clearest thinkers on durable competitive advantage, best known for the **7 Powers** framework. His perspective matters because he focuses strategy on what actually drives long-term value: not just growth or execution, but whether a business can create and defend an economically meaningful advantage.

He advises founders and operators on how to build businesses that can win over time, and his ideas have influenced leaders like Reed Hastings. Helmer is especially useful when you need to separate real strategic power from things that merely feel impressive.

## Signature Frameworks

### 7 Powers
- **When to use**: When evaluating whether a business has or can build durable competitive advantage and long-term value creation.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify whether the business has a real benefit versus competitors, such as lower cost or higher willingness to pay.
  2. Test whether there is a barrier that prevents competitors from copying that benefit.
  3. Apply the “to be or not to be” test: if there is both benefit and barrier, it may be power; if not, it is not power.
  4. Assess materiality: the advantage must be large enough to matter economically, not just exist in theory.
  5. Use the identified power to inform strategy, defense, and future expansion.
- **Example**: Netflix’s scale economies: large fixed content costs are spread across more subscribers, lowering cost per subscriber relative to smaller competitors.

### Power Progression
- **When to use**: When deciding which forms of power are realistically available at different stages of a company’s life cycle.
- **Steps**:
  1. Map the business stage: origination, takeoff, or stability.
  2. Eliminate powers that are generally unavailable early, such as branding and process power.
  3. For most startups, focus first on counter-positioning.
  4. As scale develops, evaluate whether scale economies, switching costs, or network economies are emerging.
  5. In stability, identify and defend the power you have, and use that understanding to pursue second or third acts.
- **Example**: Early-stage startups should usually focus on counter-positioning first, then later on scale economies, switching costs, and network economies.

### Benefit + Barrier (Castle and Moat)
- **When to use**: When distinguishing between a true strategic advantage and a feature or capability that is merely useful.
- **Steps**:
  1. Ask whether the business has a “castle” — a genuinely valuable economic position.
  2. Ask whether it has a “moat” — a barrier that protects that position from imitation.
  3. Separate important operational capabilities from true power.
  4. Check whether competitors can mimic the capability with hiring, consultants, or copying.
- **Example**: Netflix’s UI and recommendation engine were important operationally, but Blockbuster could mimic the UI; those capabilities alone were not the source of power.

### Three Drivers of Company Value
- **When to use**: When simplifying what actually drives business value and avoiding distraction by secondary factors.
- **Steps**:
  1. Evaluate the company’s source of power.
  2. Assess the size of the market opportunity.
  3. Assess operational excellence in execution.
  4. Recognize that many business activities matter, but they ultimately roll up into these three categories.
- **Example**: Many things feel value-creating in business, but mathematically they collapse into power, market size, and operational excellence.

### Three AI Play Types
- **When to use**: When analyzing where AI creates strategic opportunity and what kind of company is being built.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify whether the company is the core technology play itself.
  2. Determine whether the company could not exist without the technology.
  3. Determine whether the company existed before but can be improved by incorporating the technology.
  4. Analyze which powers AI may strengthen, such as scale economies, switching costs, or network economies.
- **Example**: AI is like semiconductors: Intel as the technology play, Microsoft as a company enabled by the technology, and automobiles as an existing industry improved by it.

## Core Advice
- **When you face the question of whether to think about strategy before product-market fit**: do start thinking about strategy from day one — because early choices shape which opportunities can later become power.
- **When you face an early-stage startup strategy decision**: do focus first on counter-positioning — because most startups begin by displacing an incumbent and need a refuge from direct imitation.
- **When you face claims that your moat is brand, execution speed, or a flywheel**: do test whether the advantage is both material and hard to imitate — because many “moats” are just good capabilities.
- **When you face a belief that your product has network effects**: do distinguish network effects from network economies — because existence alone is not enough; the effect must matter economically.
- **When you face pressure to rely on operational excellence as your strategy**: do treat execution as necessary but not sufficient — because it helps you win, but usually does not protect your position.
- **When you face the challenge of becoming more strategic as a PM or leader**: do regularly discuss what your company’s actual source of power is — because strategy sharpens through debate and repetition.
- **When you face uncertainty about how AI changes your business**: do analyze AI as a force that may strengthen existing powers — because it often amplifies scale, switching costs, or network effects rather than creating a new category of defensibility.
- **When you face the temptation to over-plan instead of building**: do act, experiment, and use strategy as guideposts — because progress comes from execution, not theory alone.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Founders should focus on product-market fit first and think about strategy later. → **Their view**: You should think about strategy always, even before product-market fit — because early strategic thinking helps choose opportunities more likely to support future power.
- **Conventional**: If a product has network effects, it has a strong moat. → **Their view**: Many businesses have network effects without having network economies as a true power — because the effect must be economically material.
- **Conventional**: Operational excellence and moving faster than competitors are strategic moats. → **Their view**: Operational excellence is necessary but usually not power — because it is often copyable.
- **Conventional**: Brand recognition in a startup is itself a source of defensibility. → **Their view**: Brand recognition is not the same as branding power — because awareness can be bought and may not create durable economics.
- **Conventional**: More data naturally creates a strong scale-economy moat in software and AI. → **Their view**: Data-driven scale economies are possible but rare — because competitors are often too close on the curve for the advantage to stay meaningful.
- **Conventional**: Strategy should be a broad umbrella covering everything important in the business. → **Their view**: Strategy is more useful when narrowly focused on the fundamental determinants of long-term business value — because that makes it actionable.
