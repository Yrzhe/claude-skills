# Eeke de Milliano

**Head of Product** | **Retool** | Expertise: product-management, leadership, strategy, design

## Bio
Eeke de Milliano is Head of Product at Retool and was one of the earliest product leaders at Stripe, where she helped build foundational products like Stripe Connect and Stripe Radar. Her perspective matters because she has operated at the intersection of product strategy, organizational design, and high-velocity execution in both early and scaled environments.

She is especially strong on how to create innovation without chaos: how to set direction, preserve creativity, and build product organizations that can take big swings while still shipping reliably.

## Signature Frameworks

### Minimum Viable Process
- **When to use**: When a growing company needs more consistency and coordination without crushing creativity or slowing top performers.
- **Steps**:
  1. Introduce process only when variance in execution is becoming too high.
  2. Define the minimum standard needed for clarity, quality, or coordination.
  3. Make templates and processes optional escape hatches rather than rigid rules.
  4. Tell strong performers they can go beyond or break the template if it helps them communicate better.
  5. Reassess whether the process is worth its creativity cost.
- **Example**: Eeke writes at the top of templates that if the template does not work for what someone is trying to explain, they should not use it—it is only the minimum viable bar.

### Charter → Goals → Roadmap
- **When to use**: When setting direction for a company, function, or team and deciding what level of planning is appropriate for the stage of the business.
- **Steps**:
  1. Write a charter that defines mission, vision, and strategy.
  2. Set goals that define what success looks like and how it will be measured.
  3. Create a roadmap of what will ship to achieve those goals.
  4. Apply this structure at every level: company, function, and team.
  5. Adjust the planning horizon based on maturity—short horizons for pre-PMF teams, longer horizons for mature businesses.
- **Example**: Early-stage teams often start bottom-up with a roadmap, but Eeke believes it is better to start top-down with the charter first.

### Crazy Ideas Doc
- **When to use**: When you want to create explicit permission for big thinking and surface high-upside ideas that normal planning processes would filter out.
- **Steps**:
  1. At the beginning of the year, send a blank document to the organization.
  2. Prompt people to submit ideas that probably should not be done, but could create 10x to 100x impact if they work.
  3. Allow ideas across product, marketing, and organizational design—not just core product features.
  4. Review the prior year’s list and identify which ideas were actually pursued.
  5. Fund and test the most promising ideas with small teams.
- **Example**: Retool Workflows came from the Crazy Ideas list, and the company typically does three to eight items from the prior year’s document.

### Startup-in-a-Startup Incubation Model
- **When to use**: When exploring new product lines inside an existing company without overcommitting resources too early.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start each new initiative with a very small team, often one or two people.
  2. Spend heavily on customer conversations, prototyping, and validation.
  3. Do not scale resourcing until there is evidence that something meaningful is there.
  4. Treat the parent company like a VC that funds the team with resources and access to customers.
  5. Require the team to prove ROI through engagement or revenue before expanding.
  6. Keep the team relatively separate from the core org early so it can move faster and think independently.
- **Example**: Retool launched Workflows, Mobile, and Database with one or two people each, then added resources only after customer validation was clear.

### 70/20/10 Product Investment Split
- **When to use**: When allocating product and engineering resources across core business needs, strategic expansions, and experimental bets.
- **Steps**:
  1. Spend 70% of time on the core product, including maintenance, tech debt, and core improvements.
  2. Spend 20% on strategic initiatives that are not core today but matter to company strategy.
  3. Spend 10% on bets and exploratory work.
  4. Review whether teams are overinvesting in maintenance or underinvesting in future growth.
  5. Use the portfolio to protect room for innovation.
- **Example**: Eeke uses this as a practical portfolio model to ensure the company keeps investing in both the core product and new bets.

### Build the Scooter, Not the Axle
- **When to use**: When defining an MVP and trying to deliver end-to-end customer value quickly rather than building isolated components.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the smallest complete experience that solves a real user problem.
  2. Avoid building partial infrastructure that does not yet create usable value.
  3. Ship a simple but whole product slice first.
  4. Iterate from that complete slice toward more capable versions over time.
- **Example**: For Retool Mobile, the team narrowed the MVP to field-worker inventory management rather than building a broad mobile platform first.

### Build for Your Best User, Not Your Worst User
- **When to use**: When designing early product experiences and deciding whether to optimize for ideal users or edge cases and abuse prevention.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the users who will immediately understand and benefit from the product.
  2. Design the initial experience for those users first.
  3. Avoid over-optimizing for abuse, misuse, or low-fit users too early.
  4. Treat edge cases as future problems unless they are already material.
  5. Expand to broader user needs after the core value is proven.
- **Example**: In a product review, the team worried about future abuse if a new product got very large, and Anthony pushed back that this would be a great problem to have.

## Core Advice
- **When a team is not thinking boldly enough**: Normalize failure publicly, run retrospectives, and celebrate learnings alongside wins — because people avoid ambitious work when failure feels career-limiting.
- **When big bets feel too risky**: Run smaller experiments with tiny teams and get customer feedback early — because long feedback loops make projects feel organizationally and psychologically expensive.
- **When the team is buried in bugs, incidents, or debt**: Remove the operational burden first — because innovation sits higher on the hierarchy of needs.
- **When planning only produces incremental work**: Add a “Think Bigger” section and ask what would change with 20% more time or 2x the team — because people default to current constraints.
- **When the company wants more process**: Use only minimum viable process and add escape hatches — because process reduces variance but also lowers the ceiling.
- **When teams need alignment**: Use charter, goals, and roadmap at every level — because mission, measurement, and execution need to connect.
- **When incubating new products**: Start with one- or two-person teams and scale only after validation — because small teams preserve speed and force real proof.
- **When a new product is tempted to merge with the core org too early**: Keep it separate at first — because independence helps discovery and avoids inherited complexity.
- **When MVP debates get abstract**: Build the smallest complete experience — because customers can only validate end-to-end value.
- **When edge cases dominate design discussions**: Build for your best user first — because the core users should shape the product before abuse prevention does.
- **When building a PM org**: Hire complementary strengths, not clones — because diverse PM profiles make the org more resilient.
- **When PMs feel too far from customers or technology**: Keep them close to both — because product judgment improves with direct customer and technical context.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: More process is a sign of maturity and improves performance. → **Their view**: Process is variance reducing and often lowers the ceiling by dragging top performers toward the average — because strong people need less structure and can be slowed by it.
- **Conventional**: Products should be designed around the weakest users and every possible misuse from day one. → **Their view**: Build for your best users first — because over-optimizing for edge cases distorts the product and slows learning.
- **Conventional**: Pricing is a one-way-door decision requiring extreme caution. → **Their view**: Pricing is often more reversible than people think — because you can grandfather existing users and change pricing for new ones.
- **Conventional**: New products should be tightly integrated with the core org immediately. → **Their view**: Early new product teams should often stay separate — because separation improves speed and discovery.
- **Conventional**: Innovation is mostly about generating more ideas. → **Their view**: The bigger blockers are fear of failure, operational overload, and lack of permission to think — because organizations often suppress ambition indirectly.
- **Conventional**: PMs are essential from the earliest days of a startup. → **Their view**: Developer-focused companies can go far without PMs if engineers are effectively the customer — because PM becomes more necessary as complexity grows.
