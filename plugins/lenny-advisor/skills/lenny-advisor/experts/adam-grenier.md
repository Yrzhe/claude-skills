# Adam Grenier

**Advisor and investor; formerly VP of Marketing at MasterClass** | **Independent / formerly MasterClass, Uber, Lambda School** | Expertise: growth, go-to-market, marketing strategy, product-management

## Bio
Adam Grenier is a veteran growth and marketing leader who has built growth infrastructure at Uber, led product and marketing at Lambda School, and served as VP of Marketing at MasterClass. His perspective matters because he blends classic marketing, product thinking, and experimentation into one operating model—especially useful for companies trying to scale in fast-changing markets.

He is particularly strong on evaluating new channels, diagnosing whether growth problems are really product-market-fit problems, and designing marketing organizations that work like modern product teams.

## Signature Frameworks

### Emerging Acquisition Channel Evaluation Framework
- **When to use**: When deciding whether to invest in a new or emerging acquisition channel like TikTok, Clubhouse, OTT, influencer marketing, or VR.
- **Steps**:
  1. Assess overlap between customer needs, company goals, and the channel's core strengths.
  2. Analyze the channel DNA: where it is in its lifecycle, how risky or unstable it is, and how likely the product or rules are to change.
  3. Understand the channel's monetization strategy and determine whether your company can align with it in a way that makes you valuable to the platform.
  4. Evaluate your own company DNA: risk tolerance, staffing capacity, and whether your core channels are already working.
  5. Scope the test proportionally: start small, define what signal you are looking for, and avoid overcommitting before you see momentum.
- **Example**: He contrasted Spotify exploring Clubhouse versus Paparazzi: Clubhouse's audio-first, discovery-oriented format aligned with Spotify's goals and user needs, while Paparazzi's photo-driven format did not.

### Channel DNA Analysis
- **When to use**: When a channel looks promising on the surface, but you need to understand whether it is durable, scalable, and worth the operational cost.
- **Steps**:
  1. Determine how early the channel is in its trajectory.
  2. Accept the risks of early channels: instability, changing product surfaces, weak tooling, and non-repeatable wins.
  3. Estimate how much ongoing maintenance the channel will require as it evolves.
  4. Study how the platform makes money and identify how your company can help them prove that monetization model.
- **Example**: At HotelTonight, Adam got into Facebook's mobile ads alpha by positioning the company as a valuable non-gaming case study for Facebook's monetization expansion.

### Growth CMO Operating Model
- **When to use**: When hiring or developing a senior marketing leader for a product-led or software company that needs marketing tightly integrated with product and experimentation.
- **Steps**:
  1. Use data across the full funnel, including brand, retention, and consideration—not just direct response metrics.
  2. Run marketing with an agile, iterative mindset rather than long static planning cycles.
  3. Treat experimentation as a company-wide operating principle, not just trying new channels.
  4. Integrate product and marketing deeply so messaging, funnel, and product experience evolve together.
  5. Continuously iterate on brand, storytelling, and growth systems based on evidence.
- **Example**: He argued that in modern product-led companies, product and marketing should be “married at the hip,” unlike the older model where marketing owned packaging and promotion while product was separate.

### T-Shaped Marketing Leader Assessment
- **When to use**: When hiring a marketing leader or evaluating whether someone can grow into a broader growth-oriented leadership role.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the candidate's deep specialty—the vertical part of their T.
  2. Map the broader areas they have expanded into over time.
  3. Probe how they compensate for weaker areas through hiring, collaboration, and humility.
  4. Test whether they are comfortable with chaos, ambiguity, and doing hands-on work when needed.
  5. Assess whether they can adapt to product-led, fast-changing startup environments.
- **Example**: Adam described his own T as starting in mobile marketing, then expanding into broader marketing, growth, and product; he also noted that when he had to oversee PR, he focused on understanding his limitations and hiring experts.

### Improv-Inspired Collaboration Framework
- **When to use**: When working cross-functionally, brainstorming, or trying to improve team creativity and collaboration.
- **Steps**:
  1. Use a “Yes, and” mindset to build on ideas instead of shutting them down.
  2. Acknowledge that multiple perspectives can be true at once.
  3. Add specific details to make ideas more actionable and generative.
  4. Create team accountability around collaborative behavior.
- **Example**: He used this approach at Uber when central teams and local city teams had conflicting priorities; accepting that both perspectives were valid helped preserve momentum and relationships.

## Core Advice
- **When you face pressure to jump into a hot new acquisition channel**: Do a structured evaluation of customer needs, company goals, channel strengths, channel DNA, and your own company readiness before investing — because most emerging channels do not work for most companies, and trendiness is a poor filter.
- **When you face a new channel test with unclear ROI**: Do a small, explicitly scoped experiment with predefined success signals and avoid letting it run beyond a quarter without directional progress — because early channels rarely give clean statistical proof quickly.
- **When you face a weak or unstable growth foundation**: Do not overinvest in emerging channels before you have basic volume from core channels like Google and Facebook, unless there is an unusually strong fit — because complexity compounds when fundamentals are still shaky.
- **When you face a changing macro environment or economic downturn**: Start by assuming you no longer have product-market fit and revalidate your customer, messaging, and offer — because market shifts can change the whole customer base.
- **When you face the challenge of scaling beyond early adopters**: Explicitly map how your early adopters differ from the broader market and define what evidence would prove fit beyond them — because early enthusiasm often masks weak mainstream fit.
- **When you face cross-functional disagreement**: Use a “Yes, and” approach that accepts multiple truths and builds from them rather than arguing over whose framing is correct — because it preserves momentum and relationships.
- **When you face vague positioning or weak creative ideas**: Add specific details that clarify the problem, audience, and alternative solutions — because specificity creates better strategic options.
- **When you face the need to become a more modern marketing leader**: Learn agile product development and apply its principles to marketing planning, experimentation, and iteration — because product-led companies move too quickly for static campaign planning.
- **When you face hiring a senior marketing leader for a startup**: Test for comfort with chaos, willingness to get hands-on, and the ability to adapt beyond their original specialty — because pedigree matters less than operating well in ambiguity.
- **When you face possible burnout**: Watch for a drop in adaptability and curiosity—especially a shift from exploration to “why are we wasting our time?” energy — because reduced openness to change is often an early warning sign.
- **When you face uncertainty about whether you're burned out or depressed**: Examine whether the loss of motivation extends beyond work into hobbies, community, and relationships — because depression tends to flatten motivation across life, not just work.
- **When you face recurring mental health strain**: Use therapy, trusted friends, meditation, and available company benefits to build a support system before things worsen — because problems are easier to manage early.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: If growth slows, you should find a new acquisition channel to restart momentum. → **Their view**: First assume you may no longer have product-market fit in the current market and revalidate your customer and offer — because a channel change won’t fix a deeper fit problem.
- **Conventional**: If a channel is hot, you should probably be there early. → **Their view**: Most hot channels are not worth pursuing unless their strengths clearly match your customer needs and business goals — because hype is not a strategy.
- **Conventional**: Brand marketing is a separate, slower, less measurable discipline than growth. → **Their view**: Brand should also be run with data, iteration, and experimentation — because brand can compound through learning too.
- **Conventional**: Marketing and product are separate functions; marketing promotes what product builds. → **Their view**: In modern product-led companies, product and marketing should be deeply integrated and effectively operate as one system — because growth depends on tight coordination.
- **Conventional**: Burnout mainly looks like being tired from working too much. → **Their view**: A key signal of burnout is reduced adaptability and a desire to avoid experimentation or change — because curiosity is core to growth work.
