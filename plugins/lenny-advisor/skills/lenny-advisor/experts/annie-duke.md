# Annie Duke

**Author, decision-making consultant, and Special Partner** | **First Round Capital** | Expertise: decision-making, behavioral psychology, quitting, investing

## Bio
Annie Duke is a bestselling author of *Thinking in Bets* and *Quit*, a former professional poker player with over $4 million in tournament winnings, and a decision-making advisor to companies and investors. Her perspective matters because she translates behavioral science and probabilistic thinking into practical systems that help teams make better calls under uncertainty.

She is especially valuable when decisions are messy, high-stakes, and slow to resolve: hiring, investing, roadmap planning, strategy, and knowing when to stop. Duke’s core contribution is showing how to replace vague intuition and group dynamics with explicit, testable decision processes.

## Signature Frameworks

### Discover-Discuss-Decide Separation
- **When to use**: When a team needs to make group decisions on roadmaps, forecasts, hiring, budgeting, planning, or strategy without groupthink or loud voices dominating.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the judgments, forecasts, or options you need from each participant.
  2. Collect each person's input independently and asynchronously before the meeting.
  3. Share the collected inputs with the group in advance.
  4. Use the meeting only for discussion, especially focusing on areas of disagreement rather than rehashing agreement.
  5. Make the final decision outside the meeting, either through a single decision-maker or private voting.
- **Example**: For product roadmap planning, ask each person to independently rank features and provide a short rationale before the meeting, then use the meeting to discuss differences rather than brainstorm live.

### Make the Implicit Explicit
- **When to use**: When decisions rely heavily on intuition, such as hiring, investing, forecasting, or evaluating product opportunities.
- **Steps**:
  1. Surface the hidden criteria people are already using intuitively.
  2. Define those criteria explicitly and create shared definitions.
  3. Turn them into a rubric or structured evaluation process.
  4. Record judgments and forecasts against those criteria.
  5. Review outcomes later to see which intuitions were actually predictive.
- **Example**: At First Round, partners explicitly rate factors like market, founder, and product on a 1–7 scale, define what those terms mean, and forecast whether a company will raise a Series A.

### Shorten the Feedback Loop via Correlated Signals
- **When to use**: When the ultimate outcome takes years to materialize, such as venture investing, product bets, or long-term business initiatives.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the long-term outcome you care about.
  2. List the necessary-but-not-sufficient milestones or correlated signals that must happen first.
  3. Forecast those intermediate signals at the time of the decision.
  4. Track those signals as they unfold.
  5. Use the results to evaluate decision quality much earlier than the final outcome.
- **Example**: Instead of waiting 10 years for an exit, track whether a company raises a Series A, achieves product-market fit, retains talent, or grows ARR, since those are correlated with eventual success.

### Pre-mortem with Kill Criteria
- **When to use**: When launching a project, pursuing a deal, or starting an initiative where teams tend to continue too long despite warning signs.
- **Steps**:
  1. Imagine the initiative failed in the future.
  2. Work backward to identify the early signals that would have predicted failure.
  3. Convert those signals into explicit kill criteria.
  4. Attach a pre-committed action to each signal, such as pivot, escalate, or stop.
  5. Follow those pre-commitments when the signals appear.
- **Example**: For sales RFPs, identify signals like “the customer only wants to talk about price” or “we can’t get a decision-maker in the room,” then pre-commit to actions such as killing the deal or requiring executive alignment.

### Mental Time Travel
- **When to use**: When emotions in the moment are distorting judgment, especially in personal decisions, leadership situations, or parenting.
- **Steps**:
  1. Pause and imagine how you will view the current situation in the future.
  2. Ask whether this will still matter in a week, a year, or ten years.
  3. Use that future perspective to reduce the focusing effect of the present moment.
  4. Choose based on the longer-term perspective rather than immediate emotion.
- **Example**: With her kids, Duke would say, “This is going to be so great for you when you’re 40 at Thanksgiving,” to help them see that today’s crisis would eventually become a funny story.

### Reflective Facilitation with “Nevertheless”
- **When to use**: When leaders need to make a decision after hearing dissent while preserving psychological ownership and making people feel heard.
- **Steps**:
  1. Let each person explain their reasoning without interruption.
  2. Reflect back what you heard to confirm understanding.
  3. Avoid arguing or trying to force agreement.
  4. Acknowledge the input explicitly.
  5. State the decision clearly using a formulation like “I hear you; nevertheless, this is the path we’re taking.”
- **Example**: In parenting or leadership, after hearing objections, Duke emphasizes that people should feel heard even if the final decision goes another way.

## Core Advice
- **When the loudest or most senior person may sway the group**: do discovery independently and asynchronously before the meeting — because it reduces conformity, status effects, and over-weighting of confidence.
- **When hiring depends mostly on gut feel**: extract the criteria behind your intuition and turn them into a structured rubric — because explicit criteria improve hit rates and make judgment testable.
- **When an outcome takes years to show up**: identify intermediate milestones and correlated signals, then forecast them up front — because you can evaluate decision quality much sooner.
- **When launching a project or deal**: attach explicit kill criteria and pre-committed actions to failure signals — because pre-mortems without action plans rarely change behavior.
- **When disagreement is healthy but consensus pressure is high**: reflect back each person’s reasoning, then decide without forcing alignment — because people feel ownership when they are accurately heard.
- **When emotions are overwhelming the present moment**: use mental time travel — because future perspective reduces the focusing effect.
- **When you’re considering quitting**: ask whether you would start it again today — because sunk costs and identity often keep people in bad bets too long.
- **When a new idea appears mid-meeting**: pause and have everyone write down estimates independently — because early anchoring distorts judgment.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Meetings are for discovering ideas, discussing them, and deciding together in real time. → **Their view**: Meetings should only be for discussion; discovery and decision should happen independently outside the room — because live group processes amplify conformity and status.
- **Conventional**: Good teams should leave meetings aligned and in agreement. → **Their view**: Alignment is a misleading goal; disagreement is normal and healthier than coerced consensus — because forced agreement hides real uncertainty.
- **Conventional**: Some decisions have inherently long feedback loops. → **Their view**: There is no such thing as a long feedback loop; the loop is as long as you choose it to be — because correlated signals can be tracked earlier.
- **Conventional**: Pre-mortems are valuable because imagining failure naturally improves plans. → **Their view**: Pre-mortems only matter if they produce kill criteria and pre-committed actions — because insight without commitment doesn’t change behavior.
- **Conventional**: Expert intuition should be trusted as-is. → **Their view**: Intuition must be made explicit and tested — because otherwise you never learn what’s predictive.
- **Conventional**: You should quit only when failure is obvious. → **Their view**: By the time you’re thinking about quitting, you may already be late — because sunk costs and identity bias delay exit.
