# Sachin Monga

**Head of Product** | **Substack** | Expertise: product strategy, growth, consumer social products, platform design

## Bio
Sachin Monga leads product and design at Substack as it evolves from a writing tool into a broader network for creators and readers. He brings a rare mix of startup founder experience, growth/product leadership from Facebook, and deep conviction about creator economics and user control.

His perspective matters because he thinks in systems: how to organize teams, how to build network effects without losing trust, and how to help creators build durable businesses instead of chasing short-term platform wins.

## Signature Frameworks

### Build around timeless customer problems, not product surfaces
- **When to use**: When organizing product teams at a company that is still evolving and expects to scale.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the enduring customer problem the team exists to solve.
  2. Organize teams around customer types or missions rather than app surfaces or features.
  3. Keep the structure stable as long as it continues to map to the core mission.
  4. Add new teams only when the customer problem or scale truly demands it.
- **Example**: Substack’s teams are organized into writer, reader, growth, and systems rather than app/dashboard/podcast teams.

### Facilitate first, decide later
- **When to use**: When joining a small company with a founder who has strong product opinions and the team lacks shared context.
- **Steps**:
  1. Act as a translator between founder vision and team execution.
  2. Make sure the founder understands what each team is doing.
  3. Make sure teams understand the founder’s principles and priorities.
  4. Build trust through repeated weekly check-ins and consistent follow-through.
  5. Gradually shift from facilitation to more independent decision-making as trust and context accumulate.
- **Example**: Sachin spent his first months at Substack making sure Chris knew what teams were doing and teams understood Chris’s vision before taking on a more direct product leadership role.

### Principle-led prioritization
- **When to use**: When multiple product options are possible and you need a consistent way to choose among them.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start from the company’s core principles.
  2. Ask which option gives more control to the writer or reader.
  3. Prefer the option that preserves user agency, even if it is harder to build.
  4. Use the principle as a tie-breaker when trade-offs are otherwise close.
- **Example**: For recommendations, Substack chose writer-curated recommendations over algorithmic or platform-inserted suggestions because it better preserved writer control.

### Build with writers/readers before rolling out broadly
- **When to use**: When introducing a feature that could fundamentally change how the product works.
- **Steps**:
  1. Prototype the idea quickly.
  2. Talk to a small set of relevant users before building fully.
  3. Run a limited pilot with opt-in participants.
  4. Observe behavior and iterate based on real usage.
  5. Only then expand to broader rollout.
- **Example**: Substack piloted recommendations with a small group of writers and later created the Product Lab to keep testing features with an invite-only group.

### Use network effects to increase discovery without sacrificing trust
- **When to use**: When a platform has enough supply and demand to enable cross-pollination between users.
- **Steps**:
  1. Look for organic discovery behaviors already happening.
  2. Design a mechanism that lets users recommend or surface other users directly.
  3. Make the mechanism socially legible and opt-in.
  4. Create a feedback loop that benefits both the recommender and the recommended.
  5. Expand the surface area beyond the initial entry point once the loop proves itself.
- **Example**: Writer recommendations on Substack created a goodwill loop where recommending writers get notified and new subscriptions flow to the recommended writers.

## Core Advice
- **When you are starting a newsletter or creator business and don’t know if it will work**: Start it immediately, publish a few pieces, and put a link somewhere so people can subscribe — because you learn by doing, and the biggest risk is staying in planning mode.
- **When you already have an audience on another platform**: Move your most valuable audience to a place you control, like email/subscriptions — because platform audiences are fragile and direct subscriptions give you deterministic reach.
- **When you are worried your audience will think you are asking for too much**: Ask for payment, publish consistently, and take breaks when needed without over-apologizing — because readers are usually more forgiving and supportive than creators assume.
- **When you are deciding whether to charge for your work**: Test paid subscriptions earlier than your fear suggests — because a small number of true fans can support meaningful income.
- **When you are unsure how much to publish**: Focus on consistency over intensity — because the signal comes from repetition, not a single launch spike.
- **When you are building product at a fast-growing startup**: Assume your current process will become obsolete and optimize for getting a little better every week — because high-growth companies change quickly.
- **When you are choosing between product options at Substack-like companies**: Choose the option that gives more control to writers or readers, even if it is harder to execute — because control is a core principle that preserves the long-term promise of the platform.
- **When you are launching a feature that depends on user participation**: Validate the full chain of user actions with a pilot first — because simple-seeming features can fail if too many steps must go right.
- **When you are leading a team through negative press or public chatter**: Filter aggressively for signal and ignore most of the noise — because external chatter rarely affects day-to-day execution.
- **When you are building a creator product and want to reduce burnout**: Design the product so creators can become curators or community leaders, not just solo content machines — because sustainable creator businesses need more than one-person output.
- **When you are onboarding a new creator with very different goals**: Offer different setup paths for different intent levels — because one onboarding flow will not fit everyone.
- **When you are hiring for a fast-moving startup**: Look for grit, endurance, and the ability to run through walls — because resilience matters more than familiarity with a fixed playbook.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: The best discovery systems are algorithmic and platform-controlled. → **Their view**: Prefer writer-curated recommendations that preserve writer control — because the product should optimize for agency and trust, not just platform efficiency.
- **Conventional**: A product team should organize around surfaces like app, dashboard, or podcast. → **Their view**: Organize around customer missions like writers, readers, and growth — because customer problems are more timeless than surfaces.
- **Conventional**: Creators should be cautious about asking for money or taking breaks because it may annoy their audience. → **Their view**: Readers are usually more forgiving and supportive than creators think — because subscribing is a trust signal, not a fragile transaction.
- **Conventional**: A product manager at a small company should primarily be the decision maker. → **Their view**: Early on, the PM’s job may be more about facilitation, translation, and trust-building — because shared context is often the real bottleneck.
- **Conventional**: If a feature requires too many steps, it probably won’t work. → **Their view**: A feature can still work if the value proposition is strong enough and the social loop is well designed — because network effects can overcome friction.
- **Conventional**: Substack is just a newsletter tool. → **Their view**: Substack is becoming a network and a broader home for a creator’s most valuable audience — because the product is evolving beyond single-player publishing.
