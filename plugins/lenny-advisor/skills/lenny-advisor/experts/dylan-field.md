# Dylan Field

**CEO and co-founder** | **Figma** | Expertise: design, product-management, leadership, strategy

## Bio
Dylan Field is the CEO and co-founder of Figma, the collaborative design platform that reshaped how product teams design and build software. His perspective matters because he combines strong product taste with a systems view of how design, engineering, and product management should work together.

He is especially useful when you need judgment under uncertainty: how to simplify a complex product, how to launch without overthinking, and how to turn intuition into a disciplined decision-making process.

## Signature Frameworks

### Intuition as a hypothesis generator
- **When to use**: When making product decisions under uncertainty, especially where taste and judgment matter before hard data is available.
- **Steps**:
  1. Generate hypotheses based on intuition and product taste.
  2. Invite competing hypotheses from others on the team.
  3. Debate the hypotheses openly.
  4. Look for data, examples, and user evidence that support or negate them.
  5. Converge on a working hypothesis.
  6. Move forward and keep refining as new evidence appears.
- **Example**: Field says he constantly forms hypotheses about what will work in Figma, then pressure-tests them through debate, user input, and data before deciding.

### Concrete artifacts and first-principles persuasion
- **When to use**: When trying to influence leadership or change someone's mind on an important product decision.
- **Steps**:
  1. Make the idea concrete with an artifact, example, or prototype.
  2. Encourage debate around the actual thing rather than abstractions.
  3. Ask follow-up questions to expose assumptions and gaps.
  4. Answer unresolved questions with data or evidence.
  5. Revisit the decision once the unknowns are clarified.
- **Example**: When asked what changes his mind, Field says concrete artifacts and examples work best, and that if follow-up questions reveal missing answers, the team should go find those answers and return to the discussion.

### Keep the simple things simple; make the complex things possible
- **When to use**: When designing tools that need to be powerful without becoming overwhelming.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the most common simple tasks users need to do.
  2. Ensure those tasks remain easy and obvious.
  3. Support advanced or complex workflows without forcing that complexity onto everyone.
  4. Continuously audit the product for places where local decisions have created system-level complexity.
  5. Revisit and redesign the system when complexity compounds.
- **Example**: Field says this principle is central at Figma, especially as the product grows more powerful. He notes that many parts of the product become too complex even when each individual decision seemed right at the time.

### Quality, features, deadline — choose two
- **When to use**: When planning a launch and deciding what tradeoffs to make to ship.
- **Steps**:
  1. Clarify whether the launch prioritizes quality, feature breadth, or timing.
  2. Choose the two dimensions that matter most for this release.
  3. Define the minimum bar needed for the third dimension.
  4. Ship the minimally awesome version.
  5. Iterate after launch to improve quality and expand features.
- **Example**: Field applies this to launches at Figma, contrasting fast-shipped products like FigJam and Figma Slides with Dev Mode, which took much longer because the team had to keep iterating until they truly understood developer needs.

### Influencer-user feedback loop for early adoption
- **When to use**: When launching a new product in a community where expert users can shape adoption and product quality.
- **Steps**:
  1. Map the community to identify central and respected practitioners.
  2. Select people whose work you genuinely admire.
  3. Reach out personally and ask for time or feedback.
  4. Use the conversations to learn, not just to pitch.
  5. Incorporate their feedback into the product.
  6. Let advocacy emerge naturally if the product earns it.
- **Example**: Field scraped Twitter to identify influential designers, then met with designers he admired to get feedback on Figma. He frames this less as a growth hack and more as a learning and product-improvement strategy.

### PM as framework-builder
- **When to use**: When a cross-functional team needs alignment on strategy, execution, and morale.
- **Steps**:
  1. Develop a clear point of view and strategy.
  2. Translate that strategy into a framework the team can share.
  3. Make the destination and path understandable to everyone.
  4. Coordinate design, engineering, and other functions around the objective.
  5. Celebrate milestones so the team stays cohesive and motivated.
- **Example**: Field says the best PMs at Figma create frameworks that wrap strategy and point of view together so everyone knows where they are going and how to get there.

## Core Advice
- **When you face ambiguous product decisions without enough data**: Use intuition as a starting hypothesis, then test it through debate, user feedback, and evidence — because taste should guide exploration, not replace rigor.
- **When you face resistance from leadership or need to change an executive's mind**: Bring concrete artifacts, examples, and answers to follow-up questions rather than abstract arguments — because tangible work makes tradeoffs easier to evaluate.
- **When you face growing product complexity as features accumulate**: Step back and evaluate the system as a whole, not just individual feature decisions — because locally correct choices can still create a confusing product.
- **When you face pressure to add power to a product without hurting usability**: Keep common tasks simple while making advanced tasks possible — because most users need clarity, while experts need depth.
- **When you face uncertainty about whether to launch now or wait**: Decide explicitly which two of quality, features, and deadline matter most, then ship the minimally awesome version — because tradeoffs become clearer when named.
- **When you face the temptation to stay in stealth too long**: Get the product out as fast as you reasonably can — because early shipping accelerates feedback and learning.
- **When you face the challenge of finding early adopters for a new product**: Seek out respected practitioners in your target community and ask for feedback before trying to turn them into evangelists — because expert feedback improves the product and advocacy should be earned.
- **When you face user requests that seem very specific**: Investigate the underlying need instead of taking the request literally — because users often describe symptoms, not root problems.
- **When you face the challenge of becoming a better leader as the company scales**: Treat mentorship as something that can come from anywhere — because useful frameworks can come from peers, hires, interns, founders, coaches, and even people you mentor.
- **When you face a cross-functional team that is delivering but unhappy**: Treat team morale and shared celebration as part of the PM's job — because sustained momentum depends on more than output.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Intuition is soft, unscientific, and should be minimized in product decisions. → **Their view**: Intuition is valuable because it generates hypotheses that can then be tested rigorously — because early decisions often happen before complete data exists.
- **Conventional**: Product management is mainly about process, coordination, and keeping projects on track. → **Their view**: The best PMs bring strategy, point of view, frameworks, and team cohesion — because process alone does not create great products.
- **Conventional**: Adding more features makes a product better and more competitive. → **Their view**: More features often make products worse unless simplicity is actively protected at the system level — because complexity compounds.
- **Conventional**: Early influencer outreach is mostly a growth hack for distribution. → **Their view**: The better use of influential early users is as a source of deep feedback and learning — because product quality should come before advocacy.
- **Conventional**: Advice from experienced people should be followed closely. → **Their view**: Advice is often the speaker giving themselves advice in your situation, not a universal truth — because context matters.
