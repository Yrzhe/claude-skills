# Julia Schottenstein

**Product leader leading dbt Cloud product** | **dbt Labs** | Expertise: product strategy, go-to-market, pricing, M&A, open source, platform strategy

## Bio
Julia Schottenstein leads dbt Cloud product at dbt Labs and previously worked as an investor at NEA focused on technical startups. Her perspective matters because she combines venture-style market judgment with hands-on product execution across open source, pricing, competition, and acquisitions.

She is especially valuable when you need a view on how product, distribution, and strategic optionality interact in technical companies. Julia thinks like both a builder and a buyer, which makes her advice unusually practical for startups navigating growth, monetization, and M&A.

## Signature Frameworks

### Early-stage company evaluation: People, Market, Product, Distribution
- **When to use**: When deciding whether to join, invest in, or assess an early-stage startup.
- **Steps**:
  1. Evaluate the founder/CEO: do you trust this person to lead and can they articulate both long-term vision and day-to-day details?
  2. Assess the market: is it growing, changing, and open to a new entrant?
  3. Examine the product: do users show real enthusiasm and talk about it unprompted?
  4. Assess distribution: does the company have an unfair advantage in getting to market, whether bottoms-up or enterprise?
  5. Identify weak spots and ask whether your own skills can help de-risk them.
- **Example**: Julia used this lens on dbt: Tristan Handy had rare founder qualities, cloud data warehouses were exploding, users were unusually enthusiastic about dbt, and open source gave dbt a strong ecosystem/distribution advantage.

### M&A optionality and “inflict pain” strategy
- **When to use**: When building a startup that may eventually need to be acquired, or when trying to get on the radar of likely acquirers before you are desperate.
- **Steps**:
  1. Assume only two to three buyers will find your company truly strategic.
  2. Build a real independent path if possible, because the best M&A leverage comes from not needing to sell.
  3. Identify the area where you have a competitive advantage relative to a likely buyer.
  4. Create enough market or product pressure that the buyer cannot ignore you.
  5. Stay friendly and open with incumbents and potential acquirers rather than becoming hostile.
  6. Preserve optionality by keeping relationships warm and integration paths plausible.
- **Example**: Transform pressured dbt Labs in the semantic layer by solving hard technical problems and being vocal about it, but still positioned itself as a friendly partner. That combination made dbt take notice and later acquire the company.

### Competition philosophy: Vision, Grow the Pie, Lean into Strengths
- **When to use**: When deciding how much attention to pay competitors and how to compete without losing strategic focus.
- **Steps**:
  1. Hold true to your vision and avoid getting distracted by competitor noise or criticism.
  2. Adopt a grow-the-pie mindset by expanding the overall market with partners and ecosystem players.
  3. Lean into your strengths and be explicit about where you want to be the platform versus where partners can add value.
  4. Take a long-term view and defend only the few strategic layers that truly matter.
- **Example**: At dbt Labs, Julia says they focus on their transformation and semantic standards while partnering broadly across the modern data stack instead of trying to own every adjacent category.

### Open-core boundary: open standard vs proprietary supercharger
- **When to use**: When designing an open source business model and deciding what to keep open versus what to monetize.
- **Steps**:
  1. Keep the core workflow or standard open if ecosystem adoption and interoperability matter.
  2. Define proprietary value around stateful interactions and cross-team or structural collaboration.
  3. Use the proprietary layer to supercharge development lifecycle and productionization at scale.
  4. Ensure the split is principled so the community understands what remains open and why.
- **Example**: dbt keeps core transformation logic open source, while dbt Cloud monetizes stateful workflows and collaboration features that help teams develop and operate dbt at scale.

### Pricing discovery before building
- **When to use**: When evaluating whether a new product or feature has monetizable value, or when revisiting pricing.
- **Steps**:
  1. Have willingness-to-pay conversations before building, not only after launch.
  2. Use cross-functional input from product, product marketing, and finance.
  3. Talk to dozens of customers and probe relative value rather than asking directly what they would pay.
  4. Test thresholds such as “very cheap,” “fair,” and “too expensive.”
  5. Model the business impact, then validate with customer conversations.
  6. After changes, track conversion and churn closely to learn price elasticity.
- **Example**: dbt Labs’ first pricing change involved product, product marketing, and finance; they interviewed dozens of customers and used relative value against cloud warehouse spend to guide pricing.

### Ship-first product philosophy: “Worse is better” and “Tech debt is a champagne problem”
- **When to use**: When teams are over-optimizing for perfect architecture before validating user demand.
- **Steps**:
  1. Fight perfectionism by aiming for good enough rather than ideal.
  2. Ship a simple version to get real user feedback.
  3. Treat early tech debt as evidence of usage, not failure.
  4. Rebuild only when scale and customer demand justify it.
- **Example**: dbt Cloud’s initial scheduler was a simple loop over a jobs table checking whether each job should run. It was naive but worked, and only later did the team rebuild it as scale increased to millions of runs per month.

### Embodied team learning for complex systems
- **When to use**: When a team needs to deeply internalize a new algorithm, architecture, or system behavior rather than superficially understanding it.
- **Steps**:
  1. Turn the abstract system into a physical or memorable exercise.
  2. Assign every team member a role in the system.
  3. Walk through the algorithm step by step together.
  4. Use the exercise to surface edge cases and create shared ownership.
- **Example**: Julia used rope and sticky notes to turn engineers into nodes and edges in a graph so the team could physically simulate a new DAG execution algorithm and understand it deeply.

## Core Advice
- **When deciding whether to join an early-stage startup**: Evaluate it across people, market, product, and distribution, and ask where your own strengths can offset the company’s weaknesses — because early-stage outcomes depend on more than product quality alone.
- **When a product seems promising but unproven**: Look for users who cannot stop talking about it and actively share it with teammates and peers — because unprompted enthusiasm is a stronger PMF signal than polite praise.
- **When a startup may need to be acquired someday**: Start building M&A optionality early by identifying likely strategic buyers and cultivating relationships before you need them — because the best outcomes happen when buyers already know you.
- **When dealing with a likely acquirer or incumbent competitor**: Create enough pressure that they take you seriously, but remain friendly and open — because you want strategic relevance without closing the door to partnership.
- **When a sale process is distressed**: Be transparent, cast a wider net, and clearly state that you are exploring an exit while emphasizing the team and product assets — because hiding distress is often futile and transparency can widen the buyer pool.
- **When pricing a new product or feature**: Run willingness-to-pay conversations before building and use relative value questions instead of asking directly for a price — because customers reveal value boundaries more reliably than exact numbers.
- **When architecture is getting over-engineered before launch**: Ship a simpler version first and accept tech debt if it helps you learn from real users sooner — because usage patterns are hard to predict in advance.
- **When a technical change is hard for the team to grasp**: Create a shared, memorable learning exercise so the whole team internalizes the system and its edge cases — because deep shared understanding improves execution.
- **When the product team has too many priorities**: Do fewer things and single-thread the team around one mission — because concentrated effort improves alignment and speed.
- **When you need acquisition introductions**: Use investors and corp dev teams to make sponsor introductions inside likely buyer organizations — because they are incentivized to meet relevant companies.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: If a company might acquire you someday, avoid competing too directly with them. → **Their view**: Strategically inflict pain on likely acquirers so they cannot ignore you — because buyers pay attention when you become strategically relevant.
- **Conventional**: Founders should avoid signaling that they want to sell because it looks weak or desperate. → **Their view**: In a distressed market, being public and transparent about seeking an exit can be the better strategy — because hiding it is often futile.
- **Conventional**: You should avoid tech debt and build scalable systems from the start. → **Their view**: Tech debt is often a good sign early on — because it usually means real users are using the product.
- **Conventional**: Pricing can wait until after product adoption is established. → **Their view**: You should have pricing and willingness-to-pay conversations before building — because delaying pricing increases the risk of building something nobody will buy.
- **Conventional**: To win, companies should focus on taking share from competitors. → **Their view**: Often the better strategy is to grow the pie with partners and ecosystem players — because expanding the market can create more durable value.
