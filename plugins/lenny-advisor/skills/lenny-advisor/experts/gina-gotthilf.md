# Gina Gotthilf

**Co-founder and COO** | **Latitud** | Expertise: growth, brand-building, go-to-market, Latin America startups

## Bio
Gina Gotthilf is known for helping lead Duolingo’s growth from roughly 3 million to over 200 million users, with a heavy emphasis on organic channels, product-led growth, and brand. She now co-founds and operates Latitud, where she supports the next generation of startups across Latin America.

Her perspective matters because she combines growth rigor with strong instincts about brand, messaging, and international expansion. She’s especially useful when teams need to grow without overrelying on paid acquisition or generic marketing playbooks.

## Signature Frameworks

### A-side / B-side storytelling
- **When to use**: When reflecting on careers, company journeys, or startup ecosystems to avoid only highlighting polished success narratives.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the “A-side”: the visible wins, milestones, and impressive highlights.
  2. Identify the “B-side”: the failures, layoffs, false starts, and messy in-between moments.
  3. Reframe B-side moments as temporary phases rather than permanent verdicts.
  4. Use both sides together to tell a more truthful and useful story for others.
- **Example**: Gina contrasted her A-side achievements—Duolingo growth, Bloomberg campaign, Latitud funding—with B-side moments like dropping out of college, losing visas, being fired, and getting laid off multiple times.

### Lean organic growth before paid acquisition
- **When to use**: When building an early-stage consumer product, especially before monetization and before retention is proven.
- **Steps**:
  1. Avoid paid acquisition if you do not yet have a clear LTV and sustainable CAC-to-LTV ratio.
  2. Focus on product-led growth and A/B testing to improve activation and retention.
  3. Use organic channels like PR, brand, social, and word-of-mouth to find users.
  4. Only layer in paid once you understand value delivery, retention, and unit economics.
- **Example**: At Duolingo, Gina’s mandate was to grow the product with essentially no budget, so the team relied on product improvements, brand, PR, and organic channels rather than turning on ads early.

### Landing page conversion optimization
- **When to use**: When running paid campaigns or any acquisition funnel where users click through to a landing page.
- **Steps**:
  1. Optimize for mobile first.
  2. Keep the core message and CTA above the fold, or clearly signal that users should scroll.
  3. Make copy highly skimmable by reducing text aggressively.
  4. Ensure the headline and button reinforce each other so users understand the action without reading everything.
  5. Match the landing page closely to the ad that drove the click.
  6. Use emotion intentionally without letting visuals distract from the core message.
- **Example**: On the Bloomberg campaign, Gina improved one landing page from 3% to 12% conversion by simplifying copy, optimizing for mobile, aligning message and CTA, and using emotionally resonant imagery.

### Brand voice distinctiveness test
- **When to use**: When writing product copy, notifications, emails, social posts, or any customer-facing communication.
- **Steps**:
  1. Review each piece of copy and ask whether any company could have written it.
  2. If yes, rewrite it to sound unmistakably like your brand.
  3. Define what your brand voice sounds like, including what is too much and too little.
  4. Use humor, quirk, or emotional texture consistently across channels.
  5. Be willing to take some creative risk so the brand is memorable.
- **Example**: At Duolingo, the team asked of every message, “Could this have been written by any other company, or is it Duolingo?” This led to the passive-aggressive Duo reminders and the broader irreverent brand voice.

### Dogfooding as a growth and product discipline
- **When to use**: When evaluating experiments, product changes, or new features before or during rollout.
- **Steps**:
  1. Use the product internally before relying only on metrics.
  2. Experience the feature as a user would.
  3. Catch obvious quality or motivation issues early.
  4. Combine instinct from dogfooding with data from experiments.
- **Example**: After a weak badges experiment at Duolingo, Gina realized the team had not dogfooded it; if they had, they would have immediately seen the badge was unmotivating and poorly designed.

### Global-first product simplification
- **When to use**: When expanding internationally and deciding how much to localize product behavior across markets.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start from the assumption that core human behavior is more similar than different.
  2. Roll out product changes broadly rather than creating many country-specific variants.
  3. Only localize where there is a truly material difference.
  4. Protect engineering simplicity, experimentation speed, and organizational focus.
  5. Address exceptions selectively when evidence is strong.
- **Example**: Duolingo generally treated the world as one product rather than heavily customizing by country, with exceptions like India and China where specific user behavior or regulation required changes.

## Core Advice
- **When you face pressure to use paid acquisition early in a consumer startup**: Do everything you can to delay dependence on paid and focus first on retention, product value, and organic growth — because without clear LTV, CAC is misleading and paid can become an expensive dependency.
- **When you face a conversion problem in an acquisition funnel**: Do a full review of the post-click experience, especially the landing page, instead of only optimizing the ad creative — because downstream conversion often determines whether spend actually works.
- **When you face low engagement with product messaging or lifecycle communications**: Do the work to create a distinctive brand voice that makes users feel something and that no other company could plausibly have written — because memorable communication drives recall and affinity.
- **When you face uncertainty about what feature or experiment to prioritize**: Do ROI-based prioritization and focus on the highest expected impact relative to time investment — because early teams need compounding learning, not busywork.
- **When you face a product decision and feel tempted to rely only on research or dashboards**: Do dogfooding and trust your own user experience as an input alongside data — because direct experience catches friction that metrics may miss.
- **When you face international expansion decisions**: Do less localization than you think, and only customize when there is strong evidence that a market truly behaves differently in a material way — because over-localization slows experimentation and adds complexity.
- **When you face skepticism about whether mission really matters**: Do the work to articulate and operationalize a mission that is bigger than the product itself — because mission helps with talent, PR, brand, and long-term direction.
- **When you face imposter syndrome or opportunities slightly beyond your experience**: Do say yes when you can plausibly figure it out, then learn fast and grow into the role — because stretch opportunities often create the fastest growth.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Startups should turn on paid acquisition early to scale fast. → **Their view**: Early paid acquisition is often a trap; prioritize organic growth, retention, and product value first — because paid can mask product weakness and create dependency.
- **Conventional**: International products need heavy country-by-country customization. → **Their view**: Treat users across countries more similarly and localize sparingly — because core behavior is broadly universal and over-localization slows teams down.
- **Conventional**: Brand and PR are secondary to product and performance marketing. → **Their view**: Brand and PR are deeply underappreciated growth levers — because lovable brands create word-of-mouth and emotional connection that paid channels can’t replicate.
- **Conventional**: Professional communication should be polished, neutral, and safe. → **Their view**: Irreverent, risky, emotionally resonant communication is often more effective — because people remember what makes them feel something.
- **Conventional**: Failures and messy career moments should be hidden. → **Their view**: The B-side is often the most useful and honest part of the story — because setbacks are normal and sharing them builds realism.
