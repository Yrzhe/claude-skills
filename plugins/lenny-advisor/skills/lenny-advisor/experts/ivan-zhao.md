# Ivan Zhao

**CEO and co-founder** | **Notion** | Expertise: product strategy, design, leadership, go-to-market, engineering management, AI strategy

## Bio
Ivan Zhao is the co-founder and CEO of Notion, the workspace platform known for combining notes, docs, databases, and workflows into one flexible system. His perspective matters because he built Notion through multiple resets, repeatedly rethinking product, architecture, and company structure until the company found a durable path.

He is especially valuable for founders and product leaders building horizontal products, modular software, or tools that need to balance broad appeal with deep power. Ivan’s thinking stands out for its emphasis on abstractions, taste, and the discipline of staying small and focused.

## Signature Frameworks

### Sugar-coated broccoli
- **When to use**: When your core vision is powerful but too abstract or unattractive for mainstream users to adopt directly.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the deeper capability or vision you want to bring into the world.
  2. Recognize that most users care about immediate jobs-to-be-done, not your abstract vision.
  3. Wrap the deeper capability inside a familiar, high-frequency use case people already understand.
  4. Let users discover the more powerful underlying capability over time.
- **Example**: Notion originally tried to be an easy developer tool so anyone could create software, but users did not care. The team reframed it as productivity software—notes and docs first—while hiding the no-code software-building power underneath.

### Balance value and business
- **When to use**: When deciding what product to build or how to prioritize between personal vision and market demand.
- **Steps**:
  1. Clarify what you personally want the world to have—your taste, values, and aesthetic.
  2. Clarify what the market rewards—users, revenue, and practical demand.
  3. Check whether you are too far toward self-expression or too far toward pure optimization.
  4. Adjust until the product is both authentic and genuinely useful.
- **Example**: Ivan said the first version of Notion was too much about what he wanted. Later, he found a balance: if you build too much for yourself, there are no users; too much for business, and you build a commodity.

### Reset through better abstractions
- **When to use**: When momentum is carrying the team down the wrong path, or the technical/product foundation is flawed.
- **Steps**:
  1. Question whether current progress is real progress or just momentum.
  2. Identify whether the underlying abstraction or foundation is wrong.
  3. Be willing to discard sunk costs and reset.
  4. Rebuild on a simpler, more durable abstraction that compounds faster.
- **Example**: Notion threw away code multiple times, including rebuilding after betting on Web Components before React became dominant. Ivan argued that a better abstraction can let you catch up faster than people expect.

### Small bus operating model
- **When to use**: When trying to keep a company fast, aligned, and high-talent-density as it grows.
- **Steps**:
  1. Treat the company like a small bus rather than a large bus.
  2. Optimize for fewer, stronger people rather than more headcount.
  3. Use systems and tools to reduce coordination overhead.
  4. Be deliberate about who sits on the bus because that determines speed and work quality.
- **Example**: Notion delayed hiring sales until after $10M ARR, hired its first PM at 50 people, and used Notion internally to run the company while tracking talent density and revenue per employee.

### Bricks and boxes
- **When to use**: When building a horizontal platform that must serve both broad flexibility and concrete business use cases.
- **Steps**:
  1. Build foundational modular capabilities—the bricks.
  2. Identify the packaged solution customers actually want to buy—the box.
  3. Segment users by whether they want raw flexibility or a ready-made solution.
  4. Ensure the packaged solution is still built from reusable bricks so the platform remains extensible.
- **Example**: Ivan explained that most users do not care about Lego bricks; they care about the Lego box. For enterprise, Notion had to think in terms of solutions, while still building reusable primitives underneath.

### B2C2B top-of-funnel
- **When to use**: When a horizontal product can enter through a simple consumer use case and later expand into teams and enterprises.
- **Steps**:
  1. Choose a broad, everyday use case with massive adoption potential.
  2. Make the product easy to adopt individually.
  3. Expose deeper capabilities as users mature.
  4. Let personal users bring the product into work environments.
- **Example**: Notion entered through notes and docs, then users discovered databases, tasks, and workflows. Ivan said about half of their B2B customers came from prior personal users.

## Core Advice
- **When you face years of weak traction but still believe the problem matters**: Do repeated resets instead of forcing momentum; change the product framing, technical foundation, or team shape if needed — because better abstractions compound faster than incremental work on the wrong foundation.
- **When you face a product vision users do not immediately understand**: Package the vision inside a familiar use case users already care about — because people optimize for immediate tasks, not your grand vision.
- **When you face a choice between building what you love and what the market wants**: Balance personal values with user and revenue signals — because too much self-expression creates a research project, while too much market optimization creates a commodity.
- **When you face pressure to hire aggressively to move faster**: Do more with a smaller, higher-talent-density team and invest in systems that reduce coordination overhead — because communication overhead often slows teams more than lack of headcount.
- **When you face discouragement or emotional volatility during hard company periods**: Reset personally with sleep and distance — because better rest improves judgment and keeps lows from becoming too low.
- **When you face enterprise expansion with a horizontal product**: Segment around concrete solutions, not just flexible primitives — because enterprise buyers want a budgetable outcome, not abstract building blocks.
- **When you face the temptation to hard-code features for speed**: Build reusable primitives if modularity is core to your product — because short-term parity can damage long-term coherence.
- **When you face investor attention during an upswing**: Don’t confuse investor interest with customer truth — because fundraising can become external validation instead of product progress.
- **When you face infrastructure growth that seems manageable for now**: Avoid premature optimization, but plan major scaling transitions early — because last-minute scaling crises can force painful feature freezes.
- **When you face product or strategy questions in tech**: Look outside tech and business for patterns and metaphors — because Ivan’s best ideas often come from history, tools, literature, and systems thinking.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: If you have a powerful vision, you should present it directly and trust users to appreciate it. → **Their view**: Hide the vision inside a familiar product people already want — because adoption comes through recognizable form factors.
- **Conventional**: More headcount is the main way to increase speed and output. → **Their view**: A smaller, more talented team with better systems often moves faster — because coordination overhead is the real bottleneck.
- **Conventional**: Once you have momentum, keep pushing forward and avoid throwing away work. → **Their view**: Resetting can be the fastest route to progress — because a better abstraction can compound faster than sunk-cost persistence.
- **Conventional**: Horizontal products should stay generic and let users assemble everything themselves. → **Their view**: Horizontal platforms still need packaged solutions and segmentation, especially in enterprise — because most customers buy outcomes, not primitives.
- **Conventional**: To win in a category like project management, you should hard-code category-specific features quickly. → **Their view**: Hard-coding category concepts can damage a modular system — because it may fight the architecture and the user mental model.
- **Conventional**: Horizontal software is weaker than point solutions, especially in the AI era. → **Their view**: AI can strengthen bundled horizontal products — because shared context makes reasoning, search, and automation better.
