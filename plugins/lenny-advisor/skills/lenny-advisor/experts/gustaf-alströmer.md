# Gustaf Alströmer

**Group Partner** | **Y Combinator** | Expertise: product-market-fit, go-to-market, growth, climate tech

## Bio
Gustaf Alströmer is a Group Partner at Y Combinator and a former early growth leader at Airbnb, where he built the company’s original growth team. His perspective matters because he has seen hundreds of startups up close and has a strong bias toward the behaviors that actually create product-market fit: talking to users, moving fast, and focusing on the real bottleneck.

He is especially useful when founders are stuck in theory, over-indexing on strategy, or confusing investor validation with customer demand. He also brings a practical, optimistic view of climate tech as a massive startup opportunity.

## Signature Frameworks

### YC Office Hours: What's Holding You Back?
- **When to use**: When an early-stage startup needs to identify the single biggest bottleneck preventing faster progress.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with the question: what is holding you back from moving faster?
  2. Avoid generic updates and strategy talk.
  3. Clarify the startup's current goal.
  4. Identify the few activities that actually drive that goal.
  5. Probe for the real blocker preventing progress.
  6. Use the discussion to uncover whether the issue is product, customers, speed, or prioritization.
- **Example**: In YC office hours, Gustaf focuses on uncovering what is slowing founders down rather than hearing a status report.

### Group Office Hours Accountability Loop
- **When to use**: When founders need accountability, peer learning, and emotional support during an intense building period.
- **Steps**:
  1. Ask each startup what goals they set for the last two weeks.
  2. Ask whether they hit those goals.
  3. If not, identify what got in the way.
  4. Set goals for the next two weeks.
  5. Use peer discussion to surface shared problems and solutions.
  6. Repeat on a regular cadence to create accountability and momentum.
- **Example**: Gustaf notes that founders often keep doing these group sessions for years because the format creates a uniquely intense and productive environment.

### Customer Discovery Through Observation
- **When to use**: When trying to determine whether a problem is painful enough to build a company around.
- **Steps**:
  1. Do not rely only on asking users whether they have a problem.
  2. Watch users perform the workflow in real time.
  3. Have them screen share or walk through their daily process.
  4. Observe workarounds, repetition, friction, and inefficiency.
  5. Infer the true intensity of the pain from behavior, not just words.
  6. Use those observations to shape the product.
- **Example**: Watching non-technical users repeat manual Excel tasks reveals pain more accurately than a survey ever could.

### Early Adopter Outreach Math
- **When to use**: When founders are struggling to get customer conversations or early sales and are discouraged by rejection.
- **Steps**:
  1. Assume most of the market is not made of early adopters.
  2. Expect roughly 90% of prospects to be uninterested in trying something new.
  3. Reach out to many more people than you think you need.
  4. Talk to 25 to 50 customers, not just 5.
  5. Treat rejection as filtering for early adopters rather than evidence the idea is dead.
  6. Convert discovery conversations into early customers.
- **Example**: He says founders may need to reach 10 people to find 1 early adopter willing to talk, and those conversations often become the first sales.

### Do Things That Don't Scale for Product-Market Fit
- **When to use**: When a startup is pre-product-market-fit and tempted to jump straight to scalable growth tactics.
- **Steps**:
  1. Go directly to where your customers are.
  2. Talk to them in person, over Zoom, or on the phone.
  3. Learn their workflow, tools, spending, and pain points.
  4. Build narrow solutions for early adopters first.
  5. Iterate quickly based on direct feedback.
  6. Only later shift to scalable distribution once something is clearly working.
- **Example**: Airbnb’s early trips to New York after Paul Graham asked, “Where are your customers?” became foundational to learning how to build the company.

### Founder Evaluation by Recent Progress
- **When to use**: When assessing startup quality in interviews or deciding whether a team is likely to execute well.
- **Steps**:
  1. Ask what the founders have done since they applied.
  2. Look for concrete product or customer progress over the last month or two.
  3. Prefer teams that kept building rather than preparing for the interview.
  4. Track whether each new conversation reveals meaningful new progress.
  5. Use weekly or biweekly momentum as a leading indicator of future success.
- **Example**: His favorite YC interview question is, “What have you done since you applied to YC on your product?”

## Core Advice
- **When you face uncertainty about what to build next**: do repeated customer conversations and direct observation before making product decisions — because founders who do not talk to customers fail to learn what matters, which blocks product-market fit.
- **When you face fear of customer rejection**: do the outreach anyway and reframe indifference as normal rather than catastrophic — because most people are simply busy and not early adopters.
- **When you face low response rates in early customer outreach**: do much higher-volume outreach than feels necessary and expect to talk to 25–50 people — because only a small fraction of prospects are early adopters.
- **When you face pressure to think about startup strategy too early**: do ruthless prioritization around the single most important execution goal instead of broad strategy work — because very small startups cannot do many things at once.
- **When you face the decision of whether to start a company**: do a serious check on your motivation and constraints before committing — because startups are all-consuming and not a resume step.
- **When you face a lack of technical capability on the founding team**: do everything you can to find a true technical co-founder or learn enough to build an initial prototype yourself — because early product iteration is deeply technical.
- **When you face the temptation to optimize for investor validation**: do customer work instead of confusing fundraising or program acceptance with product-market fit — because only customer pull proves the product matters.
- **When you face slow progress as a founder**: do short-cycle goal setting with explicit accountability every one to two weeks — because frequent review exposes blockers quickly and creates momentum.
- **When you face uncertainty about whether a problem is truly painful**: do live workflow observation instead of relying on survey-style questions — because users often normalize their own pain.
- **When you face interest in climate tech but lack domain expertise**: do the move anyway if you have strong startup, product, or software skills — because many climate startups need builders more than specialists.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Startup success can be predicted by hot markets, investor interest, or early buzz. → **Their view**: It is much easier to recognize failure patterns than to predict outlier winners — because YC is better at spotting common failure modes than identifying the next monster hit.
- **Conventional**: Early-stage founders should spend significant time on company strategy. → **Their view**: For very small startups, execution matters far more than strategy — because tiny teams usually only have bandwidth for one real priority.
- **Conventional**: If customers reject your product early, that is a strong negative signal. → **Their view**: The more common issue is indifference, and that is normal in early markets — because most people are not early adopters.
- **Conventional**: Starting a company is a logical career step for ambitious people. → **Their view**: Starting a company is not a career step; if it works, it becomes your career, and if it fails, it is still extremely hard — because startup life is all-consuming.
- **Conventional**: Climate tech is mostly mission-driven and less commercially attractive than software. → **Their view**: Climate tech is one of the largest economic opportunities of our time — because trillions of dollars are already being redirected.
- **Conventional**: Product quality and speed are opposing tradeoffs. → **Their view**: Talking to customers quickly is what creates real quality — because customer value defines quality.
