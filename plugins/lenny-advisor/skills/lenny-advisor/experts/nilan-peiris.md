# Nilan Peiris

**Chief Product Officer** | **Wise** | Expertise: word-of-mouth growth, product strategy, fintech, international payments

## Bio
Nilan Peiris is the Chief Product Officer at Wise, where he has helped scale a globally complex fintech product by obsessing over the customer experience, especially price, speed, and ease of use. His perspective matters because he connects product strategy, pricing, operations, and growth into one system: if the product is truly better, customers will recommend it.

He is especially useful for teams trying to build durable growth in markets where paid acquisition is expensive, trust is hard to earn, and local regulatory complexity can’t be ignored.

## Signature Frameworks

### The NPS-to-Word-of-Mouth Flywheel
- **When to use**: When you need to understand and increase recommendation-driven growth in a product with strong customer advocacy potential.
- **Steps**:
  1. Measure word of mouth directly by asking customers how they heard about you and combining survey data with attribution data.
  2. Use NPS as a proxy for recommendation propensity, not just satisfaction.
  3. Segment customers by NPS score and identify the sharp increase in referrals as scores move upward.
  4. Read the qualitative comments to identify recurring drivers of advocacy.
  5. Focus product work on the few attributes that repeatedly show up in comments and correlate with recommendation.
  6. Improve those attributes enough to move customers from neutral to promoter territory, because that increases viral coefficient.
  7. Track the resulting increase in referrals and word of mouth as the business outcome.
- **Example**: Wise found that customers scoring 7-8 on NPS told roughly twice as many people as lower scorers, and 9-10 doubled again, which helped justify focusing the company on improving recommendation drivers.

### Build a 10x Better Product, Not a Slightly Better One
- **When to use**: When incremental improvements are not enough to create advocacy, differentiation, or durable growth.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the ideal customer experience from first principles.
  2. Identify the biggest friction points in the current experience.
  3. Set a target that is dramatically better than the market baseline, not just marginally better.
  4. Invest in solving the hard systemic constraints that block that experience.
  5. Use customer feedback to validate that the improvement is truly remarkable.
  6. Launch only when the experience is good enough that customers would want to tell others about it.
- **Example**: In Singapore, Wise moved from face-to-face verification to eKYC with selfie and ID photo verification, creating a much better experience that customers talked about.

### Price-to-Cost Allocation and Route-Level Pricing
- **When to use**: When you need to lower prices sustainably in a business with heterogeneous customer and route costs.
- **Steps**:
  1. Allocate every cost back to the transaction, route, or customer that generated it.
  2. Identify the highest-cost customers or routes.
  3. Raise prices for the expensive segment so they cover their true costs.
  4. Lower prices for the rest of the base using the margin created.
  5. Continuously reduce underlying costs through engineering and operations.
  6. As costs fall, pass savings through and enter more price-sensitive segments.
- **Example**: Wise allocates support, verification, risk, and partner costs back to specific routes like AUD/GBP, then adjusts pricing so expensive users or markets pay what they cost.

### Product Marketing Inside the Product
- **When to use**: When customers experience value but do not fully perceive or remember it, limiting sharing and referrals.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the gap between actual value delivered and value perceived by the customer.
  2. Make the value visible in the product or post-transaction experience.
  3. Use clear visual proof, comparisons, or summaries to show the customer what they saved or gained.
  4. Add sharing and referral prompts at the moment of value realization.
  5. Iterate the presentation with direct customer feedback until the value is obvious.
- **Example**: Wise added a graph showing hidden bank fees versus Wise’s transparent pricing, then placed it on the success page with share and invite buttons, which tripled sharing rates.

### Work Backwards from the Ideal Experience
- **When to use**: When the current product is constrained by incremental thinking and you need a step-change in user experience.
- **Steps**:
  1. Imagine the ideal end-state experience as if the product were being built from scratch.
  2. Map the key moments in the user journey.
  3. Define what each moment should feel like if it were best-in-class.
  4. Identify the technical, operational, and regulatory blockers.
  5. Work backward to solve the blockers in the right order.
- **Example**: Lenny described Airbnb storyboarding the ideal host and guest journey, and Wise applied a similar approach to instant transfers, low prices, and account creation.

### Global-Local Product Operating Model
- **When to use**: When building a product across many countries with different regulations and local requirements.
- **Steps**:
  1. Create a single global product codebase and vision.
  2. Assign global teams ownership of core KPIs and the shared product model.
  3. Give local/regional teams ownership of market-specific conversion and compliance costs.
  4. Allow local teams to contribute directly to the shared codebase.
  5. Let the domain model evolve from repeated local patterns into reusable product structures.
  6. Split out sub-teams when a regional pattern becomes large enough to deserve dedicated ownership.
- **Example**: Wise uses a global onboarding flow with local teams handling market-specific KYC requirements, such as Japan’s unique ID photo rules.

## Core Advice
- **When you need to know whether growth is coming from word of mouth**: Ask customers directly how they heard about you and combine that with attribution data — because survey answers plus web tracking give a reliable estimate of word-of-mouth acquisition.
- **When you want to increase referrals**: Optimize for moving customers from neutral to promoter NPS scores, not just for conversion rate — because referral propensity rises sharply as NPS increases.
- **When customers keep saying the same things in feedback**: Turn those repeated comments into a small set of product pillars and KPIs — because recurring themes reveal what actually drives advocacy.
- **When a product is merely competitive but not remarkable**: Aim for a 10x better experience rather than a marginal improvement — because people recommend what feels meaningfully better than alternatives.
- **When you are tempted to copy a competitor’s infrastructure or launch a shortcut**: Avoid shortcuts that don’t change the underlying customer problem; solve the hard constraint instead — because durable growth comes from fixing the real experience and economics.
- **When your business has thin margins and expensive distribution**: Use customer-led growth channels like word of mouth because paid acquisition may be structurally uneconomic — because low take-rate businesses often can’t buy growth efficiently.
- **When customers trust your category less than they trust your brand**: Lean on customer experience and friend-to-friend trust rather than trying to solve trust with landing-page copy — because friends are more credible than marketing claims.
- **When customers appreciate value but don’t share it**: Make the value legible with explicit comparisons, savings summaries, or visual proof — because people share what they can easily understand and explain.
- **When a team wants to test everything before deciding**: Build conviction through customer conversations and data, then make a focused bet — because endless experimentation can slow progress when the core problem is already clear.
- **When a product improvement is hard to justify with short-term experiments**: Measure the underlying lever directly, such as price reduction or cost reduction, instead of waiting for perfect revenue proof — because the input often matters more than the immediate output.
- **When a market has unique regulatory or operational friction**: Treat the friction as a product opportunity and solve it in a way that becomes a customer story — because hard constraints can become differentiation.
- **When your team is stuck on a hard problem for a long time**: Keep going if the problem is strategically important; patience is part of the model — because some of the highest-leverage wins take years.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Optimize conversion rate and polish the funnel; that will drive growth. → **Their view**: To drive recommendation, you need to blow users’ socks off with an experience they didn’t know was possible. — because incremental gains don’t create advocacy.
- **Conventional**: Trust is mainly built through branding, reassurance, and trust badges. → **Their view**: The strongest trust signal is a friend’s recommendation, and the best way to earn it is to deliver an excellent experience. — because lived experience beats marketing claims.
- **Conventional**: Run lots of A/B tests to discover what to build. → **Their view**: You can’t split test your way to love; use customer insight to build conviction and make bigger bets. — because the right answer is often already visible in feedback.
- **Conventional**: Launch the fastest possible version by reusing existing infrastructure. → **Their view**: Shortcuts that don’t change the underlying economics or experience won’t create durable growth. — because initial access is not the same as advocacy.
- **Conventional**: Marketing and acquisition are separate from the product. → **Their view**: Marketing can be part of the product experience itself. — because the product can surface value and trigger sharing directly.
- **Conventional**: International businesses should treat local markets as secondary or separate. → **Their view**: Local market complexity should be built into the core operating model and codebase. — because global scale requires local reality to shape the product.
