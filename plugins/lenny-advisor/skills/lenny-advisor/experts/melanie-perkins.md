# Melanie Perkins

**CEO and co-founder** | **Canva** | Expertise: product strategy, design platforms, company building, mission-driven leadership

## Bio
Melanie Perkins co-founded Canva and turned it into one of the world’s most valuable private companies by combining a bold long-term vision with disciplined execution. Her perspective matters because she has repeatedly shown how to build a category-defining product by starting with a future state, translating it into concrete milestones, and aligning the business around a mission that scales.

She is especially known for “Column B” thinking, which starts with the world you want to create rather than the constraints you have today. She also stands out for treating mission as an operating system, not a slogan: product, goals, culture, and philanthropy all reinforce the same purpose.

## Signature Frameworks

### Column B Thinking
- **When to use**: When setting a company vision or deciding what kind of future to build toward.
- **Steps**:
  1. Imagine the ideal future you want to exist in, rather than starting from current constraints.
  2. Define what the world should look like in 10, 20, or 50 years.
  3. Work backward from that desired future instead of optimizing around today’s bricks.
  4. Translate the vision into a sequence of small, compounding steps.
- **Example**: Canva began with the belief that design would become online, collaborative, and simple, then built toward that future through Fusion Books and later Canva.

### Chaos to Clarity
- **When to use**: When turning a vague idea, belief, or problem into something real and executable.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with the chaotic raw idea, problem, or philosophy.
  2. Write it down so it exists outside your head.
  3. Turn it into a pitch deck to clarify the thinking.
  4. Refine it into designs and then a prototype.
  5. Keep adding clarity with each step until others can see and understand it.
- **Example**: A new project begins as an idea in someone’s head, then becomes a written concept, then a deck, then designs, then a prototype.

### Mission Pillars and Crazy Big Goals
- **When to use**: When you want a company mission to translate into concrete annual progress.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define a bold mission that is large enough to matter for years.
  2. Break the mission into a few mission pillars.
  3. Set Crazy Big Goals for each pillar.
  4. Take successive yearly steps toward each goal.
  5. Celebrate each milestone to reinforce momentum and culture.
- **Example**: Canva’s mission to empower everyone to design anything is broken into pillars like every ingredient, every language, and every device, with yearly goals such as launching more products, more languages, and more platforms.

### Two-Step Plan
- **When to use**: When building a company that aims to create both commercial scale and social impact.
- **Steps**:
  1. Build one of the world’s most valuable companies.
  2. Use the resulting resources, equity, and profitability to do the most good possible.
  3. Treat impact as a core operating principle, not a side project.
  4. Let step one and step two reinforce each other over time.
- **Example**: Canva pledged 1% of time, money, equity, and profitability, and later committed major donations through the Canva Foundation to fight extreme poverty.

### Closing the Loop with Customers
- **When to use**: When deciding what to build next and how to improve product-market fit.
- **Steps**:
  1. Collect explicit customer requests at scale.
  2. Tally and categorize requests into themes.
  3. Feed them directly to product teams.
  4. Validate with user testing and observation.
  5. Ship improvements and confirm the loop is closed by seeing adoption and satisfaction.
- **Example**: Canva receives over a million requests per year and has closed more than 200 loops in a year, including features like gradient text and Sheets.

## Core Advice
- **When you are starting a company and only see today’s constraints**: spend real time imagining the future you want to exist in before deciding what to build — because a compelling future creates direction, motivation, and a basis for long-term compounding.
- **When your idea feels too big or unrealistic**: take one microscopic first step that adds clarity, such as writing it down or making a deck — because big visions become real through incremental clarity-building steps, not through perfect plans.
- **When you are pitching investors and getting repeated rejection**: treat rejection as feedback, not personal failure, and use it to improve your pitch deck — because Canva’s pitch became stronger each time objections surfaced missing context and market framing.
- **When people don’t understand your product or market**: lead with the problem and the lay of the land before showing the solution — because if people don’t understand the problem, they can’t care about the solution.
- **When setting goals for a team or company**: set goals that are crazy big enough to feel slightly inadequate, then break them into measurable milestones — because ambitious goals create urgency and persistence.
- **When a roadmap or transformation seems to be taking forever**: expect timing to be unreliable and plan for goals to take longer than you think — because major rewrites and transformations often take far longer than initial estimates.
- **When your company is scaling and old systems stop working**: reinvent operating rituals for the new scale instead of copying what worked at a smaller stage or at another company — because systems must evolve with the company.
- **When you are deciding whether to build a feature or product**: start from a real customer pain point and a gap in the market, not from a solution looking for a problem — because Canva’s wedge came from a specific user need and expanded from there.
- **When you are choosing whether to add AI to a product**: embed AI only where it genuinely helps users accomplish their goals with less friction — because AI should serve the workflow, not trendiness.
- **When you are overwhelmed by work and always “on”**: create hard boundaries between work and non-work time, including no email on your phone — because constant work harms health and perspective.
- **When you want to improve product decisions**: use both explicit customer requests and direct user testing to guide what you build — because the combination reveals both demand and friction.
- **When you achieve a major milestone**: celebrate it in a memorable, physical way so the team feels the progress — because rituals make long journeys emotionally sustainable.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Start with what is feasible today and build incrementally from current constraints. → **Their view**: Start with the future you want and work backward from that “Column B” vision — because future-first thinking creates more ambitious, durable companies.
- **Conventional**: Set realistic goals so teams stay motivated and avoid disappointment. → **Their view**: Set crazy big goals that may take years longer than expected — because the size of the goal matters more than precise timing.
- **Conventional**: A mission can live as a statement on the wall while the business focuses on revenue and growth separately. → **Their view**: The mission should be operationalized into pillars, goals, product decisions, and philanthropy — because authenticity comes from alignment.
- **Conventional**: Competitor analysis should drive wedge and positioning strategy. → **Their view**: Focus on the gap in the market and the customer pain point you can uniquely solve — because the best wedge comes from real user need.
- **Conventional**: AI should be prominently featured because it is the hot trend. → **Their view**: AI should stay in the background unless it materially reduces friction and helps users achieve their goals — because utility matters more than hype.
- **Conventional**: Work harder and longer to solve scaling problems. → **Their view**: Sometimes the better move is to step away, protect your energy, and regain perspective — because constant grind can distort judgment.
