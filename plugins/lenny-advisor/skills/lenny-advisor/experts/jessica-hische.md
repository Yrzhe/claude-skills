# Jessica Hische

**Lettering Artist, Author, and Designer** | **Independent / Jessica Hische Studio** | Expertise: typography, logo design, branding, design systems

## Bio
Jessica Hische is a renowned lettering artist and designer known for custom typography, logo refreshes, book covers, and brand identity work for companies like Mailchimp, Eventbrite, and Philz Coffee, as well as clients like Apple and Nike. She brings a rare mix of craft, strategic brand thinking, and practical experience helping companies translate who they are into visual systems that actually work in the real world.

Her perspective matters most when a brand needs to balance beauty with usability: when to refresh, how much to change, what to preserve, and how typography shapes perception. She is especially valuable for founders and teams who want a brand that feels distinctive without becoming fragile or overdesigned.

## Signature Frameworks

### Logo Refresh Goal-First Process
- **When to use**: When updating an existing logo or brand without losing existing brand equity, especially for startups or creator brands with an established audience.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the core goal of the refresh: invisible refinement, utilitarian fixes, audience expansion, or repositioning.
  2. Decide how broad the first exploration should be based on those goals.
  3. Explore early directions while preserving the most recognizable brand signals where appropriate.
  4. Test concepts in real contexts early, especially if the work is solving practical issues like scaling, legibility, or avatar use.
  5. Narrow progressively from broad stylistic direction to technical refinements like weight, spacing, and detail.
  6. If needed, revisit earlier directions after learning from later explorations.
- **Example**: In refreshing Lenny's brand, Jessica kept the recognizable handwritten and campfire-adjacent feel, preserved similar color cues, and explored multiple typography directions before ultimately returning to a simpler fire-and-marshmallow concept that worked better across podcast and newsletter use cases.

### Big Picture Before Minutiae
- **When to use**: When you know a logo or brand feels off but can't yet articulate why.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with the broad question: what overall feels wrong or misaligned?
  2. Describe the vibe-level problem before naming technical details.
  3. Only after identifying the big-picture issue, move into specific letterform or layout problems.
  4. Use the broad diagnosis to guide which details actually matter.
- **Example**: Jessica advises clients not to begin with complaints like a specific letter bothering them, but to first identify the larger issue—such as the brand feeling inconsistent, immature, or mismatched to the product.

### Typography Feeling Audit
- **When to use**: When non-designers want to build intuition for how typography affects perception, or when choosing visual direction for a brand.
- **Steps**:
  1. Browse a large set of fonts from a font folder or marketplace.
  2. For each font, quickly write down the feeling it evokes without overanalyzing.
  3. Group fonts by emotional response such as calm, aggressive, feminine, vintage, or exciting.
  4. Look for shared traits across each group, such as weight, spacing, softness, width, or edge treatment.
  5. Use those patterns to inform brand choices and avoid accidental associations.
- **Example**: Jessica suggests screenshotting fonts from places like MyFonts, categorizing them by feeling, and then noticing patterns such as lighter weight and generous spacing making fonts feel calm.

### Song Exploder Your Intuition
- **When to use**: When you have a strong gut reaction to a design but don't yet understand the source of that reaction.
- **Steps**:
  1. Notice your immediate emotional response to a design.
  2. Ask yourself why it feels that way.
  3. Trace the feeling back to memories, contexts, or visual references it may resemble.
  4. Use that reverse-justification to make more intentional design decisions.
- **Example**: Jessica describes seeing a type treatment and realizing it may feel a certain way because it resembles a band flyer from your early twenties, which can reveal why it feels edgy, nostalgic, or specific to a subculture.

### Competitive Visual Landscape Analysis
- **When to use**: When creating or refreshing a brand in a crowded category and deciding whether to fit in or stand out.
- **Steps**:
  1. Collect examples of competitor brands in the category.
  2. Identify the common visual patterns, colors, and typography styles.
  3. Decide whether your strategy is to signal category membership or to differentiate from category norms.
  4. Choose visual elements accordingly.
- **Example**: Jessica notes that many fintech brands use green, so using those cues can help people instantly recognize you as fintech, while avoiding them can signal that you're intentionally different from the status quo.

### Design for Easy Use, Not Brand-Book Dependency
- **When to use**: When building brand assets for small or growing teams that may not have expert designers or a large internal brand team.
- **Steps**:
  1. Create assets that are intuitive to use across common contexts.
  2. Limit unnecessary complexity in the logo and system.
  3. Provide a flexible but coherent visual vocabulary rather than one rigid asset.
  4. Ensure the assets themselves suggest correct usage without requiring extensive documentation.
- **Example**: For Lenny's brand, Jessica created multiple compatible variations and a broader visual vocabulary because the team is small and can use assets playfully without needing a 500-page brand book.

## Core Advice
- **When you’re deciding whether to refresh a logo or brand**: do it when you’re about to expand usage significantly — because new websites, product rollouts, swag, events, and physical investments expose weaknesses in an early identity.
- **When you’re early in a startup**: keep the initial brand lightweight — because the company may pivot, and heavy upfront brand work can become wasted effort.
- **When a logo feels wrong but you can’t explain why**: diagnose the big-picture vibe first — because the real issue is often tone, maturity, or cohesion, not a single letterform.
- **When choosing typography**: run a feeling audit — because non-designers can build real intuition by connecting fonts to emotions and then to visual traits.
- **When you want a logo to be defensible**: use more custom typography — because generic fonts are easier to imitate once a brand becomes successful.
- **When you’re in a crowded category**: audit competitors and decide whether to match or reject category cues — because looking familiar can aid recognition, while divergence can signal differentiation.
- **When stakeholders want to see a “wrong” option**: show it if the cost is low — because people often need to experience the bad path to confidently choose the right one.
- **When building a brand system**: create a set of compatible assets, not just a hero mark — because real brands need to work across avatars, headlines, subheads, and many contexts.
- **When you’re burned out on one creative task**: switch projects instead of fully stopping — because variety can restore momentum without losing productivity.
- **When using AI as a creative professional**: use it for brainstorming and early iteration, but keep the craft work you love — because some manual execution is part of the creative reward, not just labor.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Brand is everything, and startups should invest heavily in branding from the beginning. → **Their view**: Early-stage companies often should not spend heavily on brand upfront — because product direction and team evolution matter more, and pivots can make early brand work obsolete.
- **Conventional**: A logo is the central driver of company culture and success. → **Their view**: The product and the people drive the company — because the logo supports expectations, but cannot substitute for substance.
- **Conventional**: Designers should tightly control process and avoid sharing in-progress files. → **Their view**: Share early when it helps solve practical problems — because real-context testing reveals scaling and legibility issues sooner.
- **Conventional**: Clients should not mix concepts from different directions. → **Their view**: Frankensteining can be valid — because the right answer often emerges from combining pieces, if the system is designed well.
- **Conventional**: AI should automate the low-level execution so humans can stay conceptual. → **Their view**: Some low-level craft is the most fulfilling part — because detailed making can be therapeutic and creatively essential.
- **Conventional**: Strong brands need extensive documentation and strict rules. → **Their view**: A good brand should be intuitive — because if it needs a huge brand book to function, it may be too complicated.
