# Ken Norton

**Executive coach for product leaders; former product leader at Google** | **Independent / brings the donuts** | Expertise: product leadership, executive coaching, leadership development, organizational psychology

## Bio
Ken Norton spent 14 years at Google working on products like Google Docs, Calendar, and Maps, and later at Google Ventures. He now coaches senior product leaders, helping them grow beyond tactics into more creative, values-aligned leadership.

His perspective matters because he focuses on the inner game of leadership: mindset, identity, values, and self-awareness. He’s especially useful when someone is technically strong but stuck, burned out, or struggling to scale into a bigger role.

## Signature Frameworks

### Creative vs. Reactive Leadership
- **When to use**: When a leader feels stuck, burned out, overly defensive, or unable to scale into a more senior role.
- **Steps**:
  1. Notice whether you are operating from fear/anxiety or from openness/curiosity.
  2. Identify the reactive posture showing up: people-pleasing, needing to be right, or controlling.
  3. Interrogate the underlying beliefs and assumptions driving that posture.
  4. Redefine leadership in a way that fits your authentic strengths and values.
  5. Practice responding from purpose, vision, and growth rather than protection and defense.
- **Example**: Ken shifted from trying to be liked in every room to aiming for long-term respect: “I want to be the type of leader where, a decade later, people say, ‘I would work with that guy again in a heartbeat.’”

### Internal Operating System Reboot
- **When to use**: When the job has become more complex than the mindset that got you there, especially during promotions or role changes.
- **Steps**:
  1. Recognize that the external world has changed and your old mental model no longer fits.
  2. Accept that the challenge is not just skill acquisition but self-complexity and meaning-making.
  3. Rebuild how you interpret events, expectations, and feedback.
  4. Use new internal narratives to adapt to the demands of the role.
  5. Test the new operating model through action and feedback.
- **Example**: Ken compared career growth to learning to drive: the car is the same, but once you're behind the wheel, you need a completely different internal operating system to thrive.

### Values-First Career Navigation
- **When to use**: When career decisions start feeling externally driven, confusing, or misaligned.
- **Steps**:
  1. Ask what you actually want, not just what title or milestone you think you should want.
  2. Keep asking why until you reach a real value or need.
  3. Translate vague goals into deeper motivations like creativity, connection, or challenge.
  4. Use those values to evaluate roles, environments, and next steps.
  5. Choose the place where your authentic way of leading can work.
- **Example**: A client who said they wanted to become VP eventually realized the real desire was more creativity and more ability to challenge others.

### Parts Work / Inner Critic Management
- **When to use**: When imposter syndrome, self-doubt, or harsh inner criticism is blocking action.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the voice or part that is speaking.
  2. Separate that part from your core self.
  3. Understand the part's protective intention.
  4. Give it a name, role, or image to create distance.
  5. Reassign it or ask it to step aside while you lead.
- **Example**: Ken described naming an inner critic “Larry Loser” and telling it, “I’ve heard from Larry. I’m going to ask Larry to step aside. Let’s go.”

### 10X Portfolio Thinking
- **When to use**: When deciding how much to invest in breakthrough innovation versus incremental improvement.
- **Steps**:
  1. Create explicit space for big, risky bets.
  2. Accept that many bold ideas will fail.
  3. Avoid over-optimizing only for safe 10% gains.
  4. Build a portfolio across core, adjacent, and moonshot work.
  5. Scale the mix to the company, team, or function level.
- **Example**: Ken referenced Google's 70-20-10 approach: 70% core business, 20% adjacent, 10% crazy bets.

### Hiring for Product Leadership Fit
- **When to use**: When hiring a product manager or evaluating whether a candidate can actually succeed in your environment.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define what the job really is before writing the job description.
  2. Assess intangibles like collaboration, influence, and leadership presence.
  3. Check whether the candidate can work with engineers and designers and inspire them.
  4. Make sure interviewer and candidate agree on what the role entails.
  5. Use the interview to test real-world fit, not just structured-question performance.
- **Example**: Ken recommends asking how the company defines a product team, because it reveals culture, collaboration, and decision-making norms.

## Core Advice
- **When you feel like you need everyone to like you in order to succeed**: Shift from seeking immediate approval to earning long-term respect through purpose, decisiveness, and consistency — because people-pleasing can work early on, but as responsibility grows it often causes loss of power, weak execution, and indecision.
- **When you notice fear, anxiety, defensiveness, or constant threat-scanning**: Treat that as a signal you are in a reactive posture and pause to re-center on curiosity and possibility — because fear is the clearest marker of reactive leadership, which is correlated with worse outcomes.
- **When your career milestone no longer feels satisfying or the next promotion feels hollow**: Ask what deeper value you are actually chasing and let that guide your next move — because external goals often mask internal needs like creativity, challenge, connection, or purpose.
- **When you keep getting advice that doesn’t seem to change anything**: Stop relying on advice alone and do the internal work of understanding your beliefs, values, and patterns — because advice can create a short-lived sugar high but rarely addresses the real constraint.
- **When you are promoted into a role that feels more complex than before**: Assume the issue is not just skill gaps; update your internal operating system for the new level of complexity — because the world may not have changed as much as your position in it, but your meaning-making must change.
- **When you are tempted to copy a dominant leadership archetype that feels inauthentic**: Redefine leadership around your authentic strengths instead of forcing yourself into someone else’s mold — because there are many effective ways to lead, and imitation can create self-doubt and poor fit.
- **When imposter syndrome shows up before a big meeting or new challenge**: Name the inner critic, separate it from yourself, and let it speak without letting it drive — because self-distancing reduces the power of the critic and helps you regain agency.
- **When you are trying to grow as a product leader**: Invest in communication, storytelling, difficult conversations, and collaboration with the same seriousness as technical/product tactics — because senior product work is mostly people work, and soft skills become the hard skills at higher levels.
- **When you are deciding whether to pursue coaching**: Choose coaching if you want help discovering your own answers; choose mentoring if you want concrete advice or a tutorial — because coaching is about self-discovery and internal change, while mentoring is more directive and tactical.
- **When you are evaluating a coach**: Do a free session, assess fit and trust, and ask what coaching means to them and how structured their approach is — because coaching is highly style-dependent, and the relationship only works when there is genuine fit.
- **When you are hiring or interviewing for product roles**: Ask how the company defines a product team and how a recent feature actually got shipped — because those questions reveal culture, empowerment, decision-making, and whether product is truly strategic or just executional.
- **When you are choosing between safe incremental work and bold innovation**: Create explicit room for moonshots and accept that failure is part of the path to breakthrough — because big innovations require environments where people can take risks without defaulting to safe small-ball improvements.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Great leaders should be confident, commanding, and able to tell people what to do. → **Their view**: Effective leadership can be empathetic, collaborative, and non-dominating; the key is not copying a single archetype — because leadership should fit the person and the context.
- **Conventional**: Advice from experienced people is the fastest way to solve career problems. → **Their view**: Advice is often low-nutrition and short-lived; real growth comes from helping people discover their own path — because advice rarely confronts the underlying issue.
- **Conventional**: Product management is mostly about product, strategy, and tactics. → **Their view**: At senior levels, product leadership is mostly about people: influence, alignment, difficult conversations, and creating conditions for collaboration — because leadership without formal authority is fundamentally relational.
- **Conventional**: Soft skills are secondary and can be learned later. → **Their view**: Communication, storytelling, and collaboration are core leadership skills and deserve deliberate training — because these become the hardest and most important skills as you advance.
- **Conventional**: Imposter syndrome is purely an internal issue to fix personally. → **Their view**: For many people, especially women and people of color, it is reinforced by real systemic bias and environmental signals — because leaders must address both inner and outer causes.
- **Conventional**: A coach should be someone who has done your exact job. → **Their view**: A coach does not need direct domain experience; sometimes not having done the job prevents them from slipping into advice-giving — because coaching is about helping the client think, not providing answers.
