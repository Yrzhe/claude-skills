# David Placek

**Founder** | **Lexicon Branding** | Expertise: positioning, go-to-market, branding, design

## Bio
David Placek is the founder of Lexicon Branding, one of the most influential naming firms in tech and consumer products. He has helped create names like Azure, Vercel, Windsurf, Sonos, Blackberry, Pentium, Swiffer, and Impossible Burger, and he’s known for treating naming as a strategic discipline rather than a subjective creative exercise.

His perspective matters because he connects branding, linguistics, cognitive science, and trademark realities into a repeatable process. If you need a name that does more than describe—one that shapes perception, creates advantage, and can scale with the business—his approach is especially valuable.

## Signature Frameworks

### Identify → Invent → Implement
- **When to use**: When creating or renaming a company, product, platform, or major feature and you want strategic advantage, not just a label.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify how the company behaves now and wants to behave in the future.
  2. Map the competitive landscape and language patterns.
  3. Create a broad creative framework, not a narrow brief.
  4. Invent with small teams, linguistic tools, and high-volume ideation.
  5. Screen for trademark, cultural, linguistic, and strategic fit.
  6. Implement by building rationale, prototypes, and internal champions.
- **Example**: For Microsoft Azure, Lexicon moved beyond a literal cloud descriptor and framed the name as a story-starting asset with sky/cloud associations.

### Behavior and Experience First
- **When to use**: When teams are stuck treating names as descriptors instead of strategic assets.
- **Steps**:
  1. Ask how the brand behaves now.
  2. Ask how it wants to behave in the future.
  3. Ask how the market should behave toward it.
  4. Translate that into the experience the name should create.
  5. Use that experience to guide sound, rhythm, imagery, and metaphor.
- **Example**: Sonos was approached as a company enabling the flow of sound, which led to a name rooted in sound rather than entertainment clichés.

### Three-Team Divergent Briefing Method
- **When to use**: When naming intangible or technical products that tend to produce generic names.
- **Steps**:
  1. Form 2–3 small teams.
  2. Give one team the real brief.
  3. Give another an adjacent disguised brief.
  4. Give a third a distant analog brief.
  5. Encourage mistakes and exploration.
  6. Compare outputs for surprising, stronger directions.
- **Example**: For Windsurf, metaphorical exploration around flow and movement surfaced a name that felt richer than direct coding language.

### Naming Diamond Exercise
- **When to use**: When a startup needs alignment fast and has limited time or budget.
- **Steps**:
  1. Draw a diamond.
  2. Top: How do we define winning?
  3. Right: What do we have to win?
  4. Bottom: What do we need to win?
  5. Left: What do we need to say to win?
  6. Generate naming directions from those answers.
- **Example**: A founder uses the exercise to stop chasing “just a name” and instead define the strategic experience the name must carry.

### Suspend Judgment, Speculate Instead
- **When to use**: When candidate names are getting killed too early because they feel unfamiliar.
- **Steps**:
  1. Generate far more options than seems necessary.
  2. Do not evaluate during generation.
  3. Narrow to a smaller set.
  4. Ask what each name could do for the brand.
  5. Prototype names in context.
  6. Look for energy and polarization.
- **Example**: Windsurf was evaluated for the world it could create—flow, motion, imagery—not for whether it felt instantly familiar.

### Competitor Launch Test
- **When to use**: When you want lightweight external feedback without asking people to judge your own name.
- **Steps**:
  1. Present the name as if a competitor launched with it.
  2. Ask what people think.
  3. Listen for expectations, imagery, and emotion.
  4. Use that reaction to assess intrigue and distinctiveness.
- **Example**: Asking how someone would react if a competitor launched as Windsurf reveals what the name does in the mind.

## Core Advice
- **When you feel pressure to choose a descriptive name**: choose a distinctive, story-starting name instead — because descriptors blend into the category and lose long-term advantage.
- **When a name feels uncomfortable at first**: don’t reject it too quickly — because strong names often need reflection before their value becomes clear.
- **When naming a technical or intangible product**: translate it into a tangible experience or metaphor first — because abstraction produces sterile names.
- **When you’re tempted to run a big brainstorm**: use small teams with different briefs — because focused divergence produces better names.
- **When time and budget are tight**: use the Naming Diamond — because it aligns the team on strategy before word-hunting.
- **When a candidate name is polarizing**: investigate it instead of smoothing it away — because tension can signal energy and distinctiveness.
- **When the .com is missing**: choose the right name first — because the domain is less important than the strategic value of the name.
- **When executives need to approve a bold name**: prototype it in real contexts — because people approve what they can imagine working in the world.
- **When a startup name was chosen as a placeholder**: revisit it later — because early tactical names often become strategic liabilities.
- **When you’re creatively blocked**: explore unrelated domains and collect resonant words — because unexpected combinations unlock better associations.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: You’ll know the right name when you see it. → **Their view**: You usually won’t; strong names often feel unfamiliar at first — because people confuse comfort with quality.
- **Conventional**: Descriptive names are best because they explain the product. → **Their view**: Descriptive names are weaker — because they disappear into the category instead of creating advantage.
- **Conventional**: Big brainstorming sessions are ideal. → **Their view**: Small teams with different briefs work better — because they create more freedom and better divergence.
- **Conventional**: A good name should make everyone comfortable. → **Their view**: Comfort is a bad sign — because the market rewards distinctiveness, not consensus.
- **Conventional**: Research should ask whether people like the name. → **Their view**: Research should test what the name makes people imagine — because imagination is what drives market response.
- **Conventional**: The .com is a prerequisite. → **Their view**: The right name matters more than the .com — because domains are now more like area codes.
- **Conventional**: Naming should start from mission and values. → **Their view**: Start with behavior and experience — because they produce more useful naming directions.
