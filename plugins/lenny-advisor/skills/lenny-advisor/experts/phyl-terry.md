# Phyl Terry

**Author, founder of Never Search Alone and creator of Job Search Councils** | **phyl.org / Never Search Alone** | Expertise: job search, career strategy, product leadership, community-building

## Bio
Phyl Terry is best known for turning job searching into a structured, community-driven process rather than a solitary grind. As the author of *Never Search Alone* and creator of Job Search Councils, he brings a product mindset to career transitions: define the problem, test the market, refine the positioning, and negotiate for the conditions needed to succeed.

His perspective matters because he has spent decades helping people navigate layoffs, interviews, and offers with more clarity and less panic. He is especially useful when someone needs to reframe a job search as a marketplace problem, not a personal failure.

## Signature Frameworks

### Job Search Council
- **When to use**: When you are searching for a job and want accountability, emotional support, and structured feedback instead of doing it alone.
- **Steps**:
  1. Apply and get matched into a council of 6-8 job seekers.
  2. Separate fast seekers (out of work) from slow seekers (currently employed) because they need different cadences.
  3. Assign a moderator, ideally a job seeker who volunteers, to guide the group.
  4. Hold a “meeting zero” to share life stories and build trust.
  5. Meet regularly: typically twice a week for fast seekers and every two weeks for slow seekers.
  6. Use the council to work through candidate market fit, listening tours, interview prep, and negotiation.
  7. Stay in the council until you land a job.
- **Example**: A senior product leader joined a council and was surprised by the openness and support; the group helped her stay accountable when emotional energy was low.

### Candidate Market Fit
- **When to use**: When you need to figure out what roles you should target in the current market, especially in a down market or after a layoff.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start by writing down what you want and what you do not want.
  2. Share the draft with your council for feedback and refinement.
  3. Do a listening tour with trusted people, former colleagues, and recruiters.
  4. Ask the golden question: “If you were in my shoes, how would you approach this?”
  5. Synthesize feedback into a narrow, one-sentence candidate market fit statement.
  6. Post or share the statement publicly and with your network.
  7. Iterate as market conditions change.
- **Example**: A designer realized her fit was companies that either lacked a design team or needed to reboot one, which made her memorable to the market.

### Listening Tour
- **When to use**: When you need market feedback on your target role, positioning, or whether your goals are realistic.
- **Steps**:
  1. Create a draft of your goals, preferences, and dealbreakers.
  2. Talk to former colleagues using reverse exit interviews.
  3. Talk to your broader network using the golden question.
  4. Talk to recruiters as advisors, not as job dispensers.
  5. Capture what the market wants and where you are a fit.
  6. Use the feedback to refine your candidate market fit and pitch.
- **Example**: A former executive learned from recruiters that he needed to be much more specific about the role he wanted; that specificity changed his search.

### Job Mission with OKRs
- **When to use**: When you start interviewing and want to improve your odds of getting the offer and setting yourself up to succeed once hired.
- **Steps**:
  1. Privately draft your own version of the job description.
  2. Turn it into a job mission with objectives and key results.
  3. Use the draft to ask better interview questions and clarify the role.
  4. After a few interviews, share the draft with the hiring manager in a live conversation.
  5. Align on what success will actually require, including resources, budget, team support, or training.
  6. Use the agreed-upon OKRs as the basis for negotiation.
- **Example**: A CPO interviewing at a private-equity-owned SaaS company asked for a $10 million check on day one to address tech debt; the company agreed and later promoted them.

### Four-Legged Negotiation
- **When to use**: When you have an offer and want to negotiate without being adversarial.
- **Steps**:
  1. First discuss what you need to succeed in the role, tied to the OKRs.
  2. Talk about resources such as tech debt, headcount, training, or support.
  3. Then discuss compensation.
  4. Ask in a collaborative way: “Are you open to X?” rather than making demands.
  5. Prefer live conversation with the hiring manager over email.
- **Example**: A candidate asked whether the company would fund the work needed to remove tech debt before discussing salary, which helped them secure a better outcome.

### Gratitude House and Interview Debrief
- **When to use**: When you need confidence before an interview and clarity immediately after one.
- **Steps**:
  1. Before interviewing, list the people who helped you get where you are.
  2. Mentally bring that support network with you into the interview.
  3. After the interview, debrief quickly with someone who can help interpret what happened.
  4. Separate real feedback from inner-critic distortions.
- **Example**: A director of product thought she had bombed an interview, but a debrief revealed she had actually done well and was moving to the next round.

## Core Advice
- **When you face a layoff or job loss and feel tempted to apply everywhere**: do not spray and pray; first define what you want and what you do not want — because a narrow target improves focus, reduces anxiety, and helps you search like a product person rather than reacting blindly.
- **When you are searching in a tough market**: treat your job search like a marketplace problem, not a personal failure — because candidate market fit is shaped by supply and demand, so role level and scope may need to shift with the market.
- **When you are emotionally drained during a search**: prioritize emotional balance as much as resume, networking, or LinkedIn — because fear and demoralization can derail the search.
- **When you are trying to figure out your target role**: write a one-page draft of your preferences, dealbreakers, and goals, then refine it with others — because most people do not clearly articulate what they want.
- **When you are unsure how the market sees you**: do a listening tour with former colleagues, your broader network, and recruiters — because you need external market feedback to calibrate your fit.
- **When you ask for advice from your network**: ask, “If you were in my shoes, how would you approach this?” — because this golden question invites thoughtful, useful feedback.
- **When you are defining your target search**: make it narrow enough to be memorable, usually 3-4 attributes in one sentence — because a spear beats a net.
- **When you are worried that narrowing your search will reduce opportunities**: be narrow in your statement but expansive in how others interpret it — because specificity helps people remember you while still allowing adjacent opportunities.
- **When you are interviewing for a senior role**: create a private job mission with OKRs before the interviews progress too far — because it helps you ask better questions and show initiative.
- **When you are negotiating an offer**: lead with what you need to succeed, then discuss money — because hiring managers respond well when you show you are thinking about outcomes.
- **When you need to ask for more compensation**: ask directly but politely, “Are you open to X?” — because a low-friction ask is easier to answer.
- **When you are building relationships with recruiters**: pick up recruiter calls even if you do not want the job and help them when you can — because recruiter relationships compound over time.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Job searching is something you should do independently and privately. → **Their view**: You should search with a Job Search Council and not alone. — because community turns anxiety into hope, accountability, and confidence.
- **Conventional**: The best job search strategy is to apply broadly and maximize volume. → **Their view**: You need a spear, not a net: narrow your target dramatically. — because specificity makes you memorable and aligns you with actual market demand.
- **Conventional**: Negotiation is about pushing hard for the highest salary possible. → **Their view**: Negotiation should start with what you need to succeed in the role, not with money. — because companies respond positively when you focus on value creation.
- **Conventional**: Asking for help is a sign of weakness or dependence. → **Their view**: Asking for help is a sign of confidence and a way to become more independent. — because it builds reputation, trust, and capability.
- **Conventional**: A senior candidate should emphasize title, scope, and ambition. → **Their view**: Sometimes the right move is to step down to an IC role or take a two-step path to the role you ultimately want. — because the market may value you differently today.
- **Conventional**: A job search is mostly about skills, resume quality, and networking volume. → **Their view**: The most important thing to manage is emotional balance. — because fear, shame, and demoralization can distort judgment.
