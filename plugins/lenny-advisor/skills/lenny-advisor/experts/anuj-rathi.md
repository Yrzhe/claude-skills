**Chief Product and Marketing Officer** | **Jupiter Money** | Expertise: product-management, strategy, design, go-to-market, marketplaces, leadership

## Bio
Anuj Rathi is a veteran consumer product leader in India, known for shaping products at scale across Flipkart, Swiggy, and Jupiter Money. He brings a rare combination of product, marketing, and marketplace thinking, with a strong bias toward practical frameworks that help teams make better decisions, align cross-functionally, and design experiences that actually change user behavior.

His perspective matters most when a company is dealing with complex consumer journeys, multi-sided marketplaces, or strategic ambiguity. He is especially useful for leaders who need to connect product decisions to business outcomes, launch execution, and organizational design.

## Signature Frameworks

### Lazy, Vain, Selfish User Lens
- **When to use**: When designing onboarding, activation, positioning, or any experience for new or marginal users.
- **Steps**:
  1. Assume users are lazy: they will not invest effort unless the value is obvious.
  2. Assume users are vain: they resist changing habits.
  3. Assume users are selfish: they want immediate personal value.
  4. Design acquisition, messaging, onboarding, and first-use experience around these realities.
  5. Keep the marketing promise continuous with the in-product experience.
- **Example**: At Swiggy, onboarding was reframed around the user’s immediate trigger and value, with marketing messages flowing directly into the app experience.

### Working Backwards via PR/FAQ as Full-Stack Launch Planning
- **When to use**: When evaluating a new product, feature, launch, or cross-functional initiative.
- **Steps**:
  1. Write a press release for the future launch.
  2. Add FAQs to surface operational, legal, compliance, pricing, and business implications.
  3. Use the document to negotiate feasibility with engineering and other stakeholders.
  4. Test whether the story is credible using customer and business quotes.
  5. Adjust scope, date, and goals until the whole system is coherent.
- **Example**: At Jupiter, FAQs are used to force explicit thinking about compliance and legal sign-off before launch.

### Three Alternative PR/FAQs
- **When to use**: When choosing among strategic options and avoiding premature convergence.
- **Steps**:
  1. Create three fully thought-through PR/FAQs for the same opportunity.
  2. Make the options meaningfully different.
  3. Compare them with leadership and stakeholders.
  4. Recommend one explicitly.
  5. Borrow useful elements from rejected options if needed.
- **Example**: For a membership program, compare two tiers, three tiers, and a points-based pricing model before choosing one.

### Show, Don’t Tell
- **When to use**: When ideating, reviewing product experiences, or aligning stakeholders.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with a specific person, not a generic persona.
  2. Reconstruct the exact context and trigger.
  3. Walk through the journey screen by screen and line by line.
  4. Map emotional states and edge cases.
  5. For marketplaces, map what happens on the other side too.
- **Example**: At Swiggy, this meant mapping the 30 minutes after an order is placed, including delays and delivery issues.

### Strategy on a Page / Growth Loop Visualization
- **When to use**: When product leaders need company-wide alignment on strategy and tradeoffs.
- **Steps**:
  1. Map market, target users, acquisition, activation, retention, and cross-sell loops on one page.
  2. Show how levers interact across the business.
  3. Create multiple strategic versions.
  4. Use the visual to align CXOs and teams.
  5. Clarify each team’s role in the system.
- **Example**: A one-page strategy can show how acquisition feeds loyalty, cross-sell, and membership economics.

### 4 BB Framework
- **When to use**: When allocating product and engineering capacity.
- **Steps**:
  1. **Brilliant Basics**: invest in foundations and technical hygiene.
  2. **Bread and Butter**: improve the current product through backlog work and iterations.
  3. **Big Bets**: fund large cross-functional initiatives.
  4. **Breaking Bad**: invest in pivots or category shifts.
  5. Decide allocation at the leadership level.
- **Example**: Expanding from food delivery into grocery or broader convenience is a “Breaking Bad” move, not a normal roadmap item.

### PM Skill Triad
- **When to use**: When evaluating PM fit or development areas.
- **Steps**:
  1. Assess raw sharpness.
  2. Assess drive and grit.
  3. Assess influence.
  4. Check whether the person is energized by building these skills.
- **Example**: A strong PM is not just analytical; they are motivated by persuasion and cross-functional influence.

### Can’t Do / Won’t Do / Not Set Up to Do
- **When to use**: When diagnosing team underperformance.
- **Steps**:
  1. Ask if the person can’t do it.
  2. Ask if they won’t do it.
  3. Ask if they were not set up to do it.
  4. Apply the right fix: coaching, alignment, or system redesign.
- **Example**: A missed goal may be a setup issue caused by weak OKRs or poor org design, not a people problem.

## Core Advice
- **When onboarding is weak**: use the lazy, vain, selfish lens — because new users need immediate value and low friction.
- **When a launch has hidden dependencies**: write a PR/FAQ — because it exposes feasibility, compliance, and business tradeoffs early.
- **When strategy feels fuzzy**: create three divergent alternatives — because comparing concrete options improves judgment.
- **When teams debate abstractly**: show the exact user journey — because specificity reveals assumptions and edge cases.
- **When choosing between speed and quality**: favor excellence over speed — because many weak ideas should remain thought experiments.
- **When working as a PM**: think full-stack — because product success depends on influence across users, engineering, marketing, and business.
- **When building for marketplaces**: model every side explicitly — because optimizing one participant can break the system.
- **When execution fails repeatedly**: diagnose can’t do / won’t do / not set up to do — because most failures are system failures.
- **When exploring AI**: pair AI with HI — because consumer value comes from the human experience around the model.
- **When something goes wrong**: postmortem yourself first — because internal accountability improves learning and performance.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Ship fast and learn from the market → **Their view**: Prefer excellence over speed when forced to choose — because many experiments are obviously weak and waste organizational energy.
- **Conventional**: Most ambitious people can become PMs → **Their view**: Many people should not be PMs unless they fit the role — because PM requires sharpness, grit, and influence.
- **Conventional**: PMs should mainly own features and UX → **Their view**: PMs must be full-stack — because outcomes depend on the whole system.
- **Conventional**: OKRs work well in most orgs → **Their view**: OKRs often fail in marketplaces — because multi-sided goals conflict and interact nonlinearly.
- **Conventional**: AI should lead product strategy → **Their view**: AI must be paired with HI — because technology alone does not create consumer value.
