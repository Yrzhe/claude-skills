# Brian Balfour

**Founder and CEO** | **Reforge** | Expertise: growth, go-to-market, product strategy, ai-strategy

## Bio
Brian Balfour is the founder and CEO of Reforge and previously led growth at HubSpot. He is known for his systems-level thinking about growth, distribution, and platform shifts, especially how startups can use new channels before incumbents fully react.

His perspective matters because he focuses less on tactics and more on the underlying mechanics of why markets change, why platforms open and close, and how companies can build durable advantage during those transitions.

## Signature Frameworks

### New Distribution Platform Four-Step Cycle
- **When to use**: Use when evaluating whether a new platform or ecosystem could become a major growth channel and when to enter.
- **Steps**:
  1. Confirm the market conditions: broad consensus around a new category, no clear winner yet, and intense competition.
  2. Identify the moat the platform winner is pursuing: network effects, data, context, or memory.
  3. Watch for the platform to open to third parties with incentives like distribution in exchange for use cases and engagement.
  4. Anticipate the platform closing: reduced organic reach, first-party product absorption, or higher monetization tolls.
- **Example**: Facebook opened to developers with feed and notification distribution, then later reduced organic access, increased monetization pressure, and absorbed major use cases like photos and events.

### Platform Betting Strategy
- **When to use**: Use when deciding how to allocate resources across emerging platforms, especially during a technology shift like AI.
- **Steps**:
  1. Decide whether you can place multiple bets or need one focused bet.
  2. If you are late-stage, spread bets across multiple plausible platforms and wait for clearer signals.
  3. If you are early-stage, choose one platform and go all in.
  4. Reassess continuously whether your chosen platform is gaining escape velocity.
- **Example**: A late-stage company can hedge across ChatGPT, Gemini, or future platforms, while a startup should pick one and commit, similar to choosing iOS early.

### How to Evaluate an Emerging Platform
- **When to use**: Use when choosing which new platform to build on or integrate with.
- **Steps**:
  1. Prioritize retention and depth of engagement over vanity metrics like raw MAU.
  2. Assess user quality and monetization potential.
  3. Analyze the value exchange: distribution, context, memory, or access.
  4. Consider pure scale and momentum last.
  5. Once you enter, immediately plan your exit strategy.
- **Example**: iOS had stronger monetization than Android, so betting only on Android often lost even if Android had more devices.

### Enter-and-Exit Platform Strategy
- **When to use**: Use when building on a platform that may eventually commoditize or constrain you.
- **Steps**:
  1. Enter early enough to benefit from the open phase.
  2. Use the platform to gain users and momentum quickly.
  3. Build defensibility outside the platform at the same time.
  4. Create moats through workflow ownership, specialized data, unique context, or micro-network effects.
  5. Reduce dependence before the platform closes.
- **Example**: Companies integrating with ChatGPT should benefit from the open phase now, but still own a durable part of the user experience before control tightens.

### AI Adoption Transformation Model
- **When to use**: Use when trying to drive real AI adoption inside a company rather than issuing top-down memos.
- **Steps**:
  1. Set hard constraints that force behavior change, such as headcount limits or proof AI cannot solve a problem before hiring.
  2. Create explicit ownership, incentives, rewards, and operational support.
  3. Recognize three groups: catalysts, converts, and anchors.
  4. Support catalysts, structure converts, and set deadlines for anchors.
  5. Measure actual usage on the ground.
  6. Identify the slowest part of the system and attack that bottleneck.
- **Example**: One company required proof that AI could not do the work before approving new headcount; another benchmarked teams to force smaller orgs and drive adoption.

## Core Advice
- **When facing an emerging platform shift like AI agents or ChatGPT integrations**: do not opt out—make a deliberate bet early — because competitors will adopt, customer expectations will shift, and you lose early distribution if you wait.
- **When deciding whether to spread resources across multiple new platforms**: only do multiple bets if you are late-stage; if you are a startup, choose one and go all in — because scarce resources get diluted fast.
- **When uncertain which platform will win**: evaluate retention and engagement depth first, then monetization quality, then value exchange, then scale — because early winners are usually not the biggest, but the stickiest.
- **When pressured to integrate with a platform that may commoditize you**: integrate early anyway, but build your exit strategy immediately — because the upside is in the open phase, while survival depends on your own moat.
- **When AI adoption is weak despite executive enthusiasm**: create hard constraints like hiring gates or AI-required prototypes — because soft encouragement rarely changes behavior.
- **When AI adoption varies across teams**: segment people into catalysts, converts, and anchors — because different groups need different management approaches.
- **When executives overestimate AI usage**: measure real ground-level behavior and talk to practitioners — because leadership narratives often diverge from actual adoption.
- **When AI speeds up one function but product output still lags**: find the slowest part of the system and fix that bottleneck — because output is constrained by the weakest link, not the fastest one.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: If a platform will eventually tax, constrain, or commoditize you, avoid building on it. → **Their view**: Participate early and benefit from the open phase while planning your exit — because you usually cannot opt out, and competitors will move first.
- **Conventional**: The best product wins. → **Their view**: Great product is necessary, but distribution is the separator — because startups win by getting distribution before incumbents can copy them.
- **Conventional**: The platform with the biggest current distribution will win. → **Their view**: Retention and engagement are better predictors — because stickiness matters more than raw size.
- **Conventional**: AI adoption is mostly about buying tools and telling teams to use them. → **Their view**: Real transformation requires hard constraints and organizational change — because behavior only shifts when the system forces it.
- **Conventional**: If AI speeds up engineering, product output will naturally rise. → **Their view**: Output may not improve unless the whole system changes — because the bottleneck often moves to PM, design, legal, or operations.
