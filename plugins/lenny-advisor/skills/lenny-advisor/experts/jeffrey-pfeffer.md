# Jeffrey Pfeffer

**Professor of Organizational Behavior at Stanford Graduate School of Business** | **Stanford Graduate School of Business** | Expertise: leadership, organizational behavior, power and influence, career strategy

## Bio
Jeffrey Pfeffer is a longtime Stanford GSB professor known for teaching the highly sought-after *Paths to Power* course and for writing *Seven Rules of Power*. His perspective matters because he translates research on organizational behavior, political skill, and influence into practical tactics for career advancement and getting things done.

He is especially useful when you need a blunt, reality-based view of how organizations actually work, not how people wish they worked. Pfeffer focuses on the gap between merit and reward, and on the skills required to build influence, visibility, and authority.

## Signature Frameworks

### The Seven Rules of Power
- **When to use**: When you want to increase your influence, advance your career, and become more effective at getting things done in organizations.
- **Steps**:
  1. Get out of your own way by dropping beliefs that power is dirty or that you do not deserve your role.
  2. Break the rules to stand out, disrupt expectations, and avoid being constrained by norms built by incumbents.
  3. Appear powerful through body language, voice, presence, and command of material.
  4. Build a powerful brand so people know who you are and what distinctive value you bring.
  5. Network relentlessly by meeting people, helping them, and becoming a connector.
  6. Use your power to make things happen, because success attracts more resources and support.
  7. Understand that success excuses almost everything, and that many people will forgive or forget how power was acquired.
- **Example**: Pfeffer points to Laura Chau for deliberate brand-building, Omid Kordestani for networking, and Donald Trump as someone who intuitively embodies many of the rules.

### Doing Power
- **When to use**: When learning about influence or power and wanting the lessons to stick through practice rather than theory alone.
- **Steps**:
  1. Choose a concrete power-related goal to pursue during a defined period.
  2. Apply class principles in the real world rather than just discussing them.
  3. Get feedback from coaches or peers on your actions and presentation.
  4. Reflect on what worked and continue practicing until the behavior becomes usable.
- **Example**: Derek Kan’s Doing Power project involved trying to become Mitt Romney’s head of economic policy, which he was offered, and which foreshadowed his later rise to senior government roles.

### Goal Setting and Development Plan for Power
- **When to use**: At the start of a growth period, role transition, or leadership development effort when you want to intentionally build influence.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define why you are here and what success would look like by the end of the period.
  2. Assess yourself on power-related attributes such as energy, focus, and willingness to tolerate conflict.
  3. Identify strengths and weaknesses.
  4. Create a development plan for what you want to build and how you will practice it.
- **Example**: In class, students first answer why they are there and what success would look like after 10 weeks, then self-rate on power attributes and build a plan.

### 10-Person Strategic Networking List
- **When to use**: When you want to build a network intentionally instead of waiting for serendipity.
- **Steps**:
  1. Write down 10 people who would matter for your career goals if they knew you and you knew them.
  2. For each person, design a path to meet or help them.
  3. Use non-cringey approaches such as sharing useful articles, making introductions, hosting dinners, or reaching out with relevance.
  4. Build the relationship through generosity and repeated contact.
- **Example**: Students identify 10 important people and figure out how to meet them, not necessarily through formal networking events.

### Personal Brand Statement
- **When to use**: When you need to differentiate yourself in a crowded field, especially for promotion, fundraising, recruiting, or deal flow.
- **Steps**:
  1. Decide how you want to be known.
  2. Write a concise statement of your distinctive value or identity.
  3. Get feedback from others on whether it is memorable and credible.
  4. Refine it and reinforce it through your work, visibility, and presentation.
- **Example**: Pfeffer describes students writing a personal brand statement and revising it with feedback; he also cites Laura Chau’s effort to become known in consumer tech despite not being at a top-tier brand-name VC firm.

### Practice-and-Coaching Progression
- **When to use**: When a behavior feels unnatural or uncomfortable and you need to build skill without overwhelming yourself.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with a small stretch outside your comfort zone.
  2. Practice the behavior in a structured way.
  3. Get coaching and feedback.
  4. Increase the difficulty gradually over time.
- **Example**: He compares it to learning tennis, lifting weights, or swimming the English Channel: you build capability incrementally.

## Core Advice
- **When you face discomfort or moral hesitation about building power**: Reframe power as a tool rather than something inherently dirty — because if you avoid power, you also avoid the behaviors required to succeed and to use influence for good.
- **When you face imposter syndrome or the urge to downplay yourself**: Stop apologizing and act as if you belong in the role you earned — because self-disempowering language leaks insecurity and teaches others to doubt your legitimacy too.
- **When you face a promotion bottleneck or feel invisible inside a company**: Build a distinctive personal brand — because people cannot promote or choose someone they do not remember.
- **When you face a crowded market or many peers with similar credentials**: Break norms strategically to stand out — because unexpected behavior attracts attention and many rules preserve incumbent advantage.
- **When you face reluctance to ask for opportunities, help, or introductions**: Ask directly — because the downside is often limited while the upside can be career-changing.
- **When you face networking aversion because it feels transactional or fake**: Approach networking through generosity — because helping others and connecting people creates mutual value and increases your centrality.
- **When you face the need to appear more credible in high-stakes settings**: Improve executive presence — because people respond strongly to nonverbal cues and presence, often more than to content alone.
- **When you face a new leadership role or formal authority**: Use the authority you have to make things happen — because visible effectiveness attracts more resources, trust, and opportunities.
- **When you face resistance from people on your critical path**: Suspend judgment and focus on workable relationships — because deciding someone is beneath you makes cooperation harder.
- **When you face the challenge of changing entrenched systems for a good cause**: Learn and apply power skills — because good intentions alone rarely move entrenched interests.
- **When you face difficulty changing your behavior through reading alone**: Get coaching, social support, and accountability — because knowledge without action usually fades.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: If you do good work, people will notice and reward you. → **Their view**: Good work is not enough; you must also make yourself visible and memorable — because promotion and opportunity require recognition.
- **Conventional**: Being liked is essential for career success. → **Their view**: Being effective matters more than being liked — because people are hired to get results, not to be universally adored.
- **Conventional**: Networking is manipulative and inauthentic. → **Their view**: Networking is fundamentally about generosity, information flow, and connecting people — because broad networks create mutual value.
- **Conventional**: Rules exist for a reason, so following them is the safest path to advancement. → **Their view**: Breaking rules strategically is often how people stand out and gain power — because rules often preserve incumbent advantage.
- **Conventional**: If you use aggressive or political tactics, it will eventually catch up with you and bring you down. → **Their view**: Success often causes people to forgive, forget, or overlook how power was acquired — because systems are often self-reinforcing.
- **Conventional**: Power is inherently bad, so good people should avoid it. → **Their view**: If power is to be used for good, good people need to acquire it — because avoiding power leaves influence to others.
- **Conventional**: Power is mostly a personality trait some people are born with. → **Their view**: Power-related behaviors are learnable skills — because presence, networking, branding, and influence can be practiced.
