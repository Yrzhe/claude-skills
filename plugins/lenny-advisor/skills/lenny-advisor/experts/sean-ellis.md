# Sean Ellis

**Growth advisor, author, and early growth operator** | **Independent / GoPractice.io / SeanEllis.me** | Expertise: growth, product-market-fit, activation, go-to-market

## Bio
Sean Ellis coined the term “growth hacking” and created some of the most widely used frameworks in modern growth, including the Sean Ellis test for product-market fit and ICE prioritization. He has led growth at companies like Dropbox, Eventbrite, and LogMeIn, and has advised companies such as Nubank and Microsoft.

His perspective matters because he focuses on the earliest, most leverageable signals of growth: whether users truly need the product, what value they actually care about, and how to build a growth system around that value. He is especially useful when a team is tempted to scale too early or optimize the wrong metric.

## Signature Frameworks

### Sean Ellis Test for Product-Market Fit
- **When to use**: Use once users have experienced the product and you want a fast leading indicator of whether it is becoming a must-have.
- **Steps**:
  1. Survey a random sample of users who have activated the product and used it recently.
  2. Ask: “How would you feel if you could no longer use this product?”
  3. Segment responses into very disappointed, somewhat disappointed, and not disappointed/not applicable.
  4. Treat “very disappointed” users as the must-have cohort.
  5. Ignore the “somewhat disappointed” cohort for PMF diagnosis.
  6. Dig into the very disappointed cohort to understand what benefit they value, why it matters, and what context they live in.
  7. Use those insights to refine positioning, onboarding, roadmap, and acquisition targeting.
- **Example**: At Lookout, Sean found only 7% were very disappointed, then discovered antivirus was the core value. Repositioning the product around antivirus and streamlining onboarding moved the score to 40% in two weeks.

### Value Delivery Engine
- **When to use**: Use after identifying the must-have value to systematically grow the product around that value.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the must-have value from the most passionate users.
  2. Define a North Star Metric that reflects units of that value being delivered.
  3. Map the full value journey: onboarding, aha moment, activation, engagement loop, referral, and revenue model.
  4. Find the highest-leverage bottlenecks in that journey.
  5. Run experiments against the bottlenecks in priority order.
- **Example**: For LogMeIn, Sean focused on improving signup-to-usage before scaling acquisition, which increased usage from 5% to 50% and unlocked much better channel economics.

### Activation-First Growth Sequence
- **When to use**: Use when a company wants to grow but users are not yet reliably reaching value.
- **Steps**:
  1. Focus first on activation and onboarding.
  2. Reduce time to value and make the first experience obvious and successful.
  3. Then improve engagement loops and referral mechanics.
  4. Then optimize monetization/revenue model.
  5. Only after those are working, scale acquisition aggressively.
- **Example**: At LogMeIn, the team froze roadmap work and focused on signup-to-usage. After activation improved, the same paid channels scaled from $10K/month to $1M/month.

### Must-Have Benefit Discovery
- **When to use**: Use when you know some users love the product but you need to understand why and how to grow it.
- **Steps**:
  1. Ask very disappointed users: “What is the primary benefit that you get?”
  2. Use open-ended responses to discover recurring benefit themes.
  3. Turn the themes into a multiple-choice survey for a new group of users.
  4. Ask the follow-up: “Why is that benefit important to you?”
  5. Translate the underlying context into messaging and acquisition hooks.
- **Example**: For Xobni, users said the benefit was “find things faster in my email.” The deeper reason was “I’m drowning in email,” which became a powerful acquisition message.

### Growth Channel Selection by Customer Context
- **When to use**: Use when deciding whether to bet on paid, SEO, referral, sales, or other channels.
- **Steps**:
  1. Talk to customers about how they found the product.
  2. Ask how they normally find products like this.
  3. Look for the natural demand source: search, word of mouth, in-product sharing, physical discovery, or sales-led motion.
  4. Match the channel to the product’s unique context and user behavior.
  5. Validate with experiments before scaling.
- **Example**: Bounce used both SEO (“luggage storage Paris”) and physical demand generation via signs in high-intent locations where travelers were already carrying luggage.

### ICE Prioritization for High-Velocity Experimentation
- **When to use**: Use when you need a simple way to prioritize growth ideas across a team.
- **Steps**:
  1. Score ideas by Impact, Confidence, and Ease.
  2. Use the framework to compare ideas systematically.
  3. Explain why ideas were not chosen to avoid political friction.
  4. Source ideas from across product, marketing, and other functions.
  5. Run the highest-ICE experiments first.
- **Example**: Sean created ICE so teams could submit growth ideas and understand why some were prioritized over others without wasting time on subjective debates.

## Core Advice
- **When you get a low Sean Ellis test score**: Ignore the “somewhat disappointed” users and focus only on the “very disappointed” cohort — because the somewhat disappointed group is signaling the product is a nice-to-have.
- **When you want to improve product-market fit quickly**: Reposition the product around the core benefit that the most passionate users already value — because clear positioning attracts the right users and repels the wrong ones.
- **When users are not retaining well**: Improve onboarding and speed to value before trying tactical retention hacks — because retention is usually about reaching the right experience quickly.
- **When you have some users who love the product but don’t know why**: Ask what primary benefit they get, then ask why that benefit matters to them — because the second layer reveals the real context.
- **When you are deciding whether to scale growth**: Wait until you have a clear must-have signal before aggressively pouring fuel on acquisition — because acquisition magnifies inefficiency.
- **When you are trying to define activation**: Choose an early, experience-based milestone that indicates the user has actually received value — because activation should be close to the first value moment.
- **When you are choosing a North Star Metric**: Pick a metric that reflects units of customer value delivered, not revenue itself — because revenue should be a result of value delivery.
- **When you are deciding which growth channel to bet on**: Start by asking customers how they found you and how they usually find products like yours — because the best channel is often embedded in context.
- **When your product has a collaborative or shareable element**: Explore referral loops, but only after the product already has strong organic word of mouth — because incentives amplify existing sharing behavior.
- **When you are tempted to overcomplicate prioritization**: Use a simple framework like ICE and keep the process lightweight — because simple systems are easier to adopt across teams.
- **When you are stuck on a growth problem**: Ask the most obvious question directly instead of jumping to solutions — because many breakthroughs come from asking the right question.
- **When you are working on a later-stage company**: Expect growth to require cross-functional collaboration across product, marketing, sales, and customer success — because modern growth spans multiple teams.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Retention should be improved with tactical engagement tricks and lifecycle nudges. → **Their view**: Retention is usually more about onboarding to the right user experience than about tactical retention hacks — because users cannot retain on value they never reached.
- **Conventional**: Once you hit 40% on the Sean Ellis test, you have product-market fit and should immediately scale. → **Their view**: The score is only a leading indicator; you still need to understand the must-have value and verify retention — because high scores can reflect switching costs.
- **Conventional**: You should listen closely to somewhat disappointed users to improve the product for the broadest audience. → **Their view**: Optimizing for somewhat disappointed users can dilute the product for the users who truly love it — because a product for everyone can become great for no one.
- **Conventional**: Referral programs can create growth if you add incentives. → **Their view**: Referral programs mostly accelerate existing word of mouth; they cannot fix a product people do not naturally want to share — because desire must come first.
- **Conventional**: Growth should start with top-of-funnel acquisition. → **Their view**: Activation and time-to-value should come first, then engagement, referral, monetization, and only then scaled acquisition — because acquisition is the most expensive lever.
- **Conventional**: Revenue should be the North Star Metric. → **Their view**: The North Star should measure customer value delivered, not revenue directly — because revenue is a lagging outcome.
