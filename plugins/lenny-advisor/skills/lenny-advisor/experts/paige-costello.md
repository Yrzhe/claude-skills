# Paige Costello

**VP / product leader** | **Asana** | Expertise: product leadership, product strategy, team management, analytics

## Bio
Paige Costello leads product for Asana’s core desktop, web, and mobile experiences, with a focus on helping teams create clarity around who is doing what by when and why. Her perspective matters because she combines strong product execution with deep customer empathy, practical planning systems, and a coaching-oriented approach to leadership.

She has also worked at Intuit and Intercom, and she is especially strong on helping PMs build confidence, make better decisions, and communicate with credibility without pretending to know everything.

## Signature Frameworks

### Double Diamond product discovery and delivery
- **When to use**: When solving ambiguous product problems and you need to avoid opinion-driven decisions.
- **Steps**:
  1. Go broad on customer selection: identify which customer segment to solve for.
  2. Narrow to one target customer.
  3. Go broad on problem discovery: explore the full set of problems that customer has.
  4. Narrow to the specific problem to solve.
  5. Go broad on solution exploration: generate multiple solution concepts.
  6. Narrow to the solution to start with.
  7. Map reviews and artifacts to the appropriate inflection point in the process.
  8. Adjust the amount of research and rigor based on problem size and ambiguity.
- **Example**: At Asana, a kickoff may start broad, then move through customer and direction selection, concept review, product spec, full experience review/design crit, and launch review.

### Rolling 12-month planning with six-month refreshes
- **When to use**: When you need strategic alignment but also want to adapt quickly to changing market conditions or new technology.
- **Steps**:
  1. Set a 12-month planning horizon.
  2. Refresh the plan every six months.
  3. Treat the first half as higher-confidence and the second half as lower-confidence.
  4. Use the refresh to incorporate new information, market shifts, or technology changes.
  5. Align product planning with go-to-market planning.
- **Example**: Asana moved from annual planning to planning every six months for a rolling 12 months.

### Trust equation for influencing skeptics
- **When to use**: When you need to build credibility with people who don’t know you well or are skeptical of your ideas.
- **Steps**:
  1. Build credibility by bringing unique insight.
  2. Demonstrate reliability through a strong say-do ratio.
  3. Show authenticity by being vulnerable and real.
  4. Reduce perceived self-interest by showing you are focused on the right outcome, not personal agenda.
- **Example**: Paige referenced the equation as credibility + reliability + authenticity over perceived self-interest.

### Experience, exposure, education
- **When to use**: When you want to grow faster as a PM or leader and avoid learning only from direct responsibility.
- **Steps**:
  1. Seek direct experience doing the work.
  2. Increase exposure by being present for decisions, discussions, and strategy even when you are not the owner.
  3. Pursue education through training, mentors, and coaches.
  4. Learn actively from all three modes rather than treating career growth as linear.
- **Example**: Paige described exposure as being in the car, hearing what’s happening, and evaluating the impact even if you are not driving.

### Above the line / below the line self-awareness
- **When to use**: When you need to assess whether you are operating from curiosity or defensiveness in a discussion or decision.
- **Steps**:
  1. Notice whether you are above the line (open, curious, playful, committed to learning).
  2. Notice whether you are below the line (committed to winning, being right, or black-and-white thinking).
  3. Adjust your mindset before making or debating decisions.
  4. Use the model to understand both your own state and the state of others.
- **Example**: Asana gives employees *The 15 Commitments of a Conscious Leader* and training on this language.

## Core Advice
- **When you need to win trust in a room where you are younger or less experienced**: Bring insight on the customer, market, competitors, numbers, and product instead of trying to sound like the expert in every domain — because people trust you when you contribute information they don’t already have and show you’ve done the work.
- **When you feel stuck in a scarcity mindset or boxed in by one obvious path**: Ask, “How might the opposite be true?” — because this reframes the problem, reduces stress, and often reveals that multiple paths can work.
- **When you are starting a new PM role or joining a new company**: Become best friends with a researcher and watch customers use the product firsthand — because direct observation often differs from what teams report or assume, and it quickly builds real customer understanding.
- **When you want to communicate confidence without sounding arrogant**: Be willing to say “I don’t know,” ask clarifying questions, and show vulnerability in the room — because authenticity and openness build more trust than armor and over-assertiveness.
- **When you are running meetings**: Start with a clear agenda, move quickly, leave room for debate, and close by checking what was missed — because good meeting design improves decision quality and keeps the group focused and engaged.
- **When you are tempted to over-plan work in detail**: Avoid locking teams into exact quarter/week/date commitments and instead align on success criteria and let teams ship iteratively — because over-specific planning creates strange scope decisions and reduces flexibility.
- **When you are deciding what to build**: Think big, ship small — because you preserve strategic ambition while keeping execution incremental and manageable.
- **When you are early in your career and hesitate to apply for roles**: Don’t self-select out; apply even if you don’t feel fully qualified — because people often underestimate their fit and growth potential, and waiting for perfect readiness slows career progress.
- **When you are leading a team and need to give feedback**: Use situation-behavior-impact framing and make the feedback concrete and observable — because this makes feedback easier to understand, internalize, and act on.
- **When you are a manager and want to avoid micromanaging**: Teach repeatable patterns and direction rather than giving one-off precise instructions — because patterns scale better and preserve team agency.
- **When you are leading a review and have too many voices in the room**: Limit the number of reviewers and assign one clear approver who owns the decision — because fewer approvals increase pace, clarity, and accountability.
- **When you are planning your career**: Evaluate roles by learning curve, environment, and problem interest rather than title or company brand — because growth depends on whether the role is stretching you, the environment supports you, and the problem is interesting enough to sustain motivation.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Product teams should plan annually and then execute against the plan. → **Their view**: Plan on a rolling 12-month horizon and refresh every six months. — because strategy changes, technology shifts, and go-to-market alignment require frequent recalibration.
- **Conventional**: A roadmap should be detailed by quarter, week, and date. → **Their view**: Too much date-level specificity causes bad scope decisions; teams should align on outcomes and ship iteratively. — because precision can distort priorities and make teams optimize for the plan instead of the problem.
- **Conventional**: Confidence means sounding certain and having all the answers. → **Their view**: Real confidence often looks like vulnerability, curiosity, and asking good questions. — because people trust openness more than armor, and inquiry leads to better decisions than performative expertise.
- **Conventional**: PMs should be the smartest person in the room and drive decisions from expertise. → **Their view**: New PMs should avoid pretending to know everything and instead focus on curiosity and bringing insight. — because trying to be the expert too early reduces learning and leads to isolated, less collaborative decisions.
- **Conventional**: Career growth is mostly about education first, then experience. → **Their view**: Exposure is a distinct and crucial third path to growth. — because being present for decisions and strategy, even without ownership, accelerates learning in ways formal education and direct experience do not.
- **Conventional**: Positive leaders should emphasize the upside and avoid dwelling on problems. → **Their view**: Sometimes teams need leaders to be explicit about what is bad or incomplete. — because if leaders hide problems, teams may perceive them as inauthentic or out of touch.
