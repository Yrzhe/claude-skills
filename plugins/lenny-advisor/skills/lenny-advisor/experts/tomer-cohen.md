# Tomer Cohen

**Chief Product Officer** | **LinkedIn** | Expertise: product strategy, AI strategy, feed/recommendation systems, product leadership

## Bio
Tomer Cohen is LinkedIn’s Chief Product Officer, where he oversees product development, design, business development, content creation, and customer operations. His perspective matters because he helped transform LinkedIn’s feed from a promotional activity stream into a high-value professional content surface, and he has been one of the clearest advocates for making LinkedIn AI-first across strategy, product, and talent.

He is especially useful when teams need to rethink a mature product, make high-stakes decisions under uncertainty, or figure out how AI should change product leadership rather than just product features.

## Signature Frameworks

### We might be wrong but we're not confused
- **When to use**: When a team needs to make decisions under uncertainty without drifting into hedging, ambiguity, or misalignment.
- **Steps**:
  1. Clarify the exact problem being solved, not just the broad product category.
  2. Force explicit disagreement vs. misunderstanding before continuing discussion.
  3. Make a decision and align everyone on the same direction.
  4. Translate the decision into execution through resourcing, priorities, and talent allocation.
  5. Accept that the decision may be wrong, but eliminate confusion so the team can learn and move together.
- **Example**: At LinkedIn, Tomer would ask teams to specify exactly what video for which audience they were building, then ensure the organization’s priorities and resources matched that decision.

### Minus-one-to-one turnaround playbook
- **When to use**: When reviving an existing product that has entrenched metrics, habits, and internal resistance.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define a new purpose for the product that is not tied to legacy use cases.
  2. Create a dedicated team and ownership model around the turnaround.
  3. Carve out a small cohort or sandbox to test the new experience without destabilizing the whole business.
  4. Use randomized or negative tests to prove the new model works.
  5. Measure downstream value, not just top-line engagement or traffic.
  6. Once evidence is strong, expand the new experience into the main product.
- **Example**: LinkedIn carved out about two million members to experience the new feed, proving the new professional-content model before rolling it out more broadly.

### AI-first product leadership
- **When to use**: When a new technology changes the core mechanics of product building and you need the organization to adapt.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start from the business objective, not from the technology.
  2. Ask what the objective of the algorithm or AI system is.
  3. Treat AI as a core product responsibility, not an engineering-only concern.
  4. Train PMs and leaders on AI fundamentals through structured education.
  5. Bring AI closer to the product team and unify ownership.
  6. Revisit roadmaps and reframe solutions around what the technology now makes possible.
  7. Converge on a few high-priority bets after allowing exploration.
- **Example**: LinkedIn created an AI academy for PMs, then asked teams in fall 2022 to throw out their roadmaps and rethink their objectives with LLMs in mind.

### Belief-led product strategy
- **When to use**: When choosing where to place bets, lead teams, or decide what to build next.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with what you believe could be true about the product’s future.
  2. Anchor ambition in the product’s potential, not current numbers.
  3. Use first-principles thinking to identify the real tradeoffs.
  4. Set a peak or destination that is inspiring and concrete.
  5. Work backward from that destination to the base camp and path.
- **Example**: Tomer believed LinkedIn could become a daily-use professional superpower for every professional, and used that belief to justify investing in the feed and AI.

### Diverge then converge
- **When to use**: When exploring a new technology or product paradigm with no established playbook.
- **Steps**:
  1. Give teams room to experiment broadly and creatively.
  2. Let multiple approaches emerge, even if some overlap.
  3. Use the exploration phase to learn what the technology can actually do.
  4. Review the strongest bets against the original objectives.
  5. Converge resources onto the few highest-value directions.
- **Example**: During LinkedIn’s early AI-first shift, teams were allowed to build and test different ideas freely, then leadership narrowed the portfolio to the four biggest bets.

## Core Advice
- **When your team is debating a product direction and people are hedging**: force the conversation to clarify whether the issue is misunderstanding or disagreement, then stop wasting time if it is truly a disagreement — because confusion creates lingering misalignment and slow execution.
- **When a product decision is made but the organization’s resources still point elsewhere**: make sure priorities are reflected in actual staffing, talent allocation, and roadmap focus — because a stated priority without resourcing is not a real priority.
- **When you’re trying to turn around an existing product with legacy baggage**: create a dedicated cohort or sandbox to prove the new experience before rolling it out broadly — because it avoids destabilizing the whole system and gives you evidence that the new model works.
- **When a product is judged mainly on traffic or top-line engagement**: measure downstream, high-value engagement instead of only counting sessions or raw clicks — because surface-level metrics can be misleading when quality matters more than volume.
- **When you’re introducing AI into an existing product organization**: start from the objective you were already trying to solve, then ask how AI can do it better — because this prevents novelty-chasing and keeps AI tied to real business outcomes.
- **When teams think AI is only an engineering or ML responsibility**: make PMs responsible for the algorithm’s objective, data strategy, and fine-tuning decisions — because AI changes product outcomes directly, so product leaders need to own the system.
- **When a new technology creates too much uncertainty and fear of control loss**: shift from controlling the full experience to controlling the ingredients and guardrails — because AI is non-deterministic, so leaders should manage inputs and constraints rather than every output.
- **When your organization is stuck in inertia and comfortable routines**: introduce deliberate chaos and time-boxed exploration — because people naturally settle into familiar patterns, and pressure can unlock new thinking.
- **When you’re deciding what career opportunities to pursue**: choose the work you genuinely care about and have conviction in, not just the most prestigious role — because sustained impact comes from excitement and willingness to go deep.
- **When you want to grow as a product leader**: study great builders closely and learn from their distinct styles rather than copying one template — because there is no single way to build well.
- **When you’re building a product for a broad audience**: design layered experiences that work for different users at different depths — because great products can serve multiple sophistication levels without flattening the experience.
- **When you’re trying to advance in product**: focus on what you built and what impact it had, not on titles or logos — because product careers are measured by outcomes and learning.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Product leaders should optimize for being right and avoid mistakes. → **Their view**: It is better to be wrong than confused. — because clarity and alignment matter more than ego.
- **Conventional**: A product’s current metrics should determine its ambition. → **Their view**: Start from the product’s potential, not today’s numbers. — because current performance can trap teams in incremental thinking.
- **Conventional**: AI should be owned primarily by engineering or ML specialists. → **Their view**: Product leaders should own AI objectives, data strategy, and fine-tuning decisions. — because AI is now a core product lever.
- **Conventional**: When introducing a new technology, start by brainstorming new features it enables. → **Their view**: Start by revisiting the original objective and ask how the technology helps solve it better. — because novelty without purpose leads to wasted effort.
- **Conventional**: A feed should maximize traffic and raw engagement. → **Their view**: A professional feed should optimize for the right people seeing the right content and for productive, safe conversations. — because quality of audience and downstream value matter more than volume.
- **Conventional**: Career growth comes from chasing the hardest or most prestigious role. → **Their view**: Career growth comes from pursuing what you care about and where you can make a real dent. — because conviction drives better long-term performance.
