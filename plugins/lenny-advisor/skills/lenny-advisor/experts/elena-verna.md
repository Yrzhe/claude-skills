# Elena Verna

**Head of Growth** | **Lovable** | Expertise: growth, product-led growth, go-to-market, pricing, AI strategy, retention

## Bio
Elena Verna is a veteran growth leader who has scaled growth at companies like Miro, Dropbox, SurveyMonkey, Netlify, and Amplitude. At Lovable, she is helping build one of the fastest-growing AI companies while adapting growth playbooks for a market where product capabilities and user expectations change constantly.

Her perspective matters because she combines classic growth rigor with a strong point of view on what breaks in AI-era companies: old funnel thinking, over-optimization, and the idea that growth can be outsourced before product-market fit is real.

## Signature Frameworks

### Growth Loops
- **When to use**: Use when you want growth that compounds through product usage, not just one-time acquisition.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify a user action that creates value beyond the initiating user.
  2. Map the reaction that action triggers in another user or system.
  3. Design the experience so that reaction leads to another valuable action.
  4. Measure whether the loop self-reinforces over time.
  5. Optimize both sides of the loop, not just the trigger.
- **Example**: In Dropbox, sharing a file creates awareness and activation for recipients, and a meaningful share of recipients sign up, making sharing a major acquisition engine.

### Race Car Framework
- **When to use**: Use when planning a balanced growth portfolio across durable engines, temporary boosts, and maintenance.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the core engines that keep growth moving.
  2. Define the fuel required to power those engines.
  3. List turbo boosts that create short-term spikes.
  4. Account for tune-ups and maintenance.
  5. Balance investment across all categories.
- **Example**: A company may have engines, paid fuel, launch spikes, and optimization work all running at once.

### Adjacent User Theory
- **When to use**: Use when the core ICP is maturing and you want expansion without a full PMF reset.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the current core user clearly.
  2. Identify adjacent users already touching the product.
  3. Find where their experience breaks.
  4. Optimize onboarding, activation, or use cases for them.
  5. Expand adoption among these users before broader product expansion.
- **Example**: A product can grow by better serving users near the core ICP rather than rebuilding the product for a totally new market.

### Minimum Lovable Product
- **When to use**: Use when launching in fast-moving AI markets where “good enough” no longer drives word of mouth.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with a minimal version.
  2. Make the first experience delightful, not just functional.
  3. Aim for a fast “wow” reaction.
  4. Ship quickly and gather feedback.
  5. Iterate toward stronger emotional resonance.
- **Example**: Lovable’s internal experiments and prototypes are framed as minimum lovable products, not MVPs.

### Product-Market Fit Treadmill
- **When to use**: Use in AI categories where model capability and user expectations shift every few months.
- **Steps**:
  1. Treat PMF as temporary.
  2. Track model and market changes continuously.
  3. Make forward-looking bets before shifts arrive.
  4. Recapture PMF repeatedly.
  5. Scale in short bursts, not as a permanent state.
- **Example**: Lovable may need to “recapture product market fit every three months” as the market evolves.

### Build in Public + Employee Socials
- **When to use**: Use when shipping velocity and transparency can create awareness, trust, and resurrection.
- **Steps**:
  1. Ship frequently and visibly.
  2. Have founders and employees post launches and learnings.
  3. Tier launches into major moments and smaller updates.
  4. Use social posting to keep the market aware.
  5. Show users that feedback becomes shipped product quickly.
- **Example**: Lovable uses founder-led and employee-led posts on X and LinkedIn to keep “noise in the market.”

### Give the Product Away as a Growth Loop
- **When to use**: Use when the product is novel and best understood through direct experience.
- **Steps**:
  1. Remove barriers with freemium access.
  2. Give away credits for events, hackathons, and community activation.
  3. Treat usage giveaways as marketing spend.
  4. Let champions distribute the product in their communities.
  5. Monetize after users experience the value.
- **Example**: Lovable gives credits for hackathons and tracks the LLM cost as marketing because it drives adoption and word of mouth.

### Hire for Fire, Agency, and Autonomy
- **When to use**: Use in chaotic, high-velocity environments where people must ship and solve problems without heavy process.
- **Steps**:
  1. Prioritize passion over pedigree.
  2. Screen for high agency.
  3. Screen for high autonomy.
  4. Use paid work trials and probation periods.
  5. Be explicit about pace and chaos so candidates self-select.
- **Example**: Lovable uses paid work trials to find people with “fire in their belly.”

## Core Advice
- **When you are tempted to hire growth before PMF**: keep growth founder-led — because growth cannot be outsourced before the company knows what works.
- **When growth is slowing and you want a growth leader to fix it**: stabilize product, market, or GTM first — because growth teams amplify momentum; they do not create it from nothing.
- **When considering a homepage redesign or rebrand**: do it only for a bigger strategic shift — because redesigns usually reset optimized experiences and need months of recovery.
- **When looking at a competitor’s onboarding or pricing page**: use it for inspiration, not copying — because context matters and copying leads to mediocrity.
- **When a growth problem feels unique**: find people who solved similar problems — because most growth issues are recurring patterns.
- **When overrelying on SEO, SEM, or platform acquisition**: invest in earned channels — because rented channels are fragile and non-compounding.
- **When one growth engine is working well**: spend part of the year testing new loops and channels — because existing engines eventually saturate.
- **When you need outside help**: test advisors with a paid workshop first — because good advisors compress learning and bad ones waste time.
- **When every change is being A/B tested**: reserve tests for high-traffic, high-stakes changes — because testing everything slows learning.
- **When roadmap items become vague “simplify onboarding” work**: focus on the underlying user problem — because simplification alone is not a strategy.
- **When operating in fast-moving AI**: spend less time on micro-optimizations and more on new loops and product bets — because the market moves too quickly for incrementalism.
- **When the product is hard to explain but easy to love once tried**: maximize first-use access — because direct experience creates the “wow” that drives word of mouth.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Hire a head of growth as early as possible → **Their view**: keep growth founder-led until PMF and data exist — because growth work before that is really PMF discovery.
- **Conventional**: A growth team can restart a slowing business → **Their view**: growth cannot fix broken product or GTM — because it amplifies existing momentum, it doesn’t create it.
- **Conventional**: Rebrands and homepage redesigns drive acquisition gains → **Their view**: they usually hurt first and rarely create immediate growth — because they reset already-optimized systems.
- **Conventional**: A/B test everything → **Their view**: over-testing is paralyzing — because many decisions should ship with judgment and be evaluated via pre/post analysis.
- **Conventional**: Simplifying flows is always good → **Their view**: simplification is often vague and low-value — because the real issue is usually confusion or poor education.
- **Conventional**: MVPs should prioritize viability → **Their view**: products need to be minimum lovable — because delight and emotional resonance matter more now.
- **Conventional**: Growth teams should mostly optimize funnels → **Their view**: in AI, they should build new loops and capabilities — because the market changes too fast for pure optimization.
- **Conventional**: Full-time jobs are the default career path → **Their view**: optimize for career optionality — because advising, consulting, interim, and fractional work can fit better.
