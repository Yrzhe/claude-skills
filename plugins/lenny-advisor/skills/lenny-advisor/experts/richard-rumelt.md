# Richard Rumelt

**Strategy scholar, author, and consultant** | **General Imagination** | Expertise: strategy, organizational decision-making, competitive advantage, business history

## Bio
Richard Rumelt is one of the most influential modern thinkers on strategy, known for *Good Strategy Bad Strategy* and *The Crux*. His perspective matters because he strips strategy down to its essentials: diagnose the real problem, choose a guiding policy, and take coherent action.

He is especially valuable when teams are confused, overextended, or mistaking goals for strategy. Rumelt’s work is a corrective to vague vision statements and bloated priority lists.

## Signature Frameworks

### The Kernel of Good Strategy
- **When to use**: Use when you need to determine whether a plan is actually strategy and not just goals, aspirations, or a list of initiatives.
- **Steps**:
  1. Diagnose the situation: identify what is really going on and what makes the challenge hard.
  2. Choose a guiding policy: decide the overall approach for dealing with the diagnosis.
  3. Define coherent actions: specify a small set of mutually reinforcing actions that implement the policy.
- **Example**: Microsoft adapting to AI: diagnose the AI shift, choose to invest in a major AI leader and integrate it into search, then execute concrete actions to make that happen.

### Action Agenda / Crux-Based Strategy
- **When to use**: Use when building strategy in a company, startup, or organization that faces many possible problems and needs to choose where to focus.
- **Steps**:
  1. State the problem clearly.
  2. Identify the most important challenge that is also addressable.
  3. Focus on the crux: the hardest part that blocks progress.
  4. Turn the choice into an action agenda with concrete steps.
  5. Commit resources and decision authority to the chosen path.
- **Example**: A company wanting to expand to Australia should not say “we want to grow there”; it should identify why expansion is hard, then build an action agenda around the specific barriers.

### Finding Power Through Asymmetry
- **When to use**: Use when evaluating whether you have a real competitive advantage or are entering a 50/50 fight.
- **Steps**:
  1. Look for asymmetries: what is different about your company, team, knowledge, or position?
  2. Identify the source of leverage: reputation, relationships, data, user base, skill, or ownership.
  3. Check whether the advantage is durable or temporary.
  4. Focus power on a target that can actually be affected.
- **Example**: IBM under Lou Gerstner used its trusted relationships with large enterprise customers as leverage to reposition the company.

### The Foundry Process
- **When to use**: Use when a leadership team needs to coalesce around a strategy inside a complex organization with competing interests.
- **Steps**:
  1. Gather the senior decision-makers in one room for a few days.
  2. List the many problems and challenges on the board.
  3. Force prioritization: identify which challenges are truly important.
  4. Test which challenges are actually addressable.
  5. Resolve disagreements about diagnosis and interests.
  6. Leave with a small set of coherent actions and a decider.
- **Example**: Rumelt described foundries where 25-50 problems are surfaced, then narrowed to one or two key challenges and an action agenda.

### Startup Strategy as Search and Adaptation
- **When to use**: Use when a startup is pre-product-market-fit and operating under high uncertainty.
- **Steps**:
  1. Treat the business as a bet, not a forecast.
  2. Start with a target customer and a solution hypothesis.
  3. Observe what is actually working in the market.
  4. Be willing to switch customers, product shape, or positioning.
  5. Move quickly as information arrives.
- **Example**: Rumelt noted that surviving startups often pivot from one customer segment to another and adjust the product over a year or two until something clicks.

### Value Denial / Perfect Product Thinking
- **When to use**: Use when looking for new product opportunities by asking what customers should be able to buy or experience but currently cannot.
- **Steps**:
  1. Ask what should exist but does not.
  2. Imagine the ideal version of a familiar object or service.
  3. Identify the barriers preventing that ideal from existing.
  4. Design around those barriers where possible.
- **Example**: He used the example of a perfect window: transparent, lets in light and air, blocks bugs and noise, and adapts to the user's needs.

## Core Advice
- **When you are tempted to write a mission or vision statement and call it strategy**: write an action agenda instead: name the problem, the crux, and the concrete steps you will take — because strategy is about solving a high-stakes challenge, not expressing aspirations.
- **When your strategy has many priorities**: cut it down to a few truly important choices and say no to the rest — because too many priorities diffuse effort and destroy focus, which is the source of strategic power.
- **When a plan sounds ambitious but vague**: force a diagnosis by asking what exactly is happening and why the problem is hard — because without diagnosis, you are just stating a desire, not solving a problem.
- **When people keep proposing goals instead of strategy**: translate goals into barriers and actions by asking what is preventing the goal and what will we do about it — because goals are ambitions; strategy requires a path through obstacles.
- **When your organization is trying to do everything at once**: choose one or two addressable challenges and build the whole effort around them — because a strategy must be coherent and focused, otherwise actions fight each other.
- **When you are unsure where your competitive advantage lies**: look for asymmetry in what you know, own, or control that others do not — because strategy depends on exploiting a difference, not competing in an even fight.
- **When your company is large and complex**: use hierarchy and a clear decider to resolve tradeoffs and prevent endless coordination loops — because organizations with many interests cannot self-organize into a strategy without authority.
- **When you are a startup founder under uncertainty**: treat the business as a bet and adapt quickly as evidence comes in — because the market reveals reality in pieces, and rigid plans fail when the environment is changing.
- **When you are stuck on a hard problem**: focus on the crux, the hardest part that blocks progress, rather than the easy parts — because solving the crux often unlocks the whole problem.
- **When you need better strategic insight**: study history, biographies, and prior cases to build analogies for the current situation — because strategy is not a physics-like science; it relies heavily on pattern recognition from past human experience.
- **When your team is debating many possible causes of a problem**: resolve the diagnosis first before debating solutions — because you cannot act coherently until you agree on what is actually causing the issue.
- **When you are choosing a market or product opportunity**: look for value denial, meaning what customers should be able to buy or do but currently cannot — because great opportunities often come from unmet needs that are obvious once you frame them correctly.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Strategy starts with mission, vision, and values. → **Their view**: Start with the problem and the action agenda; mission/vision are not prerequisites for strategy — because strategy is about solving a high-stakes challenge, not producing a polished identity statement.
- **Conventional**: A strategy can be a list of goals or priorities. → **Their view**: Goals are not strategy; they are ambitions. A strategy needs diagnosis, policy, and coherent action — because a laundry list of desired outcomes does not explain how to overcome the obstacle.
- **Conventional**: More priorities create a stronger strategy. → **Their view**: More priorities usually destroy strategy by diffusing focus — because power comes from concentration on a few things, not from trying to satisfy everyone.
- **Conventional**: Leadership is mainly about inspiring people through vision and self-improvement. → **Their view**: Management still requires explicit decisions, tradeoffs, and telling people what to do — because organizations need a decider to allocate power and choose among competing interests.
- **Conventional**: Strategy should be built from analytical tools and frameworks. → **Their view**: Analytical tools help diagnose, but they are not strategy itself — because strategy is the coherent response to the diagnosis, not the analysis alone.
- **Conventional**: Consultants or experts should be able to create strategy from outside analysis. → **Their view**: Senior executives usually already know the key issues; the hard part is agreeing on priorities and power tradeoffs — because the main barrier is organizational conflict and fear of action, not lack of information.
- **Conventional**: Forecasting the future is the basis of strategy. → **Their view**: Business is about making bets under uncertainty; forecasts are inherently unreliable — because the future is revealed in pieces, so strategy must adapt as reality emerges.
