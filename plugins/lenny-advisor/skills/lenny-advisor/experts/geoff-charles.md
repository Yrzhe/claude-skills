# Geoff Charles

**VP of Product** | **Ramp** | Expertise: product-management, leadership, strategy, go-to-market

## Bio
Geoff Charles is the VP of Product at Ramp, where he helped build one of the fastest-growing SaaS companies by pairing extreme speed with unusually strong product discipline. His perspective matters because he has scaled major product lines with very small teams and developed a product operating model built around empowerment, first-principles thinking, and tight feedback loops.

He is especially useful to seek out when you need to move faster without losing quality, organize teams for leverage, or rethink how product, engineering, design, and operations should work together.

## Signature Frameworks

### Single-threaded pod model
- **When to use**: When pursuing a major new product bet or entering a new market with a small team that must move extremely quickly.
- **Steps**:
  1. Create a very small cross-functional team with one clear goal.
  2. Remove all non-core responsibilities and distractions from that team.
  3. Shield the team from bugs, escalations, documentation, and stakeholder noise using support layers like production engineering and product operations.
  4. Set a lofty outcome and a tight timeline.
  5. Keep the effort relatively private until the team finds early traction or product-market fit.
  6. Add resources only after the initiative shows gravitational pull.
- **Example**: Ramp built an accounts payable competitor to Bill.com with three engineers, one designer, and one PM in three months, and the product later moved billions of dollars per year.

### Context over control
- **When to use**: When leading empowered product teams and trying to scale decision-making without micromanagement.
- **Steps**:
  1. Align first on the goal the team is trying to achieve.
  2. Debate the hypothesis behind the goal.
  3. Review the data informing that hypothesis.
  4. Only then discuss possible solutions.
  5. Treat strategy and roadmap as the contract between leader and team.
  6. Have leaders provide repeated context and teams escalate risks and one-way decisions.
- **Example**: Geoff said things went wrong when he prescribed solutions directly instead of aligning upstream on goals, hypotheses, and data.

### Ramp strategy doc structure
- **When to use**: When a pod or product team needs a lightweight but rigorous strategy that connects vision to execution.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the goal: what do you want to see in the world?
  2. State the hypothesis: why do you think this will work?
  3. Explain why your company is uniquely positioned to win.
  4. Define the metrics that will show success.
  5. List the initiatives required.
  6. Call out the key risks.
  7. Describe the long-term outcomes.
- **Example**: For bill payments, Ramp argued it had a right to win because it already had money movement, liability workflows, accounting integrations, risk systems, and existing employees on the platform.

### Quality guardrails for high velocity
- **When to use**: When a team wants to ship quickly without letting quality or customer experience degrade.
- **Steps**:
  1. Route every surfaced bug to be fixed quickly rather than building a large bug backlog.
  2. Track voice-of-customer feedback and send negative reviews back to the PM, designer, and tech lead.
  3. Monitor NPS and CSAT by product area.
  4. Measure operational burden as support tickets normalized by active users.
  5. Track customer confusion signals from support.
  6. Pause new feature shipping if quality or confusion metrics go into the red.
- **Example**: Ramp tracks the number of support tickets caused by customer confusion, and if that rises too much, the team must stop shipping new features and fix the experience.

### First-principles writing loop
- **When to use**: When facing ambiguous scaling, organizational, or product problems that don't have obvious playbooks.
- **Steps**:
  1. Write the core question as simply as possible at the top of a page.
  2. Think through the answer yourself before searching for external answers.
  3. Use writing to clarify your reasoning and assumptions.
  4. Only after that, read others' ideas to refine your thinking.
  5. Turn the result into a communication artifact for the team.
- **Example**: Geoff used this approach for questions like how to scale decision-making, allocate headcount fairly, avoid politics as firsthand data disappears, and decide when to double down versus pivot.

## Core Advice
- **When leadership pushes for more velocity**: use that pressure to demand empowerment, fewer meetings, less process, and sharper prioritization — because speed requires structural tradeoffs, not just urgency.
- **When a major new product opportunity appears**: form a small, single-threaded team with one goal and protect it from unrelated work — because focus and insulation let small teams move disproportionately fast.
- **When teams disagree on what to build**: debate goals, hypotheses, and data before debating solutions — because alignment on the problem produces better ownership and better answers.
- **When the calendar fills with status meetings**: move status updates async and reserve meetings for collaboration, ideation, and decisions — because status meetings consume time without increasing output.
- **When quality starts slipping during rapid shipping**: create explicit guardrails and stop new feature work when confusion or operational burden rises — because speed only works when teams feel the cost of poor quality.
- **When planning overhead slows the org down**: increase planning accuracy only where it matters, such as major launches — because planning has a cost and over-planning steals execution time.
- **When burnout becomes a concern**: focus on ownership, meaning, and visible impact rather than just reducing effort — because burnout often comes from pointless or blocked work, not hard work itself.
- **When you need to do more with fewer PMs**: distribute PM-like responsibilities across engineers, designers, and product ops — because it creates leverage and frees PMs for strategy and alignment.
- **When strategy is ambiguous and no playbook exists**: write the question down and reason from first principles before seeking outside answers — because writing clarifies judgment.
- **When hiring for a high-velocity culture**: prioritize hunger for impact and depth of thinking over pedigree alone — because novel businesses need adaptable thinkers, not just pattern matchers.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: To avoid burnout, companies should slow down and reduce intensity. → **Their view**: Velocity can reduce burnout when people feel ownership, flow, and meaningful impact — because burnout often comes from effort that feels pointless, not from speed.
- **Conventional**: Fast shipping lowers quality. → **Their view**: Higher velocity can improve quality if feedback loops are tight and teams own the consequences — because bugs and confusion get fixed faster.
- **Conventional**: PMs should tightly control specs and execution. → **Their view**: PMs should focus on strategy, context, and protection while engineers and designers own much more — because leverage comes from distributed ownership.
- **Conventional**: More planning and OKRs create alignment. → **Their view**: Heavy planning and product OKRs often create politics and waste time — because a strategy, financial plan, and roadmap are usually enough.
- **Conventional**: Support should sit outside product and optimize ticket resolution. → **Their view**: Support should report into product and be treated as a signal of product failure — because product teams should own the root cause.
- **Conventional**: Experienced hires with strong playbooks are safest. → **Their view**: In a novel business, first-principles thinking matters more — because prior patterns can mislead.
