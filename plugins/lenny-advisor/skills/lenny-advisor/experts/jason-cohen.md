## Signature Frameworks

### 5 Questions to Ask When Growth Stalls
- **When to use**: When a product that once had traction starts slowing down and you need to find the highest-leverage bottleneck.
- **Steps**:
  1. Check whether logo churn is too high.
  2. Check whether pricing is correct.
  3. Check whether existing customers are expanding enough.
  4. Check whether acquisition channels are saturated.
  5. Ask whether you actually need to keep growing.
- **Example**: If you add 100 customers per month and monthly churn is 5%, your business tops out around 2,000 customers.

### Churn Ceiling Calculation
- **When to use**: When you want a simple way to see how churn mathematically limits growth.
- **Steps**:
  1. Measure new customers added per period.
  2. Measure logo churn for the same period.
  3. Divide new customers by churn rate.
  4. Treat the result as the approximate maximum sustainable customer base.
- **Example**: 100 new customers/month divided by 5% churn = 2,000 customers.

### Cancellation Diagnosis via Open-Ended Inquiry
- **When to use**: When cancellation surveys are producing vague or misleading answers.
- **Steps**:
  1. Avoid multiple-choice cancellation reasons.
  2. Ask “What made you cancel?” instead of “Why did you cancel?”
  3. Probe past surface-level answers like “too expensive.”
  4. Talk to customers before they cancel if possible.
  5. Start with onboarding if you need a first fix.
- **Example**: At SmartBear, randomized dropdown options showed answers were heavily shaped by selection bias.

### Root-er Cause Analysis for Churn
- **When to use**: When the stated churn reason sounds generic and you need something actionable.
- **Steps**:
  1. Treat the first answer as a proximate cause.
  2. Ask what workflow, expectation, or product mismatch created it.
  3. Look for multiple contributing causes.
  4. Translate findings into product, positioning, or ICP changes.
- **Example**: “Too expensive” may really mean the product didn’t fit the customer’s workflow, such as missing a key integration.

### Pricing as Market Selection
- **When to use**: When pricing may be attracting the wrong customers or underselling value.
- **Steps**:
  1. Treat price as a signal, not just a number.
  2. Test whether higher prices actually reduce demand.
  3. Reframe pricing around the value the buyer wants most.
  4. Align packaging and contracts with the target segment.
  5. Make sure you want the market implied by the new price.
- **Example**: A product charging $300/year for enterprise buyers may be better priced at $300/month with no drop in signups.

### Value-Based Positioning for Higher Pricing
- **When to use**: When the product creates real business impact but is framed too modestly.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the outcome buyers care about most.
  2. Recast the product around that outcome.
  3. Price against the value of the outcome.
  4. Update messaging and sales around the new framing.
- **Example**: “Cut AdWords cost in half” might support $5K/month, while “double your leads” could support $40K/month for the same product.

### NRR Through Customer Value Expansion
- **When to use**: When logo churn is manageable but growth still stalls because customers aren’t expanding.
- **Steps**:
  1. Measure net revenue retention.
  2. Increase customer value through usage, seats, features, or adjacent products.
  3. Tie monetization to value growth.
  4. Use direct metrics or strong proxies for value.
  5. Think of pricing as sharing newly created value.
- **Example**: Strong SaaS businesses often have NRR above 100%; public SaaS medians are around 119% at IPO.

### Elephant Curve for Channel Saturation
- **When to use**: When acquisition slows and the team keeps pushing the same channels harder.
- **Steps**:
  1. Map which channels are still growing.
  2. Assume channels have ceilings and decay over time.
  3. Stop relying on incremental tweaks to a saturated channel.
  4. Explore new channels, partners, or ecosystems.
  5. Consider new products or markets if needed.
- **Example**: Constant Contact restarted growth with local workshops; HubSpot built agencies into a major channel.

## Core Advice
- **When growth stalls and many things could be wrong**: Diagnose in order: churn, pricing, NRR, channel saturation, then whether growth is even necessary — because lower-level problems cap everything above them.
- **When churn is hurting growth**: Do the churn ceiling math — because it makes the growth limit concrete and unavoidable.
- **When cancellation feedback is noisy**: Ask open-ended questions like “What made you cancel?” — because dropdowns create biased, low-quality data.
- **When customers say “too expensive”**: Probe deeper — because price is often a symptom of poor fit, weak onboarding, or unmet expectations.
- **When retention is weak and you need a starting point**: Fix onboarding first — because early retention improvements compound the most.
- **When you’re unsure about pricing**: Raise prices and observe demand — because many startups are dramatically underpriced.
- **When willingness to pay is low**: Reposition around the business outcome customers value most — because buyers pay more for strategic outcomes than for savings.
- **When expansion revenue is flat**: Increase customer value over time and monetize part of that growth — because NRR rises when customers truly get more value.
- **When acquisition slows in familiar channels**: Audit channel saturation and look for new distribution paths — because channels have ceilings.
- **When considering an upmarket move**: Check the full operational impact, not just the price — because pricing selects a market and changes the company.
- **When growth-at-all-costs feels wrong**: Decide explicitly whether the goal is revenue, profit, or stability — because not every company should maximize growth forever.
- **When using AI to analyze customer feedback**: Use it for themes, but review the details manually — because the unusual specifics are often what matter.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: “Too expensive” means price is the problem → **Their view**: It usually means the product didn’t deliver enough value or fit — because customers already accepted the price once.
- **Conventional**: Raising prices should reduce demand → **Their view**: In software, higher prices can preserve or increase demand — because price signals seriousness and selects the right market.
- **Conventional**: Pricing is mostly choosing the right number → **Their view**: Pricing is really positioning, packaging, and market selection — because the same product can command radically different prices depending on framing.
- **Conventional**: Root cause analysis should find one root cause → **Their view**: Most business problems have multiple interlocking causes — because complex systems rarely reduce to a single lever.
- **Conventional**: “If you’re not growing, you’re dying” is just investor hype → **Their view**: It can still be true, but the “you” may be the founder or team — because stagnation can kill energy and fulfillment even in profitable businesses.
- **Conventional**: A/B testing is the best way to improve growth → **Their view**: It’s overrated for most teams — because it can’t test strategy and often produces false confidence.
