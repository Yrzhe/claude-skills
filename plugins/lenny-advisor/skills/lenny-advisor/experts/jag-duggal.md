# Jag Duggal

**Chief Product Officer** | **Nubank** | Expertise: product strategy, go-to-market, product management, design

## Bio
Jag Duggal is the Chief Product Officer at Nubank, one of the world’s largest digital banks. He has also held senior product and strategy roles at Facebook, Google, and Quantcast, giving him a rare perspective on how to build products that can both win early customer love and scale into category-defining businesses.

His point of view matters most when teams are tempted to ship, scale, or “optimize” before they have true product-market fit. Jag consistently argues that the best products are not merely better—they are fundamentally different, deeply loved, and backed by clear strategy.

## Signature Frameworks

### Fanatical Customer Love Framework
- **When to use**: When building or evaluating products intended to drive strong word of mouth and durable retention.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with a deep, emotionally held customer pain point.
  2. Make customer love the explicit north star.
  3. Do customer discovery before building, focusing on observation and unmet pain rather than feature requests.
  4. Use product and design reviews to ask why the product is great and fundamentally different.
  5. Measure whether customers truly love the product after launch and iterate until they do.
- **Example**: At Nubank, the value “We want our customers to love us fanatically” guides tradeoffs, and teams repeatedly ask in reviews how a product is fundamentally different on quality, complexity, and price.

### Amazon Mock Press Release
- **When to use**: Before committing engineering resources to a new product or feature idea.
- **Steps**:
  1. Write a short press release aimed at the intended customer.
  2. Explain in simple terms why the customer should care.
  3. If you cannot clearly articulate the value in a couple of paragraphs, keep refining the idea before building.
- **Example**: Nubank uses the mock press release technique to force clarity on customer value before putting a single engineer on a project.

### Sean Ellis Score Gating
- **When to use**: When deciding whether a newly launched product has enough product-market fit to scale.
- **Steps**:
  1. Ask customers how disappointed they would be if the product went away.
  2. Use a simple scale: not disappointed, somewhat disappointed, very disappointed.
  3. Set a threshold for “very disappointed” that indicates product-market fit.
  4. Do not scale until the threshold is met.
  5. Analyze which cohorts exceed the threshold and learn what they value most.
- **Example**: Nubank generally uses a 50% threshold in Brazil rather than Sean Ellis’s standard 40%, because Brazilian customers are more polite and optimistic. They used this method on products like Payments Assistant and Ultravioleta.

### Bullseye Cohort Analysis
- **When to use**: When a product has mixed early traction and you need to identify who truly loves it and why.
- **Steps**:
  1. Measure product love across customer segments.
  2. Find the small cohort with the strongest Sean Ellis score or strongest engagement.
  3. Study what is uniquely true about that cohort’s behavior or needs.
  4. Adjust onboarding, product design, and roadmap to expand that bullseye segment.
- **Example**: For Payments Assistant, Nubank found that customers with four or more bills across multiple payment rails had a 70% Sean Ellis score. That insight led them to improve multi-bill, multi-rail onboarding and automation.

### Direct Customer Call Loop
- **When to use**: When teams need fast, high-signal qualitative insight without heavy research overhead.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify a small set of target customers, especially from the most relevant segment.
  2. Have the PM or designer call them directly.
  3. Listen for tone, emotion, and repeated patterns.
  4. By the fifth call, synthesize recurring themes and use them to guide iteration.
- **Example**: Jag tells PMs and designers not to wait for a formal research plan—just call 10 customers directly. By the fifth call, you can usually predict what the rest will say.

### Hypothesis-Driven User Research
- **When to use**: Before and during customer research, to avoid collecting vague or misleading feedback.
- **Steps**:
  1. Form a crisp hypothesis about the customer problem or what will excite the customer.
  2. Use research to validate or invalidate that hypothesis.
  3. Avoid becoming a lawyer for your hypothesis; act as a judge instead.
  4. Observe behavior more than asking direct leading questions.
  5. Ask indirect questions and search for the problem, not validation of your solution.
- **Example**: Nubank’s hypothesis that the traditional joint bank account is an outdated product from a different social era is researched by exploring shared financial pain points, not by pitching the idea to customers.

### Clear Strategy Before Execution
- **When to use**: When setting company or product direction, especially in startups where focus matters.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define a specific customer, problem, solution, and advantage.
  2. Make the strategy explicit enough that everyone knows what is supposed to happen.
  3. Use that clarity to focus execution on a minimal set of high-leverage activities.
  4. Monitor whether reality matches the strategic thesis and adjust quickly if not.
- **Example**: Nubank’s early strategy was to serve urban middle-class Brazilian millennials who hated credit card fees and lacked credit access, using a branchless digital model to offer a no-fee credit card.

### Fundamentally Different, Not Incrementally Better
- **When to use**: When trying to break through as an insurgent product or company in a noisy market.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the incumbent assumptions and tradeoffs in the category.
  2. Look for a disruptive vector that breaks those tradeoffs.
  3. Design for a customer experience that feels qualitatively different, not just somewhat improved.
  4. Use incremental improvements to support the core breakthrough, not replace it.
- **Example**: Nubank’s goal is not to be slightly better than banks but to redefine the experience on quality, complexity, and price, such as building a branchless digital bank and launching with credit first rather than the more obvious bank account first.

## Core Advice
- **When leadership wants to scale too early**: Do not scale until you have clear evidence of product-market fit, such as a strong Sean Ellis score and retention — because scaling a weak product turns a small problem into a big mess.
- **When customer feedback feels ambiguous**: Start with a crisp hypothesis before doing research — because without one, research becomes a pile of interesting but inconclusive conversations.
- **When you’re tempted to ask customers if they like your idea**: Observe behavior and ask indirect questions instead — because customers reveal pain better than they design solutions.
- **When research is moving too slowly**: Have PMs and designers call 10 customers directly — because direct contact surfaces emotional nuance fast.
- **When early traction is mixed**: Find the cohort that loves the product most and design to expand that bullseye segment — because the strongest adopters reveal the real source of value.
- **When a PM is pressured to support a launch they don’t believe in**: Push back early with ownership, clarity, and data — because teams own the consequences of bad launches.
- **When entering a crowded market**: Look for a disruptive vector that makes the product fundamentally different — because incremental improvements rarely create breakout word of mouth.
- **When strategy discussions get vague**: Define the target customer, core problem, unique advantage, and focused solution — because clarity improves execution and reveals when you’re off course.
- **When reviewing a product before engineering starts**: Write a mock press release explaining why the customer should care — because if you can’t explain it simply, the idea likely isn’t sharp enough yet.
- **When customer sentiment declines after launch**: Track NPS and churn obsessively — because delight without retention is not durable growth.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Execution is everything; strategy is secondary. → **Their view**: Poor strategy multiplies execution by zero — because teams can execute for years against the wrong thesis.
- **Conventional**: If a product is good enough, launch and scale it. → **Their view**: Good enough is not good enough; it must be great enough to create real customer love — because only love drives word of mouth and retention.
- **Conventional**: Customer research should be handled by researchers through formal processes. → **Their view**: PMs and designers should directly call customers themselves — because nuance gets diluted through layers.
- **Conventional**: Startups should hedge and diversify bets to reduce risk. → **Their view**: Startups need concentrated bets — because concentration is what creates breakout outcomes.
- **Conventional**: It’s safer to enter existing categories where customers already spend money. → **Their view**: The biggest winners define or reinvent categories — because insurgents can’t win on incumbent terms.
