## Bio
Nikita Miller leads product at The Knot Worldwide and has previously driven product growth and retention at Trello and product at Dooly. She is especially useful to seek out when a team has good ideas but weak follow-through, when cross-functional friction is slowing delivery, or when a remote org needs a better operating model to move faster.

## Signature Frameworks

### Roles and Responsibilities Contracting
- **When to use**: When a team is unclear on ownership, collaboration, or who should do what across product, design, engineering, and data.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the expectations for each role at the IC and manager levels.
  2. Have each leader write their own view of their role and their expectations of counterpart roles.
  3. Review the drafts together and debate differences openly.
  4. Arrive at a shared contract for responsibilities and shared responsibilities.
  5. Cascade the agreed contract throughout the org.
  6. Revisit the contract when conflict, tension, or missed ownership appears.
- **Example**: A product leader writes what they expect from the engineering manager, designer, and data scientist; each counterpart does the same, then the team aligns on a shared contract.

### Optimize for the Right Time Horizon
- **When to use**: When prioritizing work, making trade-offs, or deciding how to spend time across a quarter, year, or life stage.
- **Steps**:
  1. Ask what you are optimizing for right now.
  2. Clarify the time horizon: today, this quarter, or this year.
  3. Use that answer to make trade-offs explicit.
  4. Re-evaluate when new information changes the context.
- **Example**: During quarterly planning, she asks her team and family what they are optimizing for given the latest information from the prior quarter.

### Drive Urgency Through Output and Cycle Time
- **When to use**: When a team has good strategy and documentation but is not shipping fast enough.
- **Steps**:
  1. Look at what was actually delivered in the sprint.
  2. Ask what reached production, not just what was planned.
  3. Measure cycle time from idea to shipped work.
  4. Identify whether the bottleneck is decision velocity or execution velocity.
  5. Break work into smaller pieces to increase shipping frequency.
  6. Use experimentation backlogs and competitive context to keep urgency high.
- **Example**: Instead of asking for a list of everything the team planned, she asks, “What did you deliver this sprint?” and “How long have you been working on that?”

### Remote Problem-Solving Offsite
- **When to use**: When a distributed team is stuck on a hard, cross-functional decision.
- **Steps**:
  1. Create a tight, pre-agreed agenda before the meeting.
  2. Bring the relevant cross-functional people together in person for about two days.
  3. Use the time to align on the core decision and share context, especially data.
  4. Include meals and informal time to build trust.
  5. Return to remote work after the decision is unblocked.
- **Example**: For a strategy change, she brought the team together for two days so the data person could educate everyone and the group could land decisions faster than they could over docs and Zoom.

### Progressive Disclosure Product Strategy
- **When to use**: When building a product that should serve both simple and sophisticated users over time.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with a simple, easy-to-onboard core use case.
  2. Let users adopt the product in single-player or lightweight modes.
  3. Add sophistication as users' needs grow.
  4. Use integrations and advanced features to support more complex workflows.
- **Example**: Trello began as a simple planning tool for anyone, then grew into a more fully featured product as users and teams became more sophisticated.

### Shared Understanding Before Execution
- **When to use**: When a team is debating a problem but lacks a common factual foundation.
- **Steps**:
  1. Pause the debate.
  2. Have the relevant expert, often data, explain the underlying facts.
  3. Ensure everyone starts from the same information.
  4. Resume decision-making once the team shares the same baseline.
- **Example**: In an offsite, the team got unstuck only after the data person was given the floor to educate everyone.

## Core Advice
- **When your team has strong strategy documents but weak shipping velocity**: Measure what actually gets delivered to production each sprint and ask what is blocking faster release — because output is an indicator of whether outcomes are real.
- **When product, design, engineering, and data keep stepping on each other's toes**: Write down role expectations and counterpart expectations separately, then align on a shared contract — because most friction comes from unclear ownership and mismatched assumptions.
- **When a team is debating who owns project management**: Explicitly decide whether the PM, engineering manager, or someone else owns sprint tracking and execution follow-through — because the PM role is often overloaded by default.
- **When a team is moving too slowly on decisions**: Ask whether the bottleneck is decision velocity or execution velocity, then address the specific bottleneck — because planning and progress are not the same thing.
- **When work is stuck because tickets are too large**: Break work into smaller pieces so the team can ship more frequently and learn faster — because smaller pieces improve cycle time and visibility.
- **When a PM is trying to do everything for the team**: Share the product management load across the triad/quartet so everyone contributes to understanding what and why — because no single human can own all aspects of product development all the time.
- **When your team is remote and struggling to solve a hard problem**: Bring the right people together in person for a short, tightly scoped working session — because trust and fast back-and-forth are much easier in person.
- **When you are building for a broad audience with different sophistication levels**: Start simple and add complexity gradually through progressive disclosure and integrations — because simple tools are easier to adopt and can grow with users.
- **When your team is over-indexing on outcomes and under-indexing on execution**: Keep outcomes as the goal, but inspect output and shipping cadence as the leading indicator — because goals without delivery do not create value.
- **When a team is unclear whether to use Trello or Jira**: Use Trello for ideation, prioritization, and discovery; use Jira when the work is decided and needs detailed task breakdown and assignment — because each tool fits a different stage of ambiguity.
- **When you want to improve remote team effectiveness**: Set overlapping work hours, require strong documentation, and make asynchronous communication a core skill — because distributed teams need explicit norms.
- **When you are hiring or developing PMs**: Look for where they spike among artist, scientist, and general manager, rather than expecting a one-size-fits-all PM profile — because different teams need different strengths.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Product teams should focus almost entirely on outcomes. → **Their view**: Output matters too — because if the team is not shipping enough, outcomes do not matter much.
- **Conventional**: The PM should own most coordination and project management. → **Their view**: This responsibility should be explicitly shared, often with engineering managers — because the PM role is too overloaded and ill-defined.
- **Conventional**: Remote work can fully replace in-person collaboration if documentation is good. → **Their view**: Hard problems are much easier to solve after people have built trust in person — because shared context and disagreement are easier face-to-face.
- **Conventional**: A PM must be the person with all the answers. → **Their view**: A good PM distributes the product management load and helps the team help them find answers — because shared responsibility leads to better decisions.
- **Conventional**: Work-life balance should mean equal attention to work and life at all times. → **Their view**: Optimization is a better frame than balance — because different life stages require different trade-offs.
