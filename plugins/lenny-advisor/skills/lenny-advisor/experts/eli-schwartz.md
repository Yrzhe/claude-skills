# Eli Schwartz

**SEO advisor and author** | **Independent; author of _Product-Led SEO_** | Expertise: SEO, growth, go-to-market, product strategy

## Bio
Eli Schwartz is a longtime SEO and growth advisor who has helped companies like Quora, Coinbase, Tinder, LinkedIn, WordPress, Zapier, and SurveyMonkey build SEO strategies that connect directly to business outcomes. He is best known for arguing that SEO should be treated as a product discipline, not just a marketing channel, especially as AI Overviews and changing search behavior reshape discovery.

His perspective matters because he consistently asks a harder question than most SEO practitioners: not “How do we get traffic?” but “Should we do SEO at all, and if so, what user problem are we solving?” That makes him especially valuable for companies deciding where SEO fits in their growth stack.

## Signature Frameworks

### Product-Led SEO
- **When to use**: When deciding whether SEO is a viable growth channel and how to build SEO experiences that actually convert, especially in AI-disrupted search.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with the user, not keywords: understand the problem the user is trying to solve and what they would actually search for.
  2. Map the buyer journey and identify where search matters, especially mid-funnel or bottom-funnel intent.
  3. Design an SEO asset as a product experience, not just content: pages, templates, integrations, local pages, tools, or structured experiences.
  4. Ensure the SEO experience directly connects to what the business monetizes.
  5. Measure success by business outcomes like leads, signups, revenue, or meaningful conversions—not rankings or raw traffic.
- **Example**: At Zapier, instead of trying to rank for generic software terms, they created pages around specific integrations like Gmail + Salesforce because users were searching for ways to connect tools, not for “Zapier” itself.

### Mid-Funnel SEO in the AI Era
- **When to use**: When AI Overviews and LLMs absorb top-of-funnel discovery queries, and you need to focus SEO where users still need to take action.
- **Steps**:
  1. Assume top-of-funnel informational discovery will increasingly be answered directly by AI.
  2. Identify the point where the user moves from curiosity to intent.
  3. Build SEO pages that help users evaluate, compare, configure, or act on a solution.
  4. Optimize for searches where the user still needs a product, transaction, or decision support.
- **Example**: For travel, AI may answer “best beach vacation within two hours,” but once the user decides on Cancun, SEO matters for hotel, booking, and destination-specific searches.

### 3-Step SEO Build Process
- **When to use**: When a company is just starting SEO and needs a practical way to evaluate and launch an effort.
- **Steps**:
  1. Be the user: develop customer empathy and identify what the user would search for.
  2. Decide what asset to create: determine whether the right solution is a programmatic page set, template library, local landing pages, or another productized SEO asset.
  3. Build the product: use product, design, engineering, and research resources to create the experience.
- **Example**: For Tinder, they identified that users in a new city were solving loneliness, then built local dating pages for cities around the world rather than generic dating blog content.

### SEO Channel Fit Evaluation
- **When to use**: When deciding whether SEO deserves investment versus paid, brand, sales, events, or other growth channels.
- **Steps**:
  1. Ask whether users actually search for the problem or solution in a way that can lead to your product.
  2. Check whether the journey can convert online or whether it requires a sales-led process.
  3. Estimate the full cost of SEO, including people, CMS, engineering, design, and content.
  4. Compare expected payback speed and ROI against other channels.
  5. If the search journey does not map to conversion, deprioritize SEO.
- **Example**: He advised a gardening SaaS company that trade shows were a better investment than a $15,000/month SEO content retainer because their customers were acquired effectively at industry events, not through search.

### Programmatic vs. Editorial SEO Decision
- **When to use**: When deciding whether to scale SEO with data-driven page generation or with hand-written content.
- **Steps**:
  1. Determine whether there is a repeatable user need at scale.
  2. Check whether you have structured data or inputs that can create useful pages.
  3. Validate that each page solves a real user query, not just a theoretical keyword combination.
  4. Use programmatic SEO when scale and utility align; use editorial when nuanced explanation or persuasion is required.
- **Example**: TripAdvisor used programmatic SEO by combining hotel, location, reviews, and user-generated content into scalable property pages rather than writing editorial articles for every hotel.

### Top-Down SEO Forecasting
- **When to use**: When estimating SEO upside for a new market, product, or geography and keyword tools are too unreliable.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with the total addressable population in the market.
  2. Narrow to the relevant segment of users who could buy the product.
  3. Estimate the portion of that segment that buys or searches online.
  4. Apply an expected market penetration rate.
  5. Multiply by purchase frequency and AOV to estimate upside.
  6. Refine assumptions over time as real data comes in.
- **Example**: At Faire, instead of building a forecast from keyword volumes, he modeled SEO upside for a new country by starting from population, narrowing to likely buyers, then estimating online behavior and market penetration.

## Core Advice
- **When you face pressure to “do SEO” because competitors do it or because it seems like a default growth channel**: Do a channel-fit evaluation first and only invest if users actually search in a way that can lead to conversion — because SEO is not free and may underperform better-aligned channels.
- **When you face declining value from top-of-funnel content because AI Overviews answer generic questions directly**: Shift SEO investment toward mid-funnel and bottom-funnel experiences where users still need to evaluate options or take action — because AI is strongest at summarizing broad information, not completing decisions.
- **When you face a blank slate and want to start SEO from scratch**: Start by becoming the user: identify the exact problem they would search for, then build an SEO asset around that journey — because keyword-first SEO usually misses real intent.
- **When you face the temptation to publish lots of AI-generated blog posts**: Use AI as a tool for useful, product-connected content or descriptions—not as a machine for mass-producing generic articles — because low-value content adds crawl bloat and rarely converts.
- **When you face a decision between programmatic SEO and editorial content**: Choose programmatic SEO only if you have real user demand at scale and structured inputs that create genuinely useful pages — because scale without utility creates noise, not growth.
- **When you face internal reporting pressure to show SEO success**: Track business outcomes like leads, signups, revenue, or meaningful conversions instead of raw traffic or rankings — because traffic without conversion is not business value.
- **When you face a complex B2B SaaS buying process with multiple stakeholders and sales involvement**: Do not assume SEO should be a major growth engine early on; prioritize channels better aligned with the actual buying motion — because search may educate but not efficiently convert.
- **When you face an SEO traffic drop after a Google update**: Diagnose whether the issue is content quality and user value before spending on technical SEO audits — because many update hits are really relevance problems.
- **When you face the need to forecast SEO opportunity for a new market**: Use a top-down TAM-style model instead of relying heavily on keyword tool volume estimates — because keyword tools are often directionally useful but numerically unreliable.
- **When you face the question of whether to optimize for AI Overviews specifically**: Treat inclusion in AI Overviews primarily as a brand outcome, not a standalone SEO strategy — because visibility there usually reflects broader authority and ranking strength.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: SEO is a marketing function centered on keywords, content, and links. → **Their view**: SEO should be treated as a product problem owned collaboratively by product, marketing, design, engineering, and research — because winning SEO now requires building experiences that solve user problems along the buyer journey.
- **Conventional**: More SEO traffic is always good. → **Their view**: Traffic without conversion is often worthless and can distract teams from real business outcomes — because SEO should be judged by monetization, not vanity metrics.
- **Conventional**: Every startup should invest in SEO early because it’s free and everyone does it. → **Their view**: Many companies—especially B2B SaaS and some local businesses—should not do SEO at all, or should delay it significantly — because SEO has real costs and may not match the buying motion.
- **Conventional**: Top-of-funnel SEO is the main opportunity because it captures the most search volume. → **Their view**: Top-of-funnel is becoming less valuable in the AI era; mid-funnel and bottom-funnel are where durable SEO value remains — because AI answers broad questions directly.
- **Conventional**: Link building is the secret to SEO success. → **Their view**: Brand building is the real lever; low-quality link building is mostly noise — because genuine authority matters more than manufactured links.
- **Conventional**: Technical SEO is the best fix when rankings or traffic drop. → **Their view**: Technical SEO is often overused; many problems are actually caused by poor content quality or weak user value — because Google increasingly rewards helpfulness.
- **Conventional**: Keyword research tools provide a reliable basis for SEO forecasting. → **Their view**: Keyword tools are often materially wrong and should be used directionally, not as precise forecasting inputs — because their estimates can be off by large multiples.
- **Conventional**: AI-generated content is inherently bad for SEO. → **Their view**: AI content is fine if it is useful and connected to the product journey; the problem is low-value content, not AI itself — because Google cares about helpfulness more than authorship method.
