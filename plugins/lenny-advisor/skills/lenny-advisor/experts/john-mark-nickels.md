# John Mark Nickels

**Product leader for Uber Mobility** | **Uber** | Expertise: leadership, product strategy, marketplace pricing, mobility

## Bio
John Mark Nickels is a longtime product leader across Uber, Waymo, and DoorDash, known for building and scaling complex mobility and marketplace products like Uber Pool and pricing/matching systems. His perspective matters because he combines deep transportation-marketplace intuition with an unusually strong emphasis on conscious leadership, emotional awareness, and long-term strategic thinking.

He is especially useful when you need someone who can think both like a systems strategist and like a reflective leader. He tends to ask first-principles questions about how mobility should work in the future, while also paying close attention to the human dynamics that determine whether teams actually execute well.

## Signature Frameworks

### Conscious Leadership
- **When to use**: When leading teams, navigating conflict, or trying to increase your influence without relying on fear, status, or control.
- **Steps**:
  1. Recognize that leadership means having influence, regardless of title.
  2. Increase awareness of your internal world: beliefs, biases, emotional patterns, and inherited conditioning.
  3. Take responsibility for the influence you have on others and the environment you create.
  4. Create space for others rather than dominating with authority or certainty.
  5. Shift from fear, threat, and righteousness into curiosity, trust, and co-creation.
- **Example**: He described evolving from trying to be the loudest and “rightest” person in meetings to intentionally speaking less as a senior leader so junior people feel safer contributing.

### Allow, Then Reframe
- **When to use**: When you feel anxious, threatened, or overly attached to approval in high-stakes meetings, reviews, or presentations.
- **Steps**:
  1. Notice the thought or emotion arising instead of suppressing it.
  2. Allow it to be present rather than fighting it.
  3. Remember that thoughts and emotions are transient.
  4. Reconnect to the belief that you do not need external approval to be okay.
  5. Reframe the situation from threat to learning opportunity.
- **Example**: In stressful reviews with Uber leadership, he learned not to fight thoughts like “Dara might think I suck,” but to allow them, breathe, and treat feedback as information rather than a threat.

### Future-Back Visioning
- **When to use**: When developing product vision, long-term strategy, or trying to identify where a market is heading before deciding what to build.
- **Steps**:
  1. Step away from day-to-day operational noise.
  2. Imagine the world 5-10 years out without assuming your company is at the center.
  3. Identify likely enduring truths and trends from first principles.
  4. Paint a vivid picture of how users, systems, and environments might change.
  5. Work backward to determine what products or strategies align with that future.
  6. Bring the draft vision to a team for co-creation and refinement.
- **Example**: He imagines future cities with autonomous vehicles, fewer parking garages, more shared rides, and new transport form factors, then uses those assumptions to reason about Uber’s long-term marketplace strategy.

### First-Principles Strategy Design
- **When to use**: When an industry’s current structure feels inefficient or taken for granted, and you want to uncover strategic opportunities.
- **Steps**:
  1. Ask why the current system works the way it does.
  2. Identify inefficiencies or assumptions embedded in the status quo.
  3. Reduce the problem to physical, economic, or behavioral fundamentals.
  4. Project what becomes true if costs, constraints, or technologies change.
  5. Use those truths to infer what products or business models will matter.
- **Example**: He asks, “Why do we need a 4,000-pound vehicle to move a human three miles?” That line of thinking opens up bikes, scooters, shared rides, and new vehicle form factors.

### Vision-Execution Polarity Balance
- **When to use**: When teams are either overthinking strategy and not shipping, or shipping aggressively without enough clarity on direction.
- **Steps**:
  1. Assess whether the team is currently too far in theory/vision mode or too far in execution mode.
  2. If over-theorizing, simplify and identify a practical starting point.
  3. If over-executing, pause to revisit strategy and whether you are running through the right wall.
  4. Adjust dynamically based on company stage and certainty of direction.
  5. Once direction is clear enough, commit hard to execution.
- **Example**: He contrasted Uber’s tendency to over-model complex pricing systems in theory with DoorDash’s “ready, fire, aim” bias toward action.

### Life Objective Function
- **When to use**: When making trade-offs between work, family, ambition, and long-term fulfillment.
- **Steps**:
  1. Assume you already have an implicit objective function, whether or not you’ve articulated it.
  2. Write down your values and principles explicitly.
  3. Evaluate decisions from the perspective of your future self.
  4. Use mortality awareness to distinguish what will matter later from what feels urgent now.
  5. Choose actions today that compound toward the relationships and life outcomes you actually care about.
- **Example**: He contrasted spending Sunday night polishing a presentation versus investing that time with his daughters, noting that five years later he won’t care about the slightly better deck but will care deeply about the relationship.

### Emotion-as-Signal Mapping
- **When to use**: When making decisions, sensing team dynamics, or trying to use emotions constructively rather than suppressing them.
- **Steps**:
  1. Notice emotions in the body rather than only as thoughts.
  2. Identify the recurring physical signatures of emotions for yourself.
  3. Interpret each emotion as a signal rather than a problem.
  4. Use the signal to guide attention or action.
  5. Optionally name the emotion in the room to shift team dynamics.
- **Example**: He described fear as “something wants to be paid attention to,” sadness as “something wants to be let go of,” anger as “something is not of service,” joy as “something wants to be celebrated,” and creative energy as “something wants to be born into the world.”

## Core Advice
- **When you face anxiety before a high-stakes meeting or presentation**: Do not suppress the fear; allow the thought and emotion to be present, breathe, and remind yourself that you do not need the other person’s approval to be okay — because resisting thoughts often strengthens them and creates a self-reinforcing stress loop.
- **When you face pressure to optimize optics and impress leadership**: Focus first on the quality and purpose of the work, and treat presentations and communication as means to that end rather than the end itself — because anchoring on the mission tends to improve both the product and your career outcomes.
- **When you face difficulty creating a compelling product vision**: Step away from meetings, get into a quiet contemplative state, and imagine the world 5-10 years from now before deciding what your company should do — because long-range thinking requires a different mental mode than operational execution.
- **When you face shallow thinking or weak strategy in a domain**: Go much deeper in one problem space over time instead of constantly switching domains and topics — because nuanced strategy comes from prolonged immersion.
- **When you face a calendar full of fragmented meetings and feel unable to think clearly**: Carve out uninterrupted time for deep thinking and reduce your active priority list to one to three high-leverage items — because strategy and creativity do not emerge well from constant context switching.
- **When you face a team that is stuck in debate and not shipping**: Bias toward action once you have enough clarity, even if the plan is imperfect — because execution generates learning and feedback.
- **When you face a team that is shipping fast but may be heading in the wrong direction**: Pause execution long enough to revisit strategy and ensure you are running through the right wall — because pure execution without directional clarity can create lots of activity but little progress.
- **When you face emotionally charged workplace conversations**: Acknowledge emotions as useful signals and, when appropriate, name them explicitly in the room — because emotions contain information about risk, loss, misalignment, celebration, or creative possibility.
- **When you face trade-offs between urgent work and important life priorities**: Evaluate the decision from the perspective of your future self and ask what will matter in five years or at the end of life — because short-term incentives often crowd out what compounds most over time.
- **When you face a tendency to feel acted upon by circumstances or other people**: Practice shifting from victim mentality to responsibility by focusing on how you are relating to the situation and what agency you still have — because changing your stance increases agency, resilience, and effectiveness.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Strong leaders should be the loudest, most certain voice in the room. → **Their view**: Senior leaders often create better outcomes by speaking less, creating space, and being more aware of their influence — because power dynamics can silence junior contributors.
- **Conventional**: You should suppress fear and negative emotions at work and act purely rationally. → **Their view**: Emotions should be allowed and listened to because they contain useful information — because suppressing them can intensify stress and hide important signals.
- **Conventional**: Career success comes from polishing optics, presentations, and how you appear to leadership. → **Their view**: Over-focusing on optics can hurt you; anchoring on the mission and the actual product often leads to better outcomes and even better career progression.
- **Conventional**: Workplace decisions should be driven only by logic and data. → **Their view**: Whole-body intelligence matters: head, heart, and gut all contribute to better judgment — because embodied reactions can surface truths analysis misses.
- **Conventional**: The best way to be productive is to stay constantly busy, consume more content, and keep every minute filled. → **Their view**: Some of the best strategic thinking comes from silence, walks, hikes, and unstructured mental space — because insight needs room to emerge.
- **Conventional**: To succeed, maximize short-term work outputs whenever possible. → **Their view**: You should optimize your life using a consciously chosen long-term objective function, not just immediate work incentives — because relationships and life outcomes compound over years.
