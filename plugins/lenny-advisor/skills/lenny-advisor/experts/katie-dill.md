# Katie Dill

**Head of Design** | **Stripe** | Expertise: design, leadership, user research, product management, growth, strategy

## Bio
Katie Dill leads design at Stripe, where she oversees product design, brand and marketing creative, web presence, user research, content strategy, and design ops. She previously held design leadership roles at Airbnb and Lyft, and is especially valuable for her experience scaling design organizations in hypergrowth companies.

Her perspective matters because she treats design as a business lever, not just a visual discipline. She consistently argues that quality, beauty, and craft improve trust, usability, conversion, and revenue.

## Signature Frameworks

### Performance = Potential - Interference
- **When to use**: When leading teams and trying to improve execution, morale, and output quality.
- **Steps**:
  1. Assess the team’s current performance.
  2. Increase potential by hiring well, developing talent, and helping people grow.
  3. Identify interferences that block great work, such as poor org design, misalignment, or bad processes.
  4. Remove or reduce those interferences so talent can perform at its highest level.
  5. Repeat as the company scales, since growth creates new coordination problems.
- **Example**: At Lyft, Katie saw designers physically separated from PMs and engineers, causing wasted work and misalignment. She reorganized teams so designers sat with their cross-functional partners while preserving dedicated creative space.

### Walk the Store / Essential Journeys Review
- **When to use**: When product quality regresses over time, teams optimize locally, or leaders need a shared view of end-to-end user experience.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify a manageable set of critical user journeys.
  2. Assign engineering, product, and design leaders to own the quality of each journey.
  3. Have those leaders regularly walk the journey end-to-end as a user would.
  4. Create a friction log with screenshots and notes on what works, what doesn’t, and severity.
  5. Score the journey using a rubric covering utility, usability, desirability, and “surprisingly great.”
  6. Calibrate scores in a cross-functional review to align on standards and urgency.
  7. File bugs, coordinate with owning teams, and use findings to improve roadmap and process.
- **Example**: Stripe started with 15 critical user journeys. Leaders reviewed them quarterly and found issues like SEO and messaging mismatches early in the journey that hurt downstream understanding.

### Reach for the Stars, Land on the Moon
- **When to use**: When teams are stuck in incrementalism and need a bold but practical way to pursue better product experiences.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the ideal end-state experience, even if it feels ambitious.
  2. Map the full journey rather than optimizing isolated features.
  3. Use that vision as a North Star for sequencing work.
  4. Break the vision into staged releases that can be tested and learned from.
  5. Avoid shipping the entire vision at once, but don’t lose the coherence of the whole.
- **Example**: Katie references Brian Chesky’s “11-star experience” style of thinking: define the ideal experience first, then work backward so teams don’t stumble into the right outcome.

### Two T-Shirts Model
- **When to use**: When building strong functional craft communities without sacrificing cross-functional product execution.
- **Steps**:
  1. Embed designers with PMs and engineers around shared product goals.
  2. Create regular moments for the design function to come together as a craft community.
  3. Make it explicit that people belong to both their discipline and their product area.
  4. Use each community for different purposes: execution and alignment in one, craft and identity in the other.
- **Example**: At Airbnb, Katie described designers as wearing two T-shirts: the Design T-shirt and the Marketplace or cross-functional team T-shirt.

### Low-Maintenance Visual Work Sharing
- **When to use**: When organizations grow and people lose visibility into what other teams are building.
- **Steps**:
  1. Create a shared slide deck for in-progress work.
  2. Ask teams to add screenshots or prototypes of medium and large projects regularly.
  3. Keep the format lightweight so it doesn’t become a status burden.
  4. Share it broadly across design, product, engineering, and leadership.
  5. Use it to spot overlap, collisions, reusable patterns, and alignment opportunities early.
- **Example**: Katie has used this for over a decade: designers add work-in-progress visuals to a shared deck monthly or biweekly so others can quickly understand what’s being built.

## Core Advice
- **When you face low trust as a new leader**: listen more before driving change — because even correct changes fail if the team doesn’t trust your intent.
- **When you face pressure to choose growth over quality**: connect quality improvements to business outcomes — because quality is a growth lever, not a tradeoff.
- **When you face fragmented product experiences across teams**: do end-to-end journey reviews with cross-functional leaders — because users experience journeys, not org charts.
- **When you face debates about design vs. metrics**: align early on the shared outcome — because utility, usability, and desirability work together.
- **When you face quality regression after launch**: run recurring quality reviews — because products degrade as adjacent teams ship changes.
- **When you face overreliance on data alone**: trust your own informed judgment too — because firsthand experience reveals friction metrics may miss.
- **When you face slow iteration caused by org structure**: embed designers, PMs, and engineers closely — because separation creates wasted work and slower feedback loops.
- **When you face a culture of incrementalism**: define an ambitious end-state first — because a clear North Star prevents local optimization.
- **When you face hiring for early design teams**: prioritize taste, judgment, humility, and courage — because tools are easier to teach than character.
- **When you face poor visibility into what teams are building**: use a lightweight visual sharing ritual — because pictures create faster shared understanding than docs.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Functionality matters; beauty is a nice-to-have. → **Their view**: Beauty enhances functionality and is core to usability, trust, and business performance.
- **Conventional**: Growth and quality are competing priorities. → **Their view**: Quality is growth. — because better onboarding, clarity, and checkout improve activation and revenue.
- **Conventional**: Quality is the design team’s job or QA’s job. → **Their view**: Quality is a group effort across design, engineering, product, and leadership.
- **Conventional**: The safest path is small incremental improvements. → **Their view**: Teams need bold vision work to avoid optimizing the wrong local maxima.
- **Conventional**: Leaders should come in strong and quickly fix things. → **Their view**: Leaders should listen first and earn trust before driving change.
