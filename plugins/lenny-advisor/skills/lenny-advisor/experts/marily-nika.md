## Signature Frameworks

### AI Product Opportunity Filter
- **When to use**: When deciding whether a product problem is worth solving with AI/ML.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with a real user pain point, not a desire to use AI.
  2. Define the high-level solution you think AI could enable.
  3. Check whether you have enough data or access to relevant data.
  4. Assess feasibility with a research scientist or technical partner.
  5. Validate business viability and user desirability before investing heavily.
- **Example**: If a team wants to “add AI” to a workflow app, she would first ask what user pain exists, what data is available, and whether AI meaningfully improves the experience before any model work begins.

### Three-Bubble AI PM Decision Model
- **When to use**: When evaluating whether an AI product idea is worth pursuing.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify what users actually want or need.
  2. Confirm the idea is viable as a business.
  3. Confirm it is feasible from a research and technical perspective.
  4. Proceed only when the three areas intersect.
- **Example**: A personalized avatar feature is worth building only if users want it, the business can support it, and the underlying ML approach is feasible.

### AI Product Development Lifecycle
- **When to use**: When building an AI product from idea to launch.
- **Steps**:
  1. Understand how AI product development differs from regular product development.
  2. Define the problem and hypothesis clearly.
  3. Work with research scientists to explore model approaches.
  4. Figure out data requirements and data collection strategy.
  5. Prototype and test the solution.
  6. Decide the launch threshold and quality bar.
  7. Plan for iteration, rollback, and ongoing model improvement.
- **Example**: For a speech feature, the PM may need to define the target use case, gather labeled examples, test model quality, and set a launch bar before shipping.

### AI PM Buy-In and De-Risking Framework
- **When to use**: When asking leadership for investment in an AI/ML initiative.
- **Steps**:
  1. Use adjacent successful products as inspiration and proof points.
  2. Explain why the proposed idea is similar to something that already worked.
  3. Present a contingency or rollback plan.
  4. Clarify the maximum negative impact if the experiment fails.
  5. Build trust over time through responsible execution and learning.
- **Example**: When pitching a new AI assistant, she recommends framing it as similar to an existing successful product and adding a clear rollback plan if quality is not good enough.

### Teach-It-Back Learning Loop
- **When to use**: When learning AI or product concepts deeply.
- **Steps**:
  1. Learn the fundamentals or tool basics.
  2. Try to explain the concept simply to a non-technical audience.
  3. Use teaching, workshops, or course creation to expose gaps in understanding.
  4. Refine your mental model based on hard questions from learners.
- **Example**: If you can explain a database to a three-year-old, you likely understand it well enough to use it effectively.

## Core Advice
- **When you feel tempted to add AI because it is trendy**: start from a concrete user pain point and only then look for an AI solution — because AI should solve a real problem, not become a shiny object.
- **When you are building an MVP**: fake the AI in a prototype and test demand with users — because early validation should be fast and cheap.
- **When you have product data but no AI plan**: look for personalization, automation, fraud detection, ethics, or accuracy improvements — because behavioral data often creates meaningful AI opportunities.
- **When you are new to AI as a PM**: take an intro course, learn basic coding, and experiment with no-code tools — because fundamentals improve judgment even if you are not an engineer.
- **When you need a clearer mission statement or narrative**: use ChatGPT to rewrite it — because AI can improve clarity across audiences.
- **When you are defining user segments**: prompt ChatGPT with a specific scenario and ask who would care — because it can surface overlooked motivations and segments.
- **When deciding whether to build your own model**: do it only if you have enough unique, diverse data — because off-the-shelf models converge in quality.
- **When estimating data needs**: size the requirement by task complexity — because simple classification and speech/NLP have very different data demands.
- **When asking leadership for AI investment**: anchor the idea in adjacent wins and include a rollback plan — because bounded downside makes bets easier to approve.
- **When working with research scientists**: shadow their workflow and learn how they think — because AI PMs must bridge product, research, and business constraints.
- **When building an AI career path**: redefine success metrics if launches are slower — because research-heavy work progresses through experiments and iteration, not just launches.
- **When you want a practical prototype without coding from scratch**: use AutoML or similar no-code tools — because they let non-engineers train useful models quickly.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: AI is mainly about ChatGPT-style text generation and replacing human work. → **Their view**: AI is much broader and enhances work. — because use cases include translation, personalization, automation, fraud detection, and more.
- **Conventional**: PMs should focus on shipping products while researchers handle uncertainty separately. → **Their view**: AI PMs often need to manage the problem, not just the product. — because feasibility and solution shape emerge together.
- **Conventional**: You need to build AI into an MVP to prove the idea. → **Their view**: Prototype the experience first and fake the AI if needed. — because model training is slow and expensive.
- **Conventional**: If a model is not launching frequently, the work is less valuable for PM growth. → **Their view**: Research-heavy AI work is valuable in a different way. — because progress often comes through experiments and learning.
- **Conventional**: PMs do not need to learn coding or model basics. → **Their view**: PMs should still learn fundamentals. — because understanding the mechanics changes judgment and collaboration quality.
