# Eric Simons

**Co-founder and CEO** | **StackBlitz** | Expertise: ai-strategy, go-to-market, product-management, leadership

## Bio
Eric Simons is the co-founder and CEO of StackBlitz, the company behind Bolt, a web-based AI coding agent that went from near-shutdown to roughly $40M ARR in about five months. His perspective matters because he spent seven years building deep browser infrastructure before the market was ready, then used that technical foundation to ride one of the fastest product inflections in AI.

He is especially valuable for founders navigating long periods of uncertainty, technical bets, and hypergrowth. Eric combines infrastructure thinking, product taste, and unusually strong opinions on burn, team design, pricing, and how non-developers can build with AI.

## Signature Frameworks

### Stay alive long enough to find pull
- **When to use**: When you're building a startup around a risky technical bet and haven’t yet found strong market demand.
- **Steps**:
  1. Keep burn rate extremely low.
  2. Take many smart shots on goal instead of overcommitting early.
  3. Avoid scaling headcount or costs before you see real pull.
  4. Preserve runway so you can survive until the right market moment arrives.
  5. Once pull becomes undeniable, rapidly reorient around it.
- **Example**: StackBlitz spent years building WebContainer and stayed lean through the 2020–2021 hiring boom. That discipline kept the company alive long enough for Bolt to launch and catch the AI coding wave.

### Technology-first bet with delayed product fit
- **When to use**: When a new platform shift or infrastructure capability appears likely to matter, but the winning application is not yet obvious.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify a new enabling technology that could unlock a category shift.
  2. Build the hard underlying infrastructure before the market fully forms.
  3. Validate that the technical capability is uniquely differentiated.
  4. Experiment with multiple product directions on top of that infrastructure.
  5. Wait for an adjacent market change that makes the capability suddenly valuable.
- **Example**: StackBlitz built a browser-based operating system via WebContainer years before Bolt. Bolt only became viable once Claude Sonnet made AI-generated production-grade code good enough to exploit that infrastructure.

### High-context, low-headcount operating model
- **When to use**: When a small team needs to move extremely fast through hypergrowth or technical uncertainty.
- **Steps**:
  1. Hire people motivated by building great things, not titles.
  2. Favor fewer people with more context per person.
  3. Ensure individuals can operate front-to-back without heavy approvals.
  4. Build trust through long tenure and shared context.
  5. Use direct communication loops to minimize fidelity loss.
- **Example**: Eric described a core group of 5–7 people who had been together for 5+ years, with engineers handling support calls, fixing issues, and shipping UI changes without political overhead.

### Daily full-company sync for zero-fidelity communication
- **When to use**: During periods of extreme growth, constant fires, or rapid product iteration where misalignment is very costly.
- **Steps**:
  1. Bring the whole company together daily.
  2. Review everything front-to-back in real time.
  3. Surface issues, priorities, and tradeoffs openly.
  4. Let everyone absorb broad company context.
  5. Use that shared context so people can make better independent decisions between meetings.
- **Example**: After Bolt launched, the team met every day at 8:00 AM Pacific for at least an hour, with nearly the entire company on Zoom to review everything happening across the business.

### Prompt like a PM
- **When to use**: When non-technical users, especially PMs or founders, are using AI coding tools to build products.
- **Steps**:
  1. Treat the AI like a developer teammate.
  2. Write prompts like a Linear or JIRA ticket.
  3. Be specific about requirements that matter.
  4. Leave room for creativity where exactness is less important.
  5. Iterate with feedback and debugging rather than expecting perfect zero-shot output.
- **Example**: Eric advised PMs to talk to Bolt like they talk to a developer on their team, noting that strong PMs are often the most successful non-developer users because they know how to define scope and reduce ambiguity.

### Balance customer triage with founder-led bets
- **When to use**: When you're flooded with user requests but also see opportunities users are not yet explicitly asking for.
- **Steps**:
  1. Listen closely to customer pain and recurring requests.
  2. Reserve capacity for founder or team conviction bets.
  3. Ship capabilities users may not yet know to ask for.
  4. Use market reception to validate whether the bet was right.
  5. Continuously rebalance between reactive fixes and proactive innovation.
- **Example**: Native mobile app support was not a top explicit request, but Eric pushed for it anyway. It became the biggest thing Bolt had launched, with thousands of mobile apps created per day.

## Core Advice
- **When you face a long period without product-market pull**: keep burn low and maximize runway — because you need enough time and enough shots on goal to discover the right market moment.
- **When you face pressure to hire fast because “that’s what ambitious startups do”**: resist consensus hiring pressure unless demand clearly justifies it — because exuberant headcount growth can kill optionality.
- **When you face hypergrowth and communication breakdown risk**: run frequent, high-bandwidth syncs with the whole team — because shared context helps everyone make better independent decisions.
- **When you face a new AI product category and are unsure how to price it**: consider usage-based or inference-based pricing — because users will pay more when the ROI is tangible.
- **When you face a decision between building what users ask for and what they don’t yet imagine**: reserve room for conviction-led bets — because some of the biggest wins come from capabilities users cannot yet articulate.
- **When you face hiring for an early-stage, high-intensity startup**: prioritize people who care more about building great things than titles — because low ego and cross-functional willingness drive execution.
- **When you face using AI coding tools as a PM, founder, or designer**: write prompts like clear product specs or developer tickets — because the better you define scope, constraints, and outcomes, the better AI can execute.
- **When you face your first experience with an AI app builder and don’t know what to build**: start with a personal website using your LinkedIn bio and a few style preferences — because it’s a simple, high-success first project.
- **When you face deciding whether AI coding is only useful for prototypes**: test it on greenfield production use cases like internal tools, marketing sites, or new SaaS products — because real software can be built cheaply and quickly.
- **When you face uncertainty about what skills to build in an AI-first world**: focus on problem selection, clear articulation, product taste, and knowing how to get AI unstuck — because those become the scarce skills as building gets easier.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Startups should find the problem first and avoid building deep technology before clear demand exists. → **Their view**: A technology-first bet can work if the enabling capability is truly important and you survive long enough for the right market moment. — because StackBlitz built WebContainer years before Bolt, and the product fit emerged later.
- **Conventional**: Fast-growing startups should hire aggressively and scale headcount early. → **Their view**: Keep teams small, context-rich, and conservative on burn until demand is undeniable. — because overhiring can kill the company before the breakout moment.
- **Conventional**: Meetings are expensive and whole-company daily syncs are inefficient. → **Their view**: In hypergrowth, daily all-hands operational syncs can be worth it. — because they minimize communication loss when the company is moving too fast.
- **Conventional**: AI coding tools are mainly for developers. → **Their view**: PMs, designers, founders, and other non-developers may be the biggest beneficiaries. — because the key skill is specifying products clearly, not writing code.
- **Conventional**: Flat subscription pricing is best for AI tools because users want predictability. → **Their view**: For agentic coding products, users will accept higher, usage-linked pricing if the ROI is obvious. — because value and cost are tightly coupled.
- **Conventional**: PRDs are becoming obsolete because AI can turn rough ideas directly into prototypes. → **Their view**: Lightweight PRDs still matter, but should be minimal and often paired with live prototypes. — because a working demo communicates more than text.
- **Conventional**: College is the default path to a good career in technology. → **Their view**: Unless someone has strong conviction, it may be better to learn and explore cheaply rather than take on large debt. — because credentials do not guarantee outcomes, but motivation matters a lot.
