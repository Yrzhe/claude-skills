# Shishir Mehrotra

**Co-founder and CEO** | **Coda** | Expertise: product strategy, team rituals, growth loops, talent evaluation

## Bio
Shishir Mehrotra is the co-founder and CEO of Coda, and previously led product, engineering, and design for YouTube at Google after six years at Microsoft. He is known for first-principles thinking and for turning messy problems into practical frameworks around growth, organizational rituals, and hiring.

His perspective matters because he blends product strategy with operating-system thinking: how products grow, how teams make decisions, and how companies evaluate talent. He is especially useful when you need a crisp framework for a complex, ambiguous problem.

## Signature Frameworks

### Black Loop / Blue Loop growth model
- **When to use**: When designing growth for a platform or document-based product, especially if users can collaborate privately and publish publicly.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the core sharing behavior that causes users to create and invite others (Black Loop).
  2. Identify whether users can publish artifacts to the world in a way that creates discovery and external demand (Blue Loop).
  3. Design pricing and product friction so the share edge has no monetary barrier.
  4. Let published artifacts carry the product’s value proposition, not just the product’s marketing.
  5. Use the loop to attract different user types: builders, problem-solvers, and niche communities.
- **Example**: In Coda, a user can make a doc, share it with a team, and others may create more docs from it (Black Loop). Or they can publish it publicly in the gallery, where a niche doc like an Orange Theory workout tracker can spread and bring new users in (Blue Loop).

### Golden rituals framework
- **When to use**: When defining the operating system of a company and making culture legible to every employee.
- **Steps**:
  1. Choose a small number of rituals that represent how the company works.
  2. Name each ritual so it can become part of identity and memory.
  3. Ensure every employee learns the ritual by their first Friday.
  4. Templatize the ritual so it is easy to repeat consistently.
  5. Use the ritual to reinforce the desired culture and decision-making norms.
- **Example**: At Coda, Dory/Pulse is the golden ritual: people write opinions before seeing others’ responses, and questions are upvoted/downvoted before discussion.

### Switch-based ritual design
- **When to use**: When trying to introduce or change a team ritual or behavior.
- **Steps**:
  1. Direct the rider: explicitly tell people what the ritual is and how to do it.
  2. Motivate the elephant: give the ritual a compelling name and identity.
  3. Shape the path: create templates and defaults that make the ritual easy to follow.
  4. Embed the ritual in onboarding and recurring team processes.
- **Example**: Coda added a role column to Pulse after borrowing from Coinbase’s RAPIDS-style decision framing, which reduced confusion about whether Pulse was consensus-building or decision-making.

### Eigenquestion
- **When to use**: When facing a complex decision with many downstream implications and you need to simplify the problem space.
- **Steps**:
  1. List the candidate questions you think you need to answer.
  2. Ask which question, if answered, would answer the most other questions.
  3. Reframe the discussion around that higher-order question.
  4. Use the answer to collapse many tactical decisions into one strategic choice.
- **Example**: At YouTube, instead of debating whether to link out to ABC.com for Modern Family, the team asked whether the online video market would value consistency or comprehensiveness. Once they chose consistency, the linking decision became obvious.

### PSHE talent framework
- **When to use**: When evaluating product talent, and more broadly when leveling or assessing roles that vary by seniority and judgment.
- **Steps**:
  1. Assess Problem: can the person identify the right problem?
  2. Assess Solution: can the person generate the right solution?
  3. Assess How: can the person design the process, rituals, and execution plan?
  4. Assess Execution: can the person reliably carry out the plan?
  5. Map the role to the level where the person is strongest, recognizing that seniority shifts from execution toward problem selection.
- **Example**: A junior PM may be given a problem, solution, and how; a more senior PM may be given a problem and rough solution; a top PM may be expected to identify the problem themselves.

### Home court / away court / neutral court interviewing
- **When to use**: When designing interviews that fairly assess candidates without over-indexing on company-specific knowledge or self-reported stories.
- **Steps**:
  1. Use neutral-court questions for general problem-solving ability.
  2. Use away-court questions to let candidates showcase their own work and strengths.
  3. Use home-court questions sparingly because they can simply test whether the candidate agrees with your existing answer.
  4. Balance all three so you can see both raw ability and real-world execution.
- **Example**: Shishir’s teleporter question is neutral court; the candidate presentation at the start of the loop is away court.

## Core Advice
- **When you are trying to understand how your product grows**: Do not think only in funnels; map the loops that actually cause the product to spread — because most products grow through repeated feedback loops, not a linear acquisition-to-retention funnel.
- **When your product has a share moment**: Remove pricing friction at the share edge so users can share freely — because charging at the moment of sharing suppresses the very behavior that drives growth.
- **When you build a horizontal product that can feel intimidating to new users**: Create a publishing or template-driven entry point that starts from a real problem, not a blank canvas — because most people are problem-solvers, not blank-page builders.
- **When you want users to discover your product through content**: Let the artifact itself teach the value of the product and spread through niche communities — because people often share ideas, templates, or workflows for their own reasons.
- **When your team is debating a hard strategic choice**: Find the eigenquestion before debating the tactical options — because a single higher-order question can collapse many downstream decisions and prevent endless argument.
- **When you are designing a company ritual**: Name it, template it, and make sure every employee learns it early — because named, templated rituals become part of culture and are easier to repeat and remember.
- **When a meeting is dominated by loud voices or groupthink**: Use silent written input first, then reveal responses and discuss — because writing before discussion reduces bias and gives quieter people equal footing.
- **When you need to make a decision with many stakeholders**: Explicitly label each person’s role in the decision and what input you need from them — because this prevents confusion between informers, approvers, and deciders and reduces consensus creep.
- **When you are evaluating candidates or employees**: Use reference checks as your strongest signal, not just interviews — because people who have worked with the candidate for years can reveal judgment, problem selection, and execution quality far better than a short interview.
- **When you are running interviews**: Include a real work exercise and a candidate presentation, not just abstract questions — because work samples and presentations reveal how people think, communicate, and prioritize in a more realistic setting.
- **When you are leveling or promoting people**: Judge them by how they operate at their level of PSHE, not just by scope or quota — because scope can be an input, not an output; the real difference between levels is often judgment, problem selection, and execution style.
- **When you want to learn a framework like eigenquestions or PSHE**: Practice it in low-stakes situations first — because people learn these skills better when they are not under pressure and can experiment freely.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Growth should be modeled as a funnel. → **Their view**: Most products actually grow through loops, and understanding the loop is more important than optimizing a linear funnel — because users often create, share, and re-create in cycles.
- **Conventional**: Document and productivity products should charge for editors or collaborators. → **Their view**: Charge only when someone creates a document, not when they share or edit — because the share moment is the product’s growth moment.
- **Conventional**: The best way to resolve a hard decision is to debate the immediate tactical choice. → **Their view**: First identify the eigenquestion that answers the most downstream questions — because reframing the problem at a higher level often makes the tactical decision obvious.
- **Conventional**: Scope is the cleanest way to level people. → **Their view**: Scope is often an input, not an output; seniority should be judged by PSHE and judgment — because two people can have the same scope but very different impact.
- **Conventional**: Interviews are the best way to evaluate candidates. → **Their view**: Reference checks are usually the strongest signal — because references have seen the person over time and can speak to real behavior.
- **Conventional**: Consensus is always good in decision-making. → **Their view**: Consensus-building can become a liability if it blurs who actually decides — because rituals need explicit roles and structure.
