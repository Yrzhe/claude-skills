# Bob Moesta

**Co-founder and CEO** | **The Rewired Group** | Expertise: jobs-to-be-done, product strategy, go-to-market, user-research

## Bio
Bob Moesta is the co-creator of Jobs to Be Done with Clay Christensen and one of the most practical voices in product strategy and customer research. He has worked on thousands of products and services, and his perspective matters because he focuses on how people actually switch, choose, and make progress—not how they say they think.

He is especially valuable when teams need to understand real demand, improve conversion, or uncover why customers churn. His approach turns JTBD from a theory into a usable method for product design, sales, positioning, and innovation.

## Signature Frameworks

### Jobs to Be Done (context and outcome lens)
- **When to use**: When you need to understand why customers choose, switch to, or abandon a product.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with a recent switching event or purchase.
  2. Reconstruct the story of what happened before the decision.
  3. Identify the context and the outcome the customer wanted.
  4. Map the forces: push, pull, anxiety, and habit.
  5. Define the progress the customer is hiring the product to make.
- **Example**: Snickers may compete with coffee or a sandwich when someone needs fuel to keep working, while Milky Way may compete with wine or a brownie after an emotional event.

### Forces of Progress
- **When to use**: When diagnosing adoption, churn, or purchase hesitation.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the push from the current situation.
  2. Identify the pull of a better future.
  3. Identify anxieties about the new solution.
  4. Identify habits that keep people in place.
  5. Judge whether push and pull outweigh anxiety and habit.
  6. Reduce friction or strengthen motivation accordingly.
- **Example**: In Bob’s housing business, buyers wanted to downsize but canceled because moving and belongings created too much friction. Adding moving services and two years of storage increased sales by over 30%.

### Six Phases of Buying
- **When to use**: When designing sales, onboarding, or education to match the buyer’s stage.
- **Steps**:
  1. First thought: the need first appears.
  2. Passive looking: the buyer is problem-aware.
  3. Active looking: the buyer compares options.
  4. Deciding: the buyer makes trade-offs.
  5. First use: the buyer tries the solution.
  6. Ongoing use: the buyer builds a habit.
- **Example**: Autobooks cut the sales cycle roughly in half and increased conversion 4x by tailoring demos to whether buyers were learning, comparing, or deciding.

### Switch Interview Method
- **When to use**: When you need customer research that explains causal behavior, not just opinions.
- **Steps**:
  1. Focus on a specific recent switch or purchase.
  2. Interview people who actually made the change.
  3. Ask for the story, not abstract preferences.
  4. Probe for pushes, pulls, anxieties, habits, and trade-offs.
  5. Follow the most revealing thread instead of a rigid script.
  6. Cluster stories into pathways.
- **Example**: Interview 10 recent buyers and ask what was happening, what they hoped for, what worried them, and what they gave up. Patterns often repeat after 7–8 interviews.

### Demand-Side Sales
- **When to use**: When sales is underperforming because the process is organized around the company, not the buyer.
- **Steps**:
  1. Map the customer’s buying timeline.
  2. Identify where each prospect is in that timeline.
  3. Give information that fits that stage.
  4. Help the buyer make progress instead of pitching.
  5. Reduce uncertainty and friction at each step.
- **Example**: Autobooks replaced one standard demo with stage-specific demos, improving conversion and shortening the cycle.

### Cluster by pathways, not segments
- **When to use**: When demographic segmentation is too blunt to explain different use cases.
- **Steps**:
  1. Collect switching stories.
  2. Extract the forces in each story.
  3. Look for recurring combinations of forces.
  4. Define jobs/pathways from those patterns.
  5. Adjust packaging, messaging, and pricing by pathway.
- **Example**: Intercom found four distinct reasons customers hired the product, then turned off irrelevant features and positioned against the real alternatives.

## Core Advice
- **When you want to know why customers buy**: interview recent buyers about the story before the purchase — because switching stories reveal causality better than opinions.
- **When customers complain but don’t leave**: don’t treat complaints as demand — because “Bitchin’ ain’t switchin’.”
- **When sales conversion is weak**: match the demo to the buyer’s stage — because learning, comparing, and deciding require different information.
- **When roadmap priorities keep changing**: organize around struggling moments — because those are more stable than feature ideas.
- **When launching a new product**: study the alternatives people already use — because new products usually win by replacing something, not by creating a totally new job.
- **When churn rises**: interview churned customers about what changed in their context — because churn is a switching event with visible forces.
- **When adoption is low**: reduce friction and anxiety before adding features — because more functionality can increase uncertainty.
- **When research yields vague answers**: push for concrete stories and trade-offs — because words like “easy” and “fast” are too abstract to guide decisions.
- **When one product serves many needs**: identify distinct pathways — because one-size-fits-all messaging creates bloated products and confused positioning.
- **When the team is skeptical of JTBD**: start with one painful problem and prove it — because small wins spread more effectively than theory.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Great products create demand through better features. → **Their view**: Demand starts with a struggling moment in the customer’s life — because people buy when they need progress, not when a product exists.
- **Conventional**: JTBD is mostly about pain points and gains. → **Their view**: JTBD is about context and outcome — because the same pain means different things in different situations.
- **Conventional**: Add features to improve adoption. → **Their view**: Reduce friction, anxiety, and habit costs — because switching only happens when the forces of progress outweigh the forces of inertia.
- **Conventional**: You can define jobs in a strategy workshop. → **Their view**: You need interviews with real switchers — because the important details are usually messy and non-obvious.
- **Conventional**: If customers ask for a feature, build it. → **Their view**: Requests are not the same as trade-offs — because what people say they want often differs from what they actually choose.
- **Conventional**: Competitors are similar products. → **Their view**: Competitors are whatever customers compare when trying to make progress — because the real alternative may be a completely different category.
- **Conventional**: People switch jobs mainly for money. → **Their view**: People switch for progress — because money is only one part of the trade-off.
