# David Singleton

**Chief Technology Officer** | **Stripe** | Expertise: engineering leadership, product development, design craft, developer productivity

## Bio
David Singleton was CTO of Stripe during the recording, where he led engineering and design with an unusually product-minded, craft-driven approach. He previously served as VP of Engineering at Google, and his perspective matters because he combines deep technical leadership with a strong belief that great products come from close user contact, rigorous systems, and high standards for quality.

## Signature Frameworks

### Co-create with early users
- **When to use**: When building a new product or entering an adjacent space where a small set of sophisticated users can shape the solution.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify users already pushing the boundaries of the problem.
  2. Build direct relationships with the operators using the product.
  3. Create tight feedback loops, like shared Slack channels and frequent demos.
  4. Iterate with this alpha group until they are extremely happy.
  5. Expand only after the product is clearly working for them.
- **Example**: Stripe Billing was co-created with early users like Figma and Slack, who already used Stripe and had subscription businesses.

### Friction logging
- **When to use**: When you want to systematically improve product quality, UX, or consistency across many teams.
- **Steps**:
  1. Choose a specific user persona.
  2. Walk through the end-to-end flow as that user.
  3. Write a stream-of-consciousness log.
  4. Note friction, confusion, delay, and unnecessary effort.
  5. Also note what works well.
  6. Route findings to the right teams and prioritize fixes.
- **Example**: Singleton onboarded himself as a new Stripe user monthly, wrote a friction log, and tagged teams with issues and praise.

### Walk the Store
- **When to use**: When aligning teams around product quality, UX, and shared craft standards.
- **Steps**:
  1. Bring together product and cross-functional partners.
  2. Walk through a critical flow as a specific user.
  3. Let participants log issues in real time.
  4. Debrief together afterward.
  5. Use the session to reinforce quality standards and shared language.
- **Example**: Stripe used Friday Fireside sessions to walk the company through important product flows and discuss user needs.

### Engineercation
- **When to use**: When leaders need firsthand understanding of developer experience and tooling friction.
- **Steps**:
  1. Clear several consecutive days.
  2. Join a team as if you were an engineer.
  3. Pick a small feature that can go to production.
  4. Use the same tools, docs, review, and deployment path.
  5. Keep a friction log.
  6. Share the write-up to drive improvements.
- **Example**: Singleton spent 3–4 days shipping a small feature with a team and documenting tooling, docs, and deployment pain.

### Structured hiring loops with real-work simulations
- **When to use**: When hiring for roles where judgment, collaboration, and craft matter more than interview tricks.
- **Steps**:
  1. Use a consistent, structured interview loop.
  2. Design exercises that resemble actual work.
  3. Avoid trick questions.
  4. Let candidates collaborate naturally and use tools like Google.
  5. Compare candidates against a calibrated spectrum of responses.
- **Example**: Engineers pair-program in interviews; PMs do written exercises based on realistic product problems.

### Reliability through continuous delivery plus systematic learning
- **When to use**: When you need speed and trust in a high-stakes environment.
- **Steps**:
  1. Invest in automated test coverage.
  2. Validate changes through tests, staging, and gradual rollout.
  3. Deploy continuously instead of batching releases.
  4. Respond quickly to incidents.
  5. Review incidents deeply and remediate root causes.
  6. Prioritize remediations over roadmap work when needed.
- **Example**: Stripe deploys to its core API many times per day while maintaining very high uptime.

### Inverted-W planning
- **When to use**: When planning across a large org that needs both bottom-up insight and top-down coherence.
- **Steps**:
  1. Have teams propose their most important work.
  2. Leaders synthesize those inputs into a draft strategy.
  3. Send strategy back down for team adjustments.
  4. Bring plans back up for another synthesis pass.
  5. Push final direction back down with context.
- **Example**: Stripe uses this annually, with a lighter midyear version, to align on user needs and priorities.

## Core Advice
- **When you’re unsure what to build next**: Co-create with a small set of early users — because real users reduce guesswork better than internal debate.
- **When you need better product quality without slowing down**: Do friction logging on the highest-stakes journeys — because not all polish matters equally, and the biggest pain points compound.
- **When engineers feel disconnected from users**: Put engineers in the feedback loop — because product-minded engineers make better decisions and reduce handoff overhead.
- **When interviews feel artificial**: Use structured loops with realistic simulations — because you learn more from actual work than from puzzles.
- **When quality issues keep derailing the roadmap**: Reserve explicit capacity for polish and remediations — because quality debt eventually costs more than planned investment.
- **When leading a large org from too far away**: Periodically ship something yourself — because firsthand experience reveals friction dashboards miss.
- **When iteration is too slow**: Shorten the loop from feedback to production — because fast learning beats speculation.
- **When developer productivity is slipping**: Make it easy to report pain inside the tools — because tiny paper cuts become major drag at scale.
- **When scaling management**: Hire people you trust and delegate more than feels comfortable — because scale depends on independent judgment.
- **When values feel like slogans**: Turn them into recurring practices — because values only shape behavior when embedded in rituals and planning.
- **When using AI in a sensitive business**: Prefer internal tooling and shared presets — because governance and safety matter as much as experimentation.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Engineers should mainly implement; PMs own product thinking. → **Their view**: Engineers should be deeply product-minded and close to users — because Stripe’s best products were co-created by engineers who understood the domain.
- **Conventional**: Reliability requires moving slowly. → **Their view**: You can move fast and stay highly reliable — because reliability comes from systems, testing, and rollout discipline.
- **Conventional**: Craft is a luxury. → **Their view**: Craft in the right places is a growth lever — because better checkout experiences materially improved revenue.
- **Conventional**: Hiring should be a machine. → **Their view**: Important hiring should be personal, manager-led, and patient — because the best hires are too important for a transactional process.
- **Conventional**: Leaders should stay out of the weeds. → **Their view**: Leaders need periodic direct exposure to the work — because strategy without operational understanding is brittle.
- **Conventional**: Puzzles are the best interview signal. → **Their view**: Realistic work simulations and references are better — because they reflect actual performance, not interview theater.
