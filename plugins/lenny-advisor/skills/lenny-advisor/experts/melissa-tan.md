# Melissa Tan

**Growth leader and advisor** | **Webflow (formerly Dropbox; now returning to advising)** | Expertise: growth, go-to-market, pricing-packaging, team leadership

## Bio
Melissa Tan led growth at Webflow and was Dropbox’s first growth PM, giving her a rare view into growth across both product-led and sales-led motions. Her perspective matters because she combines hands-on experimentation with strong judgment about team design, pricing, and cross-functional execution—especially in early-stage and post-PMF companies.

## Signature Frameworks

### First-principles growth problem solving
- **When to use**: When building or optimizing growth motions, especially in unfamiliar contexts or when standard playbooks don’t seem to fit.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start from your own data and customer conversations instead of copying generic best practices.
  2. Ask probing questions to understand the real problem and constraints.
  3. Build a mental model of what is happening and which levers matter.
  4. Form hypotheses based on your product’s specific context.
  5. Test, learn, and update the model as new information comes in.
- **Example**: At Dropbox, the team learned that standard growth tactics were not enough; they had to re-execute in a more user-centric, hypothesis-driven way.

### Hiring for high-performing teams
- **When to use**: When building teams that need both innovation and strong cross-functional execution.
- **Steps**:
  1. Screen for first-principles or critical thinking, not just prior experience.
  2. Screen for humility, collaboration, and team orientation.
  3. Use live problem solving to observe how candidates think in real time.
  4. Include a presentation exercise to assess prepared thinking and communication.
  5. Give feedback before the presentation to see how candidates incorporate it.
  6. Evaluate growth mindset by watching how they respond to feedback.
- **Example**: For PM hiring, Melissa asks candidates to review a real company or pricing page, then gives feedback on their draft before the final interview.

### Flying formation for cross-functional growth
- **When to use**: When multiple teams need to work together on growth, revenue, or product changes and ownership is unclear.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define who is Driver, Accountable, Contributor, and Informed.
  2. Clarify which team owns which metrics and outcomes.
  3. Set operating rhythms like weekly metric reviews and quarterly planning.
  4. Document collaboration rules and handoffs.
  5. Use the structure to prevent overlap, confusion, and decision paralysis.
- **Example**: At Webflow, product growth owned downstream metrics from signups, while growth marketing owned signups and CAC; the teams met weekly to review metrics and initiatives.

### Developing talent through the full lifecycle
- **When to use**: When managing people you want to grow into stronger performers and long-term collaborators.
- **Steps**:
  1. Hire for mutual fit and growth mindset.
  2. Ramp quickly by clarifying success in the first 90 days.
  3. Create early wins and visibility opportunities.
  4. Give direct, specific feedback tied to observed behavior and impact.
  5. Offer support and coaching while holding high standards.
  6. Maintain the relationship over time, even after they leave.
- **Example**: Melissa often has new hires present early, then uses Loom videos and recurring feedback to help them improve and gain visibility.

### Building ownership on a team
- **When to use**: When a team is scaling and you want people to act like owners rather than task executors.
- **Steps**:
  1. Give each person a meaningful scope they can own independently.
  2. Define problems or areas, not tiny tasks.
  3. Encourage people to exhaust options when blocked by other teams.
  4. Model ownership by taking responsibility as the leader.
  5. Make accountability explicit when things go wrong.
- **Example**: Melissa pushed back on simply doubling the growth team because she wanted each PM to own a meaty problem area, not a narrow slice of the website.

## Core Advice
- **When deciding between product-led growth and sales-led growth**: choose the motion that best matches onboarding complexity, virality, and enterprise needs — because the product’s shape should determine the motion.
- **When product-led usage starts attracting larger customers**: treat enterprise feature requests as a signal to build SSO, security, and packaging — because self-serve usage reveals expansion demand.
- **When launching growth work**: start from customer data and hypotheses, not generic playbooks — because context matters more than copied tactics.
- **When an experiment is too small to matter**: right-size it to company stage and metric leverage — because early-stage teams need bigger swings.
- **When changing a product flow or checkout**: change one thing at a time and define a clear hypothesis — because otherwise you won’t know what caused the result.
- **When hiring your first growth person**: prioritize analytical ability, curiosity, and first-principles thinking over channel expertise — because early growth is discovery, not optimization.
- **When pre-PMF**: have everyone think about growth and focus on PMF, ICP, and GTM motion — because the company is still learning who it serves and how it should sell.
- **When you need activation insight but lack testing volume**: run qualitative user tests with a handful of target users — because five sessions can expose major friction quickly.
- **When building a team-first culture**: make goals explicit but reinforce collaboration — because measurement can create unhealthy competition.
- **When giving difficult feedback**: state your intention, describe the behavior and impact, and offer support — because people respond better when feedback is clearly meant to help.
- **When building a mentor relationship**: ask for advice or a conversation, not for formal mentorship — because organic relationships are easier to start and sustain.
- **When you’re overextended because you’re capable**: set a time box and hand off work after the agreed period — because ownership should not become scope creep.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Growth teams should follow established best practices and proven playbooks. → **Their view**: Build the strategy from your own data, users, and context — because generic tactics often fail when the product or market is different.
- **Conventional**: Deep expertise in a channel is the best qualification for a first growth hire. → **Their view**: Analytical thinking and curiosity matter more early on — because the first job is figuring out what works.
- **Conventional**: A good product should sell itself, so GTM can come later. → **Their view**: Go-to-market, monetization, and pricing should be considered from the start — because retrofitting them later is harder.
- **Conventional**: Hiring experienced outsiders is the fastest way to scale. → **Their view**: Developing internal talent often creates stronger long-term performance — because context and institutional knowledge compound.
- **Conventional**: Mentorship should be formalized by asking someone to be your mentor. → **Their view**: The best mentorship is organic and built through real work — because formal asks create pressure.
- **Conventional**: Ownership means doing as much as possible yourself. → **Their view**: Ownership is about meaningful scope, proactive problem solving, and accountability — because hoarding work reduces focus.
