# Jason Shah

**Product leader at Alchemy** | **Alchemy** | Expertise: product-management, leadership, design, go-to-market

## Bio
Jason Shah is a product leader and founder who has worked across Yammer, Microsoft, Amazon, Airbnb, and Alchemy. His perspective matters because he blends product craft, business strategy, hiring, and leadership into one operating model, especially in fast-moving or ambiguous environments like Web3.

He is especially useful when teams need clarity on what to build, how to influence senior leaders, how to hire in uncertain conditions, or how to think about careers beyond title-chasing.

## Signature Frameworks

### Amazon Working Backwards / PRFAQ
- **When to use**: When evaluating a new product, launch, or strategic initiative and you need clarity on customer value and execution.
- **Steps**:
  1. Write a press release that clearly announces the product.
  2. Describe the customer problem in plain language.
  3. Explain the solution and how it solves the problem.
  4. Add a realistic customer quote.
  5. Add a leadership quote tying the launch to strategy.
  6. Include rollout details and a call to action.
  7. Use specific language and numbers, not vague adjectives.
  8. Review FAQs to surface risks and objections.
- **Example**: Shah describes Amazon’s PRFAQ discipline, including the rule to avoid words like “great” and instead write concretely about outcomes, such as saving customers time.

### Reframing “Pushback” as Goal Alignment
- **When to use**: When you disagree with a founder, CEO, or senior leader and need to redirect without creating conflict.
- **Steps**:
  1. Stop thinking in terms of “pushback” or saying no.
  2. Identify the leader’s real goal beneath the proposed solution.
  3. Find the shared objective.
  4. Reframe your idea around that shared goal.
  5. Offer a better path with less cost, scope, or risk.
- **Example**: At Airbnb, instead of arguing against a concierge-style chat product for Luxury Retreats, the team reframed the goal around a “magical” luxury experience and introduced “trip designers,” leading to a simpler direction.

### Ladder vs. Map Career Framework
- **When to use**: When deciding between title/status optimization and pursuing meaningful, energizing work.
- **Steps**:
  1. Ask whether you’re optimizing for a ladder or a map.
  2. Define what matters at the macro level.
  3. Choose opportunities based on curiosity and meaning.
  4. Accept discomfort and ambiguity as part of growth.
  5. Balance exploration with enough commitment to build depth.
- **Example**: Shah used this mindset when moving to San Francisco without a job, leaving Yammer to start do.com, and later moving into Web3 because the path felt more interesting than conventional progression.

### Hiring as Marketing + Sales + Product
- **When to use**: When building teams in fast-growing or ambiguous environments.
- **Steps**:
  1. Treat hiring as marketing by building an authentic brand.
  2. Treat hiring as sales by understanding what candidates want.
  3. Treat hiring as product by iterating on roles and job descriptions.
  4. Design roles around business needs and candidate strengths.
  5. Optimize for post-hire success, not just headcount.
- **Example**: At Alchemy, Shah meets strong candidates before knowing the exact role, then shapes opportunities flexibly instead of relying on static job descriptions.

### Problem-First Product Thinking
- **When to use**: When prioritizing product bets or aligning teams on strategy.
- **Steps**:
  1. Clarify the actual problem before debating solutions.
  2. Use the problem definition to guide scope and prioritization.
  3. Check whether the team is aligned on the problem.
  4. Revisit the problem when trade-offs get unclear.
  5. Let the problem statement define success.
- **Example**: At Alchemy, Shah frames choices like SDK versus NFT API around the underlying problem: improving developer experience versus supporting NFT marketplace growth.

## Core Advice
- **When morale is low in a downturn**: create visible progress through shipping and customer traction — because morale comes from momentum, not speeches.
- **When you disagree with a founder or executive**: understand their real goal and reframe your alternative around it — because direct opposition creates defensiveness.
- **When a product idea is fuzzy**: write the press release and FAQs first — because working backwards forces clarity before building.
- **When product writing feels vague**: remove adjectives and replace them with concrete outcomes — because specific writing reflects specific thinking.
- **When choosing a career move**: ask whether it fits your life map, not just the next rung — because title optimization often misses long-term meaning.
- **When hiring in ambiguity**: iterate on the role and candidate fit like a product — because rigid job descriptions miss great talent.
- **When senior leaders are tempted to stay abstract**: stay close to the details that matter — because craft and accountability improve quality.
- **When debating product options**: define the problem first — because solution debates are unproductive without shared framing.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Pushback means disagreeing forcefully or saying no → **Their view**: The best pushback is reframing around shared goals — because alignment is more effective than opposition.
- **Conventional**: Senior leaders should stay out of the weeds → **Their view**: Great leaders are deeply in the details — because detail involvement improves quality and respect.
- **Conventional**: Career success is a ladder of titles and prestige → **Their view**: A meaningful career is more like a map — because curiosity and energy often create better long-term outcomes.
- **Conventional**: Hiring should match candidates to fixed roles → **Their view**: Hiring should be iterative and product-like — because flexibility helps companies find exceptional fit.
- **Conventional**: Web3 doesn’t need strong PMs early → **Their view**: PM becomes a competitive advantage as markets mature — because complexity and competition demand prioritization and strategy.
