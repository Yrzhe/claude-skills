## Signature Frameworks

### Skills-first hiring
- **When to use**: When title-based matching is too rigid, especially during labor market shifts or career transitions.
- **Steps**:
  1. Translate a candidate’s experience into skills, not just titles.
  2. Define roles by required capabilities.
  3. Match candidates to roles based on overlapping skills.
  4. Use learning products to close remaining gaps.
  5. Surface skills and evidence to recruiters.
- **Example**: During COVID, LinkedIn mapped hospitality workers to customer service roles by identifying transferable skills like de-escalation and negotiation.

### North Star decision-making: connect people to economic opportunity
- **When to use**: When making tradeoffs in a complex ecosystem with many teams and products.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with the mission: does this connect people to economic opportunity?
  2. Clarify the user intent.
  3. Choose the option that removes the most friction.
  4. Use the mission as a tie-breaker.
  5. Measure outcomes by real opportunity created.
- **Example**: Hari said his first product review improved once he reframed the work around LinkedIn’s mission rather than isolated product details.

### RAPID decision framework
- **When to use**: When multiple teams are involved and decision rights are unclear.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the recommender.
  2. Identify who must agree.
  3. Identify who provides input.
  4. Assign one decision-maker.
  5. Escalate quickly if stalled.
- **Example**: In LinkedIn’s interconnected ecosystem, a single named decision-maker prevents slow, ambiguous coordination.

### Five-day alignment rule
- **When to use**: When blockers or escalations are slowing execution.
- **Steps**:
  1. Raise the blocker to the manager quickly.
  2. Expect resolution or alignment within five days.
  3. If unresolved, escalate upward.
  4. Use the rule to create accountability.
- **Example**: Hari described this as a management mechanism to keep issues from lingering indefinitely.

### PM skill triangle
- **When to use**: When evaluating PM talent or helping someone break into product.
- **Steps**:
  1. Assess creativity/product sense.
  2. Assess data and pattern recognition.
  3. Assess general management and leadership.
  4. Identify the strongest edge.
  5. Target roles that fit that edge.
- **Example**: Hari said great PMs usually excel on the edges of the triangle, not in the center.

### Thinking in loops
- **When to use**: When designing products that need self-reinforcing growth.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the core value.
  2. Map downstream actions it creates.
  3. Design feedback into the system.
  4. Measure loop health.
  5. Refine using case studies and postmortems.
- **Example**: LinkedIn teaches PMs to design products so value cascades and feeds future growth.

### Idea validation via pain-point prioritization
- **When to use**: When deciding whether a new product idea is worth building.
- **Steps**:
  1. Prove the problem is real.
  2. Look for “duct tape” workarounds.
  3. List candidate solutions.
  4. Test whether the pain is acute and broad.
  5. Validate with data and clear rationale.
- **Example**: Hari teaches teams to first prove users are hacking together solutions before prioritizing an idea.

## Core Advice
- **When the PM job market is tight**: build relationships and make your capabilities visible — because cold applications are weaker when competition rises.
- **When job searching on LinkedIn**: use Open to Work, specify exact roles, and signal interest to target companies — because recruiters respond to clear intent.
- **When competing for PM roles**: attach evidence to your skills — because recruiters can evaluate proof, not just titles.
- **When transitioning into a new PM role or industry**: target roles where you have industry knowledge — because domain familiarity lowers hiring risk.
- **When product decisions are ambiguous**: anchor to a North Star — because a shared mission simplifies tradeoffs.
- **When cross-functional work stalls**: assign one decision-maker and escalate quickly — because unclear ownership slows execution.
- **When email threads drag on**: pick up the phone after three exchanges — because synchronous discussion surfaces the real disagreement faster.
- **When you want to become a stronger PM**: keep building things outside your day job — because builder muscle improves with repetition.
- **When you’re unsure of your PM strengths**: identify your place on the skill triangle — because great PMs win by leaning into a distinctive edge.
- **When planning in a large org**: define top priorities before bottom-up planning — because it reduces churn and makes tradeoffs explicit.
- **When product reviews get bloated**: shorten them and focus on the problem first — because long reviews often hide lack of clarity.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Hiring should be based mainly on titles and prior roles → **Their view**: hiring should be based on transferable skills and evidence — because title-based matching misses qualified people.
- **Conventional**: Market cooling means hiring returns to the old normal → **Their view**: skills-first and values-based behaviors may persist — because recent shifts appear structural, not temporary.
- **Conventional**: Great PMs are balanced generalists → **Their view**: great PMs usually stand out on the edges of the triangle — because exceptional combinations beat average breadth.
- **Conventional**: Large product companies win mostly through visible features and optimization → **Their view**: invisible infrastructure and systemic changes can matter more — because they reshape the marketplace itself.
- **Conventional**: Publicly signaling job seeking is risky → **Their view**: explicit job intent can be valuable and normalized — because it creates strong recruiter signals.
