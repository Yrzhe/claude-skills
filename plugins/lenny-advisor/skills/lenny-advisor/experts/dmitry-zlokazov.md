# Dmitry Zlokazov

**Head of Product** | **Revolut** | Expertise: product-management, leadership, go-to-market, design, operations, strategy

## Bio
Dmitry Zlokazov is the global Head of Product at Revolut, the fintech super app serving 50M+ customers across 50+ countries. His perspective matters because he has helped build a product organization designed for speed, ownership, and scale in one of the most complex environments in tech: regulated financial services across many markets.

He is especially known for Revolut’s “local CEO” product culture, where product owners are accountable for outcomes end-to-end, not just roadmaps. He combines high standards for execution and design with a strong bias toward deep ownership, direct customer contact, and systematic scaling.

## Signature Frameworks

### Local CEO product ownership model
- **When to use**: When you want product managers to operate with full business ownership rather than as roadmap coordinators.
- **Steps**:
  1. Create fully cross-functional teams with engineering, design, data, and operations embedded.
  2. Make the product owner the line manager for the team, responsible for what gets done.
  3. Use functional managers to ensure craft quality and how work is done.
  4. Hold the product owner accountable for customer outcomes, business metrics, roadmap, and execution.
  5. Give teams autonomy, but review outputs regularly with senior leadership and founders.
- **Example**: At Revolut, product owners run cross-functional teams, own a product area end-to-end, tie roadmaps to metrics, and present progress directly to founders.

### Zoom in, then systematize
- **When to use**: When tackling highly complex, regulated, multi-step operational problems that must later scale across markets.
- **Steps**:
  1. Go very deep on one concrete instance of the problem.
  2. Reverse engineer what worked and what failed in that instance.
  3. Zoom back out to identify the repeatable pattern.
  4. Formalize the process into an algorithmic framework with steps, quality gates, staffing needs, and timing.
  5. Reuse the framework to scale execution across many markets or teams.
- **Example**: Revolut used launches in Ireland and the Netherlands to learn how to open local bank branches, then turned that learning into a repeatable launch framework for more countries.

### New bets framework
- **When to use**: When evaluating and incubating new product ideas inside an existing company.
- **Steps**:
  1. Allow ideas to come from anyone, not just product leaders.
  2. Require a clear market opportunity and business case.
  3. Show why the company can win versus competitors.
  4. Define the customer problem and initial product concept.
  5. Get a quick green light with minimal bureaucracy.
  6. Staff a small lean team to build a narrow first version quickly.
  7. Iterate based on feedback, but do not scale until quality, retention, and core metrics are strong.
- **Example**: Dmitry described this approach for products like joint accounts and RevPoints, where a small team launched quickly, polished the experience, and then scaled to Revolut’s large customer base.

### Narrow scope, never compromise on quality
- **When to use**: When launching an MVP or early-stage product and trying to distinguish between a bad idea and bad execution.
- **Steps**:
  1. Reduce the first version to only the most critical functionality.
  2. Keep the initial audience small if needed.
  3. Invest heavily in UX, polish, speed, and aesthetics from the start.
  4. Launch and gather feedback only after the experience is genuinely lovable.
  5. Use traction and retention signals to judge the idea itself, not the sloppiness of the implementation.
- **Example**: Revolut avoids the ambiguity of a scrappy MVP by insisting on wow-quality even in v1, so a lack of traction is more likely to mean the idea is wrong, not that the product “sucks.”

### Selective deep-dive leadership
- **When to use**: When leaders oversee too many simultaneous projects to review everything deeply, but still want high standards and accountability.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the 7-10 most impactful projects at a given time.
  2. Go extremely deep into those projects, down to implementation details.
  3. Use those reviews to clarify company priorities and quality expectations.
  4. Let teams operate autonomously unless evidence suggests they are off track.
  5. Rotate focus areas over time so teams know any area may be scrutinized next.
- **Example**: Dmitry said he may sit with engineers and read code on a few critical projects, which raises discipline across the organization without micromanaging everyone.

## Core Advice
- **When building stronger product leaders**: Structure product roles around end-to-end ownership — because it develops general-management judgment, execution muscle, and founder-like accountability.
- **When facing a complex problem with hidden dependencies**: Go extremely deep on one real example first, then abstract it into a repeatable process — because deep investigation reveals root causes and edge cases.
- **When uncertain whether a new product idea is good**: Launch a narrow but highly polished first version instead of a broad, scrappy MVP — because quality helps isolate idea risk from execution risk.
- **When hiring for product roles**: Prioritize raw intellect, hunger to build, and hands-on execution over years of experience — because motivated builders often solve problems faster and challenge the status quo more effectively.
- **When trying to understand customers deeply**: Give product managers direct access to users instead of routing all research through intermediaries — because filtered summaries lose emotional nuance and context.
- **When too many projects compete for attention**: Choose a small number of the highest-impact initiatives and review them in extreme detail — because selective depth creates discipline without blanket micromanagement.
- **When internal blockers or regulatory complexity slow shipping**: Make product owners responsible for driving consensus and unblocking the team — because shipping in complex environments depends on navigation, not just feature design.
- **When a launch is “basically done”**: Treat enablement, support, marketing, and adoption as core product work — because built-but-undiscovered products still fail.
- **When choosing an early career environment**: Pick places with strong entrepreneurial spirit and founder-like ownership — because they compress learning and build judgment faster.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: The best product hires are highly experienced PMs with long resumes in the same domain. → **Their view**: Raw intellect, builder energy, and hunger matter more than experience — because experienced hires can ramp slowly and may be less motivated to challenge the status quo.
- **Conventional**: Early-stage products should be scrappy MVPs optimized for speed over polish. → **Their view**: Narrow scope, but never compromise on quality — because a low-quality MVP makes it hard to tell whether the idea or the execution failed.
- **Conventional**: Senior leaders should stay high-level and avoid implementation details. → **Their view**: Leaders should go extremely deep on a few critical projects — because deep dives reveal real understanding and raise standards across the org.
- **Conventional**: Product managers are coordinators who align functions but do not directly manage them. → **Their view**: Product leaders should act as “local CEOs” and line-manage cross-functional teams — because one owner improves accountability and speed.
- **Conventional**: Customer research can be delegated to specialized researchers. → **Their view**: Product owners should talk directly to customers — because direct contact preserves nuance that gets lost in translation.
