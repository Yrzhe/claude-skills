# Oji Udezue

**Chief Product Officer** | **Typeform** | Expertise: product strategy, product-led growth, B2B SaaS, virality

## Bio
Oji Udezue is a veteran product leader who has built and scaled products at Microsoft, Atlassian, Calendly, Twitter, Bridgewater, and Typeform. His perspective matters because he combines deep product execution experience with unusually sharp thinking about workflow selection, onboarding, customer discovery, and what actually drives adoption in B2B SaaS.

He is especially valuable when you need a product leader who prioritizes fundamentals over trendy frameworks, and who thinks clearly about where products win, how users activate, and why some products spread while others stall.

## Signature Frameworks

### Where to Fish to Find a Unicorn
- **When to use**: When evaluating startup ideas, choosing a B2B SaaS problem, or deciding where to invest time and capital.
- **Steps**:
  1. Classify the workflow by breadth: does it apply to everyone in the company or only one department?
  2. Classify the workflow by frequency: is it daily/multiple times per week or infrequent/monthly?
  3. Prioritize high-frequency, niche workflows as the best B2B SaaS wedge.
  4. Recognize that high-frequency, everyone workflows are lucrative but hard to enter because incumbents dominate them.
  5. For lower-frequency categories, look for a path to move up the frequency or breadth axis by solving a more mission-critical sub-workflow.
- **Example**: Jira is a high-frequency niche workflow; Slack, email, and calendaring are high-frequency everyone workflows; expense tools are lower-frequency everyone workflows.

### Zone of Benefit
- **When to use**: When deciding whether a product is meaningfully better than the status quo and whether users will switch or pay.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the current workflow and the post-product workflow.
  2. Measure how much shorter, faster, or more capable the new workflow is.
  3. Aim for a noticeable improvement, not a marginal one.
  4. Expect users to care when the product is roughly 2-3x better, faster, or more productive.
  5. Use the strongest-benefit users to define the ICP, since they feel the value most clearly.
- **Example**: Calendly mattered because it made scheduling dramatically easier and more respectful of the scheduler's time, not just slightly better than prior tools.

### Sharp Problem Selection
- **When to use**: When choosing what problem to build a startup or product around.
- **Steps**:
  1. Pick a problem that materially steals time, energy, money, or focus from customers.
  2. Prefer problems felt intensely by many people, or by a smaller group that can pay a lot.
  3. Validate that solving the problem would cause customers to willingly pay.
  4. Test whether the product compresses the workflow by 2-3x or more.
  5. Look for strong emotional reactions and spontaneous willingness to pay as early signals.
- **Example**: He contrasted sharp problems with mmhmm camera, which was fun but did not change a core workflow enough to become a must-have.

### Continuous Customer Discovery, Listening, and Conversations
- **When to use**: When building PLG products and trying to keep customer insight flowing into product decisions.
- **Steps**:
  1. Use discovery for a specific workflow or hypothesis you want to optimize.
  2. Automate recurring customer conversations so PMs, designers, engineering managers, and PMMs talk to customers by default.
  3. Reduce friction by putting customer conversations on calendars automatically.
  4. Add customer listening by harvesting signals from support tickets, NPS verbatims, app reviews, churn surveys, social media, G2, bug reports, and closed-won/lost data.
  5. Triage and process these signals systematically so they inform product and business decisions.
- **Example**: At Calendly, the team used community signups and automated workflows to keep customer conversations flowing; at Atlassian, bug reports and support data were key listening channels.

### Onboarding as Human Substitute
- **When to use**: When designing activation and conversion flows for self-serve or PLG products.
- **Steps**:
  1. Treat onboarding as a substitute for sales and account management at scale.
  2. Map the buyer/evaluator mindset: understanding, evaluating simplicity/value, kicking tires, and deciding to proceed.
  3. Keep mandatory setup extremely short and only include essential steps.
  4. Separate optional education and advanced setup into a random-access layer users can return to later.
  5. Use examples of good outcomes and make trial status and next steps explicit.
- **Example**: At Calendly, users were asked to connect their calendar and set defaults like working hours; those essential steps were kept short because they determined future success.

### Activation Thresholds / Aha Journey
- **When to use**: When defining product activation and measuring whether users are truly getting value.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define multiple activation thresholds rather than a single binary activation event.
  2. Track the drop-off between thresholds to understand where users lose momentum.
  3. Choose milestones that reflect real value creation, not just setup completion.
  4. Optimize for users reaching deeper activation states, not merely the first success event.
- **Example**: Calendly's activation progressed from creating the first meeting to creating five meetings in a week; Typeform's activation included publishing a form, getting responses, and reviewing insights.

### Virality as Customer-Augmented Marketing
- **When to use**: When trying to increase word of mouth and organic growth in B2B or consumer products.
- **Steps**:
  1. Build a product that solves a sharp problem exceptionally well.
  2. Ensure the product experience is simple, high quality, and worth talking about.
  3. Layer in synthetic virality tactics only after the core product is strong.
  4. Use customer support, network effects, referrals, and sharing mechanics to amplify word of mouth.
  5. Measure virality as customers marketing the product for you.
- **Example**: Calendly spread because it was so easy and respectful of schedulers' time that users naturally shared it; Slack spread through lunch-table word of mouth rather than synthetic virality.

### Forest Time
- **When to use**: When operating a team and needing strategic elevation beyond day-to-day execution.
- **Steps**:
  1. Schedule intentional time away from execution to see the forest for the trees.
  2. Use a structured worksheet to review current issues and alternative paths.
  3. Create this time for yourself and your team on a recurring basis.
  4. Use the time to improve aim before investing significant team time and money.
- **Example**: Oji gives his team a monthly day to step back and work through a forest-time worksheet.

### Bridgewater People Model
- **When to use**: When hiring, evaluating, or managing people.
- **Steps**:
  1. Assess people across three dimensions: skills, attributes, and values.
  2. Look beyond competence to personality, proclivity, and behavioral style.
  3. Use these dimensions in interviews and job descriptions.
  4. Recognize that skill-only evaluation is incomplete.
- **Example**: Oji said Bridgewater taught him to think about candidates not just by skills but also by whether they are timid or bold, and by whether their values align.

## Core Advice
- **When choosing a startup or product problem**: Pick a problem customers materially feel in their time, energy, money, or focus, and that they would willingly pay to remove — because sharp problems create a tailwind for adoption, payment, and word of mouth.
- **When evaluating whether a product is meaningfully better than the status quo**: Aim for a 2-3x improvement in speed, output, or workflow compression rather than a 20% improvement — because users usually ignore marginal gains.
- **When defining your ICP**: Look for the customers who are least price sensitive because they feel the value most strongly — because they reveal the most compelling use case.
- **When deciding where to build in B2B SaaS**: Prefer high-frequency niche workflows as the best wedge for a new company — because they are sticky enough to matter and specific enough to win.
- **When trying to move a product into a stronger market position**: Expand from low-frequency or generic use cases into a mission-critical sub-workflow for a specific department — because importance and defensibility increase with frequency and breadth.
- **When running customer discovery**: Automate customer conversations so they happen by default, not by individual initiative — because friction kills discovery.
- **When collecting customer feedback**: Treat support tickets, NPS verbatims, app reviews, churn surveys, social posts, and bug reports as a continuous listening system — because the signals already exist and just need processing.
- **When designing onboarding**: Keep mandatory onboarding to the absolute minimum and move everything else into optional, return-anytime education — because extra steps increase failure and drop-off.
- **When improving activation**: Define multiple activation milestones and optimize the drop-off between them — because a single activation event hides where users lose momentum.
- **When increasing virality**: First make the product excellent and solve a sharp problem; only then add referral or sharing mechanics — because synthetic virality without product quality fizzles.
- **When operating in a fast-moving product org**: Schedule recurring forest time to step back and reassess the strategy — because teams otherwise get trapped in tree-level execution.
- **When interviewing or evaluating talent**: Assess skills, attributes, and values separately — because skill alone does not predict fit or performance.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Frameworks are the main thing product people should rely on. → **Their view**: Frameworks are useful shortcuts, but you must understand the underlying fundamentals and adapt them to stage and context — because blind application breaks in uncertain product work.
- **Conventional**: Customer science and validation can solve most startup problems. → **Their view**: Innovation and problem-space selection still matter enormously — because validation alone can miss breakthrough opportunities.
- **Conventional**: Virality can be engineered with growth hacks or viral loops. → **Their view**: Virality is customer-augmented marketing built on a great product and a sharp problem — because weak products don’t sustain word of mouth.
- **Conventional**: Onboarding should be a rich guided tour with lots of steps and wizards. → **Their view**: The best onboarding is short, essential, and split from optional education — because extra steps create friction.
- **Conventional**: Customer discovery is mainly about talking to customers more often. → **Their view**: The real challenge is building a system for continuous listening to signals that already exist — because support, reviews, and churn data are always flowing.
- **Conventional**: Skill-based hiring is enough to predict performance. → **Their view**: You need to evaluate attributes and values too — because people are more than skills.
