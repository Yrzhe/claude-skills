# Jeetu Patel

**Chief Product Officer and President** | **Cisco** | Expertise: ai-strategy, leadership, product-management, go-to-market, operations

## Bio
Jeetu Patel is the Chief Product Officer and President at Cisco, where he is helping drive an AI-first transformation across a massive enterprise. His perspective matters because he combines product thinking, operating discipline, and leadership at scale—especially in legacy companies trying to reinvent themselves without losing trust or execution speed.

He is especially useful when you need a view on how large organizations actually change: how to choose bets, how to communicate urgency, how to build a platform company, and how to decide whether a new technology is a real megatrend or just hype.

## Signature Frameworks

### Six-part framework for building great companies
- **When to use**: When evaluating a new company, product line, strategic bet, or why a business is or is not likely to win.
- **Steps**:
  1. Assess timing first: if the market moment is wrong, even great products can fail.
  2. Evaluate market size and whether you can attack it in meaningful chunks that expand over time.
  3. Build a well-rounded team with complementary strengths rather than just people who get along.
  4. Create a great product, because product is the soul of the company and the manifestation of customer value.
  5. Protect and build brand trust, since lost brand mojo is very hard to recover.
  6. Ensure distribution, because building something good is not enough without a scalable route to market.
- **Example**: Patel points to Steve Jobs shelving the iPad in favor of the iPhone as a timing decision: the iPad later succeeded partly because the iPhone succeeded first.

### Right to win / permission to play
- **When to use**: When deciding whether to enter a market, launch a new product, or expand into an adjacent category.
- **Steps**:
  1. Ask whether your company has permission to play in the market.
  2. Determine whether customers would find it logical that your company is the one building this product.
  3. Check whether you have a route to market and distribution advantage.
  4. Focus investment only where your existing strengths create outsized returns.
  5. Say no to most adjacent ideas that lack strategic fit, even if the market looks attractive.
- **Example**: Cisco choosing AI infrastructure is a natural extension of its role in networking and trusted infrastructure, while Patel argues Cisco should not pursue B2C tech because it lacks the DNA and distribution channel for it.

### AI-first transformation in a large company
- **When to use**: When trying to move a large organization from experimentation to committed adoption of AI.
- **Steps**:
  1. Make an explicit top-down choice that the company will be AI-first.
  2. Clarify what is and is not up for debate so progress cannot be blocked by endless veto points.
  3. Do not hedge once conviction is high; when experiments work, go all in.
  4. Align employee incentives by showing that personal success depends on becoming dexterous with AI.
  5. Redefine success away from siloed fiefdoms and toward platform-level outcomes.
  6. Shift the company from a walled garden mindset to an open ecosystem mindset.
- **Example**: Patel says Cisco did not hedge on AI after recognizing the shift, and made clear that employees who do not become effective with AI will become less relevant over time.

### Platform company model: loosely coupled, tightly integrated
- **When to use**: When integrating many products or acquisitions into a coherent customer experience without forcing all-or-nothing adoption.
- **Steps**:
  1. Move away from siloed business units acting like independent GMs.
  2. Define the company as a platform rather than a holding company of products.
  3. Create consistent customer expectations across products, such as reliability, trust, elegance, and efficiency.
  4. Allow customers to adopt products incrementally rather than requiring full-stack commitment.
  5. Ensure that when customers use multiple products together, they work seamlessly.
- **Example**: Cisco’s goal is not to require customers to buy everything at once, but to make sure that when they buy two Cisco products together, “they work like magic.”

### Direct storytelling to avoid packet loss
- **When to use**: When leading large organizations where strategy and urgency can get diluted across layers.
- **Steps**:
  1. Treat the company story as a foundational operating mechanism, not a marketing exercise.
  2. Own the message personally rather than delegating it down the org chart.
  3. Communicate directly to frontline teams to avoid distortion through layers.
  4. Use the need for clarity in storytelling to simplify the business itself.
  5. Repeat the why behind decisions so context is preserved, not just instructions.
- **Example**: After advice from board member Wes Bush, Patel chose to personally tell Cisco’s story broadly rather than relying on cascading communication, comparing delegated communication loss to packet loss in networking.

### Public critique, private trust
- **When to use**: When trying to create high-performance teams that can debate openly without political posturing.
- **Steps**:
  1. Invest heavily in trust-building in private conversations.
  2. In public, critique ideas and performance directly but respectfully.
  3. Use public discussion for problem-solving rather than image management.
  4. Avoid hollow praise and “shit sandwich” feedback patterns.
  5. Make it explicit in private that you have people’s backs.
- **Example**: Patel rejects the standard management advice to “praise in public, criticize in private,” arguing that teams should be comfortable debating and critiquing in public once trust exists.

### Megatrend vs. hype cycle heuristic
- **When to use**: When deciding whether to commit major resources to an emerging technology or market shift.
- **Steps**:
  1. Ask whether the trend is easy to understand in its ultimate value for a broad population.
  2. Distinguish vanity work driven by hype from foundational shifts that will reshape industries.
  3. If it is a megatrend, do not fight it.
  4. Plan for where the technology will be in six months, not just where it is today.
- **Example**: Patel says AI is clearly a megatrend because anyone can understand asking ChatGPT a question and getting an answer, while Web3 was harder to explain and therefore more likely hype-driven.

## Core Advice
- **When a major technology shift hits a large company**: make a clear company-wide choice to go all-in, and define what is not up for debate — because hedging after early success is how large companies lose momentum.
- **When employees are anxious about AI**: frame AI adoption as essential to future relevance, while emphasizing augmentation over immediate replacement — because people act when incentives feel personal and credible.
- **When too many adjacent opportunities appear attractive**: say no unless you have a clear right to win, permission to play, and route to market — because focus compounds leverage.
- **When communication is breaking down in a big organization**: tell the story yourself and speak directly to frontline teams — because every layer adds distortion and weakens urgency.
- **When the business feels too complex to explain**: simplify until you can personally explain it clearly — because strategic clarity starts with narrative clarity.
- **When underperformance is being masked by politeness**: critique directly in public, but build trust deeply in private — because real debate beats performative harmony.
- **When evaluating a fast-moving technology like AI**: plan for the world six months ahead — because today’s constraints may be obsolete very soon.
- **When hiring or planning**: combine experienced operators with fresh thinkers — because pattern recognition and new questions are both necessary.
- **When choosing a career path**: choose hard, important problems on strong platforms — because context and leverage matter as much as talent.
- **When learning a new domain**: use AI as a teammate, not just a tool — because that mindset accelerates competence.
- **When building relationships**: be explicit about appreciation and care — because people cannot read your mind.
- **When starting from a weak network or geography**: seek the best platform, prepare obsessively, ask for help non-transactionally, and add value first — because luck only helps if you can convert it.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Large companies are inherently bad at innovation. → **Their view**: Innovation is a choice, and large companies often experiment a lot; the real failure is not doubling down when something works — because scale gives resources, but hedging kills momentum.
- **Conventional**: Praise in public and criticize in private is the best management rule. → **Their view**: Teams should be able to critique and debate in public, as long as trust is built in private — because candor improves problem-solving.
- **Conventional**: Scale makes execution harder than being small. → **Their view**: The absence of scale is often harder than scale — because scale gives you reallocation power and optionality.
- **Conventional**: AI is mainly a productivity tool or a threat to jobs. → **Their view**: AI should be thought of as a teammate and may be essential to humanity’s future — because demographic decline may require machine labor.
- **Conventional**: Entry-level hiring will disappear because AI can do junior work. → **Their view**: Cutting off entry-level talent would be a major mistake — because fresh thinking matters as much as experience.
- **Conventional**: The smartest people win. → **Their view**: Stamina, hunger, and persistence beat raw intellect — because staying power is harder to teach and often more decisive.
