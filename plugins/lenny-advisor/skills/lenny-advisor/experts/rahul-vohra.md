# Rahul Vohra

**Founder and CEO** | **Superhuman** | Expertise: product strategy, growth, product-market fit, design, pricing, retention

## Bio
Rahul Vohra is the founder and CEO of Superhuman, the premium email client known for speed, craft, and a highly opinionated product philosophy. Before Superhuman, he founded Rapportive, the first Gmail extension to scale to millions of users, which he sold to LinkedIn; that experience shaped his thinking on virality, growth, and product-market fit.

His perspective matters because he combines product taste with rigorous measurement. He is especially useful when you need to decide what to build, what feedback to ignore, how to position a premium product, and how to structure a company so the CEO can spend time on the highest-leverage work.

## Signature Frameworks

### Product Market Fit Engine
- **When to use**: When you need to systematically improve product-market fit and decide which customer feedback to act on.
- **Steps**:
  1. Ask users: “How would you feel if you could no longer use this product?”
  2. Segment responses into very disappointed, somewhat disappointed, and not disappointed.
  3. Treat “very disappointed” as the strongest signal of fit.
  4. Do not over-index on feature requests from very disappointed users.
  5. Ignore feedback from not disappointed users.
  6. Focus on the somewhat disappointed segment.
  7. Within that segment, separate core-benefit lovers from users who like the product for other reasons.
  8. Double down on what core-lovers value most.
  9. Remove objections from core-lovers-in-waiting.
- **Example**: For Superhuman, the core value was speed. Rahul focused on users who loved speed and ignored requests that would turn the product into something else.

### Positioning Before Pricing
- **When to use**: When setting a price for a product, especially a premium product that needs to support a clear market position.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the product’s market position.
  2. Ensure the position is unique, available, and strategically aligned.
  3. Validate the position through interviews and competitive comparison.
  4. Use pricing research to find a price that supports the position.
  5. Run Van Westendorp-style questions to identify acceptable ranges.
  6. Choose the price that fits the target segment and business model.
  7. Sanity-check market size and venture-scale potential.
- **Example**: Superhuman chose $30/month because it fit a premium “best email tool” position while still leaving enough market size for venture-scale revenue.

### Speed as a Strategic Position
- **When to use**: When you need one memorable attribute that differentiates a product and shapes product decisions.
- **Steps**:
  1. Interview customers to identify the pain they already feel.
  2. Find an attribute that matters and is underused by competitors.
  3. Check whether it reinforces strategy and is hard for incumbents to match.
  4. Test whether it is easy to explain in one line.
  5. Make the attribute visible in product and marketing.
- **Example**: Superhuman positioned around speed after interviews showed email felt too slow, leading to the pitch: “You have to use it, it’s really fucking fast.”

### Game Design for Business Software
- **When to use**: When you want a product to feel delightful, habit-forming, and shareable without superficial gamification.
- **Steps**:
  1. Design for intrinsic motivation, not external rewards.
  2. Build “toys” that are fun to explore.
  3. Combine toys into larger game-like systems with clear goals.
  4. Create moments of surprise and playful discovery.
  5. Make controls responsive and satisfying.
  6. Use the product to evoke emotion, not just efficiency.
- **Example**: Superhuman’s time auto-completer lets users type inputs like “2D” or “in a fortnight and a day,” making scheduling feel playful and surprising.

### Manual Concierge Onboarding
- **When to use**: When early activation, retention, and brand formation matter more than self-serve efficiency.
- **Steps**:
  1. Require new users to go through one-to-one onboarding.
  2. Use humans to teach the product and remove friction.
  3. Measure impact on engagement, retention, PMF, NPS, and virality.
  4. Use the early cohort to seed word of mouth.
  5. Stop when key segments won’t accept it and volume makes it inefficient.
  6. Replace it with world-class self-serve before removing the human layer.
- **Example**: Superhuman had about 20 people doing manual onboarding at peak before transitioning to self-serve.

### Single Decisive Reason (SDR)
- **When to use**: When making important decisions and you want to avoid weak consensus logic.
- **Steps**:
  1. Ask for the single reason that alone supports the decision.
  2. Test each reason independently if multiple are offered.
  3. Ask whether the decision still holds if only that reason is true.
  4. If no single reason is strong enough, reconsider.
  5. Use SDR to expose piles of weak justification.
- **Example**: When someone proposes a feature, Rahul asks, “What’s the SDR?” and pushes for the one reason that truly justifies building it.

### Switch Log
- **When to use**: When you want to understand where your time actually goes and reallocate toward highest-value work.
- **Steps**:
  1. Stop relying on your calendar as the source of truth.
  2. Log every task switch in Slack or to an assistant.
  3. Work on what feels right in the moment.
  4. Graph logged tasks at week’s end.
  5. Use the data to spot misalignment between intended and actual work.
- **Example**: Rahul discovered he spent only 6–7% of his week on product, design, technology, and marketing, prompting a role restructure.

## Core Advice
- **When early users give you lots of feedback**: Do not act on all of it; focus on the subset whose pain matches your core value proposition — because the wrong users can pull the product away from what makes it special.
- **When your company feels slower**: Separate unavoidable slowdown from avoidable slowdown before changing strategy — because some slowdown is market-driven, while some is org design.
- **When you are spending too little time on your highest-leverage work**: Restructure the org so you can spend more time in your zone of genius — because the CEO’s unique contribution should be protected.
- **When you need to understand your actual time allocation**: Log task switches in real time instead of trusting your calendar — because intent and reality are often different.
- **When you are deciding whether to launch before the product is polished**: Delay launch for mission-critical products if reliability and trust are central — because a broken launch can damage credibility.
- **When you are choosing what to optimize for in a product**: Pick one clear attribute and make it the company’s position — because a single attribute is easier to remember and market.
- **When you are building a premium product**: Set pricing from positioning, not from a desire to maximize signups — because price signals quality and market segment.
- **When you are considering manual onboarding**: Use concierge onboarding early if it materially improves activation and brand, but plan the self-serve replacement before scale forces it — because human onboarding can create super-fans but won’t scale forever.
- **When you are building for word of mouth**: Create something remarkable enough that users spontaneously tell others about it — because sustained growth comes from recommendation, not mechanics alone.
- **When you are designing software that should feel sticky**: Build playful, surprising interactions that users enjoy even without a task to complete — because intrinsic motivation lasts longer than rewards.
- **When you are making a high-stakes decision**: Force the team to identify one decisive reason before approving it — because weak reasons can hide a bad decision.
- **When you are building AI features**: Validate with real usage, not intuition — because adoption can be counterintuitive.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Viral products grow because of viral mechanics built into the product. → **Their view**: There is no truly viral product; sustained growth comes from word of mouth — because mechanics fade, while genuine recommendation persists.
- **Conventional**: Founders should listen closely to all early customer feedback. → **Their view**: Deliberately ignore much of it, especially outside the core segment — because broad feedback can dilute the core value proposition.
- **Conventional**: Speed of shipping is always good. → **Their view**: For mission-critical products, half-baked shipping can be worse than waiting — because trust and reliability matter more than launch speed.
- **Conventional**: Pricing should maximize adoption. → **Their view**: Premium pricing should reinforce positioning — because cheap pricing can undermine perceived quality.
- **Conventional**: Gamification means points and badges. → **Their view**: Real magic comes from game design, not rewards — because external incentives can reduce intrinsic motivation.
- **Conventional**: CEOs should spend most of their time on management. → **Their view**: CEOs should redesign the org around the work only they can do exceptionally well — because executive overload can starve the company of strategic focus.
