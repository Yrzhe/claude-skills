# Rahul Vohra

**Founder and CEO** | **Superhuman** | Expertise: product strategy, growth, product-market fit, design, pricing, positioning

## Bio
Rahul Vohra is the founder and CEO of Superhuman, the premium email client known for speed, craft, and a highly opinionated product philosophy. Before Superhuman, he founded Rapportive, the first Gmail extension to scale to millions of users, which he sold to LinkedIn; that experience shaped his thinking on virality, growth, and product-market fit.

His perspective matters because he combines founder-level product taste with unusually rigorous systems thinking. He is especially valuable when you need to sharpen positioning, diagnose product-market fit, or decide what to ignore in order to protect a product’s core value.

## Signature Frameworks

### Product Market Fit Engine
- **When to use**: When you need to systematically improve product-market fit and decide which customer feedback to act on.
- **Steps**:
  1. Ask users: “How would you feel if you could no longer use this product?”
  2. Segment responses into very disappointed, somewhat disappointed, and not disappointed.
  3. Treat “very disappointed” as the strongest signal of fit, but do not over-index on their feature requests because they already love the product.
  4. Ignore feedback from “not disappointed” users because they are too far from the core value proposition.
  5. Focus on the “somewhat disappointed” segment, especially those for whom the main benefit of the product resonates.
  6. Within that segment, separate users who love the core benefit from those who like the product for something else.
  7. Double down on what the core-lovers say they value most.
  8. Systematically remove objections from the core-lovers-in-waiting.
  9. Repeat this at the start of each planning cycle, splitting effort between strengthening what users love and removing friction.
- **Example**: For Superhuman, the core value was speed. Rahul would keep users who loved speed in focus, while disregarding feedback from users who wanted the product to be something else.

### Positioning Before Pricing
- **When to use**: When setting a price for a product, especially a premium product that needs to support a clear market position.
- **Steps**:
  1. First define the product's position in the market.
  2. Ensure the position is unique, available, and reinforces the product strategy.
  3. Validate the position through customer interviews and competitive comparison.
  4. Use pricing research to find the price point that supports the position rather than the cheapest or most adoption-friendly price.
  5. Run Van Westendorp-style price sensitivity questions to identify acceptable and compelling price ranges.
  6. Choose the price that matches the intended market segment and business model.
  7. Sanity-check the resulting market size and whether it can support venture-scale outcomes.
- **Example**: Superhuman chose $30/month because it fit a premium “best email tool” position and still left enough market size to reach venture-scale revenue.

### Speed as a Strategic Position
- **When to use**: When you need a single, memorable attribute that can differentiate a product and shape product decisions.
- **Steps**:
  1. Interview customers to identify the pain they already feel.
  2. Look for an attribute that is both important to users and underused by competitors.
  3. Check whether the attribute reinforces your product strategy and is hard for incumbents to match.
  4. Test whether the attribute is easy to explain in a cocktail-party pitch.
  5. Make the chosen attribute visible in the product experience and in marketing.
- **Example**: Superhuman positioned around speed after customer interviews showed email felt too slow, and the pitch became: “You have to use it, it's really fucking fast.”

### Game Design for Business Software
- **When to use**: When you want a product to feel delightful, habit-forming, and shareable without relying on superficial gamification.
- **Steps**:
  1. Design for intrinsic motivation rather than external rewards.
  2. Build “toys” that are fun to explore even without a goal.
  3. Combine toys into larger game-like systems with clear goals and flow.
  4. Create moments of pleasant surprise and playful discovery.
  5. Make controls responsive and satisfying so users feel mastery.
  6. Use the product to evoke emotion, not just efficiency.
- **Example**: Superhuman's time auto-completer lets users type inputs like “2D” or “in a fortnight and a day,” which makes scheduling feel playful and surprising.

### Manual Concierge Onboarding
- **When to use**: When early user activation, retention, and brand formation matter more than scaling self-serve efficiency.
- **Steps**:
  1. Require new users to go through a one-to-one onboarding experience.
  2. Use humans to teach the product, remove friction, and create early super-fans.
  3. Measure whether onboarding improves engagement, retention, PMF, NPS, and virality.
  4. Use the early cohort to seed word of mouth and brand.
  5. Stop manual onboarding when the market includes important user segments that will not accept it and when top-of-funnel volume makes it inefficient.
  6. Replace it with a world-class self-serve experience before removing the human layer.
- **Example**: Superhuman had about 20 people doing manual onboarding at peak before transitioning to self-serve.

### Single Decisive Reason (SDR)
- **When to use**: When making important decisions and you want to avoid weak consensus logic.
- **Steps**:
  1. Ask for the single reason that alone supports the decision.
  2. If multiple reasons are given, test each one independently.
  3. Ask whether the decision would still be made if only that one reason were true.
  4. If no single reason is strong enough, reconsider the decision.
  5. Use SDR to expose when a decision is being justified by a pile of weak reasons.
- **Example**: When someone proposes a feature, Rahul asks: “What's the SDR?” and pushes them to identify the one reason that would justify building it.

### Switch Log
- **When to use**: When you want to understand where your time actually goes and reallocate your calendar toward your highest-value work.
- **Steps**:
  1. Stop relying on your calendar as the source of truth.
  2. Every time you switch tasks, log the new task in Slack or to an assistant.
  3. Work on what feels right in the moment instead of forcing calendar adherence.
  4. At the end of the week, graph the logged tasks to see actual time allocation.
  5. Use the data to identify misalignment between your intended role and your real work.
- **Example**: Rahul discovered he was spending only 6-7% of his week on product, design, technology, and marketing, which led him to restructure his role.

## Core Advice
- **When early users give you lots of feedback**: Do not act on all of it; focus on the subset whose pain matches your core value proposition — because the wrong users can pull the product away from what makes it special.
- **When your company feels like it has slowed down**: Separate unavoidable slowdown from avoidable slowdown before changing strategy — because some slowdown is market-driven, while some is caused by org design and role overload.
- **When you are spending too little time on your highest-leverage work**: Restructure the org so you can spend more time in your zone of genius, even if that means hiring a president — because founder time should be concentrated where only they can add exceptional value.
- **When you need to understand your actual time allocation**: Log task switches in real time instead of trusting your calendar — because the calendar reflects intent, not reality.
- **When you are deciding whether to launch before the product is polished**: Delay launch for mission-critical products if reliability and trust are central to the category — because a broken launch can damage credibility.
- **When you are choosing what to optimize for in a product**: Pick one clear attribute and make it the company’s position — because a single attribute is easier to remember, market, and defend.
- **When you are building a premium product**: Set pricing from positioning, not from a desire to maximize signups — because the right price reinforces quality and business model.
- **When you are considering manual onboarding**: Use concierge onboarding early if it materially improves activation and brand, but plan the self-serve replacement before scale forces it — because human onboarding can create super-fans but does not scale.
- **When you are building for word of mouth**: Create something remarkable enough that users spontaneously tell others about it — because sustained growth comes from recommendation, not mechanics alone.
- **When you are designing software that should feel sticky**: Build playful, surprising interactions that users enjoy even without a task to complete — because intrinsic motivation sustains engagement.
- **When you are making a high-stakes decision**: Force the team to identify one decisive reason before approving the decision — because a pile of weak reasons can hide a bad choice.
- **When you are building AI features**: Expect user adoption to be less predictable than you think and validate with real usage, not intuition — because obvious-seeming features may underperform while unexpected ones win.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Viral products grow because of viral mechanics built into the product. → **Their view**: There is no truly viral product; sustained growth comes from word of mouth, not mechanics — because even famous viral features have limited lifetime viral factors.
- **Conventional**: Founders should listen closely to all early customer feedback. → **Their view**: You must deliberately ignore much of early feedback, especially from users outside the core segment — because acting on every request dilutes the core value.
- **Conventional**: Speed of shipping is always good, so launch early and iterate. → **Their view**: For mission-critical products, shipping a half-baked experience can be worse than waiting — because trust and reliability matter more than speed to market.
- **Conventional**: Pricing should be optimized for maximum adoption or the lowest friction entry point. → **Their view**: Premium pricing should be chosen to reinforce positioning, even if it reduces top-of-funnel conversion — because price signals quality and supports the brand.
- **Conventional**: Gamification means adding points, badges, and rewards. → **Their view**: Rewards often reduce intrinsic motivation; real product magic comes from game design, not gamification — because external rewards can crowd out continued engagement.
- **Conventional**: A CEO should spend most of their time on hiring, org building, and management. → **Their view**: A CEO should intentionally spend more time on the work only they can do exceptionally well, and redesign the org around that — because executive overload can starve the company of founder leverage.
