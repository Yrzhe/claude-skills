# Roger Martin

**Professor emeritus, strategy advisor, author** | **University of Toronto Rotman School of Management** | Expertise: strategy, competitive advantage, product strategy, management systems

## Bio
Roger Martin is one of the most influential practical strategy thinkers, best known for *Playing to Win* and for translating strategy into a disciplined set of choices rather than abstract theory. His perspective matters because he focuses on how real companies create durable advantage through integrated decisions about customers, positioning, capabilities, and operating systems.

He is especially valuable when you need a strategy that is actionable, defensible, and grounded in customer behavior—not just a slide deck or a market-sizing exercise.

## Signature Frameworks

### Strategy Choice Cascade
- **When to use**: When you need to create, evaluate, or refine a strategy for a product, business, or brand.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define your winning aspiration: what are you trying to accomplish?
  2. Choose where to play: which customers, products, channels, geographies, or value-chain positions will you target?
  3. Choose how to win: will you win through differentiation or low cost?
  4. Identify the capabilities required: what must you be especially good at that competitors do not have?
  5. Design enabling management systems: what processes, incentives, recruiting, onboarding, and operating systems will sustain those capabilities?
- **Example**: For a hypothetical FigJam expansion, Roger would ask: what customer problem are we solving, which users and channels are we targeting, will we win through lower cost or better workflow integration, what capabilities make that possible, and what systems keep those capabilities strong?

### Playing to Win vs. Playing to Play
- **When to use**: When assessing whether a company is actually competing to win or merely participating in the market.
- **Steps**:
  1. Look for evidence that customers strongly prefer your offer rather than treating it as interchangeable.
  2. Check whether you have a defensible low-cost position or a meaningful differentiated position.
  3. Test whether competitors can easily copy your offer or undercut your price.
  4. If neither is true, consider changing the playing field rather than staying in the same game.
- **Example**: Lego is a differentiated winner because kids and parents treat it as essential in toy retail, while Vanguard wins through low cost and scale in index funds.

### Betterment Over Perfection
- **When to use**: When strategy feels overwhelming and you need a practical first move.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the most painful gap between current outcomes and desired outcomes.
  2. Treat strategy as problem-solving, not as a grand master plan.
  3. Use the cascade to ask which choice—where to play, how to win, capabilities, or management systems—would reduce that gap.
  4. Improve one gap at a time, then move to the next.
- **Example**: A professor improved a course by removing the worst session every year and replacing it, which steadily improved the class over time.

### Where-to-Play as Value-Chain Design
- **When to use**: When deciding not just which market to enter, but where in the value chain to participate.
- **Steps**:
  1. Decide whether you are selling a finished product, a component, or a platform layer.
  2. Choose the vertical stage you will own versus outsource.
  3. Consider how your position interacts with distribution, partners, and customer workflow.
  4. Avoid entering a value chain position that leaves you structurally weak or dependent.
- **Example**: Four Seasons chose to exit real estate development and hotel ownership to focus on hotel management, letting wealthy owners hold the assets while Four Seasons captured the management fee and brand value.

## Core Advice
- **When strategy feels vague or overwhelming**: Start by identifying the single most painful gap between current outcomes and desired outcomes — because strategy is problem-solving, and the gap gives you a concrete target.
- **When a market looks attractive just because it is large**: Don’t justify entry with “it’s big, we can get some of that” — because size alone is not a strategy and often leads to weak, undifferentiated moves.
- **When choosing how to win**: Pick one of two durable routes: be meaningfully differentiated or be the low-cost provider — because there is no protection if you are neither.
- **When you believe you are differentiated**: Validate it through customer behavior, not internal belief — because if customers see you as interchangeable, you are not actually winning.
- **When you believe you are low cost**: Test whether competitors can undercut you and still survive — because a real cost advantage shows up in rival behavior, not self-description.
- **When considering niche cost leadership**: Be skeptical — because cost leadership usually requires dominant scale in the territory you serve.
- **When trying to differentiate**: Build around customer understanding and hard-to-copy capabilities — because “better” only matters if it is durable and valued.
- **When designing a new product or feature**: Ask where in the workflow or value chain it should live — because where-to-play shapes defensibility and economics.
- **When advantage depends on operations**: Build management systems that reinforce the capability — because capabilities do not sustain themselves.
- **When competitors can copy your strategy easily**: Change the game rather than fighting head-on — because similar where/how choices invite convergence and margin erosion.
- **When tempted to wait for perfect operations before doing strategy**: Start practicing strategy now, even on small decisions — because strategic skill comes from reps.
- **When building a moat**: Make it multi-part and hard to imitate — because simple advantages are easier to copy.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Strategy is mainly something executives do, while everyone else executes. → **Their view**: People deep in the organization make critical strategic choices too, and they should be trained to do so — because brand managers and frontline leaders often shape outcomes materially.
- **Conventional**: Business schools teach strategy well. → **Their view**: Most strategy teaching in business schools is bad and overly theoretical — because it often ignores how strategy is actually practiced.
- **Conventional**: A strong resource base is the core of strategy. → **Their view**: Strategy is about linking where you play with how you win — because resources only matter in context.
- **Conventional**: Being the best product is enough to win. → **Their view**: Being “better” is insufficient unless customers clearly prefer it and competitors cannot easily copy it — because advantage must be durable.
- **Conventional**: Competitive advantage is fleeting in modern markets. → **Their view**: Durable advantage is still possible if the strategy is multi-layered and hard to imitate — because examples like Four Seasons, Tide, Southwest, and Vanguard show long-lived wins.
- **Conventional**: You should avoid low-cost strategies because they are hard and unattractive. → **Their view**: Low-cost is a fully legitimate strategy, but it usually requires scale and commitment — because cost leadership is one of the two real winning routes.
