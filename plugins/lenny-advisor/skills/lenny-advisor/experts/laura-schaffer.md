# Laura Schaffer

**VP of Growth** | **Amplitude** | Expertise: growth, product-led growth, experimentation, developer tools

## Bio
Laura Schaffer is the VP of Growth at Amplitude and previously built growth teams and motions at Twilio and Rapid. Her perspective matters because she blends customer insight, pragmatic experimentation, and a deep understanding of user psychology—especially in complex, technical products where trust and clarity drive conversion.

She stands out for treating growth as a customer problem, not just a metrics problem. She is especially useful when you need to build PLG motions, improve onboarding, or create growth opportunities that don’t depend on waiting for permission.

## Signature Frameworks

### Career growth through customer insight and brand building
- **When to use**: When you want to grow beyond your current role and create opportunities that don't depend solely on your manager.
- **Steps**:
  1. Do your current job well and keep track of your wins.
  2. Stay close to customers and collect insights that others in the company don't have.
  3. Share those insights broadly through reports, updates, or forums so others can benefit.
  4. Become known internally as the person who understands the customer.
  5. Use that credibility to propose new work or a new function that aligns with company needs and North Star metrics.
- **Example**: At Twilio, Laura started sharing a voice-of-customer report, which led to a quarterly customer session and eventually helped her pitch and create the growth team.

### Psychology-first onboarding design
- **When to use**: When designing signup or onboarding flows for new users, especially in complex products or high-stakes work contexts.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the user's likely fears, uncertainties, and assumptions at the moment they enter the flow.
  2. Add questions or cues that reassure them they are in the right place.
  3. Use the user's answers to segment, personalize, or route the experience.
  4. If a logical step creates anxiety, embed it inside a more comfortable context rather than leading with it.
  5. Validate the experience with both quantitative and qualitative feedback.
- **Example**: Twilio added questions about coding language, use case, and product interest to signup; conversion improved by about 5%. Later, they moved the scary telecom step into a docs-based experience instead of leading with phone-number setup.

### Iterative experimentation over big-bang redesigns
- **When to use**: When you are deciding how to test a new experience, onboarding flow, or product change.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with the smallest test that can validate the core hypothesis.
  2. Use ugly, scrappy prototypes or painted doors before investing in full builds.
  3. Run experiments in front of customers as early as possible.
  4. Treat failures as directional signals rather than setbacks.
  5. Only invest in larger builds after the concept has been validated.
- **Example**: Laura's team first tested a crude version of Twilio onboarding by kicking users out of the portal into docs, which validated the idea before they built a more polished flow.

### Hypothesis validation stack
- **When to use**: When you need to decide whether an idea is worth a full A/B test or product investment.
- **Steps**:
  1. Write the hypothesis and success criteria before running anything.
  2. Use low-cost validation methods first, such as mocks, painted doors, or qualitative interviews.
  3. Reserve expensive A/B tests for ideas that have already been de-risked.
  4. Use qualitative evidence to corroborate quantitative results.
  5. Avoid retrofitting data to fit a preferred conclusion.
- **Example**: Before a full experiment, Laura would test concepts with mocks or a painted-door flow, then use interviews and usage data to confirm the result.

### PLG reset from customer problem, not product packaging
- **When to use**: When a sales-led company is trying to build or improve a product-led growth motion.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start by identifying the distinct problems of self-serve users, not by repackaging the enterprise offer.
  2. Understand the psyche and constraints of the new audience.
  3. Design the product experience around those problems.
  4. Use the product as the sales motion, rather than copying the sales process into the product.
  5. Keep existing company knowledge and momentum, but reorient it around the new user journey.
- **Example**: At Amplitude, Laura argues the PLG motion should start with the problems of startups, PMs, and analysts who need help getting to trustworthy dashboards and board-ready reporting.

### Developer conversion through proof, not persuasion
- **When to use**: When selling to developers or building developer tools.
- **Steps**:
  1. Assume developers will skip marketing pages and go straight to signup or docs.
  2. Make the self-serve path strong enough to stand on its own.
  3. Help them build a proof of concept quickly.
  4. Reduce psychological friction by meeting them in familiar contexts like code samples and docs.
  5. Expect sales to play a supporting role, not the primary conversion role.
- **Example**: Twilio's quick-deploy/code-exchange experience let non-developers and developers alike create an app without writing code first, helping them get to an aha moment.

## Core Advice
- **When you feel stuck in a role and want a bigger trajectory**: Build a reputation for customer insight by talking to users, sharing what you learn, and becoming the internal person who knows the customer best — because executives often lose day-to-day contact with customers, so fresh insight creates visibility.
- **When your manager may not naturally create the best path for your growth**: Propose your own opportunities and carve your own path instead of waiting for promotion or scope to be handed to you — because your growth is limited by your manager’s advocacy unless you create momentum yourself.
- **When you want to influence leadership without formal authority**: Share a voice-of-customer report or similar insight stream consistently until it becomes a trusted company resource — because repeated useful insight builds brand recognition and a forum for your ideas.
- **When you're proposing a new initiative to leadership**: Frame it as solving a real customer problem and tie it to a North Star metric — because leaders support ideas that surface problems they don’t already see and connect to business outcomes.
- **When you're designing signup or onboarding**: Add questions or cues that reassure users they are in the right place and can succeed — because new users often arrive anxious and uncertainty suppresses conversion.
- **When a logical onboarding step is causing drop-off**: Move the scary step into a more familiar context, like docs or a guided flow, instead of leading with it — because users may need the step, but not as the first thing they encounter.
- **When you're deciding whether to redesign a flow from scratch**: Test the riskiest assumption with a scrappy, embarrassing prototype before investing in a full redesign — because most hypotheses are wrong and cheap tests help you fail fast.
- **When you need to validate a product idea quickly**: Use painted doors, mocks, or qualitative interviews before running a full A/B test — because A/B tests are expensive and earlier validation methods can eliminate bad ideas faster.
- **When your team feels pressure to show weekly wins**: Set expectations around longer time horizons, ideally annual goals with low/medium/high buckets — because growth work is lumpy and short-term pressure encourages vanity metrics.
- **When you're debating statistical rigor for experiments**: Consider lower confidence thresholds if it lets you run materially more experiments, but pair that with qualitative validation — because the opportunity cost of too few experiments can outweigh a bit more statistical risk.
- **When you're building for developers**: Assume they will self-serve, skip marketing pages, and need to prove value themselves before talking to sales — because their job stakes are high and they need proof, not promises.
- **When your team is too dependent on engineers for every change**: Use tools that let non-engineers ship and iterate faster, such as visual builders or headless CMS workflows — because faster iteration helps you validate more ideas and avoid slow cycles.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: All friction in onboarding is bad, so the goal should be to remove every possible step. → **Their view**: Some friction is good if it reassures users, helps them orient, or reduces uncertainty — because Twilio saw a 5% signup lift after adding questions that made users feel they were in the right place.
- **Conventional**: The best way to improve a product experience is often to redesign it comprehensively. → **Their view**: Iterative, ugly, and even embarrassing tests are usually better than big-bang redesigns — because most hypotheses are wrong, so the cheapest way to learn is to validate the riskiest assumption early.
- **Conventional**: A/B testing should usually target 95% confidence before action. → **Their view**: In growth, lower confidence thresholds can be acceptable if they let you run many more experiments and corroborate with qualitative data — because the opportunity cost of running too few experiments can be higher than accepting more statistical risk.
- **Conventional**: PLG is mostly about packaging the product differently and adjusting pricing or feature gates. → **Their view**: PLG should start with the self-serve user's actual problems and psychology, not with slicing up the enterprise offer — because different audiences have different jobs, anxieties, and success criteria.
- **Conventional**: Sales can effectively convert developers if the pitch is strong enough. → **Their view**: Developers usually need to prove value themselves and will often avoid sales entirely — because their job stakes are high, so they need hands-on validation and self-serve proof before trusting a vendor.
