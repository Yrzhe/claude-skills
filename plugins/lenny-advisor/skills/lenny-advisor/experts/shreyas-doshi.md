# Shreyas Doshi

**Former PM leader** | **Stripe, Twitter, Google, Yahoo** | Expertise: product-management, leadership, strategy, go-to-market

## Bio
Shreyas Doshi is a product leader who has worked as both an engineer and PM at Yahoo, Google, Twitter, and Stripe. His perspective matters because it is grounded in high-stakes, real-world product work across companies with very different scales and operating models.

He is known for crisp, contrarian frameworks on product thinking, prioritization, team dynamics, and leadership. If you want a viewpoint that cuts through PM jargon and focuses on leverage, judgment, and decision quality, he is a strong source.

## Signature Frameworks

### Why am I so busy?
- **When to use**: When senior product work feels chronically overwhelming despite good time management.
- **Steps**:
  1. Recognize that scope, not just inefficiency, may be the real driver of busyness.
  2. Identify whether annual planning, escalations, and stakeholder coordination are consuming weeks of time.
  3. Ask whether a real product strategy exists to reduce downstream prioritization and planning work.
  4. Audit whether you are making low-quality product decisions that create future work and debt.
  5. Pause before committing to features that look reversible but are effectively one-way doors at your level.
- **Example**: At Stripe, having a real product strategy for Stripe Connect reduced planning from four to six weeks to about three days.

### Real product strategy as leverage
- **When to use**: When planning cycles are bloated and teams keep revisiting priorities.
- **Steps**:
  1. Create a coherent product strategy that is genuinely aligned across stakeholders.
  2. Use that strategy as the basis for prioritization and resource allocation.
  3. Treat escalations and new requests as inputs to a rigorous strategy, not as reasons to restart planning from scratch.
  4. Avoid false precision in headcount or roadmap allocations when the numbers will change anyway.
- **Example**: Instead of debating whether a team should have eight or nine engineers in 2025, use strategy to decide what matters and let planning become lightweight.

### Decision quality over decision speed
- **When to use**: When a feature or initiative is being framed as a quick, low-stakes decision.
- **Steps**:
  1. Do not assume a decision is a true two-way door just because it seems easy to reverse.
  2. Evaluate customer motivation, differentiation, and distribution before committing.
  3. Consider the downstream cost of launch, ramp-up, QBR scrutiny, and follow-on requests.
  4. Pause for minutes, days, or weeks if needed before locking in the decision.
- **Example**: A feature approved as a “two-way door” later became a QBR burden, required excuses about low adoption, and triggered additional roadmap commitments.

### Taste calibration
- **When to use**: When trying to improve judgment as a product leader.
- **Steps**:
  1. Separate the quality of an idea from its social proof, authority bias, or catchy framing.
  2. Notice when you are drawn to metaphors, alliterations, or famous names instead of substance.
  3. Evaluate what beliefs, content, and mentors you choose to internalize.
  4. Practice identifying what is good before results are obvious.
- **Example**: He contrasted being impressed by “two-way door” or “fail fast” with the same ideas expressed as “reversible decisions” or “fail quickly,” which feel less compelling despite identical meaning.

### Operate at your natural level
- **When to use**: When your job feels frustrating even though you like the overall mission.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify whether your preferred operating level is impact, execution, or optics.
  2. Notice where you spend most of your time versus where you are naturally strongest.
  3. Avoid forcing yourself into a level that drains you for long periods.
  4. Make career moves that align with your superpowers rather than external expectations.
- **Example**: Shreyas realized his happy place was the impact level, but senior leadership pushed him toward optics-heavy work, so he chose to move back to earlier-stage product work.

### Real listening
- **When to use**: When you want to become a better leader and collaborator.
- **Steps**:
  1. Go beyond paraphrasing and eye contact.
  2. Listen for what is unsaid, not just what is spoken.
  3. Use listening as a leadership skill, not a social courtesy.
  4. Study serious thinkers on listening to deepen the practice.
- **Example**: He pointed to Rick Rubin, Peter Drucker, and others as references for a deeper understanding of listening.

### Pre-mortem
- **When to use**: Use early in the life of an important project, launch, or initiative to surface risks before they become failures.
- **Steps**:
  1. Gather the cross-functional people involved in the launch or project.
  2. Start with the prompt: imagine this project has failed miserably six months from now.
  3. Ask each participant to silently write down what could have caused the failure.
  4. Have people share their concerns aloud, using shared labels like tiger, paper tiger, and elephant.
  5. Have the group vote on the most concerning risks.
  6. As the leader, synthesize the output into a prioritized action plan.
  7. Assign mitigation work and follow up on progress.
- **Example**: For a launch, Shreyas runs separate pre-mortems for engineering risks and go-to-market risks, then turns the output into an action plan.

### LNO framework
- **When to use**: Use when deciding how to spend limited time as a PM or other high-leverage knowledge worker.
- **Steps**:
  1. Classify tasks as L (leverage), N (neutral), or O (overhead).
  2. Identify the small number of L tasks that can create outsized impact.
  3. Do L tasks when energy and focus are highest.
  4. Reduce time spent on N and O tasks to free capacity for L tasks.
  5. Adjust the level of effort based on the task type, not the task category alone.
- **Example**: A simple expense report is usually an O task, while a contentious CEO product-review note can be an L task that deserves extra care.

### Three levels of product work
- **When to use**: Use to diagnose team conflict, clarify priorities, and decide what kind of work a team should optimize for.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify whether the current discussion is about impact, execution, or optics.
  2. Check whether different stakeholders are defaulting to different levels.
  3. Align on which level matters most given the team’s context.
  4. Use the vocabulary to resolve disagreements without personalizing them.
  5. Rebalance over time if the team is over-indexing on one level.
- **Example**: A PM arguing about launch details at the execution level may clash with a CEO focused on customer impact or brand impact.

### Execution-problem diagnosis
- **When to use**: Use when a team seems stuck in repeated execution issues or recurring cross-functional friction.
- **Steps**:
  1. Check whether the issue is truly execution-related, such as infrastructure limits or a skills gap.
  2. If the same issue keeps recurring, look for a strategy problem underneath it.
  3. If strategy is not the issue, look for a culture problem, such as incentives that punish collaboration.
  4. If the issue is localized to specific people, look for an interpersonal problem.
  5. Avoid adding more process until the real root cause is identified.
- **Example**: Two teams repeatedly misaligned on priorities may not need another review meeting; they may need a clearer strategy or a culture that rewards helping other teams.

## Core Advice
- **When annual planning is consuming weeks of your time**: Build a real, aligned product strategy first, then use it to make planning mostly mechanical — because a strong strategy collapses many prioritization debates and reduces endless coordination.
- **When you are debating roadmap details like headcount splits or minor priority differences**: Stop optimizing false precision and focus on the few strategic choices that actually matter — because those numbers often change anyway.
- **When a feature request feels urgent because stakeholders want it**: Pause and evaluate customer motivation, differentiation, and distribution before saying yes — because many features get approved without enough strategic thought and later create more work than value.
- **When someone calls a decision a “two-way door”**: Challenge that framing and assume it may be a one-way door for your team — because launch, support, and roadmap commitments make reversal much harder than the metaphor suggests.
- **When you are tempted to ship because the engineer is available**: Do not let staffing convenience drive product decisions — because availability-based decisions often produce features that should not have been built in the first place.
- **When a launched feature has weak adoption but needs to look successful in a QBR**: Treat low adoption as a signal to revisit the original decision, not as a storytelling problem — because defending a weak feature compounds the mistake.
- **When you feel chronically busy despite being organized**: Look at scope growth as the likely root cause, not just your productivity system — because productivity tricks help only up to a point.
- **When your job feels frustrating even though you like the mission**: Check whether you are spending too much time in a work level that is not your natural strength — because misalignment between your superpowers and daily work creates persistent frustration.
- **When seniority pushes you toward optics-heavy work**: Consider moving to a role or company stage that lets you spend more time at your preferred operating level — because career satisfaction improves when work matches core strengths.
- **When evaluating ideas, frameworks, or advice**: Strip away authority bias, social proof, and catchy wording before deciding what to believe — because taste improves when you judge substance independently.
- **When you think you are a good listener because you can paraphrase**: Practice deeper listening by focusing on what the other person truly means, not just what they said — because real listening is a higher-order leadership skill.
- **When you face an important launch or project**: Run a pre-mortem before execution starts — because it surfaces risks early and can prevent a painful post-mortem later.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Productivity tools and time-management tricks are the main solution to being too busy. → **Their view**: Scope growth eventually overwhelms productivity hacks; the real fix is better strategy and better decisions — because even highly organized leaders get overloaded when scope expands.
- **Conventional**: Annual planning is a necessary, detailed, multi-week ritual. → **Their view**: If you have a real product strategy, planning should be fast and lightweight — because most planning ceremony exists due to lack of alignment.
- **Conventional**: Many product decisions are “two-way doors” and should be made quickly. → **Their view**: For PM leaders, most of these are effectively one-way doors because they create downstream commitments and debt — because reversal is much harder in practice.
- **Conventional**: Execution matters more than strategy, especially in strong companies. → **Their view**: Strategy is often the missing ingredient, even at successful companies like Twitter — because strong assets still fail without a compelling strategy.
- **Conventional**: Taste is mostly about product aesthetics and polished output. → **Their view**: Taste also includes what beliefs, frameworks, and sources you choose to internalize as a leader — because internal filters shape judgment.
- **Conventional**: Senior leaders should become tougher critics and say “everything is crap” more often. → **Their view**: Being dismissive is not the same as having taste — because real taste is recognizing quality before the crowd does.
- **Conventional**: Prioritization should focus on the highest ROI work. → **Their view**: In high-leverage roles, you should focus on minimizing opportunity cost instead — because good-enough work is abundant.
- **Conventional**: Execution problems should be solved with more process, more meetings, and tighter coordination. → **Their view**: Most execution problems are actually strategy, culture, or interpersonal problems — because process fixes only create temporary relief.
