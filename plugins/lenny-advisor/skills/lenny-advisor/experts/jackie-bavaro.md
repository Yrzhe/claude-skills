# Jackie Bavaro

**Author and former Head of Product Management** | **Asana** | Expertise: product-management, strategy, leadership, career development

## Bio
Jackie Bavaro is the author of *Cracking the PM Interview* and *Cracking the PM Career*, two of the most widely used books for product managers. She was Asana’s first product manager and later Head of Product Management, which gives her a rare combination of hands-on PM experience, strategy leadership, and deep insight into how PM careers actually grow.

Her perspective matters because she focuses on the practical mechanics behind good product thinking: how strategy is built, how teams align, how PMs earn trust, and how careers progress without blindly following conventional ladder-climbing advice.

## Signature Frameworks

### Three-part product strategy
- **When to use**: When defining or evaluating a product strategy for a team, product area, or company.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the vision: an inspiring picture of the future you want to create.
  2. Create the strategic framework: identify the market, what success looks like, and the big bets required to win.
  3. Build the roadmap: work backward from the vision to determine what must happen over time to make the vision feasible.
- **Example**: At Asana, the vision was a “team brain” where teams have ambient awareness of each other’s work. The strategic framework included needs like mobile, security, understanding different kinds of work, and surfacing useful connections across tasks.

### Connecting-the-dots strategy test
- **When to use**: When a strategy feels vague, overly financial, or disconnected from actual product decisions.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start from the top-level business goal, such as a revenue target.
  2. Map the intermediate logic that connects that goal to product choices.
  3. Compare candidate product investments against that logic.
  4. Identify missing assumptions by communicating the strategy and listening for confusion.
  5. Refine the explanation until people can see how the dots connect from goal to feature.
- **Example**: A CEO says the strategy is to increase revenue by 50%. Jackie argues that is not enough; the PM must connect that target to choices like monetizing existing users more deeply or entering new markets.

### Six-month strategy ramp for new PMs
- **When to use**: When a PM joins a new team or product and wants to know when to start shaping strategy.
- **Steps**:
  1. Spend the first six months learning the existing strategy rather than replacing it.
  2. Talk to customers, analyze data, and understand the product deeply.
  3. Notice recurring patterns, pain points, and emerging ideas while doing normal PM work.
  4. After about six months, block focused time to draft one part of the strategy: vision, framework, or roadmap.
  5. Use that draft to collaborate with engineering, design, and other stakeholders.
- **Example**: Jackie advises new PMs not to arrive and immediately declare the current strategy wrong, because managers often hired them to execute and build on an existing direction.

### Repeated-disagreement signal
- **When to use**: When teams keep arguing about features or decisions without resolution.
- **Steps**:
  1. Notice recurring disagreements that feel like battles of will or values.
  2. Treat those conflicts as evidence of a deeper strategy mismatch.
  3. Write down the strategic framework or principle that would resolve the disagreement at a higher level.
  4. Use that principle to guide future decisions instead of debating each feature from scratch.
- **Example**: If people repeatedly fight over whether to add a feature, the real issue is often not the feature itself but an unspoken disagreement in strategy.

### PEARL framework
- **When to use**: When answering behavioral interview questions such as describing a project you're proud of.
- **Steps**:
  1. Problem: explain the problem and why it was worth solving.
  2. Epiphany: share the key insight that changed the approach.
  3. Action: describe what you did and the obstacles you overcame.
  4. Result: show the outcome or measurable impact.
  5. Learning: explain what you learned and how it changed your future behavior.
- **Example**: Jackie uses this to assess judgment, strategy, execution, results, and growth when asking, “Tell me about a recent project that you're proud of.”

### Future-framed career conversation
- **When to use**: When seeking promotion, management opportunities, or career growth without putting your manager on the defensive.
- **Steps**:
  1. Tell your manager the role you want in the future.
  2. Ask what you should work on now to be ready when the opportunity comes.
  3. Use the conversation to focus feedback on the capabilities that matter for that next step.
  4. If needed, also seek guidance from experienced peers or managers who understand the promotion process.
- **Example**: “I would really love to become a people manager someday. What do you suggest that I work on now so that I'll be ready when the opportunity comes up?”

## Core Advice
- **When you face constant pressure to say no as a PM**: do a temporary experiment where you say yes to the underlying problem instead of shutting down the proposed solution — because collaboration helps uncover the real issue and reduces defensiveness.
- **When you face a vague executive “strategy” that is really just a target number**: connect the business goal to customer needs, market choices, and concrete product bets — because a target is not a strategy.
- **When you face confusion or weak alignment around your strategy**: communicate repeatedly and listen for where people seem confused or make different assumptions — because confusion reveals the missing links in your logic.
- **When you face a new PM role and feel tempted to rewrite the strategy immediately**: spend six months learning first — because context comes before judgment, and early rejection can damage trust.
- **When you face repeated feature debates that never seem to resolve**: run a strategy or principles exercise to surface the deeper disagreement — because tactical conflict usually points to an unspoken strategic mismatch.
- **When you face uncertainty about whether to pursue management**: treat it as a two-way door and try it thoughtfully — because management is often lonelier and less fun than IC work.
- **When you face pressure to climb the ladder mainly for money or status**: research compensation and alternative growth paths before assuming management is the only route — because senior IC PMs can earn well and grow through scope, mission, or company changes.
- **When you face early-career PM work that seems small or constrained**: do the simple, solid version well instead of overengineering — because trust is built through reliable execution.
- **When you face a desire to get promoted**: have a future-framed conversation with your manager about the role you want and what to work on now — because it makes ambition clear without sounding entitled.
- **When you face limited exposure to strategic thinking**: study how similar tradeoffs are framed in other products and domains — because strategy often transfers across contexts.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: A good PM protects focus by saying no quickly and often. → **Their view**: A PM should often say yes to the problem and stay open to collaboration, even if the proposed solution is wrong. — because fast shutdowns create defensiveness and block learning.
- **Conventional**: Strategy is mostly about setting ambitious goals or revenue targets. → **Their view**: A target is not a strategy; strategy is the connective logic between vision, market choices, and concrete product bets. — because teams need the reasoning, not just the number.
- **Conventional**: The best PM path is to become a manager as quickly as possible. → **Their view**: Management is not inherently better and may be less enjoyable; many PMs can have strong careers and compensation as ICs. — because impact and income can grow in multiple ways.
- **Conventional**: New PMs should prove themselves by arriving with bold strategic opinions immediately. → **Their view**: New PMs should spend substantial time learning before trying to redefine strategy. — because context is required for good judgment.
- **Conventional**: Career growth is mainly about promotions and title progression. → **Their view**: Career growth can also mean changing companies, changing problem spaces, increasing mission impact, or deepening craft without title changes. — because titles are only one dimension of growth.
