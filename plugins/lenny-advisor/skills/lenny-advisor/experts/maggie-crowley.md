# Maggie Crowley

**VP of Product** | **Toast** | Expertise: product strategy, product management, team leadership, B2B SaaS

## Bio
Maggie Crowley is a product leader who has worked across startups and larger companies including Toast, Charlie Health, Drift, BevSpot, and TripAdvisor. Her perspective matters because she combines strategic thinking with a strong bias toward execution, clarity, and follow-through.

She is especially useful to seek out when you need practical product judgment: how to simplify messy problems, write strategy that actually drives decisions, and build the habits that make PMs trusted by their teams.

## Signature Frameworks

### The 3 traits of standout PMs
- **When to use**: When hiring, promoting, or self-assessing product managers across company stages.
- **Steps**:
  1. Simplify complexity down to the one thing that matters most.
  2. Follow up on results after launch and close the loop on outcomes.
  3. Carry the water by doing the hard, boring, unglamorous work no one else wants to do.
- **Example**: A PM who not only writes the strategy doc, but also checks the metrics two weeks later and hops on the sales/customer calls needed to make the product succeed.

### Strategy doc from mission to bet
- **When to use**: When defining quarterly, annual, or major product strategy; especially for broader, higher-stakes decisions.
- **Steps**:
  1. State the company mission and team mission.
  2. Lay out the landscape: business state, product state, market view, competitors, SWOT, risks, and current goals.
  3. Describe the honest current state: what works, what doesn't, customer feedback, support tickets, and technical hurdles.
  4. Identify the top opportunity or opportunities: where to play, where to win, and what unique advantage supports the bet.
  5. Spell out the challenges and what must be true for the opportunity to work.
  6. Propose the solution and sequence: what to build, how it might work, how to staff it, and how to execute it.
  7. Write a short summary at the top so the conclusion is immediately visible.
  8. Share it broadly and use disagreement to pinpoint which assumption or data point is contested.
- **Example**: For annual planning, Maggie writes a long Google doc that starts with mission, then landscape, then current state, then opportunity, then solution and plan, and shares it with engineering and design for critique.

### One-pager / PRD background-first framing
- **When to use**: When defining a feature or product initiative and aligning a cross-functional team.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with background and context.
  2. Explain the problem.
  3. Explain why the problem matters.
  4. Explain why the problem matters now.
  5. Use the doc as the home base for decisions and keep a running log of decisions made and not made.
- **Example**: A product spec that begins with why the problem exists and why it is urgent, rather than jumping straight into solution details.

### Simplify by writing, then reading aloud
- **When to use**: When your writing, strategy, or thinking feels too complex or unclear.
- **Steps**:
  1. Write the idea down in plain language.
  2. Read it out loud.
  3. Cut the first two paragraphs or any unnecessary buildup.
  4. Replace abstract prose with the exact sentence you would say in conversation.
  5. Ask a peer or manager to edit and simplify it further.
- **Example**: Maggie often tells people, “Just say that” — meaning the simple sentence they said verbally is usually the sentence that should appear in the doc.

### Follow-up loop after launch
- **When to use**: When you ship a feature or experiment and need to prove impact.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the metric before launch.
  2. Set up tracking or a dashboard.
  3. Check results shortly after launch.
  4. Put calendar reminders for 2 weeks, 1 month, and 6 months later.
  5. Share the results proactively with stakeholders.
- **Example**: After a launch, Maggie sets reminders to revisit the dashboard and then reports back to her manager without being asked.

## Core Advice
- **When you face too many priorities and competing requests**: Do the work of reducing everything to one clear priority and stay with it long enough to finish — because great PMs simplify complexity and resist the urge to constantly re-litigate priorities; impact comes from finishing, not just choosing.
- **When your writing feels bloated or hard to follow**: Read it out loud and cut the first two paragraphs, then rewrite it in the exact plain language you would use in conversation — because speaking exposes unnecessary complexity and forces clarity.
- **When you are tempted to make a doc or strategy more elaborate**: Use a rule of three; keep top-level priorities, conclusions, and next steps to three items or fewer — because three forces focus and prevents dilution of the message.
- **When you launch something and move on to the next project**: Put follow-up reminders in your calendar and report the results back to stakeholders — because this helps you learn from outcomes and makes your work visible to leaders.
- **When you want to be seen as a strong PM**: Make yourself the person who never drops the ball by proactively closing loops and surfacing progress — because managers remember PMs who create confidence that the work is under control.
- **When you are deciding whether to stay deep in one company or hop around**: If possible, stay long enough to see multiple product cycles and the consequences of your decisions — because repeated cycles build judgment and expertise faster than short stints.
- **When you think “that’s not my job”**: Take on the work anyway if it helps the product move forward — because PMs are often the emotional center of the team and are accountable for outcomes, so they need to do whatever is required.
- **When you are trying to break into product management**: Get a PM title as soon as you can, even if it means joining a startup or switching laterally internally — because once you have PM on your resume, hiring becomes much easier.
- **When you are asked to be “more strategic”**: Write out the full logic chain from mission to market landscape to current state to opportunity to solution — because strategy becomes concrete when you can show how the decision follows from the evidence.
- **When your roadmap no longer matches your strategy**: Use the strategy doc to identify bloated or legacy work and challenge whether it still deserves resources — because a written strategy exposes misalignment and helps leaders question sunk-cost work.
- **When you need feedback on a strategy or spec**: Bring it to engineering and design early and ask them to shred it — because PMs should not be precious; critique improves the work and surfaces missing assumptions.
- **When you want to build a personal brand without feeling fake**: Share real problems you are actively working on and lessons from actual work, not generic advice for likes — because authenticity and lived experience make content useful and prevent it from becoming cringe or hollow.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Great PMs are primarily strategic thinkers who excel at vision and communication. → **Their view**: Strategy is only a small slice of the job; shipping, follow-through, and doing the unglamorous work matter more — because a brilliant strategy without execution creates no impact.
- **Conventional**: Being “data-driven” is always a sign of strong product thinking. → **Their view**: Overemphasis on dashboards can be a red flag because it often replaces judgment and user understanding — because data tells you what is happening, not why.
- **Conventional**: Product content and frameworks are the best way to learn product management. → **Their view**: Content can be dangerous if people mistake the framework for the goal and apply it without context — because the best PMs adapt tools to the situation.
- **Conventional**: Rewrites and big redesigns are a clean way to modernize a product. → **Their view**: Rewrites are usually a bad idea and often become long, expensive, and low-value projects — because they encourage skipping discovery and underestimating complexity.
- **Conventional**: Ownership is the key PM trait. → **Their view**: Ownership is not enough; PMs need to make bets and accept the risk of being wrong — because the role is about choosing under uncertainty and being accountable for outcomes.
