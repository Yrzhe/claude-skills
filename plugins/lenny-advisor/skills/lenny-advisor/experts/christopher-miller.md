## Signature Frameworks

### Radical Accountability Growth Model
- **When to use**: When a growth team has a narrow charter but wants to uncover outsized opportunities across the business.
- **Steps**:
  1. Treat every meaningful business problem as potentially your problem.
  2. Look for neglected surfaces in the customer journey that no team is actively improving.
  3. Ask to take ownership rather than waiting for formal permission.
  4. Ship improvements quickly and measure whether they create step-function impact.
  5. Use wins to earn broader remit and trust from the business.
- **Example**: HubSpot’s growth team noticed the in-product pricing page and self-service purchase flow were neglected, took ownership, redesigned them, and drove a major lift in self-service revenue.

### Discoverability-Desirability-Doability Audit
- **When to use**: When improving a conversion surface such as a pricing page, signup flow, or upgrade path.
- **Steps**:
  1. Assess discoverability: can users easily find the page or action?
  2. Assess desirability: is the value proposition compelling and clearly communicated?
  3. Assess doability/usability: what friction prevents completion?
  4. Redesign the flow to improve all three dimensions.
  5. Measure downstream impact on conversion and funnel physics.
- **Example**: On HubSpot’s pricing page, the team focused on how users reached the page, how value was framed, and how to remove purchase friction.

### Customer-Problem-First Decision Framework
- **When to use**: When a team is tempted to solve a business metric problem in a way that may hurt customers.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define whether the issue is a business problem, customer problem, or efficiency problem.
  2. If it is a business problem, ask why it has not solved itself.
  3. Trace the business issue back to the underlying customer problem.
  4. List assumptions and expected downstream effects.
  5. Keep asking “why” to justify the direction and “what” to understand blast radius.
  6. Choose the solution that solves the customer problem rather than masking the symptom.
- **Example**: Miller’s teams explicitly separate business problems from customer problems in product docs to avoid customer-hostile fixes.

### PLG Readiness and Design Framework
- **When to use**: When deciding whether and how to adopt product-led growth.
- **Steps**:
  1. Ask why you want to be product-led and what outcome you expect.
  2. Define what product-led means for your business.
  3. Examine the journey from signup to activation, onboarding, and purchase.
  4. Assess whether humans are necessary at each stage and why.
  5. Evaluate customer context: buying complexity, security concerns, migration pain, decision-maker type, and category familiarity.
  6. Design a modular or hybrid motion rather than forcing full self-service everywhere.
- **Example**: HubSpot uses a hybrid PLG motion because some customers self-serve but still need sales help for security reviews or migration concerns.

### PLG Prioritization by Desired Outcome
- **When to use**: When a company wants to become more product-led but is unsure where to start.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the specific outcome: top-of-funnel demand, resource efficiency, or revenue efficiency.
  2. List the assumptions behind why PLG will help.
  3. Map the outcome to the relevant stage of the customer journey.
  4. Prioritize the intervention that best matches the goal.
  5. Avoid starting with the wrong tactic for the problem.
- **Example**: If the goal is top-of-funnel demand, Miller argues self-service checkout is usually the wrong starting point.

## Core Advice
- **When you want to break into product management**: enter through a structured environment with strong managers and PM development, or volunteer to support PMs internally — because PM skill compounds through coaching, exposure, and feedback.
- **When you want to stand out as a PM**: practice relentless curiosity — because understanding the business, customers, and adjacent functions reveals opportunities others miss.
- **When growth experiments keep failing**: make bigger bets and build resilience — because a high hit rate usually means the team is playing too small.
- **When you only have quantitative data**: talk to customers, especially non-customers and churned users — because data shows what happened, not why.
- **When a metric looks broken**: trace it back to the customer problem before acting — because short-term metric fixes can damage long-term growth.
- **When leadership pushes PLG**: start with the outcome, not the tactic — because PLG can solve different problems and needs different entry points.
- **When B2B buying is complex**: use a hybrid PLG motion — because forcing pure self-service ignores security, migration, and organizational realities.
- **When building a growth function**: invest in instrumentation, engineering, design, and tooling — because growth is still R&D and needs real support.
- **When your company is early-stage**: do PLG anyway with qualitative learning — because you can learn enough from a small number of customers to improve activation and conversion.
- **When relying on one channel**: diversify aggressively — because channels can change overnight.
- **When facing a crisis**: reduce friction and be customer-friendly where possible — because goodwill can strengthen both trust and growth.
- **When receiving hard feedback**: set ego aside and absorb it — because coachability attracts mentors and sponsors.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: PLG means fully self-service, with little or no human involvement. → **Their view**: PLG is often best as a hybrid model. — because many B2B customers still need help with security, migration, procurement, or implementation.
- **Conventional**: A high experiment win rate means the team is doing great. → **Their view**: If you’re winning too often, you’re probably thinking too small. — because meaningful growth work requires ambitious bets.
- **Conventional**: You need a huge dataset before doing PLG well. → **Their view**: You can start with qualitative research and small-sample learning. — because PLG is about the product communicating value, not just statistical scale.
- **Conventional**: Solve business problems directly. → **Their view**: Reframe them as customer problems first. — because customer-hostile fixes often create long-term damage.
- **Conventional**: Enterprise expansion is the default SaaS path. → **Their view**: SMB and mid-market focus can be strategically stronger. — because distributed revenue reduces dependence on any single account and keeps the product broadly useful.
- **Conventional**: Growth loops are the universal model. → **Their view**: In B2B SaaS, broader flywheels are often more realistic. — because classic loops are harder without strong user-generated or community dynamics.
