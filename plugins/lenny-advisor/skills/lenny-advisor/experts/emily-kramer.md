## Bio
Emily Kramer is a B2B startup marketing leader who has built marketing functions from scratch at companies like Asana, Carta, Ticketfly, and Astro. Her perspective matters because she translates marketing into practical operating systems founders can actually use—especially for early hiring, team design, and how product and marketing should work together.

She is especially useful when a company is trying to figure out what kind of marketing help it really needs, rather than just hiring by title. Her thinking is grounded in startup reality: limited resources, unclear ownership, and the need to connect strategy to measurable growth.

## Signature Frameworks

### Fuel and Engine
- **When to use**: When deciding what kind of marketing help to hire, where marketing is broken, or which growth constraint to solve first.
- **Steps**:
  1. Ignore traditional marketing sub-function labels at first.
  2. Define your fuel: the things you create that add value, such as positioning, messaging, website copy, content, templates, tools, webinars, and design assets.
  3. Define your engine: the systems that distribute fuel to the right people, such as email, SEO, paid channels, social, lifecycle flows, segmentation, ops, and tracking.
  4. Diagnose the bottleneck: ask whether growth is constrained by weak content/messaging or by poor distribution/conversion infrastructure.
  5. Hire or prioritize work based on the bottleneck, while ensuring both sides eventually exist.
- **Example**: A company may have a strong outbound engine but weak messaging, so emails are being sent with little value inside them. Another team may create great content but fail to distribute it effectively.

### Pie-Shaped Marketer
- **When to use**: When hiring the first marketer or early marketing team members at a startup.
- **Steps**:
  1. Avoid hiring a narrow specialist as the first marketer.
  2. Look for someone with deep expertise in one core area: product marketing, content/community, or growth/demand.
  3. Ensure they are proficient in a second area.
  4. Ensure they can still set strategy and manage contractors across all major marketing areas.
  5. Match the two strongest areas to the company’s business model and current bottleneck.
- **Example**: An ideal early hire might be a product marketer who also understands growth, or a product marketer who also understands content.

### Three Early Marketing Archetypes
- **When to use**: When founders need a simpler way to map marketing roles to company needs.
- **Steps**:
  1. Classify early marketing needs into three broad archetypes: content/community, growth/demand, and product marketing.
  2. Map each archetype to the company’s business model and current growth problem.
  3. For top-down sales models, lean toward product marketing or demand/growth depending on whether messaging or distribution is the bottleneck.
  4. For PLG models, ensure someone owns acquisition and web conversion, while product handles in-product experimentation with close collaboration.
- **Example**: In a top-down sales business, SDRs may already provide some engine, so the next need may be fuel. In a PLG business, if users are finding the product but the funnel is leaky, the need may be more engine.

### GACCS Marketing Brief
- **When to use**: Before launching a major marketing initiative, campaign, or product launch to align stakeholders and avoid rework.
- **Steps**:
  1. Goals: define what success looks like.
  2. Audience: specify who the work is for.
  3. Creative: clarify the unique angle or what will make it stand out.
  4. Channels: define how it will be distributed.
  5. Stakeholders: identify the DRI, contributors, and reviewers.
- **Example**: After product shares what is being built, marketing responds with a GACCS brief so product can weigh in on audience, messaging, and channels before assets are created.

### AOR/DRI Ownership Mapping
- **When to use**: When product and marketing collaboration is messy, responsibilities are unclear, or teams are scaling.
- **Steps**:
  1. List areas of responsibility independent of job titles.
  2. Assign a directly responsible individual (DRI) for each area.
  3. Make the list visible so other teams know exactly whom to involve.
  4. Update the list as the team grows and responsibilities split.
  5. Use the map to support launches, onboarding work, and cross-functional planning.
- **Example**: At Asana, a PM might own onboarding tests while a marketer owns the copy, making collaboration easy without confusion.

## Core Advice
- **When you face confusion about what kind of marketer to hire first**: Do a fuel-versus-engine diagnosis before writing the job description — because most bad early marketing hires happen when founders hire for the wrong bottleneck.
- **When you face pressure to hire someone with exact industry experience**: Prioritize business model experience over industry experience — because marketing motions differ more by GTM model than by customer vertical.
- **When you face the decision of hiring a very senior marketer from a big public company**: Do not make that person your first marketing hire unless they have real early-stage, hands-on experience — because large-company marketers are often too specialized.
- **When you face the need to hire your first marketer at an early-stage startup**: Do hire a generalist, ideally a pie-shaped marketer with depth in one area and proficiency in another — because early marketing requires strategy, execution, and breadth.
- **When you face uncertainty about whether it is too early to hire marketing**: Wait until you have at least some evidence of product-market fit and a clear answer to what the marketer would actually do — because marketing accelerates growth, it does not define it from scratch.
- **When you face a PLG growth problem**: Do separate top-of-funnel acquisition ownership from in-product experimentation ownership, but create a tight collaboration model between marketing and product — because each team has different strengths.
- **When you face friction between product and marketing teams**: Do create explicit DRI ownership and a shared planning process before the quarter starts — because ambiguity creates launch-time conflict.
- **When you face repeated launch rework or stakeholder disagreement**: Do use a lightweight brief like GACCS before creating assets — because early alignment saves time later.
- **When you face a marketing team that seems busy but hard to evaluate**: Do ask for their big bets, foundational work, and the metrics tied to each — because strong teams distinguish maintenance from step-change work.
- **When you face marketing goals framed as output counts like blog posts or campaigns**: Do replace activity goals with impact goals tied to funnel metrics and downstream conversion quality — because output can hide weak growth.
- **When you face a website that product still owns by default**: Do move toward a setup where marketing can update the website quickly, ideally without engineering bottlenecks — because messaging and conversion need constant iteration.
- **When you face interest in becoming an angel investor with an operating background**: Do clearly product-market yourself around a specific functional value-add — because founders and investors can route relevant opportunities to you more easily.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: The first marketing hire should have deep experience in your exact industry or buyer category. → **Their view**: Business model experience matters more than industry experience — because enterprise sales, top-down GTM, and PLG require fundamentally different marketing motions.
- **Conventional**: Product-led growth means product drives growth and marketing is less central. → **Their view**: PLG is often product plus marketing, not product instead of marketing — because marketing still matters for acquisition, messaging, and conversion.
- **Conventional**: You need a product marketer because that is the standard first marketing hire. → **Their view**: Diagnose the bottleneck first; product marketing is common, but not always right — because some companies need more fuel and others need more engine.
- **Conventional**: Marketing success can be judged by how much content or how many campaigns the team ships. → **Their view**: Activity metrics are often a sign of weak marketing management; impact and conversion metrics matter more — because output does not guarantee growth.
- **Conventional**: The website and conversion flow are naturally product-owned in a product-led company. → **Their view**: Marketing should usually own the website and top-of-funnel conversion experience, with product owning in-product flows — because marketing is better at messaging iteration and web conversion.
