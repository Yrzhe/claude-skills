# Andy Johns

**Mental health advocate, writer, and coach** | **Clues.Life / Heroic Hearts Project** | Expertise: mental health, burnout recovery, personal transformation, high-performer wellbeing

## Bio
Andy Johns is a former growth and product leader at Facebook, Twitter, Quora, and Wealthfront who left a high-powered tech career after severe burnout and a health scare. His perspective matters because he combines firsthand experience of high achievement and collapse with a deep focus on healing, self-inquiry, and helping other high performers recover.

He now works as a mental health advocate, writer, and coach, with a particular emphasis on burned-out high achievers and veterans. His lens is unusually practical: he looks for the body-level, emotional, and identity-level signals that something is off long before a person fully breaks down.

## Signature Frameworks

### Four-step deep personal transformation process
- **When to use**: When someone is experiencing significant burnout, existential distress, or a major identity-level life transition
- **Steps**:
  1. Suffer: acknowledge the pain rather than minimizing it; major transformation usually begins with real suffering
  2. Seek the truth: investigate the root causes of the suffering through therapy, reflection, and examining past experiences and subconscious patterns
  3. Practice self-compassion and self-love: once the truth is understood, recognize that much of the suffering is not your fault and begin acting in ways that are kind to yourself
  4. Develop compassion toward others: understanding and forgiving yourself naturally expands into empathy for other people and their conditioning
- **Example**: Andy’s own panic attacks, depression, and heart scare led him to investigate childhood trauma related to his mother’s illness and death; that truth helped him step away from his career and enter treatment as an act of self-love, which then changed how he saw others.

### Burnout detection via fundamental functions
- **When to use**: When trying to distinguish normal job stress from a deeper mental health or burnout problem
- **Steps**:
  1. Check core functions: sleep, diet, exercise, playfulness, socialization, and physical health
  2. Look for persistent disruption rather than isolated bad days
  3. Treat repeated breakdowns in these basics as a flashing red alarm
  4. Interpret bodily symptoms as signals that something in your life needs to change
- **Example**: Andy pointed to his own poor sleep, strained wellbeing, severe teeth grinding, and heart scare at 35 as obvious body-level warnings he had ignored.

### Journaling to uncover emotional triggers
- **When to use**: When someone wants to begin self-inquiry but is not ready for therapy or wants a complementary daily practice
- **Steps**:
  1. Use pen and paper regularly in a quiet setting
  2. Either write unstructured and let thoughts flow, or start with a list of recent situations where you became highly emotional or reactive
  3. For each trigger, ask repeatedly: “Why did that happen?”
  4. Keep digging until you reach a truth that feels deeply uncomfortable or like an epiphany
- **Example**: Andy suggested listing moments like a political conversation that made you irrationally angry or a social setting that triggered insecurity, then tracing the reflex back to what lies underneath it.

### Therapist selection by safety and fit
- **When to use**: When beginning therapy and trying to find the right practitioner
- **Steps**:
  1. If in acute distress, see any qualified professional as soon as possible
  2. Otherwise, “speed date” a few therapists if possible
  3. Choose someone with whom you feel safe and comfortable
  4. Prefer someone you respect intellectually and who can offer insights that genuinely expand your thinking
  5. Use the therapist as a starting router to other modalities if needed, such as somatic work or breathwork
- **Example**: Andy said the first therapist he saw helped identify that he was having panic attacks, and he described good therapists as routers who can direct you to specialists in body-based or nervous-system work.

### Pattern interruption for rewiring self-worth
- **When to use**: When someone has identified a negative core belief, such as low self-worth, and wants to change daily behavior
- **Steps**:
  1. Name the core belief and notice how it shows up in everyday interactions
  2. Catch conditioned responses in real time
  3. Replace the old response with a healthier one
  4. Repeat consistently so small actions accumulate into a new internal narrative
- **Example**: Andy shared that when praised, he used to deflect compliments. A boss told him to simply say “thank you,” and he now deliberately accepts compliments as a way to retrain his self-worth.

## Core Advice
- **When you face persistent stress and aren't sure whether it's normal or something deeper**: Do an audit of your fundamentals: sleep, relationships, physical health, diet, exercise, playfulness, and socialization — because burnout and psychological distress often show up first as disruptions in basic human functioning.
- **When you face acute emotional distress, panic, or fear that you may harm yourself**: Do the fastest possible reach-out to a therapist, psychologist, psychiatrist, counselor, or other trained professional — because in crisis, speed matters more than optimization.
- **When you face a sense that something is wrong but you're not ready for therapy**: Do a daily pen-and-paper journaling practice, either unstructured or by tracing recent emotional reactions back to their causes — because writing helps surface subconscious patterns without needing immediate external guidance.
- **When you face the challenge of finding the right therapist**: Do a few trial sessions and choose someone who makes you feel safe and whom you respect intellectually — because healing requires both nervous-system safety and trust in the therapist’s insight.
- **When you face recurring emotional overreactions or knee-jerk triggers**: Do a trigger inventory: write down the recent moments when you became highly reactive, then ask “why?” repeatedly until you hit the deeper cause — because strong reflexive reactions often reveal hidden beliefs or wounds.
- **When you face a deeply ingrained low-self-worth narrative**: Do small pattern interruptions every day, such as accepting compliments directly instead of deflecting them — because identity-level change usually happens through repeated micro-actions.
- **When you face pressure to make a huge life change because you're unhappy**: Do not assume you need a total reinvention; first consider smaller “micro transitions” like changing jobs, downsizing, or leaving a misaligned relationship — because many people need alignment, not total collapse.
- **When you face uncertainty about what healing method will work for you**: Do experiments across modalities and personalize your path instead of copying someone else’s formula — because healing is highly individual.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Career success, achievement, and climbing the next mountain will eventually make you feel whole and fulfilled. → **Their view**: Achievement can become an unhealthy adaptation and an addiction — because it may temporarily soothe pain while quietly worsening wellbeing.
- **Conventional**: If you're functioning at work, your stress is probably just normal startup pressure. → **Their view**: Many high performers are in much deeper distress than they realize — because burnout creeps up slowly and often hides behind competence.
- **Conventional**: Mental health problems are mostly in your head. → **Their view**: The body is often the clearest place to look — because suffering shows up physically through sleep disruption, grinding, heart issues, and chronic symptoms.
- **Conventional**: Transformation is mainly about learning techniques and optimizing yourself. → **Their view**: Real transformation usually starts with suffering and truth, not optimization hacks — because deep change is identity-level, not just tactical.
- **Conventional**: The best path is to follow proven systems or someone else's roadmap to healing. → **Their view**: You have to find your own path — because people are too different in history, temperament, and conditioning for one formula to fit all.
- **Conventional**: You should tightly control and optimize your life trajectory. → **Their view**: Sometimes the healthier approach is surrender — because over-control can be exhausting and misaligned.
