# Donna Lichaw

**Executive coach, speaker, and bestselling author** | **Donna Lichaw** | Expertise: leadership, executive coaching, design, strategy

## Bio
Donna Lichaw helps founders, CEOs, and executive teams become more effective leaders without losing themselves in the process. With roots in product management and design, she brings a rare blend of story, psychology, and systems thinking to leadership development.

Her perspective matters because she does not treat leadership as a generic skill set. She focuses on the internal narratives, strengths, and patterns that shape how leaders actually show up, then uses practical experiments to help them change in ways that are real, sustainable, and authentic.

## Signature Frameworks

### Story-driven leadership
- **When to use**: When a leader feels stuck, ineffective, conflicted, or trapped by a story they tell themselves about their leadership.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the story you are telling yourself.
  2. Treat it as a hypothesis, not a fact.
  3. Gather feedback from people around you.
  4. Compare your internal story with external evidence.
  5. Rewrite the story into something more accurate and useful.
  6. Lead from the new story.
- **Example**: A CEO believed he was “too nice” and therefore not taken seriously. Feedback showed his team actually experienced him as caring and heartfelt; what they wanted was clearer vision and less micromanagement.

### Data-driven story testing
- **When to use**: When you want to know whether a belief like “I’m too quiet” or “no one listens to me” is true, false, or only partly true.
- **Steps**:
  1. Name the belief clearly.
  2. Collect direct feedback.
  3. Look for patterns in how others experience you.
  4. Identify the underlying need behind the feedback.
  5. Adjust behavior or communication based on the real issue.
- **Example**: An executive was told she was too quiet in meetings. The deeper issue was that her silence read as disinterest, so she explained her processing style and clarified that she was listening.

### Kryptonite analysis
- **When to use**: When a trait, habit, insecurity, or neurodivergent pattern feels like a weakness but may also contain useful energy.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the trait you see as limiting.
  2. Ask how it serves you.
  3. Distinguish helpful use from harmful overuse.
  4. Decide whether to avoid, accommodate, or harness it.
  5. Build systems and boundaries around it.
- **Example**: Donna reframed imposter syndrome as a growth signal for a founder. It helped the founder learn quickly, but the risk was overworking and burnout.

### Superpower discovery through life stories
- **When to use**: When you want to identify your core strengths in a way that is more concrete than a personality test.
- **Steps**:
  1. Choose peak experiences from childhood, recent work, and your career path.
  2. Notice when you felt most energized and effective.
  3. Look for recurring themes across stories.
  4. Name the underlying strength in concrete terms.
  5. Apply it intentionally to future challenges.
  6. Watch for overuse.
- **Example**: One executive thought she was detail-oriented, but her stories revealed her real strength was connecting themes, people, and ideas.

### Small experiments for personal change
- **When to use**: When a behavior change or identity shift feels risky or abstract.
- **Steps**:
  1. Treat the change as a hypothesis.
  2. Run a tiny experiment first.
  3. Observe what happens.
  4. Increase the size of the experiment gradually.
  5. Decide what to keep based on the data.
- **Example**: Before asking a leader to spend a whole meeting listening, Donna first ran a 30-second role-play to surface discomfort and learn from it.

### Head, heart, hands evaluation
- **When to use**: When assessing the outcome of an experiment, interaction, or decision involving people.
- **Steps**:
  1. Check your head: what do you think happened?
  2. Check your heart: what did you feel?
  3. Check your hands/body: what physical signals showed up?
  4. Integrate all three.
  5. Choose the next action from the full picture.
- **Example**: A client said an exercise was “fine,” but their red face and burning hands revealed a much stronger emotional reaction.

### Start with the ending
- **When to use**: When someone cannot clearly articulate what they want next.
- **Steps**:
  1. Imagine a future point where things have gone exceptionally well.
  2. Describe what you see, hear, feel, and who is there.
  3. Name what you accomplished and why it matters.
  4. Work backward from that future.
  5. Turn the path into experiments.
  6. Identify the first thing to test.
- **Example**: Donna asks clients to imagine a few years out and describe the best period of their life, then reverse-engineer the journey.

## Core Advice
- **When you have a painful belief about yourself as a leader**: gather direct feedback from the people who work with you — because internal stories are often distorted or incomplete.
- **When feedback feels true but confusing**: look for the need beneath the feedback — because the literal complaint is often not the real issue.
- **When you experience imposter syndrome**: ask how it is serving you — because it may be a growth signal rather than a flaw to eliminate.
- **When you think you have a fatal weakness**: distinguish between something to avoid and something to harness — because many “kryptonite” traits contain useful energy.
- **When you are unsure of your strengths**: mine your life stories for recurring peak patterns — because strengths become clearer across contexts than in abstract labels.
- **When you feel pressure to fix everything you’re bad at**: amplify strengths and work around weaknesses — because impact usually comes from differentiation, not average competence everywhere.
- **When you want to change a behavior or identity**: run a tiny experiment first — because small tests reduce risk and produce real data.
- **When an experiment feels “fine” intellectually**: check head, heart, and body — because logic alone misses emotional and embodied truth.
- **When you feel chronically drained at work**: audit what gives versus depletes energy — because misalignment is often a role or context problem, not a motivation problem.
- **When you cannot name your next chapter**: start with a vivid ending and work backward — because a compelling future creates direction and momentum.
- **When you have a strong emotional reaction in the moment**: pause and ask, “Isn’t that interesting?” — because curiosity interrupts defensiveness.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Leadership is mainly about telling compelling stories to others. → **Their view**: The most important stories are the ones leaders tell themselves — because internal narratives shape identity, confidence, and behavior.
- **Conventional**: Leadership should be user-centered and focused primarily on others. → **Their view**: Effective leadership has to start with you first, then radiate outward — because purpose and sustainable energy must come from within.
- **Conventional**: If feedback says change a behavior, do the opposite behavior. → **Their view**: Solve for the underlying need, not the literal behavior — because the visible complaint is often a proxy.
- **Conventional**: Imposter syndrome is purely bad. → **Their view**: It can be functional if understood well — because it may signal a growth edge.
- **Conventional**: Weaknesses should mainly be fixed. → **Their view**: Many “weaknesses” are also strengths in disguise — because traits can be liabilities in one context and assets in another.
- **Conventional**: Spend lots of effort fixing what you’re bad at. → **Their view**: Double down on strengths and work around weaknesses — because people create more impact that way.
