# Teresa Torres

**Product coach, consultant, teacher, speaker, and author** | **Product Talk** | Expertise: product discovery, user research, product strategy, product management

## Bio
Teresa Torres is the author of *Continuous Discovery Habits* and the founder of Product Talk, where she teaches teams how to build customer feedback loops into everyday product development. She has coached hundreds of teams and taught roughly 11,000+ students, making her one of the most influential voices in modern product discovery.

Her perspective matters because she focuses on making discovery practical, repeatable, and embedded in the weekly rhythm of product teams. She is especially valuable when a team needs to move from opinion-driven roadmaps to evidence-based product decisions.

## Signature Frameworks

### Opportunity Solution Tree
- **When to use**: Use when you need to connect an outcome to concrete product decisions, align a team around strategy, or prioritize what to build next.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with a desired outcome at the root of the tree.
  2. Map the opportunity space by identifying unmet needs, pain points, and desires in the customer experience.
  3. Structure opportunities as moments in the customer journey or experience map, not as solutions.
  4. Decompose large opportunities into smaller, more actionable opportunities.
  5. Generate multiple solutions for the most important opportunities.
  6. Optionally add assumption tests beneath solutions to validate the riskiest assumptions.
- **Example**: For Netflix, the outcome might be “get people to watch more.” Opportunities could include deciding what to watch, evaluating whether a show is good, and the viewing experience. Under “deciding what to watch,” a specific opportunity might be “it’s hard to enter my password with the Apple TV remote.”

### Continuous Discovery
- **When to use**: Use when building digital products that are never “done” and require ongoing decisions about what to build next.
- **Steps**:
  1. Build a recurring customer feedback loop into the product team’s weekly cadence.
  2. Interview customers regularly to learn about unmet needs, pain points, and desires.
  3. Use those insights to identify opportunities.
  4. Test assumptions continuously as part of delivery work.
  5. Use the results to improve future bets and decisions.
- **Example**: A team can do one customer interview per week and several small assumption tests in parallel, rather than waiting for a large research project before shipping.

### Story-Based Customer Interviewing
- **When to use**: Use when you need reliable insight into customer behavior, context, and unmet needs.
- **Steps**:
  1. Ask about a specific recent event instead of abstract preferences.
  2. Set the scene by asking where the person was, who they were with, and what triggered the behavior.
  3. Walk through the timeline with “what happened next?” questions.
  4. Stay curious and follow the story rather than relying on a fixed script.
  5. Summarize and probe when you hear friction or a surprising detail.
- **Example**: Instead of asking “How do you decide what to watch on Netflix?” ask “Tell me about the last time you watched something on a streaming service,” then follow the timeline from deciding to watch through choosing a title.

### Assumption Testing
- **When to use**: Use when you want to validate a solution idea quickly without running a large, expensive experiment.
- **Steps**:
  1. Break an idea into its underlying assumptions.
  2. Prioritize the assumptions by risk.
  3. Design the smallest test that can validate a single assumption.
  4. Run multiple small tests in parallel across a few ideas.
  5. Use the results to compare and contrast solution options.
- **Example**: Instead of testing an entire product concept for weeks, a team can run half a dozen to a dozen small assumption tests in one week across three ideas.

### Automated Interview Recruiting
- **When to use**: Use when you want customer interviews to happen every week without the product team manually recruiting each time.
- **Steps**:
  1. Create an opt-in mechanism where customers can volunteer while using the product.
  2. Use an intercept survey or in-product prompt to ask for interview participation.
  3. Route positive responses into scheduling software automatically.
  4. For B2B buyers or decision-makers, use sales, account management, or support teams as recruiting channels.
  5. Define a weekly trigger so the team only needs to show up and conduct the interview.
- **Example**: An in-product prompt can ask, “Do you have 20 minutes to talk to us?” and, if the user says yes, send them directly to scheduling.

## Core Advice
- **When you face a feature-factory culture that prescribes solutions instead of outcomes**: Do not wait for organizational change; start working in an outcome-oriented way yourself and talk to customers anyway — because you can often change your own habits even if the company is not ready to change.
- **When you are told you are not allowed to talk to customers**: Use your personal network or adjacent teams to find people like your users and start there — because you do not need perfect internal permission to begin learning from real customer stories.
- **When leaders say there is no time for discovery**: Reframe the issue as a lack of time for project-based research, then fold discovery into weekly work instead of treating it as a separate phase — because continuous discovery fits the cadence of digital product development.
- **When you need to recruit customers consistently for interviews**: Build an opt-in recruiting system inside the product or through customer-facing teams so interviews appear on the calendar automatically — because making the desired behavior easier than the alternative increases follow-through.
- **When you are interviewing customers**: Ask about a specific recent experience rather than abstract opinions or preferences — because people are more reliable when recounting a real event in context.
- **When you want to uncover opportunities**: Focus on collecting stories and listening for unmet needs, pain points, and desires instead of just collecting facts — because opportunities emerge from stories and context.
- **When your interview feels scripted or overly long**: Use a conversational cadence and follow the timeline with simple prompts like “What happened next?” — because natural conversation surfaces more detail and keeps you from over-focusing on your next question.
- **When you hear a vague opportunity like “make it easier to use”**: Reframe it into a specific, observable pain point tied to a particular user and moment — because specific opportunities are solvable.
- **When you already think you know the solution**: Generate multiple solution options for the same opportunity before deciding — because compare-and-contrast leads to better decisions than the first obvious answer.
- **When you are deciding how much discovery to do**: Invest more discovery in core product experiences and differentiators, and less in low-risk flows that are already working — because the amount of research should match the risk of the bet.
- **When you are new to discovery and unsure how much to trust your judgment**: Overindex on doing a bit too much discovery at first, then calibrate based on what you learn about risk — because early over-investment helps build intuition.
- **When you are working in a large company with many adjacent teams**: Keep the same discovery habits, but add lateral collaboration to coordinate dependencies and maintain a coherent product experience — because larger organizations require more coordination.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Discovery is a separate phase that happens before delivery. → **Their view**: Discovery and delivery happen continuously and in parallel. — because assumption testing is part of delivery, and the goal is to improve the quality of bets over time.
- **Conventional**: You need large-scale research or many interviews to make reliable product decisions. → **Their view**: One interview is better than zero, and small data is often enough to make a better bet. — because product teams are making decisions to change behavior, not to seek universal truth.
- **Conventional**: The product manager should be the decider or CEO of the product. → **Their view**: The best teams decide collaboratively, often as a trio of PM, designer, and engineer. — because shared understanding reduces politics and improves decisions.
- **Conventional**: Good interviewing means asking many structured why/how questions. → **Their view**: Good interviewing is mostly about eliciting a real story through curiosity and timeline prompts. — because long protocols feel unnatural and produce shallow answers.
- **Conventional**: User research should be held to a high evidence standard before influencing decisions. → **Their view**: Product teams are unfairly singled out; everyone in business makes decisions with limited data. — because the real question is whether decision-making improves over time.
- **Conventional**: You should test the whole product idea with a big experiment before building. → **Their view**: Break ideas into assumptions and test the smallest risky assumption first. — because large experiments are slow and expensive.
