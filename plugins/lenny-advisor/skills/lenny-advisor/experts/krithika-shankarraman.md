# Krithika Shankarraman

**Executive in Residence** | **Thrive Capital** | Expertise: marketing, go-to-market, product marketing, AI strategy

## Bio
Krithika Shankarraman was the first marketing hire at OpenAI and Stripe, and also held early marketing leadership roles at Retool, Dropbox, and Google. She now advises Thrive Capital portfolio companies on marketing, positioning, and early go-to-market, with a perspective shaped by building category-defining companies from the ground up.

Her viewpoint matters because she does not treat marketing as a set of tactics; she treats it as diagnosis, differentiation, and customer understanding. She is especially useful when a company thinks it has a “marketing problem” but actually needs help identifying the real bottleneck.

## Signature Frameworks

### DATE framework
- **When to use**: When a company thinks it needs marketing help but hasn’t yet diagnosed the real problem.
- **Steps**:
  1. Diagnose the actual problem by inspecting the funnel and determining whether the issue is awareness, conversion, product-market fit, or something else.
  2. Analyze competitors’ approaches to identify baseline patterns, gaps, and opportunities.
  3. Take a different path by intentionally differentiating rather than copying the market.
  4. Experiment, test, validate, and scale what works while discarding what doesn’t.
- **Example**: At Retool, the team diagnosed that paid social was not driving meaningful pipeline, analyzed that competitors were leaning on content and events, chose a different path by doubling down on customer storytelling, and then tested webinars, dinners, and event formats before scaling the winners.

### Marketing diagnosis before hiring
- **When to use**: When founders want to hire a demand gen, paid growth, SEO, or content person without understanding the bottleneck.
- **Steps**:
  1. Look at top-of-funnel volume and whether it converts into sales-qualified opportunities.
  2. Assess whether the real issue is lack of demand, weak product-market fit, or poor bottom-of-funnel conversion.
  3. Match the marketing hire to the bottleneck: demand gen for top-of-funnel gaps, product marketing for positioning and competitive differentiation, or brand/community for awareness and trust.
- **Example**: If leads are coming in but prospects keep asking about competitors and pricing, she argues the company likely needs product marketing and positioning help more than another demand gen hire.

### Launch excellence as a product discipline
- **When to use**: When a company ships features but customers are not aware of them or do not understand their value.
- **Steps**:
  1. Treat launch as incomplete until the customer knows about the feature.
  2. Use marketing to communicate what the feature does and why it matters.
  3. Measure usage and engagement, not just whether the feature was technically shipped.
- **Example**: At Stripe, she found a backlog of shipped features that had never been communicated to customers and turned launch into a usage-and-engagement-oriented process rather than a code-complete milestone.

### Customer-language positioning loop
- **When to use**: When messaging feels generic or disconnected from what users actually care about.
- **Steps**:
  1. Talk to customers, support, and non-users to hear how they describe their problems.
  2. Use their language as a shortcut to stronger messaging and landing pages.
  3. Create educational content that helps users recognize use cases and nod along as they read.
- **Example**: At Stripe, support conversations revealed recurring questions like whether Stripe handled subscriptions or payouts, which led to landing pages and docs that directly answered those needs.

### Brand as a company-wide system
- **When to use**: When a company thinks brand is only a marketing function or only about visual identity.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the experience you want customers to have across every touchpoint.
  2. Align product, support, recruiting, and marketing around that experience.
  3. Use consistency to increase trust and speed, not as a separate aesthetic exercise.
- **Example**: She described brand as including product experience, customer support behavior, and candidate experience—not just marketing artifacts.

## Core Advice
- **When you think you need to hire a growth or demand gen marketer immediately**: First diagnose the funnel to see whether the real issue is awareness, conversion, product-market fit, or positioning — because throwing more top-of-funnel fuel at a leaky bottom-of-funnel problem wastes time and money.
- **When your product has strong awareness but users still say they don’t know what to do with it**: Use marketing to create use-case epiphanies and show people what the product can do in their own lives or workflows — because marketing can unlock adoption by helping users understand concrete applications, not just by generating clicks.
- **When your company is shipping features but adoption is low**: Treat launch as incomplete until customers are informed, educated, and activated on the feature — because a feature that is code-complete but invisible to users has not actually created value.
- **When your paid channels show activity but not revenue impact**: Litigate the channels against downstream outcomes like pipeline, opportunity, and revenue rather than vanity metrics — because clicks, impressions, and views can look good while contributing little to business results.
- **When competitors all seem to be using the same marketing tactics**: Study them for baseline understanding, then intentionally choose a different path that fits your unique strengths — because copying the market leads to sameness; differentiation is a core marketing advantage.
- **When your company has unusually strong customer logos or proof points**: Turn customer storytelling into a primary growth lever — because authentic customer stories are hard for competitors to replicate and can be more persuasive than generic campaigns.
- **When you are marketing a technical product to developers**: Invest in deep product understanding and high-quality, polished communication — because developers are quick to spot inaccuracies or superficiality in both code and marketing.
- **When your company is scaling quickly and new hires need to be productive fast**: Create lightweight but clear review processes and guardrails so people can operate self-sufficiently — because good process speeds up execution by making expectations and decision criteria explicit.
- **When you are deciding whether to gate a product capability behind sales**: Test whether self-serve access could unlock more demand and better align sales on higher-value deals — because some capabilities are valuable to smaller customers too, and gating them can unnecessarily constrain growth.
- **When you are pricing an AI product**: Experiment with pricing models and value metrics instead of assuming SaaS or usage-based pricing will fit — because AI value creation often does not map cleanly to traditional pricing structures, so market testing is necessary.
- **When you are building a marketing career in a fast-changing environment**: Develop a broad, adaptable skill set rather than staying narrowly specialized — because modern marketing leaders need to combine analytics, creativity, product understanding, and cross-functional collaboration.
- **When you are early in your career and tempted to outsource thinking to AI**: Use AI as an accelerator, but still learn the underlying concepts and mechanics yourself — because if the discipline changes, people who understand fundamentals will adapt better and use AI more effectively.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Marketing is mostly about top-of-funnel awareness, clicks, impressions, and demand generation. → **Their view**: Those metrics are often bullshit; marketing should be diagnosed based on the real bottleneck and the customer experience you want to create. — because a product can already have awareness and still need help with use-case understanding, positioning, or bottom-of-funnel conversion.
- **Conventional**: The best way to grow is to copy what successful companies did. → **Their view**: There is no universal playbook; copying outputs without understanding inputs leads to bad decisions. — because context, timing, competitive landscape, and company-specific variables determine what works.
- **Conventional**: Cheaper products win in competitive markets. → **Their view**: Being cheaper is a race to the bottom, especially as models and infrastructure get cheaper over time. — because durable advantage comes from differentiation, novelty, and alignment with user values and goals.
- **Conventional**: Brand and velocity are tradeoffs. → **Their view**: Good process and strong brand actually speed companies up. — because consistency creates trust and clarity, which helps teams move faster and make better decisions.
- **Conventional**: Marketing is a siloed function owned by the marketing team. → **Their view**: Marketing is a whole-company motion involving product, sales, support, recruiting, and leadership. — because the brand is the sum of many touchpoints, not just campaigns or assets.
- **Conventional**: AI will make taste and writing less important because anyone can generate decent content. → **Their view**: Taste becomes more important in the AI era because AI increases the volume of generic output. — because differentiation will come from craft, judgment, and deep understanding of the customer and product.
