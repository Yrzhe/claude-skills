# Ian McAllister

**Senior Director of Product and Tech for Vehicles** | **Uber** | Expertise: product-management, leadership, strategy, go-to-market

## Bio
Ian McAllister is a veteran product leader who has held roles at Microsoft, Amazon, Airbnb, and Uber. He is especially influential for his thinking on what separates elite product managers and for bringing Amazon’s “working backwards” discipline into practical product decision-making.

His perspective matters because he blends product craft with operating rigor: clear communication, ruthless prioritization, trust-building, and customer-first strategy. He is especially useful when you need a grounded view on how PMs actually become effective leaders, not just feature managers.

## Signature Frameworks

### Top 1% PM skill model
- **When to use**: When assessing PM capability or planning growth from junior PM to senior product leader.
- **Steps**:
  1. Build fundamentals first: communicate clearly, prioritize ruthlessly, execute reliably.
  2. Add adjacent judgment: simplify, forecast and measure, understand technical trade-offs, design, and copy.
  3. At senior levels, expand into leadership: think big, earn trust, dig for data, push back effectively, adapt to change.
  4. Stay driven by impact rather than promotion.
  5. Self-grade after meetings, docs, and decisions to improve continuously.
- **Example**: For a new PM, Ian would emphasize communication, prioritization, and execution. For a senior PM, he would emphasize communication, thinking big, earning trust, and being impact-driven.

### Working Backwards
- **When to use**: When evaluating a new product or major feature and you want to ensure it starts from a real customer problem.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with the customer and the problem, not the solution.
  2. Write the problem clearly and compellingly.
  3. Only then describe the solution.
  4. Use a press release and FAQ, or similar structure, to force clarity.
  5. Check whether there is a legitimate plan to succeed operationally, financially, and technically.
  6. Use the doc to align stakeholders before committing resources.
- **Example**: Ian noted that Jeff Bezos would challenge teams if a press release lacked a problem paragraph, because that often meant there was no real problem. He contrasted that with solution-led work like “ASIN to ASIN linking,” which failed.

### Trust as currency
- **When to use**: When trying to grow influence, earn resources, or lead larger teams.
- **Steps**:
  1. Set clear expectations through goals, forecasts, and commitments.
  2. Meet those expectations consistently.
  3. Tell the truth without evasiveness.
  4. Own mistakes and show learning.
  5. Work backwards from stakeholder goals to build alignment.
- **Example**: Ian reflected that at Airbnb, deeper alignment and trust with customer support leadership could have helped rally the organization more strongly around strategy.

### Three-part investment test
- **When to use**: When deciding whether an initiative deserves investment.
- **Steps**:
  1. Ask whether it is a big idea.
  2. Ask whether it is something your company should be doing.
  3. Ask whether there is a legitimate plan to succeed.
- **Example**: A new shale-oil extraction method might be a big idea, but it may fail the “should we do this?” test if it is not aligned with Amazon’s business.

### Operator’s Manual communication discipline
- **When to use**: When improving executive communication and day-to-day PM clarity.
- **Steps**:
  1. Answer the question first.
  2. Explain only as needed.
  3. Sometimes answer and stop talking.
  4. Avoid weasel words and vague language.
  5. Use numbers and dates when relevant.
  6. Review your own communication afterward.
- **Example**: At Microsoft, Ian learned that when asked when something would ship, context is not a substitute for a date. Strong communication means directly answering the question asked.

## Core Advice
- **When you are a new PM**: focus on communicating clearly, prioritizing ruthlessly, and executing reliably — because these are the foundation that makes everything else work.
- **When an executive asks you a question**: answer first, then explain — because clarity and brevity signal seniority and reduce confusion.
- **When evaluating a product idea**: write the customer problem before the solution — because solution-first thinking often hides weak demand.
- **When roadmap options are multiplying**: prioritize by the work that most improves the core business metric — because leverage matters more than activity.
- **When improving communication**: self-grade after meetings and docs — because communication improves through deliberate practice.
- **When career pressure pushes you toward promotion games**: optimize for impact — because promotions usually follow sustained impact.
- **When you are operating at senior levels**: ask whether the idea could be bigger — because senior PMs should widen the aperture of opportunity.
- **When cross-functional resistance appears**: spend more time understanding partner goals — because strategy fails without trust and ownership.
- **When a pitch starts with “we could combine these things”**: pause and reframe around customer pain — because ingredient-led thinking is often a sign of weak product insight.
- **When trying to grow as a leader**: treat trust as your core currency — because people give resources to those they believe will deliver.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: To advance in product, focus on promotion, visibility, and org scope. → **Their view**: Focus on impact first; promotion should be a byproduct — because sustained business impact is the strongest signal of leadership.
- **Conventional**: Working backwards is mainly about writing a press release and FAQ. → **Their view**: The real method is starting from the customer problem — because the artifact alone can be copied without changing thinking.
- **Conventional**: Strong PMs stay within roadmap and engineering coordination. → **Their view**: Great PMs act like broad owners across support, operations, marketing, and more — because product success depends on the whole system.
- **Conventional**: Junior PM success is mostly about being smart or working harder. → **Their view**: Prioritization often matters more — because better judgment can multiply impact with the same resources.
