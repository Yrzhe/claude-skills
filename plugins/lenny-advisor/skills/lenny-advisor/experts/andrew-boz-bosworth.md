# Andrew “Boz” Bosworth

**Chief Technology Officer** | **Meta** | Expertise: leadership, product-management, ai-strategy, engineering-management

## Bio
Andrew “Boz” Bosworth is Meta’s CTO and one of Facebook’s earliest engineers, with deep experience building products like News Feed, messaging, groups, and the mobile ads platform. His perspective matters because he has spent nearly two decades shaping how a high-scale, high-talent organization makes decisions, communicates, and ships products under intense scrutiny.

He now leads Meta’s AR/VR, mixed reality, and consumer hardware efforts, giving him a rare view across software, hardware, AI strategy, and organizational design.

## Signature Frameworks

### Leverage Your Leaders
- **When to use**: When you’re blocked, unclear on priorities, or trying to move faster on important work.
- **Steps**:
  1. Stop treating asking for help as weakness.
  2. Send lightweight progress updates with status, blockers, and open questions.
  3. Use low-friction framing like “no response required” when you mainly want alignment.
  4. Make any ask specific and cheap for your manager to act on.
  5. Include drafts, yes/no questions, or suggested outreach so your leader can unblock you quickly.
- **Example**: Send a 5–10 sentence email: “Here’s progress, here’s what’s blocked, no response required if this looks right.” If help is needed, attach a draft email your manager can forward.

### HPM Updates
- **When to use**: When you want a repeatable format for keeping managers or leadership informed.
- **Steps**:
  1. Share **Highlights**: the biggest things leaders need to know.
  2. Share **People**: who is struggling, at risk, or deserves recognition.
  3. Share **Me**: how you are personally doing.
- **Example**: Meta managers historically sent HPMs to their managers or leadership groups, including updates that could reach Zuckerberg and broader leadership.

### Communication Is the Job
- **When to use**: When leading teams, driving change, or diagnosing why work went off track.
- **Steps**:
  1. Assume impact happens through communication, not just good ideas.
  2. Take ownership when people misunderstand priorities or produce the wrong outcome.
  3. Clarify expectations, priorities, metrics, and roles explicitly.
  4. Repeat messages across multiple modalities.
  5. Treat silence, org structure, tone, and presence as communication too.
- **Example**: If someone spends six months on the wrong thing, Boz says the leader should own the communication failure rather than blame the individual alone.

### Signal Through Transparency with Guardrails
- **When to use**: When building a high-talent organization that needs autonomy without losing strategic coherence.
- **Steps**:
  1. Share broad information so people can act with context.
  2. Be explicit about strategic direction.
  3. Define where teams have freedom and where they are executing an assignment.
  4. Teach people to triage information and find signal in noise.
  5. Use transparency to enable bottoms-up execution within top-down priorities.
- **Example**: At Meta, major ideas may come from leadership, but teams are given room to return multiple implementation options within clear guardrails.

### Diagnose Big Wrong vs. Small Wrong
- **When to use**: When launching controversial products and interpreting negative user feedback.
- **Steps**:
  1. Separate stated preferences from revealed preferences.
  2. Ask whether the core thesis is wrong or just the execution details.
  3. Look for usage signals that contradict complaints.
  4. Fix details if the thesis is right; reconsider the thesis if behavior doesn’t validate it.
  5. Keep conviction while still listening carefully.
- **Example**: With News Feed, users were outraged but usage doubled. Boz concluded the core idea was right, while some details were mishandled.

### Career Compound Interest via Learning
- **When to use**: When choosing roles early in your career or deciding whether to stay in a domain.
- **Steps**:
  1. Optimize for learning, not title progression.
  2. Move when you stop learning enough.
  3. Give new domains time before rejecting them.
  4. Accumulate varied experiences across functions and problem types.
  5. Stay longer in roles that continue to deepen your learning.
- **Example**: Boz moved across integrity, News Feed, infrastructure, bootcamp, messaging, groups, ads, and hardware, saying it felt slow at first but later made his career “go vertical.”

### Curiosity Under Identity Threat
- **When to use**: When you feel defensive, strongly disagree, or notice your ego getting activated in conflict.
- **Steps**:
  1. Notice the feeling of identity threat.
  2. Pause the instinct to defend being right.
  3. Respond with genuine curiosity.
  4. Ask the other person to explain their view.
  5. Use curiosity to lower defensiveness and update your own view if needed.
- **Example**: Boz cites the response, “Fascinating. You have to tell me more about why you think that,” as a way to tear down walls and create openness.

## Core Advice
- **When you’re blocked or ambiguous**: Ask your manager for help early and make the ask specific — because leaders can clear obstacles faster than you can alone.
- **When you have a new manager**: Ask how they prefer updates and what cadence works — because communication styles vary and alignment is easier when you adapt.
- **When users hate a new product**: Compare what they say with what they do — because behavior often reveals value better than complaints.
- **When outsiders criticize your company**: Read criticism carefully, but don’t accept it blindly — because insiders usually know more about the underlying reality.
- **When you’re early in your career**: Choose learning and breadth over fast promotion — because experience compounds into better judgment later.
- **When you assume you won’t like a domain**: Give it real time — because interest often follows understanding and mastery.
- **When deciding what to work on**: Bias toward the company’s most important initiative or an underappreciated area where you can carry weight — because both create outsized growth and trust.
- **When communicating something important**: Repeat it in multiple formats and address objections upfront — because people absorb information differently and need their fears acknowledged.
- **When a disagreement makes you defensive**: Respond with curiosity — because it lowers ego, surfaces assumptions, and makes updating easier.
- **When downturn pressure hits long-term bets**: Preserve a balanced portfolio of future investments — because cutting all future growth can damage the company’s long-term health.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Asking your manager for help signals weakness. → **Their view**: You should leverage your leaders directly — because your job is to get the work done, not prove you can do it alone.
- **Conventional**: If users say they hate a product change, revert it. → **Their view**: Distinguish stated from revealed preferences — because behavior may show the product is working.
- **Conventional**: Bottom-up and top-down cultures are opposites. → **Their view**: The best orgs are strategically top-down with freedom inside guardrails — because clarity and autonomy can coexist.
- **Conventional**: Leadership is mostly decision-making. → **Their view**: Communication is the job — because ideas only matter if others understand and act on them.
- **Conventional**: Early career success means staying on one track. → **Their view**: Breadth and learning can outperform specialization — because varied experience compounds.
- **Conventional**: Downturns require cutting future bets. → **Their view**: Don’t kill all long-term investments — because a company without future bets eventually declines.
