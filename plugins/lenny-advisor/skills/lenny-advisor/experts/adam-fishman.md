## Signature Frameworks

### Growth Competency Model
- **When to use**: When hiring, evaluating, coaching, or structuring a growth team.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with the team’s actual gaps, not a fantasy “unicorn” profile.
  2. Evaluate candidates across four areas: growth execution, customer knowledge, growth strategy, and communication/influence.
  3. In growth execution, assess channel fluency, experimentation, and ability to productize learnings.
  4. In customer knowledge, assess data fluency, user psychology, and customer learning.
  5. In growth strategy, assess loop modeling, forecasting, and prioritization.
  6. In communication/influence, assess leadership, strategic communication, and stakeholder management.
  7. Use the model for both hiring and performance development.
- **Example**: At Wyzant, Fishman saw a mismatch between the founders’ desire for a “silver bullet” and the company’s real need for growth loops and an execution system.

### Hire for Team Balance, Not a Unicorn
- **When to use**: When making the first growth hire or building an early growth team.
- **Steps**:
  1. Map the competencies already on the team.
  2. Identify missing capabilities instead of pattern-matching to a famous operator.
  3. Prefer strong fundamentals: execution and customer knowledge.
  4. Support earlier-career hires with mentorship and outside learning.
  5. Add specialists later, after the core system exists.
- **Example**: Rather than hiring “another Adam” early, Fishman recommends hiring smart, driven builders and developing them.

### Painter vs. Architect vs. Surgeon
- **When to use**: When deciding what type of growth hire fits the stage and problem.
- **Steps**:
  1. Decide whether you need broad system-building or narrow optimization.
  2. Hire adaptable builders first.
  3. Bring in specialists only when the bottleneck is clearly in their specialty.
  4. Avoid making a narrow expert your first growth hire unless the need is obvious.
- **Example**: SEO or paid acquisition can justify a specialist later, but usually not as the first growth hire.

### Onboarding as Promise Delivery
- **When to use**: When designing or improving onboarding, activation, and early retention.
- **Steps**:
  1. Treat onboarding as the first delivery of the brand promise.
  2. Recognize it touches every user, so it has outsized leverage.
  3. Use new-user motivation to learn and qualify users.
  4. Optimize for habit formation and retention, not just completion.
  5. Revisit onboarding only when you have new insights.
- **Example**: At Patreon, onboarding improvements helped increase creators’ first- and second-month revenue by 25%.

### Human Intervention to Productized Defaults
- **When to use**: When a manual onboarding or sales-assist process is working and you want to scale it.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify high-potential users from onboarding signals.
  2. Route them to humans for guided setup.
  3. Study what those humans do that improves outcomes.
  4. Turn those behaviors into defaults, recommendations, and guardrails.
  5. Keep user choice, but make the right path easier.
- **Example**: At Patreon, creators with strong external audiences were routed to humans, and the learnings became opinionated defaults like recommended tier counts and pricing.

### Proxy Metrics for Onboarding Experiments
- **When to use**: When onboarding changes affect long-term retention or LTV, but results take too long to measure directly.
- **Steps**:
  1. Find an early behavior that predicts long-term success.
  2. Use it to evaluate experiments quickly.
  3. Pair quantitative proxies with qualitative review.
  4. Balance conversion against retention.
- **Example**: Patreon used velocity to first patron and first $100 processed as early indicators of creator success.

### PMF for Candidates
- **When to use**: When deciding whether to join a company.
- **Steps**:
  1. Evaluate People: team quality and working relationships.
  2. Evaluate Mission: whether the work matters.
  3. Evaluate Financials: whether the company is disciplined and durable.
  4. Don’t accept only two of the three.
  5. Validate with observation, reverse questions, and references.
- **Example**: Fishman says Lyft and Patreon were strong fits on all three dimensions, while other roles were weaker due to people or expectation mismatches.

### Reverse Diligence for Job Candidates
- **When to use**: During a job search or offer evaluation.
- **Steps**:
  1. Ask to observe real meetings or reviews.
  2. Ask about a recent disagreement and how it was resolved.
  3. Talk to current or former employees outside the interview loop.
  4. Request references from people who worked for the manager before.
  5. Treat your time like an investment.
- **Example**: Fishman praised a PM who asked to speak with people he had managed previously before joining.

## Core Advice
- **When hiring your first growth leader**: Do a gap analysis against the four growth competencies — because it prevents vague pattern-matching and “unicorn” hiring.
- **When choosing between a senior external hire and an earlier-career builder**: Do prioritize the smart, driven candidate with strong execution and customer knowledge — because those are the most foundational skills.
- **When deciding between external hiring and internal promotion**: Do bias toward internal transfers when possible — because they already know the customer and company context.
- **When you need SEO or paid expertise**: Do hire a specialist only after foundational growth systems are in place — because deep craft matters, but usually not first.
- **When retention is weak or users drop off early**: Do audit onboarding first — because it’s the only experience every user touches.
- **When onboarding completion is the main KPI**: Do optimize for qualified activation and retention, not just conversion — because some friction improves downstream quality.
- **When onboarding experiments take too long to measure**: Do use proxy metrics — because you need fast feedback tied to durable outcomes.
- **When manual onboarding works well**: Do productize the human behaviors that drive success — because humans reveal what the product should encode.
- **When asked to redesign onboarding from scratch**: Do wait for genuinely new insights — because cosmetic changes rarely move the needle.
- **When evaluating a company to join**: Do assess people, mission, and financials rigorously — because partial fit often leads to regret.
- **When you’re unsure about a manager or team**: Do run reverse diligence — because interviews are sales processes, not full truth.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: The best first growth hire is a senior growth leader who has done it all before. → **Their view**: Hire or promote a strong builder earlier in their growth career. — because early teams need adaptable execution more than expensive prestige.
- **Conventional**: Onboarding should minimize friction and maximize conversion. → **Their view**: Onboarding should sometimes add friction. — because the goal is qualified activation and retention, not raw completion.
- **Conventional**: Manual processes are unscalable and should be removed. → **Their view**: Manual intervention is a learning engine. — because it reveals what works before productization.
- **Conventional**: Onboarding should be continuously redesigned. → **Their view**: Revisit it only when you have new insights. — because cosmetic iteration is usually low leverage.
- **Conventional**: Candidates should mainly sell themselves in interviews. → **Their view**: Candidates should diligence the company like investors. — because time is a scarce resource and fit matters deeply.
