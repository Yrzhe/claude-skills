# Multiple guests compiled by Lenny Rachitsky

**Compilation episode featuring product, design, growth, and leadership executives sharing interview questions** | **Lenny's Podcast** | Expertise: hiring-culture, leadership, product-management, user-research

## Bio
This episode compiles favorite interview questions from senior leaders across companies like Retool, Ramp, Coda, Figma, Stripe, Linear, Slack, Asana, Adobe, Notion, and Intercom. The shared perspective is less about “how to interview” in the abstract and more about how to uncover the traits that predict success: self-awareness, judgment, authenticity, structured thinking, and comfort with ambiguity.

This profile is useful when you want to hire for product, design, growth, or leadership roles and need questions that go beyond polished resumes and rehearsed answers. The guests collectively argue that the best interviews reveal how someone thinks, learns, and behaves under uncertainty.

## Signature Frameworks

### Success Attribution Reflection
- **When to use**: When evaluating self-awareness, humility, and curiosity about a candidate’s career trajectory
- **Steps**:
  1. Ask what they attribute their success to.
  2. Remove the easy escape hatch of saying only “luck.”
  3. Listen for evidence of reflection on the real drivers of their progress.
  4. Assess whether they understand their strengths, choices, and learning patterns.
- **Example**: Eeke De Miliano asks, “To what do you attribute your success?” and explicitly says they can’t answer “luck” in order to surface self-awareness.

### Hardest Thing Diagnostic
- **When to use**: When hiring for demanding environments and you want to understand resilience, agency, and collaboration under pressure
- **Steps**:
  1. Ask the candidate about the hardest thing they have ever done.
  2. Probe why it was hard for them specifically.
  3. Explore how they overcame the challenge.
  4. Understand how they worked with others and how much agency they took.
- **Example**: Geoff Charles uses “What’s the hardest thing you’ve ever done?” to calibrate what “hard” means to a candidate and how they respond to difficulty.

### Eigenquestion Test
- **When to use**: When assessing strategic thinking, prioritization, and the ability to identify the few questions that unlock a complex problem
- **Steps**:
  1. Present a novel, ambiguous scenario.
  2. Let the candidate generate many possible questions.
  3. Constrain them to only two questions.
  4. Evaluate whether they choose the highest-leverage questions.
  5. See how they use the answers to build a coherent strategy.
- **Example**: Shishir Mehrotra’s teleportation-device prompt asks candidates to bring teleportation to market, then limits them to two questions. One strong candidate asked whether it was safe enough for humans and whether costs were primarily CapEx or OpEx, then built a go-to-market strategy from those answers.

### Controversial Decision Story Test
- **When to use**: When evaluating communication, empathy, and judgment in cross-functional conflict
- **Steps**:
  1. Ask the candidate to describe a controversial product decision they were part of.
  2. Have them explain why the issue mattered.
  3. Listen for whether they can fairly represent multiple sides of the conflict.
  4. Assess whether they communicate the situation in an even-keeled, balanced way.
- **Example**: Yuhki Yamashita uses this to see whether candidates can explain conflict clearly and demonstrate perspective-taking.

### Pride-Based Values Interview
- **When to use**: When you want to understand a candidate’s motivations, standards, taste, and definition of good work
- **Steps**:
  1. Ask what work or accomplishment they are most proud of.
  2. Ask why they are proud of it.
  3. Have them walk through the process behind it.
  4. Infer what they value, what motivates them, and what quality looks like to them.
- **Example**: Katie Dill, Karri Saarinen, and Camille Hearst all use versions of “What are you most proud of and why?” to uncover taste, judgment, and motivation.

### Ambiguity Navigation Framework
- **When to use**: When hiring product managers or leaders who must create clarity in uncertain situations
- **Steps**:
  1. Ask about a time they faced ambiguity or a challenging situation.
  2. Look for whether they imposed structure on the problem.
  3. Check whether they sought input from others rather than acting with false certainty.
  4. Listen for milestones or signals they used to test whether the path was working.
  5. Assess whether they adapted based on what they learned.
- **Example**: Jiaona Zhang says strong PM answers show a path forward through ambiguity, input-seeking behavior, and clear milestones for deciding whether to continue or change course.

### Failure and Reflection Probe
- **When to use**: When assessing accountability, learning orientation, and emotional maturity
- **Steps**:
  1. Ask about a time something went wrong.
  2. Have the candidate explain what happened and what they did next.
  3. Listen for ownership versus blame.
  4. Evaluate how they analyzed the failure and what they changed afterward.
- **Example**: Paige Costello asks candidates to describe when something failed on the product or team and uses the answer to understand mindset and self-perception under stress.

### Authenticity Through Contrarian Opinion
- **When to use**: When candidates seem overly rehearsed and you want to surface genuine thinking
- **Steps**:
  1. Ask what common belief in their field they think is inaccurate or hogwash.
  2. Require them to explain why they disagree.
  3. Observe whether they can articulate an original, defensible point of view.
  4. Use the answer to judge authenticity and independent thinking.
- **Example**: Nikhyl Singhal asks what everyone takes for granted that the candidate thinks is inaccurate because it is hard to answer without being genuinely opinionated.

### Reference Call Performance Review Question
- **When to use**: When conducting reference checks and you want actionable, non-generic feedback
- **Steps**:
  1. Ask the reference what feedback you will likely give the candidate in their first performance review.
  2. Push past generic praise.
  3. Capture likely development areas and management risks.
  4. Use the answer to prepare onboarding and coaching plans.
- **Example**: Paul Adams says this question is effective because references cannot easily dodge it and it yields enlightening, concrete feedback.

## Core Advice
- **When a candidate gives polished but generic answers**: Ask questions that force genuine reflection or opinion, such as what conventional wisdom they disagree with or what they attribute their success to — because these are difficult to game and reveal authenticity.
- **When hiring for a highly ambiguous PM or strategic role**: Use open-ended behavioral questions that test how the person creates structure, seeks input, and defines milestones — because strong product leaders chart a path through ambiguity rather than pretending certainty.
- **When you need to assess strategic thinking quickly**: Present a hypothetical scenario and constrain the candidate to one or two questions — because constraint reveals whether they can identify the highest-leverage variables.
- **When you want to understand a candidate’s motivations and standards**: Ask what they are most proud of and why, then dig into the process behind it — because pride exposes what they value and what quality means to them.
- **When hiring for demanding environments**: Ask about the hardest thing they have ever done and probe why it was hard, how they overcame it, and what role others played — because this calibrates resilience, agency, and collaboration under pressure.
- **When a candidate may lack introspection**: Ask about a decision that worked for reasons different from what they expected, or a good decision that still failed — because this surfaces whether they update their mental models from outcomes.
- **When evaluating accountability**: Ask about a time something went wrong and listen for ownership, analysis, and what they changed afterward — because effective learners describe mistakes with clarity rather than defensiveness.
- **When reference calls are vague and overly positive**: Ask, “What feedback will I be giving this person in their first performance review?” — because it reframes the conversation toward concrete developmental feedback.
- **When you want humility beyond professional polish**: Ask what siblings or parents would say about the candidate — because close family perspectives often reveal sincerity, humor, and realism.
- **When a candidate describes a product conflict**: Evaluate whether they can make the problem feel important and represent multiple sides fairly — because great leaders use storytelling and perspective-taking to align teams.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Good interview questions should focus on direct experience, polished answers, and standard competency checks. → **Their view**: Hypothetical, unusual, or low-stakes questions can be better because they reveal how people think rather than how well they rehearse.
- **Conventional**: Interviewing should focus mostly on strengths, accomplishments, and successful outcomes. → **Their view**: The most revealing questions often probe failure, limitations, and miscalibrated decisions — because introspection and learning matter more than polished success stories.
- **Conventional**: The best candidates project confidence and certainty. → **Their view**: The best candidates often show humility, seek input, and acknowledge ambiguity — because product work is inherently uncertain.
- **Conventional**: Luck should be minimized in career narratives to emphasize merit. → **Their view**: A healthy acknowledgment of luck can signal security and perspective — because refusing to admit luck may reveal insecurity.
- **Conventional**: Reference checks are mostly a formality because references only say positive things. → **Their view**: A well-phrased question can turn references into a rich source of actionable insight — because the right framing makes generic praise harder.
