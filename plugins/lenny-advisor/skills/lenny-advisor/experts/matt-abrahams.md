# Matt Abrahams

**Professor, podcast host, author, and communication coach** | **Stanford Graduate School of Business** | Expertise: public speaking, spontaneous communication, anxiety management, persuasive communication

## Bio
Matt Abrahams teaches communication at Stanford GSB and helps people become better at the moments that matter most: speaking on the spot, handling pressure, giving feedback, and communicating clearly when there’s no time to overprepare. As the host of *Think Fast, Talk Smart* and author of *Think Faster, Talk Smarter*, he brings a practical, research-backed approach to reducing anxiety and improving performance in real-world conversations.

His perspective matters because he focuses on communication as a skill you can train, not a talent you either have or don’t. He is especially useful when someone needs to sound clear, calm, and credible under pressure.

## Signature Frameworks

### Visualization for desensitization
- **When to use**: When you feel anxious before a talk, presentation, or other high-stakes speaking moment.
- **Steps**:
  1. Calm yourself with deep breathing first.
  2. Picture the room or environment where you'll speak.
  3. Visualize the audience and specific people you'll be speaking to.
  4. Mentally rehearse walking up, delivering the talk, being well received, and stepping off stage.
  5. Repeat the visualization so the situation feels familiar rather than novel.
- **Example**: Before a TEDx talk, spend five minutes picturing the stage, the audience, and the talk going well.

### Dare to be dull
- **When to use**: When you are speaking spontaneously and feel pressure to be brilliant, funny, or perfectly polished.
- **Steps**:
  1. Shift your goal from perfection to connection.
  2. Give yourself permission to simply answer the question or do the task.
  3. Reduce self-evaluation and internal judgment.
  4. Focus your attention on the audience and the message instead of on how you are performing.
- **Example**: In small talk or feedback, just answer the question or give the feedback instead of trying to produce the most impressive response.

### Cognitive reframing of anxiety as excitement
- **When to use**: When your body shows anxiety symptoms before speaking or performing.
- **Steps**:
  1. Notice the physical symptoms: faster heart rate, shallow breathing, shakiness.
  2. Interpret those symptoms as arousal rather than danger.
  3. Label the feeling as excitement instead of fear.
  4. Remind yourself of the positive opportunity: sharing your point of view or demonstrating value.
- **Example**: Instead of saying “I’m nervous about this presentation,” say “I’m excited to share this and show my value.”

### Mantra-based self-talk
- **When to use**: When negative inner dialogue is increasing anxiety before speaking.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify a short, believable phrase that counters your self-doubt.
  2. Repeat it right before speaking.
  3. Optionally write it on a post-it note or set it as a phone reminder.
  4. Use it to quiet catastrophic self-talk without overhyping yourself.
- **Example**: “I have value to add” or “Last time this went well” before a meeting or presentation.

### Conversation framing
- **When to use**: When presenting feels intimidating and you want to reduce the sense of one-way performance.
- **Steps**:
  1. Reframe the situation as a conversation rather than a presentation.
  2. Ask yourself a question and answer it aloud.
  3. Structure the talk as a series of questions and answers.
  4. Use the conversational format to lower anxiety and increase engagement.
- **Example**: Start with, “Today I have three questions I’d like to answer. Question number one is...” and then answer each one.

### Attention distraction to ease into speaking
- **When to use**: When the first minute of speaking is the hardest and you need time to settle in.
- **Steps**:
  1. Begin with a relevant question, story, video, or prompt.
  2. Shift audience attention away from you briefly.
  3. Use that moment to breathe, settle, and transition into your content.
  4. Move from presenter mode into facilitator mode.
- **Example**: A leader starts a meeting with “Good morning, let’s watch this video,” then discusses it before presenting.

### Spontaneous speaking structure
- **When to use**: When you need to answer on the spot and want a reliable way to organize your thoughts.
- **Steps**:
  1. Choose a simple structure before you speak.
  2. Use the structure to decide how to say the answer.
  3. Fill the structure with your content instead of improvising from scratch.
- **Example**: Use problem, solution, benefit for a product pitch or update.

### PREP
- **When to use**: When you need to make a clear point quickly in a meeting, feedback conversation, or Q&A.
- **Steps**:
  1. Point: state your main point.
  2. Reason: explain why you believe it.
  3. Example: give a concrete example.
  4. Point: restate the main point.
- **Example**: In a product discussion, state your feature opinion, explain why, give an example, then restate the conclusion.

### What? So what? Now what?
- **When to use**: When giving an update, broader explanation, or product/team communication.
- **Steps**:
  1. What: state what the thing is.
  2. So what: explain why it matters.
  3. Now what: explain what happens next.
- **Example**: For a feature update: what the feature is, why it matters to users, and what the team is doing next.

### 4Is feedback framework
- **When to use**: When giving structured feedback that needs to be clear, collaborative, and actionable.
- **Steps**:
  1. Information: set the context and name the issue.
  2. Impact: explain the effect on you or the team.
  3. Invitation: invite the other person into problem solving.
  4. Implications: explain the consequences or benefits of change.
- **Example**: For someone arriving late to meetings: note the repeated lateness, explain the impact on prioritization, invite them to help solve it, and describe the benefit of being prepared.

## Core Advice
- **When you feel anxious before a talk or presentation**: use visualization to mentally rehearse the room, the audience, and the talk going well — because desensitization makes the situation feel familiar rather than novel.
- **When you are trying to calm your body before speaking**: pair visualization with deep breathing and a short pre-speech routine — because calming the body helps prevent stress from reinforcing anxious thoughts.
- **When you are putting pressure on yourself to be brilliant on the spot**: dare to be dull and focus on simply answering, giving feedback, or engaging — because lowering self-evaluation frees up cognitive bandwidth.
- **When your body starts showing anxiety symptoms**: reframe the feeling as excitement and tell yourself you are energized to share your ideas — because anxiety and excitement share the same physiological arousal.
- **When negative self-talk is making you more nervous**: use a short mantra such as “I have value to add” or “I’ve got this” — because a believable phrase interrupts catastrophic inner dialogue.
- **When you are about to speak and your inner critic is loud**: personify the critic and respond to it directly instead of treating its voice as truth — because naming the critic reduces its power.
- **When the first minute of speaking feels hardest**: distract the audience briefly with a question, story, or short video so you can settle in — because shifting attention gives you time to breathe and transition.
- **When you need to speak spontaneously**: prepare in advance by learning a few reusable structures rather than trying to wing it — because spontaneity improves through preparation and pattern recognition.
- **When you need to explain something clearly in a meeting or update**: use a simple structure like what/so what/now what or PREP — because structure reduces mental load and helps listeners follow.
- **When you are giving feedback on the spot**: treat feedback as collaborative problem solving and invite the other person into the solution — because feedback lands better as joint work than as judgment.
- **When you are giving a toast or tribute**: keep it brief, use a simple structure, and make the emotion come through a story rather than a declaration — because story-driven toasts are more memorable and less self-centered.
- **When you need to apologize in the moment**: acknowledge the specific action, appreciate the impact, and state the concrete change you will make — because a real apology includes responsibility and repair.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: To calm public speaking anxiety, imagine the audience naked. → **Their view**: That advice is bad; instead, visualize yourself in the room and the audience responding well — because the useful part of visualization is desensitization and rehearsal, not demeaning the audience.
- **Conventional**: Great spontaneous speakers are naturally gifted and can just wing it. → **Their view**: You actually have to prepare to be spontaneous — because improvisation works like athletics or jazz: it requires practice.
- **Conventional**: The goal of speaking is to be impressive and perfectly polished. → **Their view**: Strive for connection over perfection by daring to be dull — because perfectionism consumes cognitive bandwidth and hurts communication.
- **Conventional**: Anxiety symptoms mean something is wrong and should be suppressed. → **Their view**: Those symptoms can be reframed as excitement and opportunity — because the body’s arousal can be interpreted differently.
- **Conventional**: Small talk is trivial and mostly useless. → **Their view**: Small talk matters because it builds relationships and often leads to meaningful connection — because many important relationships start there.
- **Conventional**: Feedback is mainly about delivering your opinion or criticism. → **Their view**: Feedback is an opportunity to problem solve collaboratively — because joint problem solving is more constructive.
- **Conventional**: A good apology is mainly about expressing regret for how the other person feels. → **Their view**: A real apology must acknowledge the specific transgression and include amends — because vague regret avoids responsibility.
