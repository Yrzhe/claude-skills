# Gokul Rajaram

**Product and business leader at DoorDash; board member at Coinbase and Pinterest; angel investor** | **DoorDash** | Expertise: product-management, leadership, go-to-market, strategy

## Bio
Gokul Rajaram is a veteran product leader who has operated at some of the most influential companies in tech, including Google, Facebook, Square, Caviar, and DoorDash. He also serves on the boards of Coinbase and Pinterest and invests as an angel, giving him a rare perspective across product, hiring, company-building, and founder evaluation.

His perspective matters because it is highly practical and pattern-based: he focuses on what actually works in real companies, especially when teams are small, growing fast, or making high-stakes hiring and career decisions. He is especially useful when you want advice that balances ambition with operational realism.

## Signature Frameworks

### Serendipity-driven career building
- **When to use**: When deciding how to grow your career, choose opportunities, and create non-linear advancement paths inside companies.
- **Steps**:
  1. Do your core job really well.
  2. Stay curious about what else is happening across the company.
  3. Build relationships with strong people doing interesting work.
  4. Be open to unexpected opportunities and inbounds.
  5. Seize promising opportunities quickly when they appear.
  6. Pay it forward to build long-term goodwill and network effects.
- **Example**: While working on Google syndication, Gokul discovered a small team building contextual ad targeting after hours, volunteered as their PM on nights and weekends, and that project became Google AdSense.

### How to choose a company to join
- **When to use**: When evaluating job opportunities, especially in startups or growth-stage companies.
- **Steps**:
  1. Assess whether the problem and customer segment give you energy.
  2. Evaluate whether the founder is authentically mission-driven rather than primarily money-driven.
  3. Ask whether the company can become the number one player in its category.
  4. Prefer winners or likely winners because talent density, brand halo, and network effects compound.
  5. For most people, especially earlier in career, favor mid-stage companies with product-market fit and growing platform potential.
  6. Check financial durability, especially in volatile markets.
- **Example**: He described advising someone to take a slightly lower role at Coinbase instead of a more senior title at a tier-two or tier-three e-commerce company because being at the category leader compounds career value.

### Startup product development process by stage
- **When to use**: When designing product planning and execution processes as a startup grows from very early stage to scale.
- **Steps**:
  1. At the earliest stage, plan week to week.
  2. Use simple tools like spreadsheets rather than heavy process or complex PM software.
  3. As the company grows, add quarterly planning on top of weekly execution.
  4. Later, add annual planning that cascades into quarterly goals and weekly work.
  5. Initially treat company strategy and product strategy as the same thing.
  6. As PMs join, create explicit product strategy docs derived from company strategy.
  7. Keep atomic cross-functional teams small, with engineers, PM, designer, and ideally an analyst.
  8. Maintain daily standups and weekly task reviews regardless of stage.
- **Example**: He described a Series A company just beginning its first real quarterly planning cycle, moving from week-to-week execution into quarterly goals.

### Hiring your first PM from within
- **When to use**: When a startup reaches the point where the founder can no longer effectively play the PM role for the engineering team.
- **Steps**:
  1. Wait until there is a real problem to solve, not just a desire to add process.
  2. Typically consider hiring a PM around 5 to 10 engineers, often around 8 to 10.
  3. First ask whether the founder can still effectively serve as PM.
  4. If not, identify an internal engineer, analyst, or designer with PM traits.
  5. Choose someone trusted by both founders and engineers.
  6. Coach them away from their old functional comfort zone and toward customer understanding and problem framing.
  7. Use engineer and design feedback to assess whether they are adding value.
- **Example**: He noted that at every company he had been at, the first PM was someone internal who transitioned from another role; Lenny confirmed Airbnb's first PM, Joe, had joined as an engineer.

### Avoiding the feature factory
- **When to use**: When teams are shipping lots of features but not clearly improving customer behavior or business outcomes.
- **Steps**:
  1. Frame work as customer or business problems, not predetermined solutions.
  2. Empower teams to generate multiple solution options.
  3. Make decision-making transparent around why one solution was chosen over another.
  4. Measure whether shipped features actually change customer behavior.
  5. Treat features with no behavioral impact as suspect, not as success.
- **Example**: He contrasted saying “build an iOS app” with stating the real problem: increase usage frequency from two times a week to five times a week.

### Leader hiring via best-in-class org mapping
- **When to use**: When hiring a senior functional leader and you need a repeatable sourcing strategy.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the function and customer context you need, such as marketing to consumers or selling to SMBs.
  2. Identify best-in-class companies in that function serving similar customers.
  3. Map the org chart, not just the top executive.
  4. Target the lieutenant or lieutenant-of-the-lieutenant rather than the top functional head.
  5. Use LinkedIn and network intelligence to reconstruct reporting lines.
  6. Recruit rising leaders who have learned inside excellent systems.
- **Example**: He said that after Square became one of the few public fintechs, other fintechs repeatedly hired lieutenants and second-line leaders from Square's compliance, risk, and payments teams.

### Founder-centric angel investing
- **When to use**: When evaluating early-stage startup investments as an angel investor.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with the founders rather than the market size alone.
  2. Listen to the founding story to assess authenticity and motivation.
  3. Prefer founders solving a problem they have deeply observed or experienced.
  4. Favor teams of two with complementary skills over solo founders.
  5. Look for evidence they can attract strong talent early.
  6. Avoid over-optimizing for market narratives and missing great people.
  7. Think long-term across multiple companies a founder may build.
- **Example**: He said many of his biggest mistakes were sins of omission—passing on strong founders because he overthought the market instead of backing the people.

## Core Advice
- **When you face a career decision about what role or company to join**: Choose based on the problem and customer segment that energize you, and prioritize mission-driven founders and likely category leaders over title — because energy comes more sustainably from the problem and customer than from abstract passion for a domain, and working at a winner compounds through talent density, brand halo, and future opportunities.
- **When you face uncertainty about whether to join an early-stage startup**: Prefer a mid-stage company with product-market fit and channel fit unless you know the founders extremely well — because mid-stage companies offer mentorship, skill development, and lower existential risk.
- **When you face the challenge of setting up product development at a very early startup**: Keep planning weekly, use simple tools like spreadsheets, and avoid over-engineering process — because early-stage teams need speed and clarity more than process overhead.
- **When you face pressure to hire your first PM**: Hire only when the founder can no longer properly support 8 to 10 engineers, and ideally promote an internal engineer, analyst, or designer into the role first — because the first PM is a trusted bridge, so cultural fit and trust matter more than polished PM credentials.
- **When you face a team that keeps shipping features without clear impact**: Reframe work around customer problems and target behavior change, not feature requests or predetermined solutions — because this prevents feature-factory behavior and forces teams to evaluate outcomes.
- **When you face the need to assess whether a new PM is working out**: Ask engineers and engineering managers directly whether the PM is adding value, and watch for PMs slipping back into their old functional comfort zone — because engineers quickly detect whether a PM improves prioritization and problem-solving.
- **When you face the question of how to structure a growing PM org**: Once you have roughly 3 to 5 PMs, have them report to a dedicated product leader rather than directly into a GM — because PMs need coaching, shared standards, and a product culture.
- **When you face the challenge of hiring a senior leader**: Source from best-in-class companies in the same functional and customer context, and target the lieutenant rather than the top executive — because rising leaders from excellent systems are often more accessible and still deeply trained.
- **When you face constant hiring needs as a founder**: Spend about two hours a day on hiring: one hour on sourcing and outreach, and one hour on conversations — because consistent time-boxed effort compounds into a strong pipeline.
- **When you face pressure to hand out senior titles early**: Delay director and VP titles as long as possible; use functional titles like lead or head of instead — because premature senior titles create future organizational traps.
- **When you face the challenge of building product growth**: Ensure a large share of new customers comes from organic channels rather than relying overwhelmingly on paid acquisition — because products that are not remarkable enough to generate word of mouth become fragile.
- **When you face the desire to start angel investing**: Clarify your unique value-add, build a public point of view through writing, and create deal flow through your network and other investors — because the best startups are oversubscribed, so founders need a reason to take your money beyond capital alone.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Career growth comes mainly from excelling in your assigned role and climbing the ladder linearly. → **Their view**: Great careers are built through curiosity, relationships, serendipity, and jumping on unexpected opportunities — because breakthrough opportunities often come from adjacent work and informal helping.
- **Conventional**: The best move is often the most senior title you can get. → **Their view**: It is often better to take a lower title at the likely category leader than a top title at a weaker company — because winner-take-most dynamics compound.
- **Conventional**: Startups should hire experienced PMs from outside as soon as product complexity increases. → **Their view**: The first PM should often be an internal engineer, analyst, or designer who already has trust and context — because the role is partly translation and trust-building.
- **Conventional**: More process and better tooling improve product development as soon as a startup starts growing. → **Their view**: Early teams should keep process lightweight and use simple tools until scale truly demands more structure — because overhead can outrun clarity.
- **Conventional**: Shipping features is evidence of product team productivity. → **Their view**: A feature that does not change customer behavior is not meaningful progress — because output is not the same as outcome.
- **Conventional**: To hire a great leader, recruit the top executive from a great company. → **Their view**: Recruit the lieutenant or second-line leader from a best-in-class company instead — because they are often more attainable and still highly trained.
- **Conventional**: Early-stage startups should use senior titles to attract talent. → **Their view**: Delay VP and director titles because they create long-term organizational and cultural problems — because titles are hard to unwind later.
- **Conventional**: Good investing starts with identifying the biggest markets. → **Their view**: At the angel stage, back exceptional founders with authentic motivation even if the market is unclear — because great founders can create or pivot into markets.
