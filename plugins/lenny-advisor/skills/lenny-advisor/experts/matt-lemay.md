# Matt LeMay

**Product leader, author, and consultant** | **Independent / author of _Impact-First Product Teams_** | Expertise: product-management, product-strategy, leadership, go-to-market

## Bio
Matt LeMay is a longtime product leader and consultant who is known for making product management practical, business-oriented, and accountable to real outcomes. He spent 13 years at Pitchfork and has since worked with hundreds of product teams, helping them connect day-to-day work to company goals instead of drifting into feature factory mode.

His perspective matters because he pushes product teams to think like business operators without turning product into a rigid process exercise. He is especially useful when a team needs sharper prioritization, clearer goals, or a more honest answer to: “Why does this team exist?”

## Signature Frameworks

### Impact-First Product Teams
- **When to use**: When a product team wants to avoid becoming a low-impact feature factory and align work to business-critical outcomes.
- **Steps**:
  1. Set team goals no more than one step away from company goals.
  2. Keep impact front and center at every step of planning and execution.
  3. Connect every piece of work back to impact using the same unit of measure as the team goal.
- **Example**: A fintech team set a goal to convert a specific number of single-product users into multi-product users, because that conversion had known customer lifetime value and directly contributed to revenue.

### CEO Test for Team Funding
- **When to use**: When a team needs to stress-test whether its work is truly valuable to the business.
- **Steps**:
  1. Ask: if I were the CEO, would I fully fund this team?
  2. If the answer is unclear, identify what information is missing.
  3. Adjust team goals, purview, or stakeholders until the team can answer confidently.
- **Example**: Matt uses this question to reveal when teams cannot clearly explain why the business should keep investing in them.

### Center-of-Gravity Goal Setting
- **When to use**: When teams are using OKRs, KPIs, or north star metrics and need a simpler way to align with company priorities.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with the company-level goal as the center of gravity.
  2. Set team goals that orbit one level around that goal.
  3. Avoid cascading goals through many layers that break the connection to business outcomes.
- **Example**: A growth team reset its work so every decision traced directly back to a company goal of bringing in one million new users.

### Subtractive Goal Clarification
- **When to use**: When a team has too many goals, too much complexity, or unclear priorities.
- **Steps**:
  1. Write down the most important goal each person thinks the team has.
  2. Pair up and repeatedly make the goal smaller and more concise.
  3. Keep subtracting until the goal is specific enough to drive decisions.
- **Example**: A team working on an ad tech platform reduced a sprawling set of goals into a single profit-oriented target that exposed a hidden company profit goal from a town hall slide.

### Impact-Based Prioritization
- **When to use**: When choosing between initiatives and needing to prioritize based on real business value rather than abstract scoring.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define impact in the same unit as the team goal.
  2. Estimate the maximum impact each option could have on that goal.
  3. Compare impact against effort and certainty, then choose the option most likely to move the business outcome.
- **Example**: For a team trying to increase multi-product adoption, Matt pushed them to estimate how many users each idea could convert, rather than relying on generic ICE scores.

### Options Plus Recommendation
- **When to use**: When pushing back on requests or presenting decisions to stakeholders.
- **Steps**:
  1. Present multiple viable options.
  2. Explain the trade-offs of each option.
  3. Recommend the option you believe best serves the business goal.
- **Example**: At Mailchimp, the team role-played how a single-option pitch invites holes and resistance, while multiple options with a recommendation create clearer decision-making.

## Core Advice
- **When you are unsure whether your team is truly valuable to the business**: Ask whether you would fully fund your own team if you were the CEO — because if you cannot justify the team in business terms, you may be doing work the company does not see as essential.
- **When your team’s goals are several layers removed from company goals**: Rewrite them so they are no more than one step away from the company’s top-line objective — because every extra abstraction layer increases drift.
- **When OKRs feel like a ritual but not a decision-making tool**: Use the company goal as the center of gravity and make team goals orbit around it — because this keeps work tied to outcomes instead of process theater.
- **When your team is tempted to focus on easy, low-risk feature work**: Actively seek high-impact work, even if it requires cross-functional coordination or executive attention — because low-impact work compounds into more low-impact work.
- **When you are setting priorities with ICE, RICE, or similar frameworks**: Define impact in the same unit as your team goal, not as an abstract score — because prioritization only works when the estimate maps to the outcome you own.
- **When a stakeholder asks for a feature that seems misaligned**: Do not simply say yes or no; present options, trade-offs, and a recommendation — because trust improves when you acknowledge uncertainty and preserve decision quality.
- **When you are asked to build something that does not obviously map to business impact**: Ask what business outcome it is meant to move and how much it contributes relative to other options — because important work should be explainable in outcome terms.
- **When your team is working in a regulated, B2B, or constrained environment**: Treat constraints as guides for product strategy rather than excuses for doing product poorly — because constraints often define the advantage.
- **When your company is growing and things seem to be going well**: Stay curious about why things are working and learn from adjacent teams — because success can hide weak attribution and create complacency.
- **When your team is afraid to take on the most important business problem**: Use subtractive thinking to simplify the goal and expose the few decisions that matter most — because clarity reduces fear and improves coordination.
- **When you are worried about being accountable for outcomes outside your control**: Accept that business success is partly outside your control, and focus on what you can influence from your seat — because this reduces anxiety and sharpens execution.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Product teams should obsess over doing OKRs, strategy, and prioritization “the right way” through detailed cascading frameworks. → **Their view**: Sweating the middle is often a distraction; the real question is whether the work is one step away from a business-critical outcome. — because abstraction layers sever the link to business value.
- **Conventional**: If leadership asks for a feature, the team should just build it, even if it feels low impact. → **Their view**: Being told to build something is not a defense; if the work is low impact, the team may still be at risk. — because companies evaluate teams on outcomes, not compliance.
- **Conventional**: PMs should act as the mini CEO and personally own all commercial thinking. → **Their view**: The PM’s job is to bring the whole team into CEO-level commercial thinking, not to be the sole owner of it. — because engineers, designers, and researchers can surface the clearest path to impact.
- **Conventional**: Best practices are the main thing product teams need to follow. → **Their view**: Best practices matter less than understanding the specific business model, constraints, and success criteria of your company. — because context beats generic advice.
- **Conventional**: Product managers should always push back hard and say no to bad ideas. → **Their view**: The better move is often to avoid yes/no framing and instead offer options with trade-offs and a recommendation. — because stakeholders may know things you do not.
- **Conventional**: If a team is doing well, it can relax and assume the current approach is working. → **Their view**: Even in good times, teams should stay curious and keep learning what is driving success. — because market conditions can change quickly.
