# Noah Weiss

**Chief Product Officer** | **Slack** | Expertise: product-management, ai-strategy, go-to-market, leadership

## Bio
Noah Weiss is the Chief Product Officer at Slack, where he has spent seven years helping the company evolve from consumer roots into a large-scale enterprise product with strong product-led growth. His background spans Google, Foursquare, and Slack, giving him a rare mix of consumer product intuition, B2B scaling experience, and practical AI product judgment.

What makes his perspective especially valuable is that he thinks in systems: growth loops, product principles, team operating models, and the tradeoffs between learning and shipping. He is particularly useful when a team is stuck in incrementalism, struggling with founder alignment, or trying to build AI features that earn trust.

## Signature Frameworks

### Take Bigger Boulder Bets / Get to the Next Hill
- **When to use**: When a product team is stuck in incremental optimization and needs a new growth or innovation frontier.
- **Steps**:
  1. Stop over-focusing on 1% metric gains.
  2. Step back and identify the next hill or adjacent opportunity.
  3. Spin up a small team or prototype in that area.
  4. Learn quickly through pilots and experimentation.
  5. Launch only when the concept is strong enough to impress.
- **Example**: Slack used this approach for huddles and clips during the pandemic, and is applying it again to AI prototypes.

### Founder-CEO Collaboration U-Curve
- **When to use**: When working with a highly opinionated, product-minded founder or CEO.
- **Steps**:
  1. Align early on strategy, goals, anti-goals, and principles.
  2. Let the team build with autonomy in the middle.
  3. Bring the founder back near the end to taste and refine.
  4. Review in real code, not just mocks.
  5. Use a collective bug bash to raise craft and polish.
- **Example**: At Slack, Stewart Butterfield would join the full team for a bug bash before launch.

### Product Principles as Shared Language
- **When to use**: When a company is scaling and needs consistent product judgment across many teams.
- **Steps**:
  1. Translate mission into a small set of product principles.
  2. Use them as guardrails for decisions.
  3. Make them the language of critique and review.
  4. Use them to reduce vague feedback and endless iteration.
- **Example**: Slack’s principles include “be a great host” and “don’t make me think.”

### Complaint-Storms
- **When to use**: When a team has lost empathy for new users or is too close to its own assumptions.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with an adjacent product, not your own.
  2. Walk through the customer journey on one screen.
  3. Have everyone call out every confusing or painful moment.
  4. Use the critique to calibrate quality and inspire ideas.
  5. Apply the same lens to your own product.
- **Example**: Slack used complaint-storms to help teams empathize with first-time users and spot onboarding friction.

### Portfolio Roadmapping
- **When to use**: When prioritizing across maintenance, innovation, and learning work.
- **Steps**:
  1. Treat the roadmap as a portfolio, not a single bucket.
  2. Balance new capabilities with incremental improvements.
  3. Balance risky bets with known bets.
  4. Balance impact work with learning work.
  5. Use short customer-love sprints for high-impact polish.
- **Example**: Slack runs two-week customer love sprints to ship low-effort, high-impact improvements.

### Customer Love Sprints
- **When to use**: When you need a fast, energizing way to improve the product experience.
- **Steps**:
  1. Build a burndown list of low-effort, high-impact fixes.
  2. Run a focused two-week sprint with product, design, and engineering.
  3. Ship as many improvements as possible.
  4. Repeat on a recurring cadence, often quarterly.
- **Example**: Slack uses these sprints to deliver delightful improvements customers notice immediately.

### Learning-First Turnaround
- **When to use**: When growth plateaus and the team doesn’t understand why.
- **Steps**:
  1. Diagnose the customer and cohort dynamics.
  2. Be willing to throw away the roadmap.
  3. Form hypotheses about new levers.
  4. Test quickly, even if short-term impact drops.
  5. Rebuild the growth engine from the new insights.
- **Example**: Slack paused impact work for about six months to understand self-serve stagnation, then doubled new paid customer growth.

## Core Advice
- **When growth or activation plateaus**: pause optimization and spend time learning what the next customer segment actually needs — because old levers often stop working after the early adopters.
- **When working with a founder who is also the functional expert**: align early, then expect less autonomy in that domain — because you’ll learn a lot from someone world-class, even if it’s frustrating.
- **When feedback keeps coming back as “not quite right”**: codify product principles — because shared standards beat vague intuition.
- **When starting a new initiative with a strong CEO**: involve them heavily at the beginning and end, but protect the middle for exploration — because that preserves both direction and creativity.
- **When the team is debating static designs too long**: prototype in real software early — because code reveals issues mocks hide.
- **When the team has lost empathy for new users**: run complaint-storms or usability sessions — because fresh-eyed criticism exposes friction fast.
- **When prioritizing feature-team work**: use a portfolio mindset — because healthy teams need both polish and bets.
- **When you want to improve customer delight quickly**: run a customer-love sprint — because small fixes can create outsized momentum.
- **When a product is hard for new users to understand**: improve comprehension before adding sophistication — because adoption stalls when value is unclear.
- **When building AI features**: make UI confidence match data quality and be transparent about uncertainty — because false confidence destroys trust.
- **When designing an AI product loop**: build feedback loops that generate training data naturally — because real usage should make the product smarter.
- **When early in your PM career**: focus on execution, local impact, and data/research fluency — because credibility comes from reliable delivery.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Remove as many clicks as possible to optimize UX. → **Their view**: More clicks can be better if they help users understand and trust what’s happening — because clarity matters more than minimalism in many non-transactional products.
- **Conventional**: PMs should act like mini-CEOs and make the decisions. → **Their view**: PMs should facilitate decision quality and pace — because dictator-style PMs create resentment and weaken ownership.
- **Conventional**: Product-led growth is mostly about freemium. → **Their view**: The real engine is making something people love enough to share with coworkers — because social capital, not pricing, is the core growth mechanism.
- **Conventional**: AI products should always appear confident and polished. → **Their view**: AI should admit uncertainty and cite sources when confidence is low — because trust matters more than flash.
- **Conventional**: A roadmap should maximize constant impact. → **Their view**: Sometimes you should sacrifice short-term impact to learn the next growth lever — because learning can unlock the next S-curve.
- **Conventional**: Build for yourself or people like you. → **Their view**: That works early, but broader audiences require structured beta programs and real-world feedback — because intuition degrades as the customer base diversifies.
