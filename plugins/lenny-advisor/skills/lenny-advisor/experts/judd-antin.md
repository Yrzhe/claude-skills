# Judd Antin

**Consultant; former Head of Research at Airbnb and early builder of the user research practice at Facebook/Meta** | **Independent consultant (formerly Airbnb and Meta)** | Expertise: user-research, design, leadership, strategy, metrics, product-management

## Bio
Judd Antin is a veteran UX research and design leader who helped build research as a strategic function at Facebook/Meta and later led research and broader design work at Airbnb. His perspective matters because he has operated at the level where research either becomes a business lever or gets reduced to a service function.

He is especially known for pushing research to be more integrated with product, more multi-method, and more directly tied to company outcomes. If you want a view on how research earns influence inside high-performing product organizations, Judd is a strong source.

## Signature Frameworks

### Macro / Middle-Range / Micro Research
- **When to use**: Use this to decide what kind of research to run and to diagnose why research is or isn’t driving impact.
- **Steps**:
  1. Classify the question as macro, middle-range, or micro.
  2. Use macro research for strategic, forward-looking, market, and competitive questions.
  3. Use micro research for specific usability, funnel, and interaction questions.
  4. Be cautious with middle-range research unless it is tied to a business problem, funnel stage, OKR, or decision.
  5. Prioritize macro and micro work when trying to increase impact and reduce performative research.
- **Example**: A concept-car or competitive landscape study is macro. “How do hosts feel about payment options?” is middle-range and may be too vague unless tied to a concrete business decision.

### The Five-Tool Researcher
- **When to use**: Use this when hiring, developing, or evaluating researchers in modern product organizations.
- **Steps**:
  1. Build formative/generative research skills.
  2. Build evaluative research skills.
  3. Develop strong survey design capability.
  4. Learn applied statistics and experimentation literacy.
  5. Add technical fluency like SQL, dashboard literacy, or prompt engineering.
- **Example**: Judd describes the best researchers as a Swiss army knife. A strong candidate can move from interviews to surveys to experiment interpretation to data tooling.

### We Don’t Validate, We Falsify
- **When to use**: Use this when teams want research to confirm a decision they’ve already made.
- **Steps**:
  1. Assume the idea may be wrong.
  2. Frame research to uncover failure modes and blind spots.
  3. Ask what would change the decision, not what would justify it.
  4. Use findings to refine, redirect, or kill the idea quickly.
- **Example**: Instead of “run a quick user study to validate our assumptions,” ask “where are we most likely wrong, and what would make us change course?”

### Integrated Research Partnership Model
- **When to use**: Use this when structuring product teams so research influences decisions instead of acting as a reactive service.
- **Steps**:
  1. Embed researchers from the beginning of planning through launch and iteration.
  2. Share success metrics across PMs, designers, engineers, and researchers.
  3. Build ongoing relationships, not project-by-project handoffs.
  4. Measure success by whether key meetings happen with the researcher in the room.
  5. Protect researcher capacity so they can focus on a few high-priority efforts.
- **Example**: Judd’s success metric was: “when they won’t have that meeting without you.”

### Swiss Army Knife Interview Test
- **When to use**: Use this to assess methodological breadth and judgment in researcher interviews.
- **Steps**:
  1. Give a juicy, open-ended research question.
  2. Ask how the candidate would approach it in a day, a week, and a month.
  3. Look for multiple methods, not one favorite method.
  4. Evaluate whether they can adapt rigor to the decision at hand.
- **Example**: A strong candidate explains how they’d use interviews, surveys, and data depending on time and stakes.

### Gut Check via System 2 and Wisdom of the Crowd
- **When to use**: Use this when leaders have strong intuition but want to reduce bias.
- **Steps**:
  1. Treat intuition as useful pattern recognition, but not truth.
  2. Slow down into more analytic thinking.
  3. Bring in diverse perspectives and evidence.
  4. Compare gut instinct with structured evidence before deciding.
- **Example**: A leader thinks a feature will work, but researchers bring in user evidence and cross-functional perspectives that reveal hidden risks.

## Core Advice
- **When research is requested late just to “validate assumptions”**: Reframe it to test what could be wrong — because validation after the fact is usually symbolic, not decision-changing.
- **When research is treated like a service desk**: Embed the researcher from the start — because influence comes from shaping the question, not only answering it.
- **When a study is broad but vague**: Tie it to a business problem, funnel stage, OKR, or strategic choice — because interesting findings without a decision context are easy to ignore.
- **When teams say research slows them down**: Use fast micro research for usability and funnel issues — because quick answers often prevent bigger downstream costs.
- **When hiring or leveling researchers**: Prioritize multi-method breadth plus technical fluency — because one-method researchers tend to overuse their favorite tool.
- **When you want research to matter more**: Learn the language of the business — because research becomes influential when it connects to metrics, strategy, and growth.
- **When research capacity is overloaded**: Say no more often and focus on fewer high-value projects — because spread-thin research becomes reactive and low-impact.
- **When intuition and evidence conflict**: Use intuition as a starting point, then check it with analytic thinking and diverse evidence — because intuition is useful but biased.
- **When an A/B test raises “why?” questions**: Pair it with qualitative or mixed-method research — because experiments show impact, not mechanism.
- **When dogfooding feels sufficient**: Use it to generate hypotheses, not prioritize them — because internal teams are not real users.
- **When insights are fragmented across teams**: Integrate UX research, market research, customer feedback, and data science — because siloed insights reduce clarity.
- **When choosing a customer sentiment metric**: Prefer CSAT over NPS — because Judd sees NPS as weaker on survey quality and business correlation.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: User research is mainly about empathy and understanding users, separate from business concerns. → **Their view**: Research must be profit- and strategy-aware — because it survives and influences decisions only when it overlaps with business outcomes.
- **Conventional**: More user-centered rituals automatically make teams customer obsessed. → **Their view**: Much of this is “user-centered performance” — because symbolic rituals often don’t change decisions.
- **Conventional**: Research is slow and delays teams. → **Their view**: Good research often speeds teams up — because it prevents shipping the wrong thing.
- **Conventional**: Usability work is low-status compared with strategic research. → **Their view**: Micro usability research can be the highest-value work — because tiny fixes can drive outsized business impact.
- **Conventional**: Anyone can do user research. → **Their view**: Talking to users is not the same as doing research well — because rigor, bias control, and synthesis matter.
- **Conventional**: A/B tests are the gold standard. → **Their view**: Experiments are necessary but insufficient — because they rarely explain why something happened.
- **Conventional**: Dogfooding is enough to prioritize product issues. → **Their view**: Dogfooding is useful but dangerous for prioritization — because internal users differ from real users.
- **Conventional**: NPS is the best loyalty metric. → **Their view**: NPS is overrated and often inferior to CSAT — because its measurement properties are weaker.
