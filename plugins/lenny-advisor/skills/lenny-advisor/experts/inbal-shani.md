# Inbal Shani

**Chief Product Officer** | **GitHub** | Expertise: ai-strategy, product-management, leadership, engineering-management

## Bio
Inbal Shani is the Chief Product Officer at GitHub, where she leads product strategy for the platform behind GitHub Copilot. She has deep experience across AI, developer tools, and large-scale product leadership from roles at GitHub, AWS, Microsoft, and Amazon Robotics.

Her perspective matters because she sits at the intersection of product, engineering, and AI adoption. She is especially useful when you need to decide whether AI is actually the right solution, how to embed it into real workflows, and how to measure whether it is creating genuine value.

## Signature Frameworks

### Problem-first AI adoption
- **When to use**: When deciding whether and how to add AI to a product, workflow, or company process.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with the customer or workflow problem you are trying to solve.
  2. Identify where manual work, friction, or inefficiency exists.
  3. Evaluate whether AI is the best tool to reduce that friction or improve outcomes.
  4. Design the AI experience to fit naturally into the user workflow.
  5. Avoid adding AI just because it is trendy.
- **Example**: GitHub did not start with “what should we do with AI?” It started with the problem that developers spend too little time coding and too much time on surrounding tasks, which led to Copilot as a productivity tool.

### Working backwards from the developer experience
- **When to use**: When designing developer tools or AI-assisted workflows that need strong adoption.
- **Steps**:
  1. Put yourself in the developer’s shoes.
  2. Map what the user is already trying to do in their workflow.
  3. Remove friction, waiting, and extra steps.
  4. Make the tool intuitive and seamlessly integrated.
  5. Validate internally before shipping externally.
- **Example**: Copilot was designed so developers would not need to explicitly ask for help every time; instead, it fades into the background and offers suggestions in context.

### Human-in-the-loop AI development
- **When to use**: When deploying AI in software development or other high-stakes workflows.
- **Steps**:
  1. Treat AI as an assistant, not a replacement.
  2. Keep humans responsible for judgment, creativity, and final decisions.
  3. Use AI to automate repetitive or lower-enjoyment tasks.
  4. Shift human effort toward systems thinking, architecture, and product understanding.
  5. Measure outcomes beyond raw speed.
- **Example**: Shani repeatedly emphasized that “Copilot is a copilot, not a pilot” and argued companies should not reduce engineering headcount because developers are still needed for innovation, oversight, and system-level thinking.

### Multi-metric AI success measurement
- **When to use**: When evaluating whether AI tools are actually improving engineering outcomes.
- **Steps**:
  1. Avoid relying on a single metric like time saved or lines of code.
  2. Measure quality-related outcomes such as code quality and security improvements.
  3. Track workflow outcomes such as review speed, collaboration, and time to value.
  4. Combine these signals into a broader view of productivity and developer happiness.
  5. Tailor metrics to the use case, such as security metrics for security tools.
- **Example**: For GitHub Advanced Security, the right metric is not productivity but outcomes like preventing secret leaks or catching issues before shipping.

### Innovation through flexible experimentation
- **When to use**: When trying to create breakthrough products or discover new product opportunities.
- **Steps**:
  1. Create bandwidth for experimentation.
  2. Accept that not every experiment will succeed.
  3. Gather ideas from customers, community, product, design, engineering, and field teams.
  4. Let teams pitch ideas and explore proofs of concept.
  5. Keep research connected to product and engineering so ideas can become real products.
- **Example**: GitHub Next is a research team focused on the three-to-five-year horizon, but its success comes from staying tightly connected to product and engineering rather than becoming an isolated paper-writing group.

### Change management for new leaders
- **When to use**: When entering a new leadership role and trying to drive change in an existing team.
- **Steps**:
  1. Observe the current environment and understand why things work the way they do.
  2. Explain clearly why change is needed.
  3. Bring the team along rather than imposing change immediately.
  4. Adapt your style to the team and cultural context.
  5. Pace change so people can absorb and support it.
- **Example**: At TomTom, she moved too fast early on, trying to fix everything immediately, and learned that she needed to slow down, explain the why, and take the team with her.

## Core Advice
- **When you face pressure to add AI because everyone else is doing it**: start with the customer problem and work backward to whether AI is actually the right solution — because this avoids superficial “AI plastering” and leads to real adoption.
- **When you want low-friction AI adoption**: make the experience seamless, intuitive, and embedded in the existing workflow — because users will not tolerate extra complexity.
- **When executives want to cut engineers because AI boosts productivity**: use AI to free up time for higher-value work rather than reducing headcount — because developers still create innovation, oversight, and system-level thinking.
- **When measuring AI impact on engineering**: use a basket of metrics including quality, security, collaboration, review speed, and time to value — because speed alone can reward bad code.
- **When AI increases code generation**: invest more in AI-driven testing across unit, integration, load, performance, infrastructure, and security testing — because more code means more need for validation.
- **When building innovative products inside a large company**: create room for experimentation and accept that some experiments will fail — because breakthroughs require learning, not guaranteed success.
- **When searching for the next big product opportunity**: spend significant time with customers and communities — because the best ideas often come from unresolved pain points.
- **When entering a new leadership role**: slow down, explain the why, and bring the team along — because change fails when people feel rushed or excluded.
- **When teams are unsure how to use AI tools**: let people use AI in the way that best fits their preferences and strengths — because different developers work best in different modes.
- **When preparing engineers for an AI-heavy future**: train them to think in systems, architecture, and product context — because human value shifts toward big-picture reasoning.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Generative AI will soon replace software engineers and much of human work. → **Their view**: AI will remain a copilot, not a pilot — because creativity, judgment, and innovation still require humans.
- **Conventional**: If AI makes engineers more productive, companies should need fewer engineers. → **Their view**: Companies should not cut engineers; they should use AI to unlock more innovation and reduce burnout — because reclaimed time can be redirected to higher-value work.
- **Conventional**: The main way to measure AI success is time saved or coding speed. → **Their view**: Time is only a partial signal; the better lens is time to value plus quality, security, and developer happiness — because fast output can still be bad output.
- **Conventional**: The most important AI advances in software development are code generation features. → **Their view**: AI-driven testing is underhyped and may become one of the most important areas — because generated code increases the need for validation.
- **Conventional**: The future of AI will be dominated by one generalized LLM approach. → **Their view**: The future is hybrid: general-purpose LLMs plus specialized models for specific domains — because high-safety and specialized contexts need tailored systems.
- **Conventional**: Innovation teams should be tightly structured and managed with fixed creativity allocations. → **Their view**: Innovation should be encouraged flexibly rather than over-structured — because creativity is organic and rigid systems can suppress it.
