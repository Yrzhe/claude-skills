# Brandon Chu

**VP of Product** | **Shopify** | Expertise: product-management, leadership, platform strategy, writing

## Bio
Brandon Chu is a VP of Product at Shopify with deep experience spanning product management, platform strategy, and executive leadership inside a fast-scaling, highly technical company. He’s especially influential for how he thinks about product leadership: less as ownership of opinions, and more as helping teams make better decisions, move faster, and build with clarity.

His perspective matters because he combines hands-on product judgment with systems thinking about organizations, platforms, and communication. He is also unusually strong on writing as a leadership tool, making him especially useful for PMs who want to grow beyond execution into broader influence.

## Signature Frameworks

### Shopify PM mandate: help teams ship the right thing at the right time in the right way
- **When to use**: When defining the role of product managers and clarifying decision-making across product, design, and engineering.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start from servant leadership: the PM’s job is to help teams, not act as a dictator.
  2. Ensure the team is solving the right problem, not just shipping output.
  3. Time decisions and delivery so the work happens when it matters most.
  4. Guide execution quality so the solution is delivered in the right way.
- **Example**: At Shopify, everyone is expected to think about product. The PM is accountable for helping the team arrive at the right answer, not monopolizing product judgment.

### Annual investment-plan planning with chaotic execution
- **When to use**: When aligning a large organization around broad strategic direction while preserving team autonomy and adaptability.
- **Steps**:
  1. Create annual investment plans for large parts of the org around vision and directional outcomes.
  2. Align those plans with executives and sometimes the board, including headcount implications.
  3. Let directors and teams propose how their groups will contribute over the next quarter.
  4. Use peer and leadership buy-in to build momentum behind the bets.
  5. Continuously revisit priorities and abandon work quickly if the world changes.
- **Example**: A VP-level investment plan at Shopify may guide roughly 20% of the company, after which teams move “chaotically” toward the goal and are expected to change course if needed.

### Decision triage for PMs
- **When to use**: When facing many product decisions and needing to decide where to spend deep thinking versus moving quickly.
- **Steps**:
  1. Determine how important the decision actually is.
  2. Assess whether the decision is reversible or irreversible.
  3. Assess whether it affects many users in a material way.
  4. Spend disproportionate time on the few critical decisions.
  5. For lower-stakes decisions, trust your gut or delegate to preserve velocity.
- **Example**: Chu argues PMs should focus deeply on the tiny number of truly important decisions and make the rest quickly so they don’t become a blocker.

### Platform PM principles-first approach
- **When to use**: When building platforms, APIs, or ecosystems with multiple constituencies and long feedback cycles.
- **Steps**:
  1. Accept that platform work has much longer validation cycles than user-facing product work.
  2. Create intermediate milestones and celebrations for infrastructure and API progress.
  3. Define the platform’s governing principles before making policy or design decisions.
  4. Explicitly rank the constituencies the platform serves when tradeoffs arise.
  5. Use those principles to guide decisions on data sharing, policy, UX boundaries, and ecosystem incentives.
- **Example**: Shopify may choose merchant-first data policies even when developers dislike them, because the company’s principles prioritize entrepreneurs over other constituencies.

### Remote work with bursts
- **When to use**: When operating as a remote-first or remote-only company but still needing high-bandwidth collaboration and team cohesion.
- **Steps**:
  1. Default to remote work for day-to-day operations.
  2. Acknowledge that in-person time still matters for creative, high-velocity work.
  3. Create recurring team gatherings (“bursts”) for collaboration and bonding.
  4. Reduce logistical friction with tooling that handles travel, lodging, food, and check-in.
  5. Track participation and outcomes to identify teams that may need reconnection.
- **Example**: Shopify built internal tooling so a team can choose a burst location and have flights, hotels, and logistics handled automatically.

## Core Advice
- **When you’re trying to grow as a PM beyond the basics**: develop founder-like skills such as storytelling, motivating teams, making high-conviction calls, and leading through ambiguity — because senior PM growth is driven by leadership, not just product mechanics.
- **When you have too many product decisions at once**: classify which decisions are truly important, then spend most of your time on those and make the rest quickly or delegate them — because attention is scarce and velocity matters.
- **When the market changes fast or a crisis hits**: throw away existing roadmaps if needed and narrow focus to the few things that matter most right now — because sunk-cost thinking is expensive in volatile environments.
- **When you want to become a better communicator**: write regularly, even if you never publish it — because writing forces clarity of thought.
- **When you’re afraid to share unfinished ideas**: share early drafts and rough thinking with others — because feedback reveals the gap between your intent and what others actually understand.
- **When you’re unsure whether writing is worth the time**: treat writing as a career investment, not a content exercise — because it sharpens thinking, pre-onboards teammates, and increases influence.
- **When you’re choosing between platform PM and user-facing PM work**: choose based on what energizes you, and consider moving between both over time — because each builds different muscles.
- **When platform work feels slow and intangible**: create internal milestones and celebrate alpha/beta progress — because long feedback loops can otherwise drain momentum.
- **When you’re building a platform with multiple stakeholders**: define principles and stakeholder hierarchy before policy or UX decisions — because inconsistency creates downstream conflict.
- **When you feel limited in PM perspective or technical confidence**: start a real side project or small company and build something yourself — because firsthand experience with shipping, sales, and support broadens judgment.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Product managers are the primary owners of product thinking and should have the strongest say in product decisions. → **Their view**: Everyone in the company should participate in product thinking; PMs are there to help teams ship the right thing, not monopolize product judgment — because good product decisions come from the whole organization.
- **Conventional**: Good planning means sticking to the roadmap and avoiding disruption. → **Their view**: You should be willing to throw away plans quickly when reality changes — because software and markets move too fast for rigid plans.
- **Conventional**: Remote companies should solve remote-work problems mainly with async processes and tools. → **Their view**: A key way to make remote work better is to intentionally get people together in person for bursts of creative work — because high-bandwidth collaboration still matters.
- **Conventional**: Writing is optional for PMs unless they want a public audience. → **Their view**: Writing is essential for PMs even if nothing is ever published — because it clarifies thinking and improves communication.
- **Conventional**: To improve as a PM, focus only on PM-specific skills inside your current role. → **Their view**: One of the best ways to level up is to start a side hustle or build a company yourself — because firsthand experience teaches empathy, technical understanding, and business judgment.
