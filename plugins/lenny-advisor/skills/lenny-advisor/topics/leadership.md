# Leadership

## Overview
Leadership in this collection is about making better decisions, building strong teams, and creating systems that scale. The recurring theme is that good leaders combine self-awareness, structured judgment, and operational rigor rather than relying on charisma or intuition alone.  
The most useful leadership skills here are: knowing what you value, asking for better input, hiring for the actual problem, and designing environments where people and systems can succeed.

## Key Frameworks

### Curiosity Loops (Ada Chen Rekhi)
- **When to use**: When you need better input for an important decision and want contextual advice rather than generic opinions.
- **Steps**:
  1. Form a specific, unbiased question that asks for rationale.
  2. Choose a mix of subject matter experts and people who know you well.
  3. Make the ask lightweight and easy to answer quickly.
  4. Collect responses and look for surprises, disagreements, and missing angles.
  5. Thank people and share how their input affected your decision.
- **Example**: Ada emailed 10-11 people with nine possible podcast topics and asked them to pick the top two or three and explain why. In about 20 minutes, she got rich input that shaped the conversation.

### Values Exercise / Inner Scorecard (Ada Chen Rekhi)
- **When to use**: When making career or life decisions that may be distorted by status, money, titles, or others’ expectations.
- **Steps**:
  1. Review a list of possible values and select the ones that resonate.
  2. Group and narrow them to a smaller set.
  3. Stack-rank the most important values for your current season of life.
  4. Translate them into 3-5 sentences that define your personal scorecard.
  5. Use that scorecard to evaluate decisions against external pressure.
- **Example**: Ada used this to evaluate a high-profile job that looked great on paper but failed her top values because of travel and grueling hours.

### Explore and Exploit (Ada Chen Rekhi)
- **When to use**: When planning your early career or deciding whether to broaden experiences versus go deeper in a proven path.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start in exploration mode early in your career.
  2. Treat each move as a hypothesis-driven experiment.
  3. Extract explicit learnings about strengths, preferences, and gaps.
  4. Switch to exploit mode once you find a rich area worth deepening.
  5. Use exploit mode intentionally to build specific capabilities.
- **Example**: Ada explored Microsoft, startup marketing, and founding. After acquisition by LinkedIn, she used the role to learn growth, subscriptions, and pricing.

### Don’t Be the Frog (Ada Chen Rekhi)
- **When to use**: When deciding whether to stay in a role that feels increasingly misaligned or stagnant.
- **Steps**:
  1. Watch whether your environment is improving or deteriorating.
  2. Check whether you are still learning and being challenged.
  3. Separate temporary discomfort from inertia and drift.
  4. Try to change the situation through conversations or role shaping.
  5. If growth is unavailable, use capacity to build skills or relationships elsewhere.
- **Example**: Ada warns that small issues can compound until you realize you have stopped learning and are trapped by inertia.

### Eating Your Vegetables (Ada Chen Rekhi)
- **When to use**: When you dislike a skill or activity and need to know whether you truly dislike it or are just inexperienced.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify a skill that matters but feels uncomfortable.
  2. Commit to repeated exposure instead of judging it once.
  3. Create a concrete rule or ritual that forces practice.
  4. Repeat enough times to separate beginner discomfort from real dislike.
  5. Reflect on whether it becomes easier, more useful, or more enjoyable.
- **Example**: Ada forced herself to attend one networking event per week and hand out 10 business cards. Over time, she got much better and built lasting relationships.

### Growth Competency Model (Adam Fishman)
- **When to use**: When hiring, evaluating, coaching, or structuring a growth team.
- **Steps**:
  1. Assess the team’s actual gaps instead of searching for a mythical unicorn.
  2. Evaluate candidates across growth execution, customer knowledge, growth strategy, and communication/influence.
  3. Probe execution skills: channels, experimentation, and productizing learnings.
  4. Probe customer knowledge: data fluency, user psychology, and learning from customers.
  5. Probe strategy: growth loops, forecasting, prioritization, and roadmapping.
  6. Probe communication: leadership and stakeholder management.
  7. Use the model for hiring and performance feedback.
- **Example**: Fishman argued Wyzant needed a strategy for growth loops and execution systems, not a “silver bullet” growth answer.

### Onboarding as Promise Delivery (Adam Fishman)
- **When to use**: When designing or improving onboarding, activation, and early retention.
- **Steps**:
  1. Treat onboarding as the first moment the product must deliver on the market promise.
  2. Recognize onboarding touches 100% of users.
  3. Use new-user motivation to learn about and qualify users.
  4. Optimize for habit formation and retention, not just conversion.
  5. Revisit onboarding only when you have genuinely new insights.
- **Example**: At Patreon, onboarding improvements and human-guided interventions improved first- and second-month revenue by 25%.

## Decision Guide
- If you need better input for a big decision, consider **Curiosity Loops** because it surfaces contextual advice and blind spots — per **Ada Chen Rekhi**.
- If a career opportunity looks impressive but feels off, use the **Values Exercise / Inner Scorecard** because it filters out status bias — per **Ada Chen Rekhi**.
- If you are early in your career and unsure what fits, use **Explore and Exploit** because exploration is the fastest way to learn what you want — per **Ada Chen Rekhi**.
- If a role is slowly draining you, use **Don’t Be the Frog** because inertia can hide long-term misalignment — per **Ada Chen Rekhi**.
- If you dislike an important skill, use **Eating Your Vegetables** because repeated exposure helps distinguish discomfort from true dislike — per **Ada Chen Rekhi**.
- If you are hiring for growth, use the **Growth Competency Model** because it prevents vague, unicorn-based hiring — per **Adam Fishman**.
- If you are making a first growth hire, **hire for team balance, not a unicorn** because the best candidate depends on the team’s missing skills — per **Adam Fishman**.
- If you need a growth specialist, use **Painter vs. Architect vs. Surgeon** because specialists are usually best after the core system exists — per **Adam Fishman**.
- If onboarding is weak, use **Onboarding as Promise Delivery** because onboarding is the highest-leverage universal surface — per **Adam Fishman**.
- If manual onboarding works well, use **Human Intervention to Productized Defaults** because human behavior can be turned into scalable product rules — per **Adam Fishman**.
- If onboarding experiments take too long to measure, use **Proxy Metrics for Onboarding Experiments** because early indicators can predict long-term success — per **Adam Fishman**.
- If you are evaluating a job offer, use **PMF for Candidates** and **Reverse Diligence** because interviews are sales processes and you need to assess people, mission, and financials yourself — per **Adam Fishman**.

## Expert Consensus
- Leadership starts with self-awareness: know your values, strengths, and current season of life.
- Good decisions require structured input, not just intuition or one-off advice.
- Leaders should hire for the actual problem and the team’s missing capabilities.
- Growth and onboarding work best when they are tied to real user value and retention.
- Manual, human-led processes often reveal what should later be productized.
- Career and company decisions should be evaluated with diligence, not optimism alone.

## Points of Debate
- **Coach vs. broader support system** (Ada Chen Rekhi):  
  - **View A**: Get a coach when you are stuck.  
  - **View B**: Most people do not need a coach; mentors, courses, communities, and structured advice gathering may work better.
- **Senior growth leader vs. earlier-career builder** (Adam Fishman):  
  - **View A**: Hire the most experienced growth leader you can find.  
  - **View B**: Hire smart, driven people with strong execution and customer knowledge, then develop them.
- **Conversion-first onboarding vs. retention-first onboarding** (Adam Fishman):  
  - **View A**: Maximize onboarding completion and reduce friction.  
  - **View B**: Add the right friction if it improves qualification, habit formation, and downstream retention.
- **Specialist-first vs. generalist-first growth hiring** (Adam Fishman):  
  - **View A**: Bring in a deep specialist early for SEO, paid, or another channel.  
  - **View B**: Start with broad builders; bring in specialists only after the core system is clear.
- **Channel chasing vs. PMF revalidation** (Adam Grenier):  
  - **View A**: If growth slows, find a new channel.  
  - **View B**: First assume you may have lost product-market fit and revalidate the customer and offer.
