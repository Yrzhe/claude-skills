# Hiring Culture

## Overview
Hiring culture is the set of principles, rituals, and decision rules that shape who gets hired, how people are evaluated, and how teams are built. It matters because early hiring decisions compound into product quality, execution speed, manager effectiveness, and long-term retention. The strongest advice across experts is to hire for the actual problem, not for prestige, and to make expectations, feedback, and team fit explicit.

## Key Frameworks

### Growth Competency Model (Adam Fishman)
- **When to use**: Use when hiring, evaluating, coaching, or structuring a growth team, especially when founders are unclear what kind of growth leader they actually need.
- **Steps**:
  1. Assess the team’s current gaps instead of searching for a mythical all-in-one growth unicorn.
  2. Evaluate candidates across four competency areas: growth execution, customer knowledge, growth strategy, and communication and influence.
  3. Within growth execution, assess channel fluency, experimentation, and ability to productize learnings.
  4. Within customer knowledge, assess data fluency/instrumentation, user psychology, and ability to learn from customers over time.
  5. Within growth strategy, assess growth loop modeling, capital allocation and forecasting, and prioritization/roadmapping.
  6. Within communication and influence, assess strategic communication, team leadership, and stakeholder management.
  7. Use the model both forward-looking for hiring and backward-looking for performance feedback and development.
- **Example**: Fishman described a mismatch at Wyzant where founders wanted a “silver bullet” growth answer, while what the company really needed was a strategy for adding growth loops and a system for executing them.

### Hire for Team Balance, Not a Unicorn (Adam Fishman)
- **When to use**: Use when building an early growth team or making the first growth hire.
- **Steps**:
  1. Map which competencies already exist on the team.
  2. Identify the missing capabilities rather than pattern-matching to a famous or senior operator.
  3. Prefer candidates who are strong in foundational areas like execution and customer knowledge.
  4. Support less-experienced hires with mentorship, advisors, and external education if internal coaching is weak.
  5. Add specialists later once the core growth system is in place.
- **Example**: Fishman said founders often ask how to find “another Adam” earlier in career, but he recommends instead hiring smart, driven people with strong execution and customer knowledge, then developing them.

### Painter vs. Architect vs. Surgeon (Adam Fishman)
- **When to use**: Use when deciding what type of growth hire to make based on company stage and problem type.
- **Steps**:
  1. Determine whether you need broad foundational growth building or narrow expert optimization.
  2. Hire broad, adaptable builders first to establish the system.
  3. Only bring in a specialist “surgeon” once you know the area is important and needs deep expertise.
  4. Avoid making a narrow specialist your first growth hire unless the company’s immediate bottleneck is clearly in that specialty.
- **Example**: Fishman specifically called out SEO and paid growth as areas where a specialist can make sense later, but not usually as the first growth hire.

### Onboarding as Promise Delivery (Adam Fishman)
- **When to use**: Use when designing or improving onboarding, activation, and early retention.
- **Steps**:
  1. Treat onboarding as the first moment where the product must deliver on the brand promise made in the market.
  2. Recognize that onboarding is touched by 100% of users, making it the highest-leverage universal product surface.
  3. Use the high motivation of new users to learn about them and qualify them appropriately.
  4. Optimize onboarding not just for conversion, but for habit formation and retention.
  5. Revisit onboarding when you gain genuinely new customer or product insights, not just for cosmetic redesigns.
- **Example**: At Patreon, onboarding improvements and human-guided interventions improved creators’ first- and second-month revenue by 25%, which materially improved LTV.

### Human Intervention to Productized Defaults (Adam Fishman)
- **When to use**: Use when a manual onboarding or sales assist process is producing strong results and you want to scale it.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify high-potential users during onboarding using observable signals.
  2. Route those users to humans who can guide them to successful setup and behavior.
  3. Study what the humans consistently do that improves outcomes.
  4. Translate those successful behaviors into product defaults, recommendations, and guardrails.
  5. Preserve user choice, but make the right path easier and the wrong path harder.
- **Example**: At Patreon, creators with large and engaged audiences were identified by connecting external accounts like YouTube or Instagram. High-potential creators were routed to humans, and the learnings were later productized into “opinionated defaults” such as recommended tier counts and pricing structures.

### PMF for Candidates (Adam Fishman)
- **When to use**: Use when evaluating whether to join a company, especially in uncertain markets or after experiencing poor job fit.
- **Steps**:
  1. Evaluate the company on People: whether you can work productively with the team, leaders, and peers.
  2. Evaluate the company on Mission: whether success at the company creates meaningful positive outcomes for customers and society.
  3. Evaluate the company on Financials: whether the company shows fiscal discipline and durable planning.
  4. Do not compromise by accepting only two out of the three dimensions.
  5. Use observation, reverse behavioral questions, and backchannel references to validate your assessment.
- **Example**: Fishman said Lyft and Patreon were strong fits on people, mission, and financials, while Wyzant and Imperfect Foods were misses due largely to people dynamics and expectation mismatches.

### Reverse Diligence for Job Candidates (Adam Fishman)
- **When to use**: Use during a job search to assess leadership quality, team dynamics, and manager fit.
- **Steps**:
  1. Ask to observe a team meeting, product review, executive meeting, or offsite if possible.
  2. Ask leaders to describe a recent disagreement, where people differed, and how they resolved it.
  3. Talk to current or former employees who are not part of the interview sales process.
  4. Request references from people who have worked for the hiring manager before.
  5. Treat your time as a scarce investment and perform diligence with the same seriousness as an investor.
- **Example**: Fishman praised a former PM, Tal Raviv, for asking to speak with people Fishman had managed previously in order to understand what it would really be like to work for him.

### Functions and Missions Org Model (Alex Hardiman)
- **When to use**: When structuring a large product organization that needs both strong craft standards and cross-functional execution against business goals.
- **Steps**:
  1. Create functional homes for disciplines like product and design to manage craft excellence, career growth, promotion processes, and community of practice.
  2. Create cross-functional missions aligned to major company outcomes.
  3. Group missions into categories such as consumer, monetization, and platform.
  4. Embed the right domain experts into each mission, including editors where the product directly shapes journalism.
  5. Let mission teams pursue shared goals while functions maintain standards and talent development.
- **Example**: At The New York Times, Hardiman oversees product and design as functions, while work happens in cross-functional missions such as consumer products, subscriber growth, digital advertising, and shared platforms.

## Decision Guide
- If you are hiring your first growth leader, consider the **Growth Competency Model** because it forces a gap analysis instead of a vague search for “the best growth person” — per **Adam Fishman**.
- If your team is small and missing core execution, consider **Hire for Team Balance, Not a Unicorn** because foundational builders matter more than famous operators — per **Adam Fishman**.
- If you think you need a paid or SEO specialist first, use **Painter vs. Architect vs. Surgeon** because narrow expertise is usually best after the growth system exists — per **Adam Fishman**.
- If onboarding is weak, use **Onboarding as Promise Delivery** because onboarding is the universal surface that shapes activation and retention — per **Adam Fishman**.
- If manual onboarding or sales support is working, use **Human Intervention to Productized Defaults** because human behavior can reveal what should become product logic — per **Adam Fishman**.
- If onboarding experiments take too long to measure, use **Proxy Metrics for Onboarding Experiments** because early predictors let you move faster without losing sight of LTV — per **Adam Fishman**.
- If you are deciding whether to join a company, use **PMF for Candidates** because people, mission, and financials together predict fit better than prestige — per **Adam Fishman**.
- If you are unsure what a company is really like, use **Reverse Diligence for Job Candidates** because interviews are sales processes and real signals come from observation and backchannels — per **Adam Fishman**.
- If you are organizing a large product org, use **Functions and Missions Org Model** because it separates craft excellence from business execution — per **Alex Hardiman**.
- If your company is in a crisis or sudden market shift, use **Wartime Product Management** because the roadmap must reset around the new reality — per **Alex Hardiman**.
- If your product depends on trust or quality, use **Editorial Judgment + Algorithmic Scale** because engagement alone is not enough to preserve standards — per **Alex Hardiman**.
- If you need to give hard feedback, use **Difficult feedback conversation script** because clarity and directness are more helpful than avoidance — per **Alisa Cohn**.

## Expert Consensus
- Hire for the actual job and stage of the company, not for a generic “senior leader” profile.
- Early hires should usually be adaptable builders with strong execution and customer understanding.
- Culture is defined by standards, accountability, and how work gets done, not perks.
- Candidates should evaluate companies as carefully as companies evaluate candidates.
- Hard feedback, clear expectations, and explicit decision-making improve performance and retention.

## Points of Debate
- **Senior growth leader vs. earlier-career builder** (Adam Fishman):  
  - **View A**: Hire a highly experienced growth leader because they have done it before.  
  - **View B**: Hire or promote someone earlier in their growth career if they are strong in execution and customer knowledge, because they are often a better fit for the real problem.
- **Maximum conversion vs. qualified activation** (Adam Fishman):  
  - **View A**: Optimize onboarding for the highest possible completion rate.  
  - **View B**: Accept more friction if it improves downstream retention and user quality.
- **Manual process vs. scalable product** (Adam Fishman):  
  - **View A**: Manual intervention is a temporary workaround and should be minimized.  
  - **View B**: Manual intervention is a learning tool that can reveal what to productize.
- **Engagement-first algorithms vs. editorial judgment** (Alex Hardiman):  
  - **View A**: Let user engagement drive ranking and recommendations.  
  - **View B**: Use expert judgment to guide algorithms in trust-sensitive products.
- **Employee happiness vs. winning culture** (Alisa Cohn):  
  - **View A**: Great leaders keep people happy and avoid discomfort.  
  - **View B**: Great leaders create clarity, accountability, and results, even when that means hard conversations.
