# Growth

## Overview
Growth is the discipline of connecting more users to product value, then compounding that value through acquisition, activation, retention, monetization, and referral. The strongest growth teams do not chase hacks; they build systems, use experimentation, and make the product easier to understand, adopt, and return to. Growth also includes how you hire, how you evaluate channels, how you model loops, and how you decide where to invest limited time and capital.

## Key Frameworks

### Growth Loops (Elena Verna)
- **When to use**: When designing sustainable growth systems beyond a linear funnel, especially when product actions can generate more users, engagement, or monetization.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify a user action that creates value beyond the initiating user.
  2. Map the reaction that action triggers in another user or system.
  3. Design the experience so that reaction leads to another valuable action.
  4. Measure whether the loop is self-reinforcing and can compound over time.
  5. Invest in optimizing both sides of the loop, not just the initial trigger.
- **Example**: At Dropbox, sharing files creates awareness and activation for recipients; a meaningful percentage of recipients sign up, making sharing responsible for over 50% of acquisition.

### Race Car Framework (Elena Verna)
- **When to use**: When planning a balanced growth strategy across short-term boosts, ongoing engines, and maintenance work.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the core engines that keep growth moving (e.g., PLG loop, content, referrals).
  2. Define the fuel required to power those engines (e.g., paid spend, content input).
  3. List turbo boosts that create temporary spikes (e.g., events, launches).
  4. Account for tune-ups and maintenance needed to keep systems performing.
  5. Balance investment across these categories instead of over-focusing on one.
- **Example**: Elena separates initiatives into engines, fuel, turbo boosts like big user conferences, and tune-ups or optimizations.

### Adjacent User Theory (Elena Verna, Bangaly Kaba)
- **When to use**: When growth from the core ICP is maturing and you want to expand by serving nearby user segments, or when new cohorts show worse activation/retention than early adopters.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the current core user and ICP clearly.
  2. Identify the next adjacent user already touching the product or workflow.
  3. Observe how that user behaves differently from the core user.
  4. Use the product from their perspective, ideally with a fresh account.
  5. Watch cohort curves and activation/retention for newer users.
  6. Adapt onboarding, activation, or use cases for those adjacent users.
- **Example**: At Instagram, users in new markets had different phones and lower tech familiarity, and conversion/retention degraded even without product changes. That signaled the product needed to fit the adjacent user better. (Bangaly Kaba)

### Understand, Identify, Execute (Bangaly Kaba)
- **When to use**: When growth is stalling and the team is tempted to ship features before understanding the root cause.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with first-principles understanding of what is actually happening.
  2. Reserve roadmap space for "understand work" — data analysis, research, instrumentation.
  3. Use data, research, funnel analysis, and technical investigation to de-risk the problem.
  4. Identify the best solution opportunities based on what you learned.
  5. Execute on the highest-conviction bets.
  6. Feed learnings back into the next cycle.
- **Example**: At Instagram, the signup funnel initially only tracked starts and completions. The team first instrumented the full funnel to see where users dropped off before making major changes.

### Aha Moment Identification Framework (Hila Qu)
- **When to use**: When improving activation and trying to define the user milestone most predictive of long-term retention and conversion.
- **Steps**:
  1. Brainstorm candidate high-value actions that likely indicate users are experiencing value.
  2. Run correlation analysis on those actions against conversion and retention outcomes.
  3. Compare uplift versus baseline to identify the strongest candidate actions.
  4. If no single action dominates, combine actions into a composite activation metric.
  5. Run experiments to drive more users to those actions and validate causation, not just correlation.
- **Example**: At GitLab, the team identified "two users using two features in the first 14 days" as the activation metric because it captured both collaboration and platform breadth.

### Do > Show > Tell Onboarding Principle (Hila Qu)
- **When to use**: When designing onboarding and activation experiences, especially for products with complex setup.
- **Steps**:
  1. Let users perform the core action directly whenever possible.
  2. If direct action is hard, show them a realistic sample or template experience.
  3. Use explanation only as a fallback, not the primary onboarding method.
  4. Support onboarding with lifecycle emails that bring users back to complete key actions.
- **Example**: Hila advised a company to provide sample videos or sample actions so users could experience value before doing the full setup themselves.

### Growth Competency Model (Adam Fishman)
- **When to use**: When hiring, evaluating, coaching, or structuring a growth team.
- **Steps**:
  1. Assess the team's current gaps instead of searching for a mythical all-in-one growth unicorn.
  2. Evaluate candidates across four competency areas: growth execution, customer knowledge, growth strategy, and communication and influence.
  3. Within growth execution, assess channel fluency, experimentation, and ability to productize learnings.
  4. Within customer knowledge, assess data fluency/instrumentation, user psychology, and ability to learn from customers over time.
  5. Within growth strategy, assess growth loop modeling, capital allocation and forecasting, and prioritization/roadmapping.
  6. Within communication and influence, assess strategic communication, team leadership, and stakeholder management.
  7. Use the model both forward-looking for hiring and backward-looking for performance feedback and development.
- **Example**: At Wyzant, founders wanted a "silver bullet" growth answer, but the real need was a system for adding and executing growth loops.

### Onboarding as Promise Delivery (Adam Fishman)
- **When to use**: When designing or improving onboarding, activation, and early retention.
- **Steps**:
  1. Treat onboarding as the first moment where the product must deliver on the brand promise made in the market.
  2. Recognize that onboarding is touched by 100% of users, making it the highest-leverage universal product surface.
  3. Use the high motivation of new users to learn about them and qualify them appropriately.
  4. Optimize onboarding not just for conversion, but for habit formation and retention.
  5. Revisit onboarding when you gain genuinely new customer or product insights, not just for cosmetic redesigns.
- **Example**: At Patreon, onboarding improvements and human-guided interventions improved creators' first- and second-month revenue by 25%.

### Emerging Acquisition Channel Evaluation Framework (Adam Grenier)
- **When to use**: When deciding whether to invest in a new or emerging acquisition channel.
- **Steps**:
  1. Assess overlap between customer needs, company goals, and the channel's core strengths.
  2. Analyze the channel DNA: lifecycle stage, risk, product instability.
  3. Understand the channel's monetization strategy and whether your company can align with it.
  4. Evaluate your own company DNA: risk tolerance, staffing capacity, and whether core channels already work.
  5. Scope the test proportionally: start small, define the signal you want, avoid overcommitting.
- **Example**: Spotify exploring Clubhouse fit better than Paparazzi because the channel format aligned with Spotify's goals and user behavior.

## Decision Guide
- If growth is stalling and the team is shipping but not learning, start with **Understand, Identify, Execute** because root-cause analysis raises future win rate — per **Bangaly Kaba**.
- If new cohorts retain worse than early adopters, use **Adjacent User Theory** because the next wave of users likely needs a different experience — per **Bangaly Kaba** and **Elena Verna**.
- If you need to design sustainable compounding growth, use **Growth Loops** because they replace linear funnels with self-reinforcing systems — per **Elena Verna**.
- If growth investment feels unbalanced, use the **Race Car Framework** to audit engines, fuel, turbo boosts, and tune-ups — per **Elena Verna**.
- If activation is vague, use the **Aha Moment Identification Framework** because a concrete activation metric gives the team a goalpost — per **Hila Qu**.
- If onboarding feels confusing, use **Do > Show > Tell** because users learn faster by doing than by reading — per **Hila Qu**.
- If onboarding is weak, consider **Onboarding as Promise Delivery** because every user touches it and it shapes activation and retention — per **Adam Fishman**.
- If you need to hire your first growth leader, use the **Growth Competency Model** because it forces a gap analysis instead of a vague search for a unicorn — per **Adam Fishman**.
- If you are evaluating a hot new channel, use the **Emerging Acquisition Channel Evaluation Framework** because trendiness is not enough; fit and channel DNA matter — per **Adam Grenier**.
- If growth slows in a changing market, first revalidate PMF before chasing channels because the real issue may be customer fit, not acquisition — per **Adam Grenier**.
- If your product depends on algorithms or ML, use **Human-in-the-loop algorithm design** because humans must own judgment, intent, and long-term tradeoffs — per **Adriel Frederick**.
- If one growth engine is working well, start testing new loops every 18 months because growth engines saturate — per **Elena Verna**.
- If overdependent on SEO/SEM/platforms, build earned channels like referrals and sharing loops because rented channels are fragile — per **Elena Verna**.

## Expert Consensus
- Growth is about connecting users to value, not just increasing traffic or conversion.
- Activation and retention are often higher-leverage than top-of-funnel acquisition.
- Strong growth teams combine experimentation with deep customer understanding.
- Onboarding is one of the highest-leverage growth surfaces because it shapes activation, retention, and qualification.
- Growth loops compound; linear funnels do not.
- Teams should reserve time for understanding work before execution.
- New channels should be evaluated for fit, durability, and company readiness, not hype.
- Adjacent users often need different onboarding and activation paths than core users.

## Points of Debate
- **Ship more experiments vs. understand first** (Bangaly Kaba vs. conventional):
  - **View A**: Faster testing means faster growth.
  - **View B**: Teams should reserve time for root-cause analysis or they will keep optimizing the wrong thing.
  - **Reasoning**: Understanding raises win rate; volume without learning creates activity, not signal.

- **Top-of-funnel acquisition vs. activation/retention focus** (Bangaly Kaba, Elena Verna):
  - **View A**: Grow by acquiring more users.
  - **View B**: Grow by getting more existing users to activate and retain.
  - **Reasoning**: Acquisition without retention is a leaky bucket; activation often unlocks more growth than more traffic.

- **Conversion-first onboarding vs. retention-first onboarding** (Adam Fishman, Hila Qu):
  - **View A**: Reduce friction and maximize completion.
  - **View B**: Introduce the right friction if it improves user quality and long-term retention.
  - **Reasoning**: The second view treats onboarding as qualification and habit formation, not just signup completion.

- **A/B test everything vs. use judgment** (Elena Verna):
  - **View A**: Rigorous testing on every change.
  - **View B**: Many decisions should be made with judgment and evaluated with pre/post analysis.
  - **Reasoning**: Testing everything can be paralyzing; speed matters, especially in fast-moving markets like AI.

- **Senior unicorn vs. balanced team** (Adam Fishman):
  - **View A**: Hire a highly experienced growth leader who has done it before.
  - **View B**: Hire a strong builder with execution and customer knowledge, then fill gaps around them.
  - **Reasoning**: Early teams need adaptability and foundational skill more than expensive seniority.

- **Hot channel adoption vs. channel-fit discipline** (Adam Grenier):
  - **View A**: Be early on every promising new channel.
  - **View B**: Only invest when the channel's strengths match your customer, goals, and operating model.
  - **Reasoning**: Trendiness is a weak predictor of ROI.
