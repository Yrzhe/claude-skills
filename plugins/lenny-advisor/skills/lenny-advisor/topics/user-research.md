# User Research

## Overview
User research is the practice of learning directly from users to make better product, growth, and business decisions. It matters because data shows what is happening, but research explains why, who is affected, and what to do next. The best teams use research to reduce risk, uncover hidden friction, and validate strategy before they invest heavily.

## Key Frameworks

### Curiosity Loops (Ada Chen Rekhi)
- **When to use**: When you need better input for an important decision and want contextual advice rather than generic opinions.
- **Steps**:
  1. Form a good question that is specific, unbiased, and asks for rationale.
  2. Choose the right people: a mix of subject matter experts and people who know you well.
  3. Make the ask lightweight so it is easy to respond to quickly.
  4. Collect and process the responses, looking for surprises, disagreements, and missing angles.
  5. Close the loop by thanking people and sharing how their input affected your decision.
- **Example**: Ada emailed 10-11 people with nine possible podcast topics and asked them to pick the top two or three and explain why. In about 20 minutes, she got rich input that shaped the conversation.

### Values Exercise / Inner Scorecard (Ada Chen Rekhi)
- **When to use**: When making career or life decisions that may be distorted by status, money, titles, or others' expectations.
- **Steps**:
  1. Review a list of possible values and select the ones that resonate.
  2. Group and narrow them down into a smaller set.
  3. Stack-rank the most important values for your current season of life.
  4. Translate them into 3-5 sentences that define your personal scorecard.
  5. Use that scorecard to evaluate decisions and compare them against external pressures.
- **Example**: Ada used this to evaluate a high-profile job that looked great on paper but failed her top values because of travel and hours, so she turned it down.

### Explore and Exploit (Ada Chen Rekhi)
- **When to use**: When planning your early career or deciding whether to broaden your experiences versus go deeper in a proven path.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start in exploration mode early in your career to test different environments, functions, and industries.
  2. Treat each move as a hypothesis-driven experiment about what you like and what fits.
  3. Extract explicit learnings from each role about your strengths, preferences, and gaps.
  4. Switch into exploit mode once you find a rich area worth going deeper on.
  5. Use exploit mode intentionally to gain specific capabilities, not just titles.
- **Example**: Ada explored corporate life, startup marketing, and founding. After acquisition, she used LinkedIn to exploit specific learning goals like growth, subscriptions, and pricing.

### Eating Your Vegetables (Ada Chen Rekhi)
- **When to use**: When you dislike a skill or activity and need to determine whether you truly dislike it or are just inexperienced and bad at it.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify a skill that matters for your goals but feels uncomfortable.
  2. Commit to repeated exposure rather than judging it after one attempt.
  3. Create a concrete rule or ritual that forces practice.
  4. Repeat enough times to separate beginner discomfort from genuine dislike.
  5. Reflect on whether the skill becomes easier, more useful, or more enjoyable over time.
- **Example**: Ada forced herself to attend one networking event per week and hand out 10 business cards. Over time, she got much better and built durable relationships.

### Growth Competency Model (Adam Fishman)
- **When to use**: Use when hiring, evaluating, coaching, or structuring a growth team, especially when founders are unclear what kind of growth leader they need.
- **Steps**:
  1. Assess the team’s current gaps instead of searching for a mythical all-in-one growth unicorn.
  2. Evaluate candidates across four competency areas: growth execution, customer knowledge, growth strategy, and communication and influence.
  3. Within growth execution, assess channel fluency, experimentation, and ability to productize learnings.
  4. Within customer knowledge, assess data fluency/instrumentation, user psychology, and ability to learn from customers over time.
  5. Within growth strategy, assess growth loop modeling, capital allocation and forecasting, and prioritization/roadmapping.
  6. Within communication and influence, assess strategic communication, team leadership, and stakeholder management.
  7. Use the model both forward-looking for hiring and backward-looking for performance feedback and development.
- **Example**: Fishman argued that teams often want a “silver bullet” growth answer when they really need a system for adding growth loops and executing them.

### Onboarding as Promise Delivery (Adam Fishman)
- **When to use**: Use when designing or improving onboarding, activation, and early retention.
- **Steps**:
  1. Treat onboarding as the first moment where the product must deliver on the brand promise made in the market.
  2. Recognize that onboarding is touched by 100% of users, making it the highest-leverage universal product surface.
  3. Use the high motivation of new users to learn about them and qualify them appropriately.
  4. Optimize onboarding not just for conversion, but for habit formation and retention.
  5. Revisit onboarding when you gain genuinely new customer or product insights, not just for cosmetic redesigns.
- **Example**: At Patreon, onboarding improvements and human-guided interventions improved creators’ first- and second-month revenue by 25%.

### Human Intervention to Productized Defaults (Adam Fishman)
- **When to use**: Use when a manual onboarding or sales assist process is producing strong results and you want to scale it.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify high-potential users during onboarding using observable signals.
  2. Route those users to humans who can guide them to successful setup and behavior.
  3. Study what the humans consistently do that improves outcomes.
  4. Translate those successful behaviors into product defaults, recommendations, and guardrails.
  5. Preserve user choice, but make the right path easier and the wrong path harder.
- **Example**: At Patreon, high-potential creators were routed to humans, and the learnings were later productized into opinionated defaults like recommended tier counts and pricing structures.

### Marginal User Analysis (Adriel Frederick)
- **When to use**: When trying to improve activation, signup conversion, onboarding, or growth in products with large user funnels.
- **Steps**:
  1. Find the user segment that is just on the cusp of succeeding but currently struggles to convert.
  2. Also inspect the worst-case user experience to reveal the full set of product failures.
  3. Observe real users directly instead of relying only on funnel data.
  4. List all friction points in their experience, including issues not visible in the funnel.
  5. Prioritize the barriers that are both important and realistically fixable.
  6. Iterate on making the product “stupid easy” for that user to succeed.
- **Example**: At Facebook, Adriel studied users in countries with high traffic but poor conversion and found issues like wrong language defaults, phone formatting problems, and latency.

## Decision Guide
- If you need contextual advice for a high-stakes choice, consider **Curiosity Loops** because it surfaces blind spots and disagreements — per **Ada Chen Rekhi**.
- If a job looks impressive but feels off, use **Values Exercise / Inner Scorecard** because it filters out status bias — per **Ada Chen Rekhi**.
- If you are early in your career and unsure what path fits, use **Explore and Exploit** because exploration is the fastest way to learn your preferences — per **Ada Chen Rekhi**.
- If a skill feels unpleasant and you are tempted to quit immediately, use **Eating Your Vegetables** because discomfort may just mean you are a beginner — per **Ada Chen Rekhi**.
- If you are hiring or defining a growth role, use **Growth Competency Model** because it prevents vague “unicorn” hiring — per **Adam Fishman**.
- If you need a first growth hire, use **Hire for Team Balance, Not a Unicorn** because the best hire depends on the team’s missing capabilities — per **Adam Fishman**.
- If you are deciding between a generalist and a specialist growth hire, use **Painter vs. Architect vs. Surgeon** because early teams usually need builders before surgeons — per **Adam Fishman**.
- If onboarding is weak, use **Onboarding as Promise Delivery** because onboarding is the universal surface that shapes activation and retention — per **Adam Fishman**.
- If manual onboarding works well, use **Human Intervention to Productized Defaults** because human behavior can reveal scalable product patterns — per **Adam Fishman**.
- If long-term retention is hard to measure quickly, use **Proxy Metrics for Onboarding Experiments** because early indicators let you move faster without losing sight of durable outcomes — per **Adam Fishman**.
- If funnel data shows drop-off but not the cause, use **Marginal User Analysis** because direct observation reveals hidden friction — per **Adriel Frederick**.
- If growth feels stuck, focus on **Core Growth Loop Simplification** because durable growth comes from making the core actions easier, not from hacks — per **Adriel Frederick**.
- If your product depends on ML or ranking systems, use **Human-in-the-loop algorithm design** because humans must define intent, constraints, and tradeoffs — per **Adriel Frederick**.
- If you are building an innovation team inside a larger company, use **R&D team integration framework** because the team must feel core to the mission, not separate from it — per **Adriel Frederick**.

## Expert Consensus
- User research is most valuable when it informs a real decision, not when it is done as a ritual.
- Direct user observation is often more revealing than dashboards alone.
- The best research is specific, lightweight, and tied to a clear hypothesis.
- Research should uncover both friction and fit: who the product is for, what they need, and what blocks success.
- Good teams close the loop by turning insights into product changes, not just notes.

## Points of Debate
- **Advice vs. input** (Ada Chen Rekhi): Some people think you should follow expert advice directly; Ada argues advice should be treated as input, because the value is in context, surprises, and blind spots.
- **Coach vs. broader support** (Ada Chen Rekhi): Conventional wisdom says career growth requires a coach; Ada argues most people get more value from mentors, courses, communities, or structured advice gathering.
- **Conversion vs. retention in onboarding** (Adam Fishman): One view is to maximize onboarding completion; the other is to accept lower conversion if it improves user quality and long-term retention.
- **Manual vs. scalable** (Adam Fishman): Some teams avoid manual intervention because it does not scale; Fishman argues manual help is often the fastest way to learn what should be productized.
- **Data-led vs. user-led growth** (Adriel Frederick): Some teams trust funnels to tell them what to build; Adriel argues data is only a clue and must be paired with direct user research.

## Key Metrics & Benchmarks
- **Patreon onboarding**: first- and second-month revenue improved by **25%** after onboarding and human-guided interventions.
- **Airbnb supply growth**: homes scaled from about **100,000 in 2012 to over 6 million today**.
- **Airbnb market liquidity**: **300 listings with 100 reviewed listings** was a key threshold for growth to take off in a market.
- **Airbnb trust threshold**: when hosts had **more than 10 reviews**, everything changed; with **less than 3 reviews**, nothing changed.
- **CMX community benchmark**: **86%** of companies said community is critical to their mission; **69%** planned to increase investment.
- **First Round survey**: **80%** of startups were investing in community; **28%** said community was their moat.
- **Salesforce community support**: **83%** of customer questions were answered by other customers.
- **Duolingo community scale**: **2,600 events per month** with a community team of **3 people**.
