# Positioning

## Overview
Positioning is the work of making your product easy to understand, easy to choose, and hard to confuse with alternatives. It matters because weak positioning creates slow sales cycles, bad-fit customers, churn, and internal misalignment even when the product is good. Strong positioning clarifies who the product is for, what it replaces, why it matters now, and why your approach is the best fit.

## Key Frameworks

### Strategic Narrative (by Andy Raskin)
- **When to use**: When you need a unifying story that explains why the product matters, creates urgency, differentiates beyond features, and scales across sales, marketing, product, recruiting, and fundraising.
- **Steps**:
  1. Name the shift from the old game to the new game in concise, memorable terms.
  2. Name the stakes by showing why the shift matters now.
  3. Name the object of the new game: the buyer mission or rallying cry.
  4. Show why winning the new game is hard by identifying obstacles.
  5. Explain how your product helps overcome those obstacles, with proof.
- **Example**: Zuora framed the shift as moving from transactions to the subscription economy, showed that winners were already adopting subscriptions, defined the goal as turning customers into subscribers, outlined obstacles like lifetime value measurement, and positioned Zuora as the system that makes it possible.

### Old Game to New Game Framing (by Andy Raskin)
- **When to use**: When your pitch sounds like feature comparison or problem-solution selling and you need movement and urgency.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the old worldview buyers still operate in.
  2. Define the new worldview that is emerging.
  3. Give both worlds short, sticky names.
  4. Use those names consistently across the company.
- **Example**: Salesforce framed the market as software versus cloud, not just a better CRM than Siebel.

### The 5-Part Positioning Framework (by April Dunford)
- **When to use**: When a product is underperforming, the market is confused, teams are misaligned, or you need sharper differentiation.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define competitive alternatives, including the status quo.
  2. List differentiated capabilities.
  3. Translate capabilities into value themes.
  4. Identify best-fit customers.
  5. Choose the market category that makes the value obvious.
- **Example**: Help Scout’s alternatives included Zendesk, other support tools, and shared inboxes; its differentiated value was more human, relationship-oriented support for DTC and e-commerce brands.

### Sales Narrative Mapping from Positioning (by April Dunford)
- **When to use**: After positioning is defined and you need a repeatable story for sales, marketing, product, and leadership.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start from the customer’s worldview.
  2. Explain why the problem matters now.
  3. Show how alternatives frame the problem incorrectly.
  4. Introduce your differentiated value in that context.
  5. Equip the buyer to justify the choice internally.
- **Example**: Help Scout’s narrative starts with customer service as a growth driver, then contrasts that with tools that treat support as a cost center.

### Positioning Diagnosis via Sales Calls (by April Dunford)
- **When to use**: When you suspect weak positioning but do not have a single metric to prove it.
- **Steps**:
  1. Listen to early sales calls.
  2. Look for confusion signals.
  3. Watch for wrong-category comparisons.
  4. Notice value skepticism.
  5. Use the pattern to diagnose understanding, category, or value problems.
- **Example**: Prospects saying “back up to the beginning,” “you’re just like Salesforce,” or “I can do that in a spreadsheet” are strong positioning signals.

### Product Positioning Statement Framework (by Arielle Jackson)
- **When to use**: When a startup needs a clear shared explanation of what it does, who it is for, and why it is different.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the target audience for the next 18 months.
  2. Identify the audience’s problem or unmet need.
  3. Describe how they solve it today.
  4. Clarify what you make and how it works.
  5. Draft: “For [target audience] who [need], [product] is a [category] that [benefit], unlike [alternative], it [differentiator].”
  6. Pressure-test the language with real customer speech.
- **Example**: Square Stand: “turn your iPad into a point of sale,” with the foil being the ugly cash register and legacy POS.

### Concentric Circles Audience Framework (by Arielle Jackson)
- **When to use**: When founders are too broad and need to define who they will actively target first.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with TAM.
  2. Narrow through progressively smaller circles.
  3. Define the target audience for the next 18 months.
  4. Create a vivid model persona.
  5. Keep each narrower circle inside the broader one.
- **Example**: Eero’s target audience was “tech-savvy dads,” with a detailed suburban dad persona.

### Category Creation Decision Framework (by Barbra Gago)
- **When to use**: When deciding whether to create a new category or compete in an existing one.
- **Steps**:
  1. Assess market size and ambition.
  2. Check whether buyers already have budget for the problem.
  3. Listen to customer language.
  4. Evaluate differentiation from existing categories.
  5. Consider whether multiple companies can occupy the category.
  6. Fall back to the existing category if new language does not stick.
- **Example**: Miro moved beyond “online whiteboard” to “visual collaboration”; Greenhouse abandoned “recruiting optimization platform” because buyers still budgeted for an ATS.

## Decision Guide
- If your pitch sounds like feature bragging, consider **Strategic Narrative** because it reframes the story around a market shift rather than a product checklist — per **Andy Raskin**.
- If buyers are comparing you to incumbents on the wrong terms, use **Old Game to New Game Framing** because it changes the frame of reference — per **Andy Raskin**.
- If prospects are slow to act, raise the stakes with **Strategic Narrative** because urgency comes from change, winners, and downside risk — per **Andy Raskin**.
- If your team cannot agree on what the product is, use **The 5-Part Positioning Framework** because it forces alignment on alternatives, value, customers, and category — per **April Dunford**.
- If sales calls reveal confusion, wrong comparisons, or “spreadsheet” objections, run a **Positioning Diagnosis via Sales Calls** because the problem is often framing, not product quality — per **April Dunford**.
- If you are early and still learning the market, treat positioning as a **thesis, not truth** because over-tightening too soon can lock in bad assumptions — per **April Dunford**.
- If you need a repeatable sales story after defining positioning, use **Sales Narrative Mapping** because sales needs a story they can actually tell — per **April Dunford**.
- If you are defining who to target in B2B, use **Concentric Circles Audience Framework** because company-level fit matters more than broad persona lists — per **Arielle Jackson**.
- If your messaging sounds like jargon, use the **Bar Test** because positioning should sound like something customers would actually say — per **Arielle Jackson**.
- If you are naming a startup or product, do **positioning first** because names work best when grounded in what the company means — per **Arielle Jackson**.
- If you are deciding whether to invent a new category, use **Category Creation Decision Framework** because category language only matters if buyers adopt it and budget for it — per **Barbra Gago**.
- If your product is in a crowded market, start with **competitive alternatives** rather than customer benefits because differentiation only matters relative to what you beat — per **April Dunford**.

## Expert Consensus
- Positioning is not just messaging; it shapes sales, product, marketing, hiring, and fundraising.
- The strongest positioning starts with the customer’s reality, not internal product language.
- Differentiation must be tied to a real alternative the buyer would otherwise choose.
- The status quo is often the biggest competitor in B2B.
- Early-stage companies should learn from the market before locking positioning too tightly.

## Points of Debate
- **Story-first vs. category-first**
  - **Story-first** (Andy Raskin): the narrative of the market shift matters more than the category label.
  - **Category-first** (some conventional GTM thinking): category naming is the main lever for differentiation.
- **Tight positioning early vs. loose positioning early**
  - **Tight**: clear positioning improves focus and consistency.
  - **Loose** (April Dunford): early startups should keep positioning broad enough to let the market reveal the real segment.
- **Broad persona mapping vs. champion-first focus**
  - **Broad**: map every stakeholder in the buying committee.
  - **Champion-first** (April Dunford): optimize for the champion who drives the shortlist and internal selling.
- **New category vs. existing category**
  - **New category** (Barbra Gago): useful when the market is too small or the product is fundamentally different.
  - **Existing category**: better when buyers already budget for the problem and new language does not stick.
- **Brand as visuals vs. brand as meaning**
  - **Visuals-first**: brand is mainly logo, color, and design.
  - **Meaning-first** (Arielle Jackson): brand is what people think you are; visuals are only one expression of it.

## Key Metrics & Benchmarks
- **40% to 60%** of B2B purchase processes result in **“no decision”**.
- **Over 2 million** sales calls were analyzed in the research behind that finding.
- **Over 200** B2B technology companies have used an evolving version of April Dunford’s positioning framework.
- **36%** of Eventbrite creators first learned about Eventbrite through word of mouth.
- **34%** of Eventbrite creators first learned about Eventbrite by attending events produced by other Eventbrite creators.
- **Approximately 1%** of Square readers later became Square merchants.
- **11%** of Aalto buyers convert to sellers.
- Eventbrite’s demand-to-supply conversion rate **more than tripled** through sustained iteration.
- Pinterest reached **over 1 million users** about a year after shifting early adoption to female bloggers.
- Discord’s initial wedge targeted a niche with only **4 million registered players**.
