# Pricing

## Overview
Pricing is not just a monetization lever; it is a product and strategy decision that shapes adoption, retention, and business model quality. The strongest pricing work ties price to customer value, buyer behavior, and long-term outcomes rather than to vanity metrics or simple usage counts. In AI and software businesses, pricing also needs to reflect who gets value, who pays, and how much human judgment is still required.

## Key Frameworks

### Outcome-Based Pricing for Agents (Bret Taylor)
- **When to use**: When your AI product autonomously completes a measurable business task.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the business outcome your product can complete without human intervention.
  2. Define a measurable success event tied to customer value.
  3. Price based on that outcome rather than seats, tokens, or generic usage.
  4. Align internal operations so you only win when the customer gets the result.
- **Example**: At Sierra, if an AI agent resolves a customer issue without requiring a human call-center agent, Sierra charges based on that successful resolution.

### Interrogate the Story You're Telling Yourself (Bret Taylor)
- **When to use**: When a customer says price is the issue, but the real problem may be elsewhere.
- **Steps**:
  1. Write down the explanation you currently believe.
  2. Assume your first explanation may be biased toward your own skill set.
  3. Test alternative explanations with partners from other functions.
  4. Seek intellectually honest input from people close to the problem and outside advisors.
  5. Orient strategy only after pressure-testing the root cause.
- **Example**: A customer may say the product is “too expensive,” when the deeper issue is lack of differentiation.

### Choose GTM Based on Buyer-User Dynamics (Bret Taylor)
- **When to use**: When deciding how to sell a software or AI product.
- **Steps**:
  1. Map who uses the product and who buys it.
  2. If engineers can independently adopt it, consider developer-led growth.
  3. If the user and buyer are the same person, consider product-led growth.
  4. If the buyer is a line-of-business leader or procurement function, use direct sales.
  5. Validate the purchasing and evaluation process before committing to a motion.
- **Example**: Products sold to finance teams often fail as pure PLG because the end user is not the budget holder.

### Optimize for Absolute Outcomes, Not Local Conversion Rates (Archie Abrams)
- **When to use**: When multiple teams own different funnel stages and local KPI optimization creates perverse incentives.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define success as the total number of users who reach a meaningful outcome.
  2. Check whether improving a local rate is being achieved by making the previous step harder.
  3. Encourage teams to reduce friction earlier in the funnel.
  4. Evaluate whether more people ultimately reach activation or value creation in absolute terms.
- **Example**: A team owning signup-to-activation can improve conversion by making signup harder; Shopify instead focuses on total merchants activated.

### Long-Term Cohort Value Experimentation (Archie Abrams)
- **When to use**: When short-term conversion may not reflect true business impact.
- **Steps**:
  1. Run an experiment and evaluate the short-term result quickly.
  2. Preserve cohort assignment for later follow-up.
  3. Track downstream outcomes over 3, 6, 9, and 12+ months.
  4. Measure success on long-term cohort value, not local funnel lift alone.
  5. Recalibrate which short-term metrics are predictive.
- **Example**: Shopify keeps experiment cohorts intact and later finds that 30-40% of apparently positive short-term tests show no long-term GMV lift.

### Monetary Friction Reduction (Archie Abrams)
- **When to use**: When early users face financial barriers that block trial, persistence, or value realization.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify barriers such as trial length, upfront price, failed payments, or missing credits.
  2. Test trials, incentives, credits, or pricing changes.
  3. Measure whether reduced friction expands access to high-potential users.
  4. Validate impact over the long term.
- **Example**: Lowering early monetary friction at Shopify can give entrepreneurs more runway to make their business work.

### Hybrid Self-Serve Plus Sales Journey Design (Archie Abrams)
- **When to use**: When a company has both product-led and sales-led motions.
- **Steps**:
  1. Avoid forcing users to choose upfront between self-serve and sales.
  2. Create pathways from self-serve into sales and back again.
  3. Rebuild attribution and LTV models for mixed journeys.
  4. Use incrementality testing to understand true channel impact.
- **Example**: A merchant may start self-serve, then talk to sales, requiring new attribution logic.

### Human-in-the-Loop Algorithm Design (Adriel Frederick)
- **When to use**: When pricing depends on algorithmic systems, optimization, or ranking.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define product intent and strategic goals before optimizing.
  2. Decide what the algorithm should decide versus what humans should decide.
  3. Identify judgment calls algorithms cannot reliably infer.
  4. Design interfaces that give humans the right information.
  5. Use the algorithm to amplify human intent, not replace it.
  6. Continuously refine the split between people and machines.
- **Example**: In mobility pricing, local teams may need to respond to snowstorms, fuel changes, taxes, or competitor moves.

## Decision Guide
- If a customer says “too expensive,” consider **Interrogate the Story You're Telling Yourself** because price may be a symptom of weak differentiation — per **Bret Taylor**.
- If your AI product completes a clear business task end to end, consider **Outcome-Based Pricing for Agents** because value is easiest to align to the result — per **Bret Taylor**.
- If the buyer and user are different people, consider **Choose GTM Based on Buyer-User Dynamics** because pricing and packaging must match the purchase path — per **Bret Taylor**.
- If your funnel team is optimizing a local conversion rate, consider **Optimize for Absolute Outcomes, Not Local Conversion Rates** because rate gains can hide lower total throughput — per **Archie Abrams**.
- If short-term tests look good but business value is unclear, consider **Long-Term Cohort Value Experimentation** because many wins disappear over time — per **Archie Abrams**.
- If early users are price-sensitive but high-potential, consider **Monetary Friction Reduction** because lower upfront friction can unlock runway and success — per **Archie Abrams**.
- If your product has both self-serve and sales motions, consider **Hybrid Self-Serve Plus Sales Journey Design** because forcing a single path can reduce conversion — per **Archie Abrams**.
- If pricing is driven by dynamic market conditions, consider **Human-in-the-Loop Algorithm Design** because humans need to handle context algorithms miss — per **Adriel Frederick**.
- If you are tempted to optimize only for short-term conversion, consider **Long-Term Cohort Value Experimentation** because durable revenue matters more than immediate lift — per **Archie Abrams**.
- If your pricing model is based on seats or tokens but the product autonomously delivers outcomes, consider **Outcome-Based Pricing for Agents** because it better reflects customer value — per **Bret Taylor**.
- If you are changing signup or onboarding pricing friction, consider **Monetary Friction Reduction** because early commitment barriers often suppress activation — per **Archie Abrams**.
- If your pricing decision is really a product positioning decision, consider **Interrogate the Story You're Telling Yourself** because the real issue may be product-market fit, not price — per **Bret Taylor**.

## Expert Consensus
- Pricing should be tied to customer value, not just internal convenience.
- Short-term conversion metrics are often misleading without long-term follow-up.
- The buyer, user, and value recipient must be mapped before choosing a pricing model.
- Reducing friction can improve outcomes, but only if it unlocks real customer success.
- Pricing decisions should be tested with real behavior, not just opinions.

## Points of Debate
- **Seats/usage vs outcomes** (Bret Taylor vs conventional SaaS thinking):  
  - **Outcome pricing**: Best when software completes a measurable job and can align incentives directly.  
  - **Seats/usage pricing**: Simpler to implement and still useful when outcomes are hard to measure or shared across many users.
- **Conversion rate vs absolute outcomes** (Archie Abrams vs common funnel management):  
  - **Absolute outcomes**: Better because they prevent teams from gaming local rates.  
  - **Conversion rates**: Still useful for diagnosing friction and comparing steps, but can mislead if used as the primary goal.
- **Short-term wins vs long-term cohort value** (Archie Abrams vs fast experimentation culture):  
  - **Long-term value**: Necessary because many apparent wins fade or pull forward demand.  
  - **Short-term metrics**: Useful for speed, but insufficient as the final decision criterion.
- **Human judgment vs algorithmic optimization** (Adriel Frederick vs “let the model decide”):  
  - **Human-in-the-loop**: Better for strategic, contextual, or high-stakes pricing decisions.  
  - **Fully automated optimization**: Useful for scale, but risky when the system cannot see market context or intent.

## Key Metrics & Benchmarks
- Median time from idea to feeling PMF across 24 top B2B startups: **~2 years**.
- Time from working product to feeling PMF: **9-18 months**.
- Most companies shipped an alpha product in **1-3 months**.
- Shopify experiment follow-up: **30-40%** of experiments that look positive short term show **no long-term GMV lift**.
- Notable PMF outliers: **Figma, Airtable, and Slack took 4+ years** to find PMF.
- Early traction examples:
  - **Gong**: 12 design partners, 11 bought after beta.
  - **Zip and Persona**: PMF after first 10 live, successful customers.
  - **Amplitude**: zero to $1M ARR in under 9 months.
  - **Datadog**: $1.5M revenue in 2015, $10M in 2016, projected $30M in 2017.
