# Strategy

## Overview
Strategy is about making better choices under uncertainty: what to do, what to ignore, and how to sequence decisions over time. In Lenny’s conversations, the strongest strategy advice is usually practical and decision-oriented: use structured input, define the real problem, evaluate tradeoffs explicitly, and avoid optimizing for vanity metrics or hype.

## Key Frameworks

### Curiosity Loops (Ada Chen Rekhi)
- **When to use**: When you need better input for an important decision and want contextual advice rather than generic opinions.
- **Steps**:
  1. Form a good question that is specific, unbiased, and asks for rationale.
  2. Choose the right people: a mix of subject matter experts and people who know you well.
  3. Make the ask lightweight so it is easy to respond to quickly.
  4. Collect and process the responses, looking for surprises, disagreements, and missing angles.
  5. Close the loop by thanking people and sharing how their input affected your decision.
- **Example**: Ada emailed 10–11 people with nine possible podcast topics and asked them to pick the top two or three and explain why. In about 20 minutes, she got rich input that shaped the conversation.

### Values Exercise / Inner Scorecard (Ada Chen Rekhi)
- **When to use**: When making career or life decisions that may be distorted by status, money, titles, or others’ expectations.
- **Steps**:
  1. Review a list of possible values and select the ones that resonate.
  2. Group and narrow them down into a smaller set.
  3. Stack-rank the most important values for your current season of life.
  4. Translate them into 3–5 sentences that define your personal scorecard.
  5. Use that scorecard to evaluate decisions against external pressure.
- **Example**: Ada used this to reject a high-profile job that looked great on paper but failed her top values because of travel and grueling hours.

### Explore and Exploit (Ada Chen Rekhi)
- **When to use**: When planning your early career or deciding whether to broaden your experiences versus go deeper in a proven path.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start in exploration mode early in your career to test different environments, functions, and industries.
  2. Treat each move as a hypothesis-driven experiment.
  3. Extract explicit learnings from each role about strengths, preferences, and gaps.
  4. Switch into exploit mode once you find a rich area worth going deeper on.
  5. Use exploit mode intentionally to gain specific capabilities, not just titles.
- **Example**: Ada explored Microsoft, Mochi Media, and founding a company, then used LinkedIn to deliberately learn growth, subscriptions, and pricing.

### Growth Competency Model (Adam Fishman)
- **When to use**: When hiring, evaluating, coaching, or structuring a growth team.
- **Steps**:
  1. Assess the team’s current gaps instead of searching for a mythical all-in-one growth unicorn.
  2. Evaluate candidates across four areas: growth execution, customer knowledge, growth strategy, and communication/influence.
  3. Within execution, assess channel fluency, experimentation, and productizing learnings.
  4. Within customer knowledge, assess data fluency, user psychology, and customer learning.
  5. Within strategy, assess growth loops, capital allocation/forecasting, and prioritization.
  6. Within communication/influence, assess strategic communication, leadership, and stakeholder management.
  7. Use it for both hiring and performance development.
- **Example**: Fishman argued Wyzant needed a system for growth loops and execution, not a “silver bullet” growth leader.

### Onboarding as Promise Delivery (Adam Fishman)
- **When to use**: When designing or improving onboarding, activation, and early retention.
- **Steps**:
  1. Treat onboarding as the first moment the product must deliver on the brand promise.
  2. Recognize onboarding is touched by 100% of users.
  3. Use new-user motivation to learn about and qualify users.
  4. Optimize for habit formation and retention, not just conversion.
  5. Revisit onboarding only when you have genuinely new insights.
- **Example**: At Patreon, onboarding improvements and human-guided interventions improved first- and second-month creator revenue by 25%.

### Emerging Acquisition Channel Evaluation Framework (Adam Grenier)
- **When to use**: When deciding whether to invest in a new or emerging acquisition channel like TikTok, Clubhouse, OTT, influencer marketing, or VR.
- **Steps**:
  1. Assess overlap between customer needs, company goals, and the channel’s strengths.
  2. Analyze channel DNA: lifecycle stage, instability, and likelihood of product/rule changes.
  3. Understand the channel’s monetization model and whether you can help it succeed.
  4. Evaluate your company DNA: risk tolerance, staffing, and whether core channels already work.
  5. Scope the test proportionally and define the signal you want.
- **Example**: Spotify exploring Clubhouse fit better than Paparazzi because the channel’s audio-first, discovery-oriented format matched Spotify’s goals.

### Human-in-the-loop Algorithm Design (Adriel Frederick)
- **When to use**: When building products that rely on ML, optimization, pricing, ranking, or other algorithmic systems.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define product intent and strategic goals first.
  2. Decide what the algorithm should do versus what humans should do.
  3. Identify judgment calls algorithms cannot reliably infer.
  4. Design interfaces that give humans the right information.
  5. Use the algorithm to amplify human intent at scale.
  6. Continuously observe outcomes and refine the split.
- **Example**: At Lyft, pricing needed humans in the loop because local teams could see snowstorms, fuel changes, taxes, and competitor moves that algorithms missed.

## Decision Guide
- If you need better input for a big decision, consider **Curiosity Loops** because it surfaces contextual advice and blind spots — per **Ada Chen Rekhi**.
- If a career move looks impressive but feels off, use the **Values Exercise / Inner Scorecard** because it filters out status bias — per **Ada Chen Rekhi**.
- If you are early in your career and unsure what fits, use **Explore and Exploit** because exploration is the best way to learn what you want and what you’re good at — per **Ada Chen Rekhi**.
- If you feel stuck in a role and are drifting, use **Don’t Be the Frog** because small misalignments compound into stagnation — per **Ada Chen Rekhi**.
- If you dislike a skill but it matters, use **Eating Your Vegetables** because repeated exposure separates beginner discomfort from true dislike — per **Ada Chen Rekhi**.
- If you are hiring a growth leader, use the **Growth Competency Model** because it prevents vague “unicorn” hiring — per **Adam Fishman**.
- If you are making the first growth hire, **Hire for Team Balance, Not a Unicorn** because the team usually needs foundational builders more than a famous specialist — per **Adam Fishman**.
- If you need to choose between broad builders and specialists, use **Painter vs. Architect vs. Surgeon** because stage and bottleneck should determine the hire — per **Adam Fishman**.
- If onboarding is weak, start with **Onboarding as Promise Delivery** because it is the highest-leverage universal product surface — per **Adam Fishman**.
- If manual onboarding or sales assist works well, use **Human Intervention to Productized Defaults** because humans can reveal what should become scalable product behavior — per **Adam Fishman**.
- If onboarding experiments take too long to measure, use **Proxy Metrics for Onboarding Experiments** because early indicators let you move faster without losing sight of retention — per **Adam Fishman**.
- If you are evaluating a job offer, use **PMF for Candidates** and **Reverse Diligence for Job Candidates** because you should diligence the company as seriously as it diligences you — per **Adam Fishman**.
- If a new acquisition channel looks exciting, use the **Emerging Acquisition Channel Evaluation Framework** because trendiness alone is a bad reason to invest — per **Adam Grenier**.
- If growth slows, first revalidate product-market fit before chasing channels because the market may have changed — per **Adam Grenier**.
- If you are building AI systems, use **Human-in-the-loop Algorithm Design** because autonomy should increase only after trust and evidence are earned — per **Adriel Frederick**.

## Expert Consensus
- Strategy should start with the problem, not the solution.
- Good decisions require explicit tradeoffs, not vague optimism.
- Early-stage teams should favor learning and adaptability over rigid specialization.
- Growth work should connect users to value faster, not just increase top-of-funnel volume.
- Human judgment still matters in systems that use data, algorithms, or AI.

## Points of Debate
- **Hire senior specialist vs. adaptable builder** (Adam Fishman):  
  - **Senior specialist**: useful when a narrow bottleneck is clearly identified, especially in areas like SEO or paid growth.  
  - **Adaptable builder**: better first hire because early teams need broad execution, customer understanding, and flexibility.
- **Optimize onboarding for conversion vs. retention** (Adam Fishman):  
  - **Conversion-first**: reduces friction and increases signup completion.  
  - **Retention-first**: may intentionally add friction to qualify users and improve downstream LTV.
- **Explore new channels vs. double down on core channels** (Adam Grenier):  
  - **Explore**: necessary when core growth is saturated or a new channel has strong fit.  
  - **Exploit core channels**: safer when fundamentals are not yet stable; shiny channels often distract from PMF issues.
- **Use a coach vs. use broader support** (Ada Chen Rekhi):  
  - **Coach**: helpful when trust and fit are strong.  
  - **Broader support**: mentors, communities, and structured advice gathering often solve the problem more efficiently.
