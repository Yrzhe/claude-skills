# Christina Wodtke

**Lecturer at Stanford; author, speaker, and consultant on product development and OKRs** | **Stanford University** | Expertise: OKRs, product-management, strategy, leadership

## Bio
Christina Wodtke is a Stanford lecturer and longtime product leader who has worked at LinkedIn, MySpace, Zynga, and Yahoo. She is best known for helping teams implement OKRs in a way that actually changes behavior, not just planning documents, and for her book *Radical Focus*.

Her perspective matters because she combines product strategy, team leadership, and operating cadence. She’s especially useful when a team needs to turn vague ambition into focused execution without drowning in process.

## Signature Frameworks

### Mission → Strategy → Quarterly OKRs → Weekly Actions
- **When to use**: When translating long-term purpose into concrete quarterly priorities and weekly execution.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define a mission or vision that can guide the next ~5 years.
  2. Create a strategy as a strongly held hypothesis about how to win.
  3. Turn the strategy into a quarterly objective.
  4. Define key results by asking, “How do we know we succeeded?”
  5. Each week, choose actions that move the team toward the outcome.
- **Example**: In a game company, Q1 is about figuring out what the game is and what users find interesting, Q2 is about prototypes and validation, Q3 is about building, and Q4 is about marketing and launch.

### The “How do we know?” Key Result Design Method
- **When to use**: When an objective feels inspiring but vague.
- **Steps**:
  1. Write an objective that describes the meaningful quarterly outcome.
  2. Ask repeatedly, “How do we know?”
  3. List possible signals: user testing, conversion, feasibility, revenue, reach, quality.
  4. Choose about three key results that triangulate success.
  5. Use the quarter to learn whether your measures were right.
- **Example**: For an interior design magazine, success might be measured through member conversion, weekly engagement like liking products, and qualitative panel feedback every two weeks.

### Three-Key-Result Triangulation
- **When to use**: When selecting a balanced set of key results for one objective.
- **Steps**:
  1. Include one hard quantitative metric.
  2. Include one quality or delight measure.
  3. Include one business-impact metric when relevant.
  4. Keep the set small.
- **Example**: For a launch, measure revenue, reach, and delight, with delight tracked via Metacritic, survey data, or NPS.

### Healthy OKR Cadence
- **When to use**: When building the operating rhythm around OKRs.
- **Steps**:
  1. On Mondays, commit to what you’ll do this week.
  2. Use a short weekly update: confidence, what you did, what you’ll do next.
  3. On Fridays, celebrate wins.
  4. At quarter-end, grade lightly and focus on learning.
  5. Set next quarter’s OKRs quickly.
- **Example**: Some CEOs saw meaningful improvement just by starting Friday celebrations before fully rolling out OKRs.

### Pilot OKRs with the Best Team
- **When to use**: When introducing OKRs for the first time or repairing a broken rollout.
- **Steps**:
  1. Choose the strongest multidisciplinary team.
  2. Give them guidance and let them experiment for a quarter.
  3. Have them identify where OKRs fit or clash with culture.
  4. Use their learnings as a template for two more teams.
  5. Expand gradually.
- **Example**: “We’re thinking about OKRs. Can you guys see if it works?” then check back after three months.

### 10-Minute Key Result Brainstorm
- **When to use**: When teams default to obvious or weak measures.
- **Steps**:
  1. Spend 10 full minutes brainstorming every possible measure.
  2. Expect obvious ideas first.
  3. Push through the awkward lull.
  4. Capture weird ideas later.
  5. Use those to find better measures.
- **Example**: The best measurement ideas often appear only after the obvious ones are exhausted.

### Fast Peer Review Instead of Hierarchical Approval
- **When to use**: When OKR approval cycles are slowing planning.
- **Steps**:
  1. Draft the team’s OKRs.
  2. Send them to three adjacent teams or stakeholders.
  3. Ask for feedback within 24 hours.
  4. Incorporate feasibility or alignment feedback.
  5. Skip heavy boss-driven approval chains.
- **Example**: A client process where teams get feedback from three relevant teams instead of waiting for formal managerial approval.

### Storytelling Structure for Product Leaders
- **When to use**: When presenting ideas or persuading stakeholders.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with a hook: mystery, secret, surprise, or success.
  2. Use the middle for the core message.
  3. End with success and celebration.
  4. Ask trusted listeners for feedback.
- **Example**: Many TED Talks work because they are stories with facts embedded inside.

## Core Advice
- **When you face a focus problem or too many competing priorities**: Do fewer OKRs and make one big rock unmistakably important for the quarter — because scattered effort creates shallow progress everywhere.
- **When you face pressure to use OKRs to fix a dysfunctional company**: Do foundational work first on strategy, empowered teams, and psychological safety — because OKRs amplify health; they don’t create it.
- **When you face vague quarterly goals**: Do the exercise of asking, “How do we know we succeeded?” — because it turns aspiration into measurable outcomes.
- **When you face fluffy or uninspiring objectives**: Do write objectives that are meaningful and motivating, not just task statements — because objectives should energize, not administrate.
- **When you face key results that are really task lists**: Do rewrite them as outcomes or evidence of progress — because tasks don’t show whether the business moved.
- **When you face boring OKR review meetings**: Do keep the meeting at the right altitude and discuss whether the strategy is working — because leaders should focus on decisions, not IC-level tracking.
- **When you face uncertainty about measuring quality, delight, or retention**: Do combine hard metrics with qualitative research and retention signals — because easy numbers can hide bad user experience.
- **When you face a first-time OKR rollout**: Do start with a small pilot on your best team — because strong teams reveal fit and create a reusable template.
- **When you face long planning cycles for quarterly OKRs**: Do optimize for speed and keep planning short — because over-planning steals time from shipping.
- **When you face weak weekly execution against quarterly goals**: Do establish Monday commitments and Friday celebrations — because rituals create accountability and momentum.
- **When you face status updates that feel useless**: Do use a short weekly format: confidence, what you did, what you’ll do next — because it surfaces blockers and is easy to scan.
- **When you face debates about whether a stretch goal is too ambitious**: Do set goals that feel uncomfortable but not doomed — because ambition should motivate, not demoralize.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: OKRs are a management system that can fix execution and alignment problems on their own. → **Their view**: OKRs are a vitamin, not a medicine — because they work best in already healthy organizations.
- **Conventional**: A good OKR process is highly structured, detailed, and precise. → **Their view**: Simple OKRs are better — because complexity creates bureaucracy and distracts from outcomes.
- **Conventional**: Status emails and recurring rituals are bureaucratic overhead. → **Their view**: Short weekly updates and Friday celebrations are among the most valuable parts — because they reinforce focus and morale.
- **Conventional**: Precise grading is essential to make OKRs rigorous. → **Their view**: Retrospective learning matters more than scoring precision — because fake precision wastes time.
- **Conventional**: Product managers should primarily focus on users and product sense. → **Their view**: PMs fundamentally serve the business — because someone must steward the business side of the product trio.
- **Conventional**: Aspiring PMs should try to get into product as early as possible. → **Their view**: You probably shouldn’t start in product — because engineering, design, or startup experience builds better judgment.
- **Conventional**: Scale is a sign of a superior product culture. → **Their view**: Scale is just a tactic — because small companies can have stronger purpose and healthier execution.
