# Chip Conley

**Founder of MEA (Modern Elder Academy); former Head of Global Hospitality and Strategy at Airbnb** | **MEA** | Expertise: leadership, hiring-culture, strategy, product-management, user-research

## Bio
Chip Conley is a hospitality entrepreneur who built Joie de Vivre into the second-largest boutique hotel chain in the U.S., then joined Airbnb as Brian Chesky’s in-house mentor and Head of Global Hospitality and Strategy. His perspective matters because he bridges old-school hospitality, startup speed, and the psychology of aging, making him unusually strong on culture, leadership, and reinvention.

He now leads MEA, a “midlife wisdom school” focused on transitions, purpose, and reframing aging as an advantage. If you want advice on how experienced operators can thrive in fast-moving companies, or how to turn life transitions into strategic growth, his viewpoint is especially valuable.

## Signature Frameworks

### Mutual Mentorship / Modern Elder
- **When to use**: When integrating experienced operators into younger, fast-moving teams or when older professionals want to stay effective in tech.
- **Steps**:
  1. Show up as both teacher and learner.
  2. Lead with humility about what you don't know.
  3. Offer pattern recognition, emotional intelligence, and context from experience.
  4. Actively learn new tools, language, and technical workflows from younger teammates.
  5. Build trust through curiosity, respect, and positive energy.
- **Example**: At Airbnb, Conley mentored younger leaders on management and hospitality while younger employees taught him Google Docs, iPhone features, and tech product norms.

### Founder Meeting Alignment Reset
- **When to use**: When presenting to a founder-mode CEO or any strong-willed executive who may derail meetings or create confusion.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start by explicitly stating the intention of the work or iteration.
  2. Define what success looks like.
  3. Clarify what the meeting needs to accomplish.
  4. Use those agreed principles as anchors throughout the discussion.
  5. If alignment is missing, pause the meeting and resolve alignment before debating details.
- **Example**: Conley suggested opening meetings with Brian Chesky by saying: let’s get clear on the intention of this product iteration, what defines success, and what we want to accomplish in this meeting.

### Minimal Deck for Combustible Founders
- **When to use**: When presenting to leaders who frequently jump topics, challenge assumptions, or take meetings off-script.
- **Steps**:
  1. Keep the deck as short as possible.
  2. Use the first slides to establish principles, goals, and constraints.
  3. Avoid over-structuring the presentation around a fixed sequence.
  4. Be prepared to navigate dynamically rather than relying on slide order.
  5. Use the deck as support, not as the meeting itself.
- **Example**: Conley said he limited decks with Brian because the conversation could quickly go off path, making a heavily sequenced presentation useless.

### Intergenerational Brain Diversity
- **When to use**: When building teams, designing collaboration models, or deciding whether to hire more age-diverse talent.
- **Steps**:
  1. Pair younger employees’ fluid intelligence with older employees’ crystallized intelligence.
  2. Use younger team members for speed, focus, and linear problem-solving.
  3. Use older team members for pattern recognition, systems thinking, and connecting dots.
  4. Create roles and interactions where both sides teach each other.
  5. Value “invisible productivity”—making others better—not just individual output.
- **Example**: Conley described how older employees at Airbnb could spot blind spots, like the risk of going mobile-only for older hosts, while younger teams excelled at execution speed.

### Peak Employee Pyramid
- **When to use**: When designing employee experience, motivation systems, or culture strategy.
- **Steps**:
  1. Ensure compensation is fair and sufficient.
  2. Build systems for recognition so people feel seen and appreciated.
  3. Create meaning by connecting work to purpose and impact.
  4. Adjust the relative weight of each layer based on the type of organization or role.
- **Example**: Conley explained that in some jobs money dominates, while in nonprofits meaning is much larger; the differentiator often comes from recognition and meaning, not just pay.

### Peak Customer Pyramid
- **When to use**: When refining positioning, customer experience, or brand strategy.
- **Steps**:
  1. First meet customer expectations.
  2. Then meet their desires.
  3. Then identify and serve their unrecognized needs.
  4. Use the top-level need as an organizing principle for product, marketing, and operations.
- **Example**: At Airbnb, this led to the insight that the company was not just in home sharing but in the “belong anywhere” business, which shaped host education and marketing.

### Culture Add over Culture Fit
- **When to use**: When hiring, evaluating team health, or trying to avoid conformity disguised as culture.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the core traits of the culture clearly.
  2. Assess whether a candidate can thrive within those norms.
  3. Look for ways the person can expand or improve the culture, not just match it.
  4. Avoid using “fit” as a proxy for sameness.
  5. Use employee feedback and observation to see whether culture is actually working.
- **Example**: Conley argued that “culture fit” can exclude people who are different, while “culture add” better captures the value of diversity in strengthening a company.

### Midlife Chrysalis
- **When to use**: When navigating midlife transitions, identity shifts, burnout, or reinvention.
- **Steps**:
  1. Reframe midlife from crisis to transformation.
  2. Accept that old identities and expectations may be dissolving.
  3. Use the transition to reassess purpose, success, and priorities.
  4. Experiment with new skills, roles, or ways of living.
  5. Treat the period as a cocoon for metamorphosis rather than a breakdown.
- **Example**: After a near-death experience and burnout running Joie de Vivre, Conley sold his company, joined Airbnb, and later founded MEA as a school for navigating these transitions.

### Anticipated Regret Prompt
- **When to use**: When stuck, procrastinating on growth, or deciding whether to learn something later in life.
- **Steps**:
  1. Ask: 10 years from now, what will I regret if I don’t learn or do now?
  2. Identify the skills, experiences, or relationships that matter most.
  3. Use the answer to prioritize action today.
  4. Treat anticipated regret as a source of wisdom, not fear.
- **Example**: When Conley moved to Baja at 56, this question pushed him to learn Spanish and surfing despite initially thinking he was too old.

### Emotional Equations
- **When to use**: When trying to make difficult emotions more understandable and actionable.
- **Steps**:
  1. Translate a vague emotional state into a simple equation.
  2. Identify the variables driving the feeling.
  3. Change the variables you can influence.
  4. Use the equation to make the emotion more concrete and less overwhelming.
- **Example**: Conley shared “despair = suffering - meaning” and “anxiety = uncertainty x powerlessness,” then suggested making an anxiety balance sheet of what you know, don’t know, can control, and can’t control.

## Core Advice
- **When you face a founder-mode leader who dominates meetings and shifts direction constantly**: Do a front-end alignment reset: clearly state the intention, success criteria, and desired outcome of the meeting before discussing details — because this creates a shared frame you can return to when the conversation gets derailed.
- **When you face skepticism as an older employee in a younger tech company**: Do both mentoring and learning at the same time—be openly curious, admit what you don’t know, and contribute wisdom where you have it — because older employees succeed when they combine humility with pattern recognition and energy.
- **When you face pressure to make product decisions without enough customer reality**: Do direct customer immersion, especially with overlooked user segments, and bring those insights back into decision-making — because firsthand exposure reveals blind spots internal teams miss.
- **When you face hiring decisions in the AI era**: Do more hiring for generalists who are curious, energetic, and broad problem-solvers—not just narrow specialists — because AI increases the value of synthesis and cross-domain thinking.
- **When you face ageism or fear becoming less relevant in tech**: Do everything you can to project curiosity, positive energy, and passionate engagement rather than relying on past accomplishments — because people notice energy and learning orientation more than age.
- **When you face burnout or a sense that your current path no longer fits**: Do a reframe from “midlife crisis” to “midlife chrysalis” and treat the period as a transition for reinvention — because it reduces shame and opens up experimentation.
- **When you face uncertainty about whether to learn something new later in life**: Do the 10-year regret exercise: ask what you’ll regret not learning or doing now — because anticipated regret clarifies priorities and turns aspiration into action.
- **When you face anxiety that feels vague and overwhelming**: Do an anxiety balance sheet: list what you know, don’t know, can control, and can’t control — because turning anxiety into concrete variables reduces free-floating fear.
- **When you face the challenge of evaluating a company during interviews**: Do ask multiple interviewers to describe the culture in 3-5 adjectives and name the biggest cultural problem in the company — because comparing answers reveals coherence and self-awareness.
- **When you face the need to stand out as an experienced candidate**: Do present your experience through thorny problems solved, the skills used, and the outcomes achieved—not just job titles and bullet points — because this makes your value legible quickly.
- **When you face a distributed or remote organization**: Do invest more intentionally in culture and create regular in-person gatherings when possible — because culture is harder to transmit when people are distributed.
- **When you face a choice between maximizing compensation and preserving meaning**: Do evaluate work through the lens of compensation, recognition, and meaning rather than money alone — because long-term motivation often depends on recognition and meaning.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Tech companies should optimize for young talent because younger workers are faster, cheaper, and more current. → **Their view**: Older workers can be a strategic advantage because they bring crystallized intelligence, emotional intelligence, and pattern recognition — because the best teams combine speed with systems thinking.
- **Conventional**: Culture fit is the right way to hire people who will succeed in a company. → **Their view**: Companies should hire for culture add, not culture fit — because “fit” often becomes a coded way to reward sameness.
- **Conventional**: Midlife is mainly a crisis period marked by decline and loss. → **Their view**: Midlife is a chrysalis, a transformative stage that can lead to greater happiness and reinvention — because many people become happier once they let go of old expectations.
- **Conventional**: Aging is something to resist; anti-aging is the goal. → **Their view**: A pro-aging mindset is healthier and can literally extend life — because positive beliefs about aging are associated with better outcomes.
- **Conventional**: The product in a tech company is the software interface and conversion funnel. → **Their view**: In some businesses, the real product includes the lived experience and the people delivering it — because hospitality is part of the product.
- **Conventional**: Specialists will dominate the future because technology keeps getting more complex. → **Their view**: AI is increasing the value of generalists — because broad thinkers are better at synthesis and cross-domain judgment.
