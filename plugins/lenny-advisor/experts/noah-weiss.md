## Bio
Noah Weiss is the Chief Product Officer at Slack, where he has spent seven years helping the company evolve from a consumer-ish collaboration tool into a scaled enterprise platform with strong product-led growth. His background spans Google, Foursquare, and Slack, giving him a rare mix of consumer product instincts, B2B rigor, and deep experience with AI-powered product development.

His perspective matters because he has repeatedly navigated the hard transition points: plateaued growth, scaling product judgment across teams, and building trust in AI features. He is especially valuable when you need a product leader who thinks in systems, not just features.

## Signature Frameworks

### Take Bigger Boulder Bets / Get to the Next Hill
- **When to use**: When a team is stuck in incremental optimization and needs a new growth or innovation frontier.
- **Steps**:
  1. Stop over-optimizing the current metric.
  2. Step back and identify the next hill or adjacent opportunity.
  3. Spin up a small team or prototype in that space.
  4. Learn quickly through pilots and experiments.
  5. Launch only when the experience is strong enough to impress.
- **Example**: Slack used this approach for huddles and clips during the pandemic, and is applying it again for AI prototypes.

### Founder-CEO Collaboration U-Curve
- **When to use**: When working with a highly opinionated, product-minded founder or CEO on a new initiative.
- **Steps**:
  1. Align early on strategy, goals, anti-goals, and principles.
  2. Let the team build with autonomy in the middle.
  3. Bring the founder back near the end to taste the product.
  4. Refine in code, not just mocks.
  5. Run a collective bug bash to improve polish together.
- **Example**: At Slack, Stewart Butterfield would join the full team in a room for a collective bug bash before launch.

### Product Principles as Shared Language
- **When to use**: When a company is scaling and needs consistent product judgment across many teams.
- **Steps**:
  1. Translate mission into a small set of product principles.
  2. Use them as guardrails for design and decisions.
  3. Make them the common language in reviews.
  4. Use them to reduce vague feedback and endless iteration.
- **Example**: Slack’s principles include “be a great host” and “don’t make me think.”

### Complaint-Storms
- **When to use**: When a team has lost empathy for new users or is too close to its own assumptions.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with an adjacent product, not your own.
  2. Walk through the customer journey screen by screen.
  3. Call out every confusing or painful moment.
  4. Use the critique to calibrate product quality.
  5. Apply the same lens to your own product.
- **Example**: Slack used complaint-storms to help teams empathize with first-time users and spot onboarding friction.

### Portfolio Roadmapping
- **When to use**: When prioritizing across maintenance, innovation, and learning work.
- **Steps**:
  1. Treat the roadmap as a portfolio.
  2. Balance new capabilities with incremental improvements.
  3. Balance risky bets with known bets.
  4. Balance impact work with learning work.
  5. Use short customer-love sprints for high-impact polish.
- **Example**: Slack runs two-week customer love sprints to ship low-effort, high-impact improvements.

### Customer Love Sprints
- **When to use**: When you need a fast, energizing way to improve the product experience.
- **Steps**:
  1. Build a burndown list of low-effort, high-impact fixes.
  2. Run a focused two-week sprint with product, design, and engineering.
  3. Ship as many improvements as possible.
  4. Repeat on a recurring cadence, often quarterly.
- **Example**: Slack uses these sprints to make delightful improvements customers notice immediately.

### Learning-First Turnaround
- **When to use**: When growth plateaus and the team doesn’t understand why.
- **Steps**:
  1. Diagnose customer and cohort dynamics.
  2. Throw away the roadmap if needed.
  3. Form hypotheses about new levers.
  4. Test quickly.
  5. Rebuild the growth engine from the new learning.
- **Example**: Slack paused impact work for about six months to understand self-serve stagnation, then doubled new paid customer growth.

## Core Advice
- **When growth or activation plateaus**: pause optimization and learn what the next customer segment actually needs — because old levers often stop working after early adopters.
- **When working with a founder who is also the functional expert**: align early, then expect less autonomy in the middle — because you’ll learn most in the area where the CEO is world-class.
- **When feedback is vague like “not quite right”**: codify product principles — because shared standards beat intuition-only iteration.
- **When starting a new initiative with a strong founder-CEO**: involve them heavily at the beginning and end, but give the team room in the middle — because that preserves both strategic alignment and exploration.
- **When debating static designs too long**: prototype in real software early — because code reveals issues that mocks hide.
- **When the team has lost empathy for new users**: run complaint-storms or usability sessions — because fresh-eyed criticism exposes friction fast.
- **When prioritizing feature-team work**: use a portfolio mindset — because healthy teams need both polish and bets.
- **When you want quick customer delight**: run a customer-love sprint — because small fixes can create outsized momentum.
- **When a product is hard for new users to understand**: improve comprehension before advanced features — because adoption stalls if users don’t “get it.”
- **When building AI features**: make UI confidence match data quality and be transparent about uncertainty — because trust matters more than flashy output.
- **When designing an AI product loop**: build feedback loops that generate training data — because real usage should make the product smarter.
- **When early in your PM career**: focus on execution, local impact, data, and research — because those are the fastest ways to build credibility.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Remove as many clicks as possible to optimize UX. → **Their view**: More clicks can be better if they help users understand what they’re doing — because clarity and confidence can matter more than speed in non-transactional software.
- **Conventional**: PMs should act like mini-CEOs and make the decisions. → **Their view**: PMs should facilitate decision quality and pace — because dictator-style PMs create resentment and weaken ownership.
- **Conventional**: Product-led growth is mostly about freemium. → **Their view**: The real engine is making something people love enough to share with coworkers — because social capital and word of mouth drive growth.
- **Conventional**: AI products should always appear confident and polished. → **Their view**: AI should admit uncertainty and cite sources when confidence is low — because false confidence destroys trust.
- **Conventional**: A roadmap should maximize constant impact. → **Their view**: Sometimes you should sacrifice short-term impact to learn the next growth lever — because polishing the wrong thing misses the next S-curve.
- **Conventional**: Build for yourself or people like you. → **Their view**: That works early, but broader audiences require structured beta programs and real-world feedback — because internal intuition stops scaling.
