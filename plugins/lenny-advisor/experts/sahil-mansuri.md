# Sahil Mansuri

**CEO and Founder** | **Bravado** | Expertise: B2B sales, sales leadership, revenue strategy, sales compensation, go-to-market, pricing

## Bio
Sahil Mansuri is the CEO and founder of Bravado, a large sales community and SaaS platform for B2B sales professionals. He has spent his entire career in sales, including selling through the 2008–2009 downturn, leading enterprise sales at Glassdoor, and building systems for sales benchmarking, recruiting, and community-driven learning.

His perspective matters because he thinks about sales as a full business system, not just a quota function. He is especially useful when markets get volatile, when retention matters more than acquisition, and when teams need to rethink incentives, pricing, and the role of the founder.

## Signature Frameworks

### Conservative annual plan with milestone-based unlocks
- **When to use**: When visibility is low and the market is volatile, especially during recessions or demand shocks.
- **Steps**:
  1. Set a conservative baseline plan for the year.
  2. Define short-term milestones or checkpoints.
  3. Pre-agree on what happens if milestones are hit or missed.
  4. Unlock additional spend or growth investment only when targets are met.
  5. Reforecast regularly instead of defending the original plan.
- **Example**: If a company did $10M in revenue last year, plan for $9M next year, then use Q1 performance to decide whether to raise or lower the rest-of-year forecast.

### Retention-first sales compensation design
- **When to use**: When new customer acquisition is harder and retention, renewals, and expansion matter more to business health.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the metrics that truly matter to the business.
  2. Reward reps not just for new bookings, but for renewals, retention, and expansion.
  3. Add kickers for customers who renew or expand after the sale.
  4. Measure rep performance against the renewal quality of the accounts they bring in.
  5. Align incentives across the customer, the rep, and the business.
- **Example**: A rep who closes fewer deals but brings in customers that renew, provide references, and expand should be rewarded more than a rep who closes more deals that churn quickly.

### Patch the leaky bucket before pouring more water in
- **When to use**: When acquisition is slowing and the business is tempted to keep pushing new sales despite weak retention.
- **Steps**:
  1. Shift focus from top-of-funnel growth to customer survival and retention.
  2. Move your strongest people onto post-sale/customer success work.
  3. Use customer insights and support to reduce churn.
  4. Create value that helps customers navigate the downturn.
  5. Only re-accelerate acquisition once the existing base is stable.
- **Example**: Bravado moved its best people into customer success to make sure existing customers would not leave during the downturn.

### Customer-insight engine as retention product
- **When to use**: When you have unique data about a customer segment and need to create value beyond the core product.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the unique cross-sectional data your company sees.
  2. Turn that data into benchmarks, reports, or research.
  3. Package the insights as customer-only value.
  4. Use product marketing and analysts to create ongoing research output.
  5. Make the customer feel smarter and better equipped to survive the market.
- **Example**: Bravado tells customers how companies like them are adjusting hiring, quotas, comp plans, and quota attainment if they stay subscribed.

### Warm-intro selling loop
- **When to use**: When cold outreach is weak and trust is the main bottleneck in closing deals.
- **Steps**:
  1. Use customer events or strong customer relationships to surface referrals.
  2. Ask customers who else has the same problem.
  3. Request a direct introduction, ideally by text rather than email.
  4. Keep the introducer on the thread long enough to ensure follow-through.
  5. Persist politely until the meeting happens.
- **Example**: After a customer mentioned a prospect, Sahil asked for a text intro and kept the introducer in the thread until the meeting was actually booked.

### Teach-first enterprise selling
- **When to use**: When selling into sophisticated buyers who need a reason to engage beyond your product pitch.
- **Steps**:
  1. Research the buyer’s world deeply.
  2. Create a custom insight or report that changes how they think.
  3. Lead with the insight, not the product.
  4. Use the insight to earn a strategic conversation.
  5. Let the product become the natural next step after value is established.
- **Example**: Sahil sent Sheryl Sandberg a custom report on Facebook’s Glassdoor reviews, salary bands, and CEO approval ratings, which led to a meeting and a major deal.

### Founder-as-salesperson model
- **When to use**: When thinking about the role of a CEO, fundraising, hiring, or early company building.
- **Steps**:
  1. Sell yourself on the company idea.
  2. Sell talented people to join before traction exists.
  3. Sell investors on the vision and potential.
  4. Sell customers on trying an immature product.
  5. Keep selling through press, hiring, and expansion.
- **Example**: Sahil argued that a CEO is constantly selling: to themselves, to employees, to investors, to customers, and to the market.

### Change the rules of the game, not just the price
- **When to use**: When the current business model is broken by market conditions and small optimizations won’t fix it.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start from the customer’s current constraints.
  2. Reimagine the offer structure, not just the price point.
  3. Experiment with new pricing or employment models.
  4. Launch a new model quickly and learn from it.
  5. Scale the model that fits the new market reality.
- **Example**: Bravado Flex introduced contract, fractional, and 100% commission sales roles to match a market where companies could not hire full-time reps.

## Core Advice
- **When you face a recession with limited visibility**: Do set a conservative annual plan, but pair it with explicit milestones that unlock more spend or growth if hit — because this avoids both reckless optimism and paralysis.
- **When your forecast is uncertain quarter to quarter**: Do reforecast regularly and pre-agree with your board and leadership on what happens at each checkpoint — because predetermined rules reduce bias and wishful thinking.
- **When new customer acquisition gets harder**: Do shift attention from cold prospecting toward retention, renewals, upsells, and cross-sells in the existing base — because keeping current customers alive is often the fastest path to survival.
- **When your sales comp plan only rewards new bookings**: Do add incentives for renewals, expansion, and customer quality, not just closed-won ARR — because churny revenue is less valuable than durable revenue.
- **When your best reps are still focused on hunting new business in a frozen market**: Do move some of your strongest sellers into customer success or post-sale roles — because protecting the base can be the highest-leverage use of top talent.
- **When your product has unique data about a customer segment**: Do turn that data into customer-only benchmarks, reports, and advisory content — because insight makes you more than a tool.
- **When cold email and cold calling stop working well**: Do prioritize warm intros, especially through existing customers and trusted relationships — because trust matters more when uncertainty is high.
- **When you get a promising referral**: Do ask for a direct text introduction and keep the introducer on the thread until the meeting is secured — because email threads die easily.
- **When you are selling into a sophisticated buyer**: Do lead with a custom insight or research artifact that teaches them something useful — because the fastest way to earn attention is to change how they think.
- **When your current business model is constrained by the market**: Do experiment with new pricing or delivery models instead of making tiny price cuts — because structural problems need structural changes.
- **When you need growth but cannot afford full-time hires**: Do consider fractional, contract, or commission-only models to match supply and demand — because flexibility can unlock talent and revenue.
- **When you want to close enterprise deals in a downturn**: Do invest heavily in bespoke, high-value outreach that demonstrates deep understanding of the buyer’s world — because generic outbound is weak in tough markets.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Sales comp should primarily reward new ARR and quota attainment. → **Their view**: Comp should also reward retention, renewals, expansion, and customer quality — because durable revenue matters more in capital-constrained markets.
- **Conventional**: The best salespeople should always be hunting for new business. → **Their view**: In a downturn, your best sellers may be more valuable in customer success or retention roles — because protecting the base can be the highest-leverage move.
- **Conventional**: Cold outreach is the default way to generate pipeline. → **Their view**: Warm intros and customer-driven referrals become far more effective when trust is scarce — because people rely on familiar relationships in uncertainty.
- **Conventional**: You can solve a broken growth model by optimizing price or process a little. → **Their view**: Sometimes you need to change the entire business model or rules of the game — because structural shifts require structural responses.
- **Conventional**: Sales is about persuasion and pushing product. → **Their view**: Great sales is about teaching, helping, and changing the buyer’s perspective — because insight creates trust.
- **Conventional**: A CEO is mostly an operator or strategist. → **Their view**: A CEO is fundamentally a salesperson — because founders are constantly selling to every stakeholder.
