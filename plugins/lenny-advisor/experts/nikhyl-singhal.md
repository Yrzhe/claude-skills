# Nikhyl Singhal

**Product leader, coach, and mentor** | **Meta** | Expertise: product leadership, career development, consumer products, management coaching

## Bio
Nikhyl Singhal leads product teams for the Facebook app at Meta and has held senior product leadership roles at Google and Credit Karma. He also co-founded startups, which gives him a rare perspective across early-stage ambiguity, scaled consumer products, and leadership at large tech companies.

His perspective matters because he thinks about careers the way product leaders think about roadmaps: with an end state, versioning, tradeoffs, and long-term compounding. He is especially useful for PMs and leaders who want practical guidance on promotions, management, and building a career that is more than a sequence of titles.

## Signature Frameworks

### Work backward from your career end state
- **When to use**: When deciding whether to take a new job, stay put, or plan your next move
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the long-term end state you want, not just the next title.
  2. Work backward from that end state to identify the job after next and the job after that.
  3. Use current and next roles as stepping stones in service of that longer arc.
  4. Evaluate whether a move is truly forward progress or just a lateral escape from current frustration.
- **Example**: Instead of leaving because “my boss sucks,” ask whether the next role helps you get to your boss’s boss’s job or to founding a company.

### Career as a product roadmap
- **When to use**: When planning a multi-year career trajectory
- **Steps**:
  1. Imagine the ideal version of your career outcome.
  2. Break that outcome into versions: version one, version two, version three.
  3. Choose experiences that build toward the next version.
  4. Continuously update the roadmap as your goals evolve.
- **Example**: Define the great end state first, then map the sequence of roles and experiences that get you there.

### Diagnose promotion blockers with four lenses
- **When to use**: When someone is stuck and not getting promoted
- **Steps**:
  1. Check whether there is real advocacy for the person.
  2. Check whether the next role actually exists.
  3. Assess whether the person is being impatient relative to the role’s natural timeline.
  4. Determine whether there is a genuine development gap that must be addressed.
- **Example**: A high performer may be blocked not because they are unqualified, but because the org has no open next-level role or no advocate who sees their impact.

### Build a story for your skip job
- **When to use**: When choosing projects, roles, or responsibilities in the present
- **Steps**:
  1. Write the paragraph you want to tell about this role in 6, 12, and 24 months.
  2. Include the problem solved, the skill built, and the headwind overcome.
  3. Use “I” language to make the story about your growth and contribution.
  4. If the story is weak, change the work so the story becomes compelling.
- **Example**: “I solved X, built Y skill, and overcame Z challenge” becomes the narrative that supports your next move.

### Share the steering wheel in management
- **When to use**: When transitioning into management or managing a new team
- **Steps**:
  1. Treat management as a shared control system, not command-and-control.
  2. Earn the right to be involved by asking what you can help with.
  3. Wait for invitation rather than forcing your way into every problem.
  4. Pick one or two areas where you can truly partner and add value.
- **Example**: He describes the manager as a sidecar on a motorcycle: attached, useful, but not the driver.

### Reboot the meeting operating system
- **When to use**: When joining a scaled organization or trying to improve execution
- **Steps**:
  1. Treat meetings as a product with versions and feedback loops.
  2. Redesign meeting cadence, attendees, and decision rights.
  3. Review and iterate every quarter.
  4. Use process improvements to unlock better delegation and faster decisions.
- **Example**: On his teams, meetings are on “version seven” and get reworked every 90 days based on feedback.

### Use community as a learning engine
- **When to use**: When formal training and manager coaching are insufficient
- **Steps**:
  1. Join or create a trusted peer community.
  2. Create psychological safety for honest discussion.
  3. Share real problems and compare notes with others in similar roles.
  4. Use the group to normalize challenges and exchange practical solutions.
- **Example**: He built The Skip CPO community via WhatsApp and Zoom so senior product leaders could compare notes on difficult CEO and org challenges.

### Identify the shadow of your superpower
- **When to use**: When feedback says your strength is also causing problems
- **Steps**:
  1. List your most celebrated strengths.
  2. Look for contradictory feedback or recurring complaints.
  3. Assume the strength may be overused at the next level.
  4. Rewire the behavior without denying the underlying strength.
- **Example**: A person known as a great collaborator may struggle once leadership requires strong opinions and disagreement.

## Core Advice
- **When you face a frustrating job and feel tempted to leave immediately**: Ask whether the issue is truly career-defining or just a short-term annoyance, and evaluate the move against your long-term end state — because many complaints exist in multiple jobs, and leaving can create a lateral move rather than real progress.
- **When you are choosing between promotion and career growth**: Treat promotion as a mechanism, not the goal; ask what the promotion is in service of — because a promotion without a longer-term purpose can become an empty milestone.
- **When you are early in your PM career**: Pick one lane to become world-class in, such as craft, domain, growth, organizational ambiguity, or team leadership — because early specialization helps you build a clear skill identity.
- **When you are deciding what experiences to seek early in your career**: Prioritize a diverse set of experiences across stages, markets, and problem types — because seeing pre-PMF, PMF, scale, consumer, and B2B contexts broadens your options.
- **When you are at a company that may be “ex-growth”**: Assess whether the company is still searching for product-market fit while carrying a scaled valuation and large team; if so, consider leaving sooner rather than later — because the upside may no longer match the risk.
- **When you are unsure whether your company has product-market fit**: Look for strong customer pull and low-friction acquisition rather than relying only on financial metrics — because real pull shows up in behavior, not just spreadsheets.
- **When you are trying to get promoted but it is not happening**: Diagnose whether the blocker is lack of advocacy, no available next role, impatience, or a real development gap — because each cause requires a different response.
- **When you suspect your manager is not seeing your impact**: Actively pull feedback from peers, frequent collaborators, and less frequent observers, then triangulate the signal — because manager-only feedback can miss the real pattern.
- **When you receive feedback that stings**: Repeat it back accurately, thank the person, and make them feel safe enough to be even more direct — because people become more honest when they feel heard.
- **When you become a new manager**: Do not assume you are automatically qualified; focus first on earning trust and learning how to share responsibility — because management is a distinct skill set from product building.
- **When you are a senior leader with a strong reputation**: Look for the downside of your strengths and be willing to change behaviors that once made you successful — because what got you here can limit you at the next level.
- **When you are planning your long-term career and life**: Start designing your act three now so you are not left without a motivating North Star later — because a long career can leave people lost after the early goals are achieved.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: A promotion is the main marker of career progress. → **Their view**: Promotion is just a company-specific system; career should be judged by the long-term arc and the story you are building — because people often chase promotions only to leave immediately after.
- **Conventional**: If you can build products well, you are ready to manage people. → **Their view**: Product-building skill and management skill are largely different — because tech often promotes builders into management without training.
- **Conventional**: Senior leaders mainly need more feedback on their weaknesses. → **Their view**: Senior leaders often need help seeing the shadow side of their strengths — because the traits that created success can become liabilities.
- **Conventional**: The best career move is to keep collecting prestigious logos on your resume. → **Their view**: Chasing logos for their own sake can be shallow; diverse, meaningful experiences matter more — because breadth across product, market, and stage makes you a better builder.
- **Conventional**: Management is the natural next step for PMs who want to grow. → **Their view**: The IC path is a real and often better long-term path for many PMs — because strong IC tracks will matter more as companies flatten.
- **Conventional**: Learning happens primarily through managers and formal L&D programs. → **Their view**: Community is often a more powerful learning engine — because peers create safety, authenticity, and practical problem-solving.
