## Bio
Jason Cohen is a four-time founder and two-time unicorn founder, best known for founding WP Engine and for writing deeply practical startup advice at A Smart Bear. His perspective matters because it blends operator experience, pricing intuition, and hard-won lessons about what actually drives growth, retention, and market fit in real software businesses.

He is especially useful when a company is stuck and needs a disciplined way to diagnose the bottleneck instead of chasing random tactics. Cohen’s thinking is concrete, contrarian, and rooted in how customers, markets, and incentives really behave.

## Signature Frameworks

### 5 Questions to Ask When Growth Stalls
- **When to use**: When a product that once had traction starts slowing down and you need to find the highest-leverage problem first.
- **Steps**:
  1. Check whether logo churn is too high.
  2. Evaluate whether pricing is correct.
  3. Measure whether existing customers are expanding enough.
  4. Test whether acquisition channels are saturated.
  5. Decide whether growth is actually the right goal.
- **Example**: If you add 100 customers per month and churn 5% monthly, your ceiling is about 2,000 customers.

### Churn Ceiling Calculation
- **When to use**: When you want a simple way to see how churn mathematically caps growth.
- **Steps**:
  1. Measure new customers added per period.
  2. Measure logo churn for the same period.
  3. Divide new customers by churn rate.
  4. Treat the result as the approximate maximum sustainable customer base.
- **Example**: 100 new customers per month at 5% monthly churn implies a ceiling of 2,000 customers.

### Cancellation Diagnosis via Open-Ended Inquiry
- **When to use**: When cancellation surveys are producing vague, biased, or low-value data.
- **Steps**:
  1. Avoid multiple-choice cancellation reasons.
  2. Ask “What made you cancel?” instead of “Why did you cancel?”
  3. Probe beyond surface reasons like “too expensive.”
  4. Talk to at-risk customers before they leave.
  5. Start with onboarding if you need a first retention lever.
- **Example**: At SmartBear, randomized dropdown order showed the “top reason” for cancellation was largely selection bias.

### Root-er Cause Analysis for Churn
- **When to use**: When the stated churn reason is generic and you need something actionable.
- **Steps**:
  1. Treat the first answer as a proximate cause.
  2. Ask what product, workflow, expectation, or market mismatch caused it.
  3. Look for multiple contributing causes.
  4. Convert findings into product, positioning, integration, onboarding, or ICP changes.
- **Example**: “Too expensive” may really mean the product didn’t fit the customer’s workflow, such as missing a key integration.

### Pricing as Market Selection
- **When to use**: When pricing may be attracting the wrong customers or underselling the product.
- **Steps**:
  1. Treat price as a signal, not just a number.
  2. Test whether higher prices change demand.
  3. Reframe pricing around the value the buyer wants most.
  4. Align packaging and contracts with the target segment.
  5. Make sure you want the market implied by the price.
- **Example**: A founder charging $300/year for an enterprise product was advised to charge $300/month; signups stayed the same.

### Value-Based Positioning for Higher Pricing
- **When to use**: When the product creates real business impact but is framed too narrowly.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the outcome buyers care about most.
  2. Translate the product into that outcome.
  3. Price against the value of the outcome.
  4. Update messaging and sales language accordingly.
- **Example**: A product sold as “cut AdWords cost in half” might command $5K/month, but “double your leads” could support $40K/month.

### NRR Through Customer Value Expansion
- **When to use**: When logo churn is manageable but growth still stalls.
- **Steps**:
  1. Measure net revenue retention.
  2. Increase customer value through features, usage, seats, or adjacent products.
  3. Tie monetization to value growth.
  4. Use direct or proxy measures of customer value.
  5. Think of pricing as sharing newly created value.
- **Example**: Public SaaS companies often have NRR above 100%; Cohen cites median IPO SaaS NRR around 119%.

### Elephant Curve for Channel Saturation
- **When to use**: When acquisition slows and the team keeps pushing the same channels.
- **Steps**:
  1. Map which channels are still growing versus saturated.
  2. Assume channels have ceilings and decay over time.
  3. Don’t rely on incremental product tweaks plus more spend in a saturated channel.
  4. Explore new channels, partners, or ecosystem distribution.
  5. Consider new products or markets if needed.
- **Example**: Constant Contact revived growth with local workshops; HubSpot built agencies into a major channel.

## Core Advice
- **When growth stalls and many things could be wrong**: diagnose in order: churn, pricing, NRR, channel saturation, then whether growth is even necessary — because lower-level problems cap everything above them.
- **When churn is the issue**: do the churn ceiling math — because it makes retention’s impact on scale impossible to ignore.
- **When cancellation feedback is noisy**: ask open-ended “What made you cancel?” — because dropdowns and “why” questions invite weak data.
- **When customers say “too expensive”**: probe deeper — because price is often a symptom of poor fit, onboarding, or unmet expectations.
- **When retention is weak**: start with onboarding — because early-life churn is where small improvements compound most.
- **When you’re unsure about pricing**: raise prices and observe demand — because many startups are dramatically underpriced.
- **When willingness to pay is low**: reposition around the business outcome customers want — because buyers pay more for strategic outcomes than for savings.
- **When expansion revenue is flat**: create more customer value first, then monetize part of it — because NRR rises when customers genuinely get more value.
- **When acquisition slows in familiar channels**: audit channel saturation and look for new distribution paths — because channels decay and have ceilings.
- **When pressure to move upmarket appears**: check the full strategic implications before changing price — because pricing selects the market, not just the revenue number.
- **When growth-at-all-costs feels automatic**: decide explicitly whether growth, profit, or stability is the right goal — because not every company should maximize growth forever.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: If customers say they canceled because the product was too expensive, price is the problem. → **Their view**: Price is usually not the real issue; the deeper problem is unmet value or product/workflow mismatch — because customers already accepted the price once.
- **Conventional**: Raising prices should reduce demand. → **Their view**: In software, higher prices can preserve or increase demand — because price also signals quality and selects the right market.
- **Conventional**: Pricing is mostly about picking the right number. → **Their view**: Pricing is inseparable from positioning, packaging, and market selection — because the same product can justify radically different prices depending on framing.
- **Conventional**: There is one root cause for churn. → **Their view**: Most churn has multiple interlocking causes — because complex systems rarely reduce to a single explanation.
- **Conventional**: “If you’re not growing, you’re dying” is just investor hype. → **Their view**: It can be true for the founder or team even if the company is profitable — because stagnation can kill energy, learning, and fulfillment.
- **Conventional**: A/B testing is the best way to improve growth. → **Their view**: A/B testing is overrated for many teams — because it cannot test strategy and often produces misleading wins.
