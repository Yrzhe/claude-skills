## Signature Frameworks

### Growth Loops
- **When to use**: Use when designing sustainable growth systems beyond a linear funnel, especially when product actions can generate more users, engagement, or monetization.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify a user action that creates value beyond the initiating user.
  2. Map the reaction that action triggers in another user or system.
  3. Design the experience so that reaction leads to another valuable action.
  4. Measure whether the loop is self-reinforcing and can compound over time.
  5. Invest in optimizing both sides of the loop, not just the initial trigger.
- **Example**: At Dropbox, sharing files creates awareness and activation for recipients, and a meaningful percentage of recipients sign up, making sharing responsible for over 50% of acquisition.

### Race Car Framework
- **When to use**: Use when planning a balanced growth strategy across short-term boosts, ongoing engines, and maintenance work.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the core engines that keep growth moving.
  2. Define the fuel required to power those engines, such as paid spend or content input.
  3. List turbo boosts that can create temporary spikes, such as events or launches.
  4. Account for tune-ups and maintenance needed to keep systems performing.
  5. Balance investment across these categories instead of over-focusing on one.
- **Example**: Elena describes it as a way to separate initiatives into engines, fuel, turbo boosts like big user conferences, and tune-ups or optimizations.

### Adjacent User Theory
- **When to use**: Use when growth from the core ICP is maturing and you want to expand growth by serving nearby user segments without a full product-market-fit reset.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the current core user and ICP clearly.
  2. Identify adjacent users already touching the product or workflow.
  3. Analyze where their experience breaks or underperforms.
  4. Optimize onboarding, activation, or use cases for those adjacent users.
  5. Use growth tactics to expand adoption among them before broader product expansion.
- **Example**: Elena references it as a way for growth teams to bring in users outside the core ICP by optimizing their experiences, adding growth without requiring a full PMF expansion.

### Minimum Lovable Product
- **When to use**: When launching new products or experiments in fast-moving AI markets where basic viability is no longer enough to trigger word of mouth.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start with a minimal version of the product or feature.
  2. Ensure the first experience creates delight, not just utility.
  3. Focus on whether the product produces a “wow” reaction quickly.
  4. Ship fast, gather feedback immediately, and iterate toward stronger emotional resonance.
- **Example**: Elena describes building and launching internal prototypes and initiatives like SheBuilds and other Lovable experiments as “minimum lovable product” rather than MVPs.

### Product-Market Fit Treadmill
- **When to use**: When operating in AI categories where underlying model capabilities and user expectations change every few months.
- **Steps**:
  1. Assume product-market fit is temporary, not durable.
  2. Track changes in underlying model capabilities and customer expectations continuously.
  3. Make forward-looking product bets before the next model shift arrives.
  4. Recapture product-market fit repeatedly, then scale in short bursts.
  5. Avoid assuming that past fit guarantees future demand.
- **Example**: Elena says Lovable effectively has to “recapture product market fit every three months” because both LLM capabilities and customer expectations keep shifting.

### Build in Public + Employee Socials
- **When to use**: When you are an early-stage or fast-growing company that can use shipping velocity and transparency to drive awareness, resurrection, and trust.
- **Steps**:
  1. Ship frequently and visibly.
  2. Have founders and employees post about launches, learnings, and progress.
  3. Tier launches into major moments and smaller ongoing updates.
  4. Use social posting to keep the market aware that the product is evolving.
  5. Let users see that feedback turns into shipped product quickly.
- **Example**: Lovable uses founder-led and employee-led social posting on X and LinkedIn, alongside constant shipping, to maintain “noise in the market” and bring users back.

### Give the Product Away as a Growth Loop
- **When to use**: When your product is novel, mind-blowing, and easier to understand through direct use than through explanation.
- **Steps**:
  1. Remove barriers to first use with freemium access.
  2. Go beyond freemium by giving away credits for events, hackathons, and community-led activation.
  3. Treat usage giveaways as marketing spend, not margin leakage.
  4. Empower users and champions to distribute the product inside their own communities or companies.
  5. Monetize after users experience the product’s value and want more usage.
- **Example**: Lovable gives away credits for hackathons and user-led events, and Elena says they track those LLM costs as marketing costs because they drive adoption and word of mouth.

### Hire for Fire, Agency, and Autonomy
- **When to use**: When building in chaotic, high-velocity environments where people must ship, market, and solve problems without heavy process.
- **Steps**:
  1. Prioritize candidates with deep passion for the work, not just pedigree.
  2. Screen for high agency: willingness to own problems end to end.
  3. Screen for high autonomy: ability to operate across functions without waiting for direction.
  4. Use paid work trials and probation periods to test fit in real conditions.
  5. Be explicit about the pace and chaos so candidates self-select appropriately.
- **Example**: Elena explains that Lovable uses paid work trials and looks for people with “fire in their belly” who can create clarity out of chaos.

## Core Advice
- **When you need growth before PMF**: keep it founder-led — because growth cannot be outsourced before the company knows what works.
- **When growth is slowing**: fix product, market, or GTM first — because growth teams amplify momentum; they don’t create it from nothing.
- **When considering a homepage redesign**: only do it for a strategic shift, and expect a temporary dip — because redesigns reset optimized systems.
- **When looking at competitors’ funnels**: borrow patterns, not solutions — because context matters and copying leads to mediocrity.
- **When a problem feels unique**: find someone who has solved a similar one — because most growth problems are recurring patterns.
- **When overdependent on SEO/SEM/platforms**: build earned channels like referrals and sharing loops — because rented channels are fragile.
- **When one growth engine is working well**: start testing new loops every 18 months — because growth engines saturate.
- **When you need outside help**: test advisors with a paid workshop first — because good advisors compress learning, but fit matters.
- **When experimentation becomes dogma**: use judgment and pre/post analysis more often — because testing everything slows learning.
- **When a roadmap item is “simplify onboarding”**: define the actual user problem first — because simplification alone is not a strategy.
- **When operating in AI**: spend more time on new loops and product bets than micro-optimizations — because the market moves too fast for incrementalism.
- **When the product is hard to explain**: get more people to try it directly — because wow moments drive conversion and word of mouth.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Hire a head of growth as early as possible. → **Their view**: Delay growth hiring until PMF and data exist — because founders must discover distribution and retention first.
- **Conventional**: A strong growth team can restart a slowing business. → **Their view**: Growth cannot fix broken product or GTM — because it only amplifies existing momentum.
- **Conventional**: Rebrands and homepage redesigns unlock growth. → **Their view**: They usually hurt first and rarely create immediate lift — because they reset already-optimized experiences.
- **Conventional**: A/B test everything. → **Their view**: Testing everything is paralyzing — because many decisions should be made with judgment and evaluated with pre/post analysis.
- **Conventional**: Simplify flows and reduce friction. → **Their view**: Simplification is vague unless tied to a real user problem — because the issue is often confusion or poor education.
- **Conventional**: MVP is enough. → **Their view**: Products now need to be minimum lovable — because delight and shareability matter more in modern software.
- **Conventional**: Growth should mostly optimize funnels. → **Their view**: In AI, growth should invent new loops and capabilities — because optimization is too incremental.
- **Conventional**: Full-time jobs are the default career path. → **Their view**: People should optimize for career optionality — because advising, consulting, fractional, and creator paths may fit better.
