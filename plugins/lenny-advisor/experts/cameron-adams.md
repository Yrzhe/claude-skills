# Cameron Adams

**Co-founder and Chief Product Officer** | **Canva** | Expertise: product-management, design, leadership, go-to-market, growth, ai-strategy

## Bio
Cameron Adams is the co-founder and CPO of Canva, where he helped build one of the world’s most successful product-led software companies. His perspective matters because he combines deep design instincts with product strategy, scaling leadership, and a strong belief that product quality and user delight are the engine of growth.

He is especially useful to seek out when you need a view on how to scale a consumer or prosumer product without losing craft, how to build a coaching culture, or how to make AI and growth channels serve real user outcomes rather than gimmicks.

## Signature Frameworks

### Giving Away Your Legos
- **When to use**: When a startup is scaling and people need to move from doing the work themselves to building systems and leverage.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the work you currently own that has become central to your identity.
  2. Notice when doing it yourself is limiting your impact.
  3. Hand off execution or build systems to distribute the work.
  4. Shift satisfaction from personal contribution to enabling others.
  5. Use the freed-up time for higher-scope work.
- **Example**: An early Canva email copywriter had to stop writing every email personally and instead build translation workflows and a broader team for global scale.

### Coaches Not Managers
- **When to use**: When you want to develop people through craft-specific growth in a fast-scaling company.
- **Steps**:
  1. Assign each employee a coach from the same craft area.
  2. Use the coach to assess skills and growth trajectory.
  3. Hold regular one-on-ones and development conversations.
  4. Pair coaching with recurring 360 feedback.
  5. Train coaches so development quality scales.
- **Example**: At Canva, product managers are coached by product leaders rather than managed in a traditional hierarchy.

### Joyful MVP Bar
- **When to use**: When deciding whether an early product is ready to launch, especially for consumer or prosumer tools.
- **Steps**:
  1. Understand the problem deeply through research.
  2. Don’t ship just because it works.
  3. Test whether users feel excitement and delight.
  4. Accept rough edges only if the experience is still compelling.
  5. Launch once a target segment reliably loves it.
- **Example**: Canva spent about a year building before launch because a merely functional design tool would not have created word of mouth.

### Persona Discovery Through Emotional User Testing
- **When to use**: When you have a broad vision but need the best initial ICP or wedge market.
- **Steps**:
  1. Test with multiple user groups.
  2. Measure emotional intensity, not just utility.
  3. Find the segment with the strongest excitement.
  4. Tailor onboarding and messaging to that segment.
  5. Lean into that segment for go-to-market.
- **Example**: Canva chose social media managers as an early audience because they showed unusually strong excitement and urgent need.

### Blank-Page Onboarding Reduction
- **When to use**: When first use is intimidating or confusing, especially in creative tools.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the first friction point.
  2. Replace open-ended starts with a guided first action.
  3. Make the first action easy and surprising.
  4. Chain small steps into a visible accomplishment.
  5. Use the early win to build confidence.
- **Example**: Canva guided users to search for a monkey, drag an image, and complete a few steps to make a design.

### End-to-End SEO Product Loop
- **When to use**: When SEO can drive intent-based acquisition for many use cases.
- **Steps**:
  1. Map the jobs your product solves.
  2. Identify search queries tied to those jobs.
  3. Build landing pages that match search intent.
  4. Connect each page to a relevant template or starting point.
  5. Deliver a fast, delightful in-product outcome.
- **Example**: For “Halloween poster,” Canva aimed to rank, land users on a matching page, and route them into a template that quickly produced a finished design.

### Three-Pillar AI Integration Strategy
- **When to use**: When integrating AI without becoming a generic wrapper.
- **Steps**:
  1. Build proprietary AI where you have unique advantage.
  2. Partner externally for commodity capabilities.
  3. Let third parties extend the platform.
  4. Keep the user goal central.
  5. Judge AI by outcome quality and speed.
- **Example**: Canva builds some AI in-house, partners with OpenAI and RunwayML, and supports AI developers through its app ecosystem.

## Core Advice
- **When you feel pressure to launch an MVP fast**: don’t launch until it creates real delight, not just utility — because word of mouth depends on users loving the experience.
- **When you don’t know your initial customer**: test multiple segments and choose the one with the strongest emotional reaction — because excitement reveals both pain and pull.
- **When activation is low because first use feels overwhelming**: replace the blank page with tiny guided steps — because early wins reduce fear and build confidence.
- **When people are holding onto responsibilities too long**: encourage them to give away their Legos — because scaling requires leverage, not personal ownership of everything.
- **When you need to grow people in a fast-scaling org**: use craft-specific coaches — because same-discipline guidance scales development better than classic management.
- **When you’re tempted to copy another company’s PM model**: design your operating model from first principles — because culture and product type should shape the system.
- **When you want SEO to matter**: treat it as an end-to-end product loop — because traffic alone doesn’t create growth unless the post-click experience delivers.
- **When expanding internationally**: localize early and deeply — because global markets can reshape product direction and unlock major growth.
- **When fundraising pressure is high**: build toward profitability — because strategic independence improves your negotiating position.
- **When adding AI features**: integrate AI only where it improves user outcomes — because generic AI wrappers are not durable products.

## Contrarian Takes
- **Conventional**: Launch as fast as possible, even if the MVP is rough. → **Their view**: The MVP must be joyful enough to create excitement and word of mouth — because a weak first impression can kill the growth engine.
- **Conventional**: Don’t build for yourself. → **Their view**: Building for yourself can be a strength if you truly share the customer’s problem — because it improves speed and quality judgment.
- **Conventional**: Great companies need traditional managers. → **Their view**: Coaching can outperform management for craft development — because it scales skill-building better.
- **Conventional**: Copy best-practice PM models from top companies. → **Their view**: Build a PM model that fits your culture and product — because one company’s system may not transfer.
- **Conventional**: AI strategy means shipping chatbot-style features quickly. → **Their view**: AI should be embedded into workflows and outcomes — because durable value comes from helping users do real work better.
- **Conventional**: Freemium is mainly a pricing tactic. → **Their view**: Freemium can be mission-aligned — because access and business model can reinforce each other.
