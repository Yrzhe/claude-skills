# Design

## Overview
Design is not just visual polish. In Lenny’s ecosystem, design covers product experience, decision-making, collaboration, systems thinking, and how teams turn user insight into better products.

The strongest theme is that good design is intentional: it starts with the user problem, uses the right mix of human judgment and data, and is tightly connected to product, growth, and business outcomes.

## Key Frameworks

### Curiosity Loops (Ada Chen Rekhi)
- **When to use**: When you need better input for an important decision and want contextual advice rather than generic opinions.
- **Steps**:
  1. Form a good question that is specific, unbiased, and asks for rationale.
  2. Choose the right people: a mix of subject matter experts and people who know you well.
  3. Make the ask lightweight so it is easy to respond to quickly.
  4. Collect and process the responses, looking for surprises, disagreements, and missing angles.
  5. Close the loop by thanking people and sharing how their input affected your decision.
- **Example**: Ada emailed about 10–11 people with nine possible topics and asked them to pick the top two or three that resonated and explain why.

### Values Exercise / Inner Scorecard (Ada Chen Rekhi)
- **When to use**: When making career or life decisions that may be distorted by status, money, titles, or others’ expectations.
- **Steps**:
  1. Review a list of possible values and select the ones that resonate.
  2. Group and narrow them down into a smaller set.
  3. Stack-rank the most important values for your current season of life.
  4. Translate them into 3–5 sentences that define your personal scorecard.
  5. Use that scorecard to evaluate decisions and compare them against external pressures.
- **Example**: Ada used this to reject a high-profile job that looked great on paper but violated her top values.

### Explore and Exploit (Ada Chen Rekhi)
- **When to use**: When planning your early career or deciding whether to broaden your experiences versus go deeper in a proven path.
- **Steps**:
  1. Start in exploration mode early in your career to test different environments, functions, and industries.
  2. Treat each move as a hypothesis-driven experiment about what you like and what fits.
  3. Extract explicit learnings from each role about your strengths, preferences, and gaps.
  4. Switch into exploit mode once you find a rich area worth going deeper on.
  5. Use exploit mode intentionally to gain specific capabilities, not just titles.
- **Example**: Ada used big-company time to learn growth, subscriptions, and pricing so she could become a better founder.

### Don’t Be the Frog (Ada Chen Rekhi)
- **When to use**: When deciding whether to stay in a role that feels increasingly misaligned or stagnant.
- **Steps**:
  1. Pay attention to whether conditions are improving or deteriorating over time.
  2. Assess whether you are still learning, growing, and being challenged.
  3. Identify whether discomfort is temporary and productive or a sign of inertia and drift.
  4. Try to change the situation proactively through conversations, projects, or role shaping.
  5. If the environment cannot provide growth, use the extra capacity to build skills or relationships elsewhere.
- **Example**: Ada warned against staying too long because each issue seems small in isolation until you realize you have stopped learning.

### Eating Your Vegetables (Ada Chen Rekhi)
- **When to use**: When you dislike a skill or activity and need to determine whether you truly dislike it or are just inexperienced and bad at it.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify a skill that matters for your goals but feels uncomfortable.
  2. Commit to repeated exposure rather than judging it after one attempt.
  3. Create a concrete rule or ritual that forces practice.
  4. Repeat enough times to separate beginner discomfort from genuine dislike.
  5. Reflect on whether the skill becomes easier, more useful, or more enjoyable over time.
- **Example**: Ada forced herself to network weekly until it became a durable strength.

### Human-in-the-loop Algorithm Design (Adriel Frederick)
- **When to use**: When building products that rely heavily on machine learning, optimization, pricing, ranking, or other algorithmic systems.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the product intent and strategic goals before optimizing anything.
  2. Decide explicitly what decisions the algorithm should make versus what decisions humans should make.
  3. Identify constraints and judgment calls algorithms cannot reliably infer.
  4. Design interfaces and tools that give humans the right information to make strategic decisions.
  5. Use the algorithm to amplify human intent at scale rather than operate as an unchecked black box.
  6. Continuously observe outcomes and refine the division of responsibility.
- **Example**: At Lyft, pricing needed people in the loop because local teams could see snowstorms, taxes, and competitor moves that models could miss.

### Marginal User Analysis (Adriel Frederick)
- **When to use**: When trying to improve activation, signup conversion, onboarding, or growth in products with large user funnels.
- **Steps**:
  1. Find the user segment that is just on the cusp of succeeding but currently struggles to convert.
  2. Also inspect the worst-case user experience to reveal the full set of product failures.
  3. Observe real users directly instead of relying only on funnel data.
  4. List all friction points in their experience, including issues not visible in the funnel.
  5. Prioritize the barriers that are both important and realistically fixable.
  6. Iterate on making the product “stupid easy” for that user to succeed.
- **Example**: At Facebook, Adriel found issues like wrong language defaults and phone formatting problems in high-traffic, low-conversion markets.

### Gamification Pillars: Core Loop, Metagame, Profile (Albert Cheng)
- **When to use**: When building habit-forming products that require repeated engagement over time.
- **Steps**:
  1. Tighten the core loop so users can complete a meaningful action, get a reward, and be prompted to return.
  2. Build a metagame with longer-term goals such as leaderboards, achievements, or progression paths.
  3. Create a profile that reflects user investment and progress over time.
  4. Ensure all three reinforce each other to sustain motivation.
- **Example**: At Duolingo, lessons and streaks formed the core loop, while paths, leaderboards, and profiles reinforced long-term engagement.

## Decision Guide
- If you need better input for a big decision, consider **Curiosity Loops** because it surfaces contextual advice and blind spots — per **Ada Chen Rekhi**.
- If a career move looks impressive but feels off, use the **Values Exercise / Inner Scorecard** because it filters out status pressure — per **Ada Chen Rekhi**.
- If you are early in your career and unsure what fits, use **Explore and Exploit** because exploration is the fastest way to learn your strengths and preferences — per **Ada Chen Rekhi**.
- If a role feels increasingly misaligned, do a **trend check** with **Don’t Be the Frog** because small frustrations compound into stagnation — per **Ada Chen Rekhi**.
- If you dislike a skill but it matters for your goals, use **Eating Your Vegetables** because repeated exposure distinguishes discomfort from true dislike — per **Ada Chen Rekhi**.
- If you are designing an ML-heavy product, use **Human-in-the-loop Algorithm Design** because algorithms need human judgment for intent and context — per **Adriel Frederick**.
- If activation or conversion is weak, use **Marginal User Analysis** because funnel data alone will not reveal the real friction — per **Adriel Frederick**.
- If growth is slow, focus on **core loop simplification** before hacks because durable growth comes from making value easier to reach — per **Adriel Frederick**.
- If you are building a habit product, use the **Gamification Pillars** because repeated engagement needs a core loop plus long-term motivation — per **Albert Cheng**.
- If you are deciding whether to invest in a new channel, use **channel DNA analysis** because trendiness is not enough; fit and durability matter — per **Adam Grenier**.
- If you are hiring a senior marketing leader, assess for **T-shaped breadth plus chaos tolerance** because startup leaders need depth, range, and adaptability — per **Adam Grenier**.
- If teams are disagreeing cross-functionally, use an **improv-style “Yes, and” approach** because it preserves momentum and respects multiple truths — per **Adam Grenier**.

## Expert Consensus
- Start with the user problem, not the solution.
- Design should be tied to business outcomes, not treated as decoration.
- Human judgment still matters, especially where context, intent, and tradeoffs are important.
- The best systems are iterative: observe, learn, refine, repeat.
- Good design often means reducing friction until the right action becomes obvious and easy.

## Points of Debate
- **Human judgment vs. algorithmic optimization**
  - **Human-in-the-loop** (Adriel Frederick): algorithms are powerful but should not make strategic decisions alone.
  - **More automation**: some teams want to push autonomy further once models are reliable.
- **Exploration vs. exploitation**
  - **Explore first** (Ada Chen Rekhi): early career should prioritize learning and discovery.
  - **Exploit sooner**: once a strong path is found, depth and compounding matter more than breadth.
- **Big bets vs. incremental experiments**
  - **Cannonballs** (Adriel Frederick): major product changes can matter more than many small tests.
  - **Lead bullets**: smaller experiments reduce risk and help refine direction.
- **Separate innovation teams vs. integrated teams**
  - **Integrated model** (Adriel Frederick): innovation must feel core to avoid “organ rejection.”
  - **Protected special teams**: some organizations still isolate innovation to shield it from the core business.
