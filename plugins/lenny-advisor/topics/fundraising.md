# Fundraising

## Overview
Fundraising is not just about getting cash in the bank. It is about choosing the right capital structure, timing, investors, and narrative so the company can grow without losing control of its future. The best advice from Lenny’s ecosystem emphasizes leverage: raise only when the capital materially improves your odds, and understand the long-term cost of taking it.

## Key Frameworks

### Fundraising and capital decision framework (Ryan Hoover)
- **When to use**: When deciding whether to raise money at all.
- **Steps**:
  1. Ask why you need to raise and what the money will be used for.
  2. Check whether you can bootstrap longer or grow with less capital.
  3. Evaluate the long-term implications of dilution, investor expectations, and pace.
  4. Choose investors who fit your stage and goals.
  5. Remember that fundraising can create a treadmill that is hard to exit.
- **Example**: Product Hunt raised because it needed to hire and the founder lacked personal capital, but he said he might not raise for a similar company today.

### Conservative annual plan with milestone-based unlocks (Sahil Mansuri)
- **When to use**: When the market is volatile or visibility is low.
- **Steps**:
  1. Set a conservative baseline plan for the year.
  2. Define short-term milestones or checkpoints.
  3. Pre-agree on what happens if milestones are hit or missed.
  4. Unlock more spend or growth only when targets are met.
  5. Reforecast regularly instead of defending the original plan.
- **Example**: If last year’s revenue was $10M, plan for $9M next year, then use Q1 performance to decide whether to raise or lower the rest-of-year forecast.

### Retention-first sales compensation design (Sahil Mansuri)
- **When to use**: When fundraising depends on showing durable unit economics and customer quality.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify the metrics that truly matter to the business.
  2. Reward reps for renewals, retention, and expansion, not just new bookings.
  3. Add kickers for customers who renew or expand.
  4. Measure rep performance by the quality of accounts they bring in.
  5. Align incentives across customer, rep, and business.
- **Example**: A rep who closes fewer deals but brings in customers that renew and expand should be rewarded more than a rep with high churn.

### Hypothesis-to-data product strategy (Mayur Kamat)
- **When to use**: When fundraising depends on proving growth, conversion, or retention quickly.
- **Steps**:
  1. Form a clear hypothesis about the expected impact.
  2. Design a fast experiment to test it.
  3. Use the data to scale or stop the idea.
  4. Repeat rapidly so strategy emerges from what works.
- **Example**: At N26, dashboards and p-values were used to decide, “This is working. Let’s do more of these.”

### Experimentation as product science (Mayur Kamat)
- **When to use**: When investors want evidence, not opinions.
- **Steps**:
  1. Instrument the product so ideas can be tested.
  2. Use experiments to create a shared source of truth.
  3. Make decisions based on evidence rather than hierarchy.
  4. Build a culture where PMs use a scientific method.
- **Example**: Experiments let teams answer critics with, “We have done this experiment six times and it has failed.”

### The Three Elements of Breakthrough Startup Ideas (Mike Maples Jr.)
- **When to use**: When fundraising requires a compelling “why now” and future potential.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify an external inflection.
  2. Develop a non-obvious insight that changes behavior.
  3. Check founder-future fit.
  4. Stress test whether the idea is truly from the future.
  5. Iterate if the insight is right but the first product expression is wrong.
- **Example**: Lyft combined the iPhone 4S GPS chip inflection with the insight of “Airbnb for cars.”

### Movement-Based Go-to-Market (Mike Maples Jr.)
- **When to use**: When fundraising benefits from early believers, not just customers.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define a higher-purpose future people can believe in.
  2. Frame the current world as inadequate.
  3. Show why the alternative is better for a specific group.
  4. Recruit emotionally committed early believers.
  5. Let the movement accumulate until the new view becomes conventional.
- **Example**: Airbnb’s “live like a local” framing created a movement around a different travel experience.

### Column B thinking (Melanie Perkins)
- **When to use**: When fundraising is tied to a long-term vision, not a short-term feature.
- **Steps**:
  1. Imagine the ideal future you want to exist in.
  2. Define what the world should look like in 10, 20, or 50 years.
  3. Work backward from that future.
  4. Translate the vision into small, compounding steps.
- **Example**: Canva started from the belief that design would become online, collaborative, and simple.

## Decision Guide
- If you are unsure whether you need capital, consider the **fundraising and capital decision framework** because the first question is whether the money changes the company’s odds — per **Ryan Hoover**.
- If you have enough runway to choose timing, use a **conservative annual plan with milestone-based unlocks** because it preserves leverage and avoids overcommitting — per **Sahil Mansuri**.
- If your business depends on durable revenue quality, use **retention-first sales compensation design** because investors care about churn, expansion, and repeatability — per **Sahil Mansuri**.
- If your product decisions are still opinion-driven, use **experimentation as product science** because data creates credibility in a fundraise — per **Mayur Kamat**.
- If you need to show traction fast, use **hypothesis-to-data product strategy** because investors respond to clear learning loops and measurable progress — per **Mayur Kamat**.
- If your pitch lacks a strong “why now,” use **the three elements of breakthrough startup ideas** because inflections and founder-future fit make the story investable — per **Mike Maples Jr.**.
- If you are building a category, use **movement-based go-to-market** because investors back companies that can recruit believers, not just users — per **Mike Maples Jr.**.
- If your company vision feels incremental, use **Column B thinking** because future-first narratives are more compelling than current-constraint narratives — per **Melanie Perkins**.
- If you are in a volatile market, use **milestone-based unlocks** because it lets you raise or spend in stages instead of betting everything upfront — per **Sahil Mansuri**.
- If your company is early and you can bootstrap, delay fundraising because every round adds dilution and expectations — per **Ryan Hoover**.
- If your business needs proof before scale, run smaller experiments first because a strong learning loop is often more valuable than a big raise — per **Mayur Kamat**.
- If you are pitching a long-horizon company, anchor the story in a future state because investors fund compounding visions, not just present-day features — per **Melanie Perkins**.

## Expert Consensus
- Fundraising should be a strategic choice, not a default.
- Capital is most useful when it accelerates a clear, validated path.
- The quality of the story matters: investors want a credible future, not just current traction.
- Fast learning and measurable progress strengthen fundraising more than vague ambition.
- The best companies use capital to compound, not to mask weak fundamentals.

## Points of Debate
- **Raise early vs. bootstrap longer**
  - **Raise early** (implied by growth-first operators): capital can buy speed, hiring, and market capture.
  - **Bootstrap longer** (Ryan Hoover): fundraising creates dilution and a treadmill that is hard to exit.
- **Big vision vs. milestone realism**
  - **Big vision** (Melanie Perkins): ambitious goals create momentum and direction.
  - **Milestone realism** (Sahil Mansuri): conservative planning and staged unlocks reduce downside in uncertain markets.
- **Strategy-first vs. experiment-first**
  - **Strategy-first**: some founders want a clear narrative before testing.
  - **Experiment-first** (Mayur Kamat): rapid tests should determine what scales; strategy should emerge from evidence.
- **Fundraising as validation vs. fundraising as necessity**
  - **Validation view**: a raise signals market belief and can help recruiting.
  - **Necessity view** (Ryan Hoover): only raise when the money materially changes the company’s ability to win.

## Key Metrics & Benchmarks
- Founders typically fundraise only **5–10 times** in their life.
- A VC partner generally makes only **1–3 early-stage investments per year**.
- A Series A process often involves speaking to **50–60 funds**.
- YC estimates you may need to speak to **30 funds to get one term sheet**.
- It can take **3–6 weeks** from signing a term sheet to money in the bank.
- It is hard to keep a term sheet open for more than **1–2 weeks**.
- If runway is only **4–6 months**, consider a bridge round from inside investors.
- Founders should ideally have at least **8 months of runway** before starting a raise.
