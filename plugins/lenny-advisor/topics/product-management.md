# Product Management

## Overview
Product management is about connecting users to value, then using data, judgment, and cross-functional leadership to improve that connection over time. The strongest product managers do not just ship features; they define the problem, choose the right tradeoffs, and build systems that scale learning and execution.

This topic matters because most product failures come from solving the wrong problem, optimizing the wrong metric, or hiring the wrong kind of leader for the stage of the company.

## Key Frameworks

### Growth Competency Model (Adam Fishman)
- **When to use**: Hiring, evaluating, coaching, or structuring a growth team.
- **Steps**:
  1. Assess the team’s current gaps instead of searching for a mythical all-in-one growth unicorn.
  2. Evaluate candidates across four competency areas: growth execution, customer knowledge, growth strategy, and communication and influence.
  3. Within growth execution, assess channel fluency, experimentation, and ability to productize learnings.
  4. Within customer knowledge, assess data fluency/instrumentation, user psychology, and ability to learn from customers over time.
  5. Within growth strategy, assess growth loop modeling, capital allocation and forecasting, and prioritization/roadmapping.
  6. Within communication and influence, assess strategic communication, team leadership, and stakeholder management.
  7. Use the model both forward-looking for hiring and backward-looking for performance feedback and development.
- **Example**: At Wyzant, founders wanted a “silver bullet” growth answer, but the real need was a strategy for adding growth loops and a system for executing them.

### Hire for Team Balance, Not a Unicorn (Adam Fishman)
- **When to use**: Building an early growth team or making the first growth hire.
- **Steps**:
  1. Map which competencies already exist on the team.
  2. Identify the missing capabilities rather than pattern-matching to a famous or senior operator.
  3. Prefer candidates who are strong in foundational areas like execution and customer knowledge.
  4. Support less-experienced hires with mentorship, advisors, and external education if internal coaching is weak.
  5. Add specialists later once the core growth system is in place.
- **Example**: Fishman recommends hiring smart, driven people with strong execution and customer knowledge, then developing them.

### Painter vs. Architect vs. Surgeon (Adam Fishman)
- **When to use**: Deciding what type of growth hire to make based on company stage and problem type.
- **Steps**:
  1. Determine whether you need broad foundational growth building or narrow expert optimization.
  2. Hire broad, adaptable builders first to establish the system.
  3. Only bring in a specialist “surgeon” once you know the area is important and needs deep expertise.
  4. Avoid making a narrow specialist your first growth hire unless the company’s immediate bottleneck is clearly in that specialty.
- **Example**: SEO and paid growth can justify specialists later, but usually not as the first growth hire.

### Onboarding as Promise Delivery (Adam Fishman)
- **When to use**: Designing or improving onboarding, activation, and early retention.
- **Steps**:
  1. Treat onboarding as the first moment where the product must deliver on the brand promise made in the market.
  2. Recognize that onboarding is touched by 100% of users, making it the highest-leverage universal product surface.
  3. Use the high motivation of new users to learn about them and qualify them appropriately.
  4. Optimize onboarding not just for conversion, but for habit formation and retention.
  5. Revisit onboarding when you gain genuinely new customer or product insights, not just for cosmetic redesigns.
- **Example**: At Patreon, onboarding improvements and human-guided interventions improved creators’ first- and second-month revenue by 25%.

### Human Intervention to Productized Defaults (Adam Fishman)
- **When to use**: When a manual onboarding or sales assist process is producing strong results and you want to scale it.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify high-potential users during onboarding using observable signals.
  2. Route those users to humans who can guide them to successful setup and behavior.
  3. Study what the humans consistently do that improves outcomes.
  4. Translate those successful behaviors into product defaults, recommendations, and guardrails.
  5. Preserve user choice, but make the right path easier and the wrong path harder.
- **Example**: Patreon routed high-potential creators to humans, then productized the learnings into opinionated defaults like recommended tier counts and pricing structures.

### Proxy Metrics for Onboarding Experiments (Adam Fishman)
- **When to use**: When onboarding changes affect long-term retention or LTV, but you cannot wait months for results.
- **Steps**:
  1. Identify an early behavior that strongly predicts long-term success.
  2. Use that proxy metric to evaluate onboarding experiments quickly.
  3. Supplement quantitative proxies with qualitative review of who is getting through onboarding and what behaviors they exhibit.
  4. Balance conversion and retention tradeoffs rather than maximizing top-of-funnel completion alone.
- **Example**: At Patreon, velocity to first patron and first $100 processed were early indicators of eventual creator success.

### Emerging Acquisition Channel Evaluation Framework (Adam Grenier)
- **When to use**: Deciding whether to invest in a new or emerging acquisition channel like TikTok, Clubhouse, OTT, influencer marketing, or VR.
- **Steps**:
  1. Assess overlap between customer needs, company goals, and the channel’s core strengths.
  2. Analyze the channel DNA: lifecycle stage, instability, and likelihood of product or rule changes.
  3. Understand the channel’s monetization strategy and whether your company can align with it.
  4. Evaluate your own company DNA: risk tolerance, staffing capacity, and whether core channels are already working.
  5. Scope the test proportionally: start small, define the signal, and avoid overcommitting before momentum appears.
- **Example**: Spotify exploring Clubhouse fit better than Paparazzi because Clubhouse’s audio-first, discovery-oriented format aligned with Spotify’s goals.

### Human-in-the-loop Algorithm Design (Adriel Frederick)
- **When to use**: Building products that rely heavily on machine learning, optimization, pricing, ranking, or other algorithmic systems.
- **Steps**:
  1. Define the product intent and strategic goals before optimizing anything.
  2. Decide explicitly what decisions the algorithm should make versus what decisions humans should make.
  3. Identify the constraints and judgment calls algorithms cannot reliably infer.
  4. Design interfaces and tools that give humans the right information to make strategic decisions.
  5. Use the algorithm to amplify human intent at scale rather than operate as an unchecked black box.
  6. Continuously observe outcomes and refine the division of responsibility.
- **Example**: At Lyft, pricing systems needed people in the loop because local teams had to respond to snowstorms, fuel price changes, taxes, and competitive moves.

## Decision Guide
- If you are hiring your first growth leader, consider the **Growth Competency Model** because it forces a gap analysis instead of a vague unicorn search — per **Adam Fishman**.
- If your team is small and missing core growth execution, hire for **team balance, not a unicorn** because foundational builders matter more than senior pedigree — per **Adam Fishman**.
- If the bottleneck is a deep specialty like SEO or paid acquisition, use **Painter vs. Architect vs. Surgeon** because specialists are best after the system exists — per **Adam Fishman**.
- If onboarding conversion is high but retention is weak, use **Onboarding as Promise Delivery** because onboarding should qualify users and set habits, not just reduce friction — per **Adam Fishman**.
- If manual onboarding or sales-assist is working well, use **Human Intervention to Productized Defaults** because humans reveal the winning behaviors to scale — per **Adam Fishman**.
- If onboarding experiments take too long to measure, use **Proxy Metrics for Onboarding Experiments** because early predictors let you move faster without losing sight of LTV — per **Adam Fishman**.
- If you are evaluating a trendy new acquisition channel, use the **Emerging Acquisition Channel Evaluation Framework** because channel fit matters more than hype — per **Adam Grenier**.
- If a channel looks promising but seems unstable, use **Channel DNA Analysis** because early channels often change quickly and require ongoing maintenance — per **Adam Grenier**.
- If your company is product-led and marketing feels disconnected, use the **Growth CMO Operating Model** because product and marketing should operate as one system — per **Adam Grenier**.
- If you are hiring a senior marketing leader, use the **T-Shaped Marketing Leader Assessment** because startup leaders need depth plus adaptability — per **Adam Grenier**.
- If your product depends on ML or ranking, use **Human-in-the-loop Algorithm Design** because product managers must define where judgment stays human — per **Adriel Frederick**.
- If growth is slow and the funnel is unclear, use **Marginal user analysis** because direct observation reveals friction metrics miss — per **Adriel Frederick**.

## Expert Consensus
- Great product work starts with the user problem, not the solution.
- The best PMs and growth leaders combine data fluency with strong judgment.
- Early-stage teams usually need adaptable builders before narrow specialists.
- Onboarding is one of the highest-leverage surfaces because every user touches it.
- Durable growth comes from improving core value delivery, not from hacks.

## Points of Debate
- **Hire senior growth leaders vs. earlier-career builders** (Adam Fishman): Conventional wisdom favors seniority; Fishman argues many early teams need strong execution and customer knowledge more than a “done it before” operator.
- **Optimize onboarding for conversion vs. retention** (Adam Fishman): One view says remove friction; the other says add the right friction to improve user quality and downstream outcomes.
- **Manual processes vs. productization** (Adam Fishman): Some see manual intervention as a scaling failure; Fishman sees it as a learning engine that can reveal what should be productized.
- **Channel chasing vs. PMF revalidation** (Adam Grenier): Common advice is to find a new channel when growth slows; Grenier argues you should first assume product-market fit may have shifted.
- **Algorithm-first vs. human-in-the-loop** (Adriel Frederick): Pure automation is tempting, but Frederick argues product intent and human judgment must shape algorithmic systems.

## Key Metrics & Benchmarks
- **Patreon onboarding**: first- and second-month creator revenue improved by **25%** after onboarding and human-guided interventions.
- **Grammarly freemium sampling**: upgrade rates **nearly doubled** when premium suggestions were sampled inside the free experience.
- **Chess.com post-loss coaching**: game reviews increased **25%**, subscriptions **20%**, and retention improved after changing the post-game experience.
- **Lenny’s audience**: newsletter reached **1 million subscribers**; Slack community has **20,000+ members**.
- **Content production**: newsletter posts take **10–30 hours** each; podcast episodes take **3–5 hours**.
